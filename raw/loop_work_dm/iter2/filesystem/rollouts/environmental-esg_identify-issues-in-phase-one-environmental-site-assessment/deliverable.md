       1. Executive Summary  
      This memorandum reports the environmental issues identified in the due-diligence document production for Cornerstone Industrial Partners LLC’s (    “    Cornerstone    ”         or the         “    Buyer    ”    ) proposed acquisition of the former Breckenridge Manufacturing Co. (    “    Breckenridge    ”         or the         “    Seller    ”    ) industrial property at 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo, Ohio (the         “    Toledo Property    ”    ). The production also contains a separate, self-contained set of environmental documents for a             different         Cornerstone transaction — the acquisition of 2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, Ohio from Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc. (the         “    Dayton Property    ”    ). Both sets have been reviewed in full and are reported below; the commingling of two unrelated transaction files in a single production is itself a material due-diligence file-integrity issue (Issue 1).  
      The controlling transaction documents — the Asset Purchase Agreement dated September 1, 2024 (the         “    Purchase Agreement    ”    ) and Great Lakes Capital Bank’s environmental due-diligence requirements letter dated September 10, 2024 (the         “    Lender Letter    ”    ) — both unambiguously identify the Toledo Property as the subject of         “    the proposed acquisition.    ”         The Greenfield Environmental Consultants Inc. (    “    Greenfield    ”    ) Phase I ESA for the Toledo Property (ASTM E1527-21, dated October 25, 2024) is consistent with those documents. The Dayton documents (a Terravista Environmental Consulting Inc. Phase I ESA dated April 18, 2025, a 2004 UST closure report, and a GeoSearch database summary) describe a different seller, a different purchase price ($4,200,000), different counsel (Bridgewell & Hatch LLP), and a different ASTM standard (E1527-13), and post-date the Toledo closing schedule.  
          Headline conclusions (Toledo Property — the proposed acquisition):  
          The Greenfield Phase I ESA identified             two on-site Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs)    : (i) former trichloroethylene (TCE) vapor-degreasing operations from 1961–1992, including an elevated PID reading of             42.3 ppm         at a floor crack and an abandoned, leaking         “    WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE    ”         drum; and (ii) former hexavalent-chromium and nickel electroplating from 1965–2004, with visible chromium staining and a sump containing liquid with an iridescent sheen. Both require a Phase II ESA.  
          The most serious defect in the Phase I ESA is an             omission    : Greenfield             did not classify the off-site tetrachloroethylene (PCE) groundwater plume         from the former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility (4900 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, immediately north and hydraulically upgradient) as a REC, even though PCE was detected at             87 µg/L at monitoring well MW-7 — approximately 120 feet north of the property boundary — which is 17.4 times the Ohio groundwater standard of 5 µg/L    , the plume is documented as migrating south-southeast toward the Toledo Property, the property lies within a designated Ohio EPA Groundwater Management Zone, and the 2018 VAP Covenant Not to Sue covers only the dry-cleaner parcel. The Lender Letter expressly requires that off-site plume migration be identified as a REC. This omission is rated             CRITICAL         (Issue 10).  
          The Seller’s representations in §§ 4.12 and 4.13 of the Purchase Agreement appear to be             breached or inaccurate         in multiple respects: Schedule 4.13 omits at least two historical USTs (a 2,000-gallon waste-solvent UST and a 500-gallon heating-oil UST, both of unknown status and possibly still in place); Schedule 4.12(c) omits the 2003 Ohio EPA Notice of Violation and resulting consent order; and § 4.12(b)’s         “    at all times material compliance    ”         representation is contradicted by that NOV and by the absence of any hazardous-waste manifest for 39 years of electroplating (1965–2003). The Seller’s principal denied knowledge of the 1988 solvent spill that his own employees cleaned up, which may support a fraud/intentional-misrepresentation claim piercing the indemnification cap and survival period (Issue 15, Issue 16).  
          The indemnification package is             likely insufficient         for the contamination profile: Pre-Closing Environmental Liabilities are capped at             $1,750,000 (20% of price)         and survive only             three (3) years    . TCE and hexavalent-chromium remediation routinely exceeds that figure and can take longer than three years to manifest. The Lender Letter expressly refuses to rely on seller indemnity alone.  
          The Lender’s conditional requirements are             fully triggered         by the RECs: a Phase II ESA with quantified remediation cost estimates, Pollution Legal Liability (PLL) insurance of             not less than $5,000,000         for             10 years    , a remediation escrow at             125%         of the most-likely estimate if it exceeds $250,000, a remediation plan if costs exceed $500,000, and a vapor-intrusion assessment (TCE and PCE are both VOCs). Unresolved data gaps could cause the Lender to decline the loan entirely.  
          The Buyer’s contractual termination right under § 6.3(b) — exercisable if RECs could cause Remediation Costs exceeding             $500,000    , by written notice no later than             5:00 p.m. Eastern on October 31, 2024         — was available on the face of the Greenfield report (dated October 25, 2024). Whether that notice was given is a critical open item; if it was not, the termination right was             automatically waived         (§ 6.3(c)), although indemnification rights survive.  
          Headline conclusions (Dayton Property — separate transaction, documents included in the production):  
          The Terravista Phase I ESA identified two on-site RECs (former above-ground storage tanks in the western yard; Building 2 electroplating floor staining) and classified the former Central Valley Chemical Co. operations in Building 3 as a Historical REC (HREC).  
          The Central Valley Chemical HREC classification is             likely incorrect (CRITICAL, Issue 28)    : the 2011 VAP No Further Action (NFA) letter relied upon covers only         “    Parcel A    ”         (a 2.3-acre southern UST area) and expressly         “    does not address environmental conditions in areas of the Subject Property outside the Parcel A boundary.    ”         Building 3 is outside Parcel A. The 1981 Ohio EPA enforcement action against Central Valley Chemical is unresolved, the 1971 aerial photograph shows ~100+ drums with no secondary containment on the north side of Building 3, and chlorinated solvents were handled there. This condition may be an unaddressed REC, not an HREC.  
          The 2004 UST closure report shows that             Tank 3 (4,000-gallon waste-oil UST) closure was never completed    : the tank had a through-wall perforation and stained, odorous soil, seven confirmation samples were collected, but the analytical results were         “    pending    ”         at report time and             no supplemental report was ever located         in the Seller’s files, the consultant’s files, or Ohio EPA records. No Tank 3 closure letter has been produced (Issue 29).  
          A cross-document data-integrity conflict exists:             EPA RCRA ID OHD098712345 is assigned to two different facilities         — Valtec (Dayton) in the Dayton documents and Great Lakes Metal Finishing Inc. (4860 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo, an adjacent property) in the Toledo EDR. EPA RCRA IDs are intended to be unique; this conflict undermines the reliability of both database reviews (Issue 2).

         5. Detailed Findings — Document-Set Integrity & Cross-Document Conflicts  
       Issue 1 — Two unrelated transaction files commingled in a single production [HIGH]  
          Description/Evidence.         The production contains two complete, self-contained environmental due-diligence files for two different Cornerstone acquisitions of two different properties from two different sellers:  
              Toledo Property         — 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo, Lucas County, Ohio 43611 (Lucas County Parcel Nos. 18-04562-001/002/003, ~14.7 acres); seller Breckenridge Manufacturing Co.; purchase price $8,750,000; lender Great Lakes Capital Bank (Loan No. GLCB-2024-08173, $6,125,000); counsel Whitfield & Crane LLP (Sarah Castellano); Phase I ESA by Greenfield (ASTM E1527-21, dated October 25, 2024); Purchase Agreement dated September 1, 2024; scheduled closing December 15, 2024.  
              Dayton Property         — 2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, Montgomery County, Ohio 45414 (Tax Parcel R72-04410-0025, ~14.7 acres); seller Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc.; purchase price $4,200,000; no lender identified in the production; counsel Bridgewell & Hatch LLP (Nadia Khoury); Phase I ESA by Terravista (ASTM E1527-13, dated April 18, 2025).  
      The Purchase Agreement and the Lender Letter — the two documents that define         “    the proposed acquisition    ”         — both identify the Toledo Property. The Dayton documents describe a separate transaction that post-dates the Toledo closing schedule (the Terravista ESA is dated April 18, 2025). Notably, both properties are approximately 14.7 acres, a coincidence that may have contributed to the commingling.  
          Consequence.         Reliance on the wrong file for the wrong property would produce materially incorrect due-diligence conclusions (e.g., applying the Dayton VAP/NFA analysis to the Toledo property, or vice versa). The commingling also creates the data-integrity conflict in Issue 2.  
          Recommendation.         Immediately confirm with Cornerstone which transaction is the subject of this engagement. Segregate the two files. If both are live Cornerstone acquisitions, prepare separate issues memos; do not cross-apply findings. All remaining findings below are labeled by property.  
        Issue 2 — Duplicate EPA RCRA ID OHD098712345 assigned to two different facilities [HIGH]  
          Description/Evidence.         The same EPA RCRA Identification Number,             OHD098712345    , is attributed to two different facilities in two different documents:  
              Dayton documents:         The Terravista Phase I ESA (§ 3.3) and the GeoSearch database summary (Listing 1) both list             Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc.         at 2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, as a RCRA Small Quantity Generator under EPA ID             OHD098712345    , with waste codes D006 (cadmium), D007 (chromium), F001 (spent halogenated solvents used in degreasing), and F002 (spent halogenated solvents).  
              Toledo documents:         The EDR database summary (Adjacent Property Listings, Map ID A7) lists             Great Lakes Metal Finishing Inc.         at 4860 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo (east-northeast, ~350 ft from the Toledo Property), as a RCRA Small Quantity Generator under EPA ID             OHD098712345    , with waste codes D007 (chromium) and D008 (lead), active since 1992.  
      EPA RCRA ID numbers are intended to be unique to a single facility. The waste codes also differ between the two attributions (Dayton shows D006/D007/F001/F002; Toledo-adjacent shows D007/D008).  
          Consequence.         A duplicate or erroneously shared ID can conflate the compliance histories of two unrelated facilities and undermine the reliability of the database reviews for both properties. If the ID is erroneously shared, the         “    no violations    ”         compliance history attributed to one facility may actually belong to the other.  
          Recommendation.         Verify the correct EPA RCRA ID for each facility directly with Ohio EPA / U.S. EPA (e-Manifest, RCRAInfo). Do not rely on the database attributions until reconciled. If a data error is confirmed, request corrected database reports for both properties.

         6. Detailed Findings — Toledo Property (the Proposed Acquisition)  
       Issue 4 — REC: Former TCE vapor-degreasing operations (1961–1992); elevated PID; no closure [HIGH]  
          Description/Evidence.         Trichloroethylene (TCE) was used as a vapor-degreasing solvent in the northwest corner of the main manufacturing building from 1961 through approximately 1992 — more than 30 years (Greenfield ESA §§ 1.0, 5.3, 6.3.1, 9.1). The former degreasing area (~4,000 sq ft) exhibits dark floor-slab staining penetrating the concrete and wall staining up to ~3 ft. A photoionization detector (PID) reading of             42.3 ppm         was recorded at a crack in the floor slab, against background readings of             <0.5 ppm         elsewhere in the building — an elevated reading indicative of VOC vapors emanating from beneath the slab. No closure documentation, decommissioning records, or soil/groundwater confirmation samples were located for the degreasing area following the reported cessation of TCE use in 1992. The ventilation system in the area is non-functional (disconnected ductwork, sealed exhaust ports).  
          Consequence.         TCE is a chlorinated solvent and a CERCLA hazardous substance; it is persistent in groundwater, forms dense non-aqueous-phase liquid (DNAPL), and poses a vapor-intrusion risk. A 42.3 ppm PID reading at a floor crack strongly suggests residual subsurface contamination. Thirty-plus years of degreasing with no closure characterization is a material liability for soil, groundwater, and vapor intrusion.  
          Recommendation.         Commission a Phase II ESA (ASTM E1903-19) for the former degreasing area: advance soil borings through the floor slab; install temporary or permanent groundwater monitoring wells; analyze for VOCs including TCE and its degradation products (cis-1,2-DCE, vinyl chloride); and perform a vapor-intrusion assessment (sub-slab soil gas and indoor air) given the elevated PID reading. This is also required by the Lender (Issue 18).  
        Issue 5 — REC: Abandoned, leaking         “    WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE    ”         drum; stained soil [HIGH]  
          Description/Evidence.         A 55-gallon drum labeled         “    WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE    ”         was observed on the ground surface behind (south side of) the maintenance/utility building (Greenfield ESA §§ 6.2.3, 9.1). The drum is approximately half full, with a significantly rusted bottom and evidence of past leakage. Stained soil (~3 ft × 3 ft, dark and oily, vegetation absent) was observed beneath and surrounding the drum. The Seller’s principal, Gerald Breckenridge III, stated the drum had been         “    there for years    ”         and was         “    probably from the early ’90s.    ”         PID readings were not taken at the drum location.  
          Consequence.         An abandoned, leaking drum of waste TCE is an active, ongoing release to soil and a direct-contact and vapor-intrusion source. It is also a RCRA hazardous-waste accumulation violation if still on site at closing (the Buyer would inherit it).  
          Recommendation.         Immediately arrange for characterization, removal, and lawful disposal of the drum and visibly impacted soil by a licensed hazardous-waste contractor             before closing         (so the Buyer does not take title to an active waste violation). Advance soil borings at the drum location and analyze for VOCs. Include this location in the Phase II groundwater program.  
        Issue 6 — REC: 1988 undocumented degreasing-solvent spill [HIGH]  
          Description/Evidence.         The City of Toledo Fire Department incident report dated             March 14, 1988         documents a         “    chemical spill    ”         at 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive involving an         “    unknown quantity of degreasing solvent    ”         released from an overturned drum (Greenfield ESA §§ 5.1.2, 7.2; Ohio EPA response is consistent). Cleanup was performed by Breckenridge Manufacturing employees using absorbent materials. No follow-up inspection by the Fire Department and no environmental assessment were conducted. The spill does not appear in ERNS (EDR summary, ERNS note).  
          Consequence.         An undocumented solvent spill of unknown quantity, with no environmental follow-up, is an uncharacterized release. Combined with Issues 4 and 5, it corroborates a pattern of chlorinated-solvent releases in the northwest portion of the property.  
          Recommendation.         Include the 1988 spill in the Phase II scope (it falls within the former degreasing area / drum-handling areas). Obtain the full Fire Department incident report. Investigate whether any soil was removed and where it was disposed.  
        Issue 7 — REC: Former hexavalent-chromium/nickel electroplating (1965–2004); staining; sump with sheen [HIGH]  
          Description/Evidence.         Electroplating using hexavalent chromium and nickel was conducted in the south wing of the main manufacturing building from 1965 through 2004 — approximately 39 years (Greenfield ESA §§ 1.0, 5.3, 6.3.2, 9.1). The former electroplating room (~8,000 sq ft) exhibits prominent blue-green staining on the concrete floor and lower walls (to ~4 ft), consistent with chromium residues, and the floor shows pitting, etching, and chemical degradation. An in-ground concrete sump (~3 ft × 3 ft, ~3 ft deep) in the center of the room contains approximately             four inches of liquid with a visible iridescent sheen and a greenish tint    ; the source was not determined and the liquid was not sampled. Floor drains connect to the sump. The ventilation system is non-operational.  
          Consequence.         Hexavalent chromium (Cr(VI)) is a potent carcinogen and a CERCLA/RCRA hazardous substance; nickel compounds are also regulated. Visible chromium staining and a sump containing liquid with a sheen over 39 years of operations indicate likely releases through the degraded floor slab to the subsurface. The sump liquid is an uncharacterized waste.  
          Recommendation.         Phase II ESA: characterize the sump liquid (metals including Cr(VI) and total chromium, nickel, VOCs, pH); advance soil borings through and beneath the floor slab in the former electroplating area; install groundwater monitoring wells and analyze for metals (Cr(VI), total Cr, nickel). This is also required by the Lender (Issue 18).  
        Issue 8 — 39-year hazardous-waste manifest gap (1965–2003) [HIGH]  
          Description/Evidence.         Hazardous-waste disposal documentation exists             only for calendar year 2004    : six manifests documenting the disposal of             14,200 pounds         of hazardous waste under RCRA codes             D007 (chromium)         and             F006 (electroplating sludge)    , transported by Tri-County Environmental Hauling Inc. (Greenfield ESA §§ 1.0, 5.3, 7.1, 9.1; Ohio EPA response § 1; EDR summary, RCRA-SQG row).             No manifests, biennial reports, or annual hazardous-waste generation reports are on file with Ohio EPA for any reporting year other than 2004         — despite 39 years of electroplating (1965–2004). Ohio EPA’s response expressly flags this as         “    notable.    ”         The Seller’s principal stated records for 1965–2003 were         “    not available.    ”  
          Consequence.         The absence of disposal documentation for 39 years of chromium-bearing waste generation raises the inference that wastes were not properly manifested, transported, or disposed — a RCRA compliance failure and a potential source of on-site burial, accumulation, or off-site liability. It also means the volume and disposition of historical plating waste is unknown, complicating remediation scoping.  
          Recommendation.         Demand production of all waste records for 1965–2003 (Issue 16). If none exist, document the gap and factor it into remediation-cost estimating (assume worst-case on-site accumulation). Consider whether the gap supports a fraud/intentional-misrepresentation theory (Issue 15).

## 5. Detailed Findings — Document-Set Integrity & Cross-Document Conflicts

### Issue 1 — Two unrelated transaction files commingled in a single production [HIGH]

**Description/Evidence.** The production contains two complete, self-contained environmental due-diligence files for two different Cornerstone acquisitions of two different properties from two different sellers:

- **Toledo Property** — 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo, Lucas County, Ohio 43611 (Lucas County Parcel Nos. 18-04562-001/002/003, ~14.7 acres); seller Breckenridge Manufacturing Co.; purchase price $8,750,000; lender Great Lakes Capital Bank (Loan No. GLCB-2024-08173, $6,125,000); counsel Whitfield & Crane LLP (Sarah Castellano); Phase I ESA by Greenfield (ASTM E1527-21, dated October 25, 2024); Purchase Agreement dated September 1, 2024; scheduled closing December 15, 2024.
- **Dayton Property** — 2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, Montgomery County, Ohio 45414 (Tax Parcel R72-04410-0025, ~14.7 acres); seller Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc.; purchase price $4,200,000; no lender identified in the production; counsel Bridgewell & Hatch LLP (Nadia Khoury); Phase I ESA by Terravista (ASTM E1527-13, dated April 18, 2025).

The Purchase Agreement and the Lender Letter — the two documents that define "the proposed acquisition" — both identify the Toledo Property. The Dayton documents describe a separate transaction that post-dates the Toledo closing schedule (the Terravista ESA is dated April 18, 2025). Notably, both properties are approximately 14.7 acres, a coincidence that may have contributed to the commingling.

**Consequence.** Reliance on the wrong file for the wrong property would produce materially incorrect due-diligence conclusions (e.g., applying the Dayton VAP/NFA analysis to the Toledo property, or vice versa). The commingling also creates the data-integrity conflict in Issue 2.

**Recommendation.** Immediately confirm with Cornerstone which transaction is the subject of this engagement. Segregate the two files. If both are live Cornerstone acquisitions, prepare separate issues memos; do not cross-apply findings. All remaining findings below are labeled by property.

### Issue 2 — Duplicate EPA RCRA ID OHD098712345 assigned to two different facilities [HIGH]

**Description/Evidence.** The same EPA RCRA Identification Number, **OHD098712345**, is attributed to two different facilities in two different documents:

- **Dayton documents:** The Terravista Phase I ESA (§ 3.3) and the GeoSearch database summary (Listing 1) both list **Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc.** at 2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, as a RCRA Small Quantity Generator under EPA ID **OHD098712345**, with waste codes D006 (cadmium), D007 (chromium), F001 (spent halogenated solvents used in degreasing), and F002 (spent halogenated solvents).
- **Toledo documents:** The EDR database summary (Adjacent Property Listings, Map ID A7) lists **Great Lakes Metal Finishing Inc.** at 4860 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo (east-northeast, ~350 ft from the Toledo Property), as a RCRA Small Quantity Generator under EPA ID **OHD098712345**, with waste codes D007 (chromium) and D008 (lead), active since 1992.

EPA RCRA ID numbers are intended to be unique to a single facility. The waste codes also differ between the two attributions (Dayton shows D006/D007/F001/F002; Toledo-adjacent shows D007/D008).

**Consequence.** A duplicate or erroneously shared ID can conflate the compliance histories of two unrelated facilities and undermine the reliability of the database reviews for both properties. If the ID is erroneously shared, the "no violations" compliance history attributed to one facility may actually belong to the other.

**Recommendation.** Verify the correct EPA RCRA ID for each facility directly with Ohio EPA / U.S. EPA (e-Manifest, RCRAInfo). Do not rely on the database attributions until reconciled. If a data error is confirmed, request corrected database reports for both properties.

## 6. Detailed Findings — Toledo Property (the Proposed Acquisition)

### Issue 4 — REC: Former TCE vapor-degreasing operations (1961–1992); elevated PID; no closure [HIGH]

**Description/Evidence.** Trichloroethylene (TCE) was used as a vapor-degreasing solvent in the northwest corner of the main manufacturing building from 1961 through approximately 1992 — more than 30 years (Greenfield ESA §§ 1.0, 5.3, 6.3.1, 9.1). The former degreasing area (~4,000 sq ft) exhibits dark floor-slab staining penetrating the concrete and wall staining up to ~3 ft. A photoionization detector (PID) reading of **42.3 ppm** was recorded at a crack in the floor slab, against background readings of **<0.5 ppm** elsewhere in the building — an elevated reading indicative of VOC vapors emanating from beneath the slab. No closure documentation, decommissioning records, or soil/groundwater confirmation samples were located for the degreasing area following the reported cessation of TCE use in 1992. The ventilation system in the area is non-functional (disconnected ductwork, sealed exhaust ports).

**Consequence.** TCE is a chlorinated solvent and a CERCLA hazardous substance; it is persistent in groundwater, forms dense non-aqueous-phase liquid (DNAPL), and poses a vapor-intrusion risk. A 42.3 ppm PID reading at a floor crack strongly suggests residual subsurface contamination. Thirty-plus years of degreasing with no closure characterization is a material liability for soil, groundwater, and vapor intrusion.

**Recommendation.** Commission a Phase II ESA (ASTM E1903-19) for the former degreasing area: advance soil borings through the floor slab; install temporary or permanent groundwater monitoring wells; analyze for VOCs including TCE and its degradation products (cis-1,2-DCE, vinyl chloride); and perform a vapor-intrusion assessment (sub-slab soil gas and indoor air) given the elevated PID reading. This is also required by the Lender (Issue 18).

### Issue 5 — REC: Abandoned, leaking "WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE" drum; stained soil [HIGH]

**Description/Evidence.** A 55-gallon drum labeled "WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE" was observed on the ground surface behind (south side of) the maintenance/utility building (Greenfield ESA §§ 6.2.3, 9.1). The drum is approximately half full, with a significantly rusted bottom and evidence of past leakage. Stained soil (~3 ft × 3 ft, dark and oily, vegetation absent) was observed beneath and surrounding the drum. The Seller's principal, Gerald Breckenridge III, stated the drum had been "there for years" and was "probably from the early '90s." PID readings were not taken at the drum location.

**Consequence.** An abandoned, leaking drum of waste TCE is an active, ongoing release to soil and a direct-contact and vapor-intrusion source. It is also a RCRA hazardous-waste accumulation violation if still on site at closing (the Buyer would inherit it).

**Recommendation.** Immediately arrange for characterization, removal, and lawful disposal of the drum and visibly impacted soil by a licensed hazardous-waste contractor **before closing** (so the Buyer does not take title to an active waste violation). Advance soil borings at the drum location and analyze for VOCs. Include this location in the Phase II groundwater program.

### Issue 6 — REC: 1988 undocumented degreasing-solvent spill [HIGH]

**Description/Evidence.** The City of Toledo Fire Department incident report dated **March 14, 1988** documents a "chemical spill" at 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive involving an "unknown quantity of degreasing solvent" released from an overturned drum (Greenfield ESA §§ 5.1.2, 7.2; Ohio EPA response is consistent). ## 7. Detailed Findings — Dayton Property (Separate Transaction)

The following findings pertain to the Dayton Property (2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, Ohio; seller Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc.; purchase price $4,200,000). They are reported here because the Dayton documents were included in the production and must be addressed — either as part of a separate Cornerstone acquisition or to resolve the document-set commingling (Issue 1). They are **first-class findings**, not appendix items.

### Issue 26 — REC: Western yard former ASTs; stressed vegetation and darkened soil [HIGH]

**Description/Evidence.** An area of stressed/absent vegetation and darkened soil, approximately **40 ft × 60 ft**, was observed in the western portion of the Dayton Property, ~150 ft west of Building 1 and ~75 ft east of the western boundary (Terravista ESA §§ 6.3.1, 8.1.1). The soil is reddish-brown to black. The location corresponds to three above-ground storage tanks depicted on the **1942 Sanborn map** annotated "oil storage" for the Dayton Forge & Die Company; the tanks were removed between 1950 and 1960 (no longer on the 1960 Sanborn map; absent in the 1962 aerial). No liquid sheen, odor, or free product was observed at reconnaissance.

**Consequence.** Stressed vegetation and darkened soil at the location of former petroleum ASTs indicate a potential petroleum release to soil during the ~15–20 years the ASTs were present. The area is unpaved and unimproved.

**Recommendation.** Phase II ESA: advance soil borings in the western yard; analyze for DRO and GRO; compare to Ohio EPA generic cleanup standards for commercial/industrial use; consider groundwater sampling for off-site migration. (Terravista's recommendation.)

### Issue 27 — REC: Building 2 electroplating floor staining; hexavalent chromium/nickel [HIGH]

**Description/Evidence.** Floor staining approximately **15 ft × 20 ft** (dark, greenish-blue and dark-brown hues) was observed in Building 2 near the chemical-drum-storage area, between the drum pallets and the western end of the chromium plating line (Terravista ESA §§ 6.4.2, 8.1.2). Building 2 houses active chromium (hexavalent) and nickel electroplating since ~1987. Chemicals include sulfuric, hydrochloric, and chromic acids, alkaline cleaners, and nickel sulfate. Floor drains connect to an on-site industrial wastewater pretreatment system discharging to the City of Dayton POTW under **NPDES pretreatment permit 2PB00417**. The acid-resistant epoxy floor coating shows wear and deterioration in the staining area. The facility manager attributed the staining to "routine operational spillage."

**Consequence.** Visible staining in an active hexavalent-chromium/nickel electroplating area, with a deteriorated floor coating, indicates releases of plating chemicals to the floor with potential migration through the slab over decades. Hexavalent chromium and nickel are regulated hazardous substances.

**Recommendation.** Phase II ESA: subsurface investigation beneath/adjacent to Building 2 in the staining and drum-storage areas; analyze soil for metals (Cr(VI), total chromium, nickel). (Terravista's recommendation.)

### Issue 28 — CRITICAL: Central Valley Chemical (Building 3) likely misclassified as an HREC [CRITICAL]

**Description/Evidence.** Terravista classified the former Central Valley Chemical Co. operations in Building 3 as an **HREC** (Terravista ESA §§ 1.0, 8.3.2), relying on the **September 22, 2011 Ohio EPA VAP No Further Action (NFA) letter** issued by Certified Professional Jonathan Wertz of Apex Environmental Solutions. However, the NFA letter:

- Covers a **2.3-acre area designated "Parcel A" in the southern portion** of the property, described as the "**former diesel underground storage tank area and associated petroleum-impacted soils**" (Terravista ESA §§ 4.2, 5.2.5, 8.3.1, 8.3.2).
- **"Does not address environmental conditions in areas of the Subject Property outside the Parcel A boundary"** (Terravista ESA § 5.2.5).

Building 3 (where Central Valley Chemical operated a chemical-blending facility from ~1975 to 1981, handling industrial cleaning solvents and degreasers including **chlorinated solvents**) is **not** within the southern Parcel A. Additional evidence:

- The **1971 aerial photograph** shows outdoor drum storage of an estimated **100 or more drums** on the north side of Building 3, with no secondary containment or cover, on an unpaved surface (Terravista ESA §§ 5.2.2, 8.3.2).
- An **Ohio EPA enforcement action was taken against Central Valley Chemical in 1981**, after which it ceased operations; the specific details and disposition are **not available** (Ohio EPA file records remain pending — Issue 33) (Terravista ESA § 8.3.2).
- Exterior loading-dock staining (~8 ft × 12 ft, dark gray to black, faint petroleum-like sheen) was observed on the north side of Building 3 during reconnaissance, corresponding to the 1971 drum-storage area; the facility manager stated the staining predates Valtec's 1987 acquisition (Terravista ESA § 6.3.4).
- Building 3's interior floor exhibits multiple cracks, patches, and ~3 inches of differential settlement at the northeast corner (Terravista ESA § 6.4.3).

**Consequence.** The NFA letter does **not** cover Building 3 or the Central Valley Chemical operations. Terravista's HREC classification — which requires that a past release has been "addressed to the satisfaction of the applicable regulatory authority" — appears to be **incorrect** as applied to Building 3, because the only regulatory closure in the record (the 2011 NFA) covers the southern UST area, not Building 3. The 1981 enforcement action is unresolved. Chlorinated solvents at a chemical-blending facility with 100+ uncontained drums and observed exterior staining pose **vapor-intrusion and groundwater risks** and may constitute an **unaddressed REC**, not an HREC. This is the most significant substantive defect in the Dayton Phase I ESA.

**Recommendation.** (1) Reclassify the Central Valley Chemical / Building 3 condition as a **REC** (or at minimum, an unresolved condition pending investigation) — it has not been demonstrated to be addressed to the satisfaction of the regulatory authority. (2) Obtain the 1981 Ohio EPA enforcement records (Issue 33) to determine the disposition. (3) Commission a Phase II for Building 3 and the north-side loading-dock/drum-storage area: soil borings, groundwater wells, and vapor-intrusion assessment, analyzing for chlorinated solvents (PCE, TCE and degradation products). (4) Do not rely on the 2011 NFA letter for Building 3 conditions.

### Issue 29 — Tank 3 (4,000-gallon waste-oil UST) closure incomplete; results missing [HIGH]

**Description/Evidence.** The Redland UST Closure Report (November 12, 2004) documents that Tank 3 (4,000-gallon waste-oil UST, installed ~1972 by Millrace Industrial Park LLC) exhibited **moderate to heavy external corrosion with one visible through-wall perforation (~1.5 inches)**, ~4 inches of dark viscous oily residue, and **stained soil (dark black, strong petroleum/waste-oil odor) at the excavation base and the lower ~2 ft of the south and west sidewalls** (Redland report §§ 3.3, 4.2, 5.5). **Seven confirmation soil samples were collected on September 17, 2004**, but the **analytical results were "pending" at the time of report preparation**; a handwritten annotation reads "Lab results pending — will supplement." **No supplemental report was ever located** — not in the Seller's files, not in Redland's files, and not in publicly available Ohio EPA records (editor's note). The Tank 3 excavation was **temporarily backfilled with native soils without compaction control** and asphalt-surfaced. The Terravista ESA (§ 5.2.5) confirms that **documentation of the closure letter for Tank 3 was not provided**; the facility manager was "confident" it was issued but could not locate it. The 2011 VAP NFA letter describes Parcel A as the "former diesel underground storage tank area" — its express terms focus on **diesel**, and it is unclear whether the **waste-oil** UST (Tank 3) is fully encompassed.

**Consequence.** Tank 3 had a through-wall perforation and visibly impacted soil, but its closure was never completed in the record: no analytical results, no supplemental report, and no closure letter produced. This is an uncharacterized potential release of waste oil (which may contain chlorinated solvents given the facility's history) within the southern yard. The HREC-1 classification (Terravista ESA § 8.3.1) treats all three USTs as addressed by the 2011 NFA, but Tank 3's closure is not documented.

**Recommendation.** (1) Obtain the Tank 3 analytical results and closure letter from Redland, Great Lakes Analytical Laboratory, and Ohio EPA BUSTR; if they do not exist, treat Tank 3 as an unaddressed release. (2) Clarify whether the 2011 NFA letter's Phase II (Apex, 2009–2010) specifically assessed the Tank 3 (waste-oil) location, or only the diesel USTs. (3) Include the Tank 3 location in any Phase II for the Dayton Property (waste-oil parameters, VOCs). (4) Reclassify HREC-1 to reflect that Tank 3's closure is unverified.

### Issue 30 — Tank 2 (10,000-gallon diesel UST) confirmed release; groundwater uncertainty [MEDIUM]

**Description/Evidence.** Tank 2 (10,000-gallon diesel UST, installed ~1965) exhibited heavy corrosion with **multiple through-wall perforations (at least three holes, largest ~2 inches)**, stained soil, strong petroleum odor, and sheen (Redland report §§ 3.2, 4.2, 5.3). Initial confirmation samples showed **DRO up to 1,250 mg/kg** (exceeding the 500 mg/kg BUSTR action level) at four of seven locations. Over-excavation (to ~16 ft bgs, ~3 ft lateral) removed **~85 cubic yards** of petroleum-impacted soil to a Subtitle D landfill; confirmation DRO max was **280 mg/kg** (below action level) (Redland report §§ 4.3, 5.4). Groundwater was not encountered (max depth 16 ft; water table estimated 15–25 ft bgs), but the report notes that "the potential for petroleum impacts to groundwater cannot be ruled out" given the excavation depth's proximity to the seasonal high water table and the elevated DRO at the Tank 2 base (~12 ft bgs). Tank 2 is within Parcel A, which was assessed by Apex (2009–2010) and received the 2011 NFA letter.

**Consequence.** Tank 2's release was addressed by over-excavation and is within the NFA-covered Parcel A, so the residual risk is lower than Tank 3 (Issue 29). However, the report's own caveat about groundwater and the fact that no groundwater was encountered (so none was sampled) leave a residual uncertainty, partially mitigated by the Apex Phase II groundwater program underlying the NFA.

**Recommendation.** Confirm that the Apex 2009–2010 Phase II included groundwater monitoring wells in the Tank 2 area and that the NFA's risk assessment addressed the Tank 2 release. If not, targeted groundwater assessment may be warranted. Lower priority than Issues 28–29.

## 10. Cross-Cutting Observations

1. **Two chlorinated-solvent profiles, both under-classified.** Both the Toledo Property (TCE degreasing, Issue 4; off-site PCE plume, Issue 10) and the Dayton Property (Central Valley Chemical chlorinated solvents, Issue 28; F001/F002 waste codes, Issue 34) present chlorinated-solvent concerns that the respective Phase I ESAs under-classified or omitted. Chlorinated solvents are the highest-stakes contamination type for vapor intrusion and groundwater; both files warrant targeted Phase II vapor and groundwater programs.

2. **Hexavalent chromium at both properties.** Both properties conducted hexavalent-chromium/nickel electroplating (Toledo: 1965–2004, Issue 7; Dayton: ongoing since ~1987, Issue 27). Both have visible staining and floor degradation. Both require metals-focused Phase II investigation.

3. **Unregistered/unclosed USTs at both properties.** Toledo has two unregistered USTs of unknown status (Issues 11, 12); Dayton has Tank 3 with an incomplete closure (Issue 29). In both cases, the Phase I ESA either under-classified or relied on regulatory closure that did not actually cover the condition.

4. **Seller reliability is a recurring theme (Toledo).** The Toledo Seller's denials (Issue 15) and the breached schedules (Issue 16) indicate that the Seller's knowledge-qualified representations cannot be trusted at face value. This elevates the importance of the fraud exception (Issue 17) and of independent verification (Phase II, Ohio EPA files).

5. **The 14.7-acre coincidence and the duplicate EPA ID (Issues 1, 2).** Both properties are ~14.7 acres, and the same EPA RCRA ID appears in both files. These coincidences likely contributed to the file commingling and must be resolved before any reliance is placed on either database review.

6. **Regulatory closures are narrower than the Phase I ESAs imply.** The Toledo dry-cleaner VAP covenant (2018) covers only the dry-cleaner parcel (Issue 10); the Dayton VAP NFA (2011) covers only Parcel A (Issues 28, 35). In both cases, the Phase I ESA either implied or stated broader coverage than the regulatory documents actually provide. This is a systematic due-diligence error to correct.

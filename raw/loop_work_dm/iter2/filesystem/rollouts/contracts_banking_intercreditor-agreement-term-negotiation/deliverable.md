       I. Introduction and Purpose  
      You have asked Kesswick, Tanaka & Moreau LLP (    “    KTM    ”    ) to prepare proposed compromise language for the four contested provisions of the Intercreditor Agreement (the         “    ICA    ”    ) to be entered into among Cartwright National Bank, N.A. (    “    Cartwright,    ”         as First Lien Administrative Agent and Collateral Agent, the         “    First Lien Agent    ”         or         “    1L Agent    ”    ), Ridgeline Capital Partners, LP (    “    Ridgeline,    ”         as Second Lien Administrative Agent and Collateral Agent, the         “    Second Lien Agent    ”         or         “    2L Agent    ”    ), and Meridian Industrial Holdings, Inc. (    “    Meridian    ”         or the         “    Borrower    ”    ), dated as of July 31, 2025. Negotiations commenced on May 12, 2025, and have reached an impasse on four provisions: (i) the Standstill Period (Section 3.01(b)); (ii) the Second Lien Purchase Right (Section 5.01); (iii) Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness (Section 2.03(c)); and (iv) the Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds (Section 4.02).  
      This memorandum sets forth, for each of the four contested provisions, (a) full contractual drafting of the proposed compromise language, (b) a negotiation rationale explaining what each side gains and concedes and why the compromise falls within each side’s stated flexibility, (c) citations to the KTM market survey of 42 first lien/second lien intercreditor agreements closed between January 2022 and December 2024 (the         “    Market Survey    ”    ), and (d) quantification of the economic impacts where the record permits.  
          The four compromises are presented as a single, indivisible, integrated package.         This is deliberate and is the central recommendation of this memorandum. Each side’s willingness to move on any one provision is conditioned on reciprocal movement on the others; the concessions are mutually offsetting and were calibrated against one another. Unbundling the package would permit either agent to accept the provisions favorable to its side while re-litigating the others, which would collapse the balance and return the parties to the positional impasse that has stalled negotiations since mid-June. The indivisibility of the package is addressed in detail in Section IX below.

There is no realistic prospect of an extension of the existing facility. The compromise package is designed to be credible and closeable within this window without a further round of positional bargaining.

         Two features of this capital structure drive the negotiation.             First    , the Total LTV of 97.66% means aggregate secured indebtedness nearly equals enterprise value; a decline in TEV of only 2.34% pushes Total LTV above 100%, at which point the Second Lien Secured Parties are impaired and their incentives diverge from those of the First Lien Secured Parties.             Second    , the second lien equity cushion of 25.78% ($165,000,000 in absolute terms) is thin by any measure and is the primary safeguard the Second Lien Lenders have; the structural protections in the ICA are, for them, the principal protection of their investment. These two facts pull in opposite directions on each contested provision and are the reason a balanced package, rather than a one-sided outcome, is both appropriate and achievable.  
          Appraisal caveat relevant to every provision.         The $640,000,000 TEV is a             going-concern         value on a debt-free, cash-free basis; it is expressly             not         a liquidation, forced-sale, or orderly wind-down value. In the distressed scenarios in which the standstill, the purchase right, and the proceeds waterfall actually operate, realizable value would be materially below the going-concern TEV. This caveat reinforces             both         the First Lien Agent’s concern about collateral erosion during a prolonged standstill             and         the Second Lien Agent’s concern that a prolonged standstill permits value to decline below the second lien’s already-thin cushion. It is one reason the compromise standstill is set at the market median rather than at either extreme.  
          Negotiation posture of each side. The Second Lien Agent’s position letter (Naomi Oduya, Sternfeld Yates & Carroll LLP, June 23, 2025) advances 2L-protective formulations, grounded in the same thin cushion viewed from the junior perspective and in Ridgeline’s fund-level structural constraints (in particular, the limited partnership agreement’s restriction on open-ended revolving-credit commitment obligations). In the course of the negotiation, each side has signaled, on a without-prejudice basis, zones within which it could move; the compromise package below is constructed to land within the overlap of those zones on each provision. The Market Survey — 42 broadly syndicated 1L/2L ICAs closed January 2022–December 2024, all New York law (95%) or Delaware law (5%), with a median 1L facility of $475M, median 2L facility of $150M, and median total secured debt of $625M, i.e., a population centered on structures comparable to Meridian’s — supplies the objective benchmark against which each compromise is measured.

        IV. Provision 1 — Standstill Period (Section 3.01(b))  
       A. Proposed Compromise Language  
          Section 3.01(b) — Standstill Period.         Following the occurrence and during the continuance of a Second Lien Default, the Second Lien Agent shall not exercise any rights or remedies with respect to the Collateral for a period of one hundred eighty (180) days (the         “    Standstill Period    ”    ) commencing on the date of actual receipt by the Second Lien Agent of a written notice (a         “    Blockage Notice    ”    ) from the First Lien Agent specifying in reasonable detail the Second Lien Default giving rise thereto. The Standstill Period shall terminate prior to the expiration of such one hundred eighty (180)-day period upon the occurrence of any of the following early termination events, each of which shall be objectively verifiable:  
              First Lien Enforcement Inaction.         The First Lien Agent has not commenced an Enforcement Action with respect to the Collateral and has not taken any material step to enforce, collect, or realize upon the First Lien Obligations or the Collateral for a period of sixty (60) consecutive days during the Standstill Period;  
              Disposition of Substantially All Collateral / Credit Bid.         The First Lien Agent commences a foreclosure, liquidation, or other disposition of substantially all of the Collateral, or credit bids all or substantially all of the First Lien Obligations in connection with any sale or disposition of Collateral (including under Section 363 of the Bankruptcy Code); or  
              Impairing Plan or Sale Motion Without 2L Consent.         A plan of reorganization is filed in an Insolvency Proceeding that provides for the impairment of the Second Lien Obligations without the consent of the Second Lien Agent, or a motion is filed under Section 363 of the Bankruptcy Code seeking to sell substantially all of the assets of the Borrower and its Subsidiaries free and clear of the Liens securing the Second Lien Obligations without the consent of the Second Lien Agent.  
      Upon the occurrence of any such early termination event, the Standstill Period shall immediately terminate and the Second Lien Agent may exercise any and all rights and remedies available to it under the Second Lien Loan Documents and applicable law with respect to the Collateral, subject to Section 3.03 and the priority and subordination provisions of this Agreement. There shall be no more than two (2) Blockage Notices (and no more than two (2) Standstill Periods) in effect during any rolling three hundred sixty-five (365)-day period, and the aggregate number of days during which a Standstill Period is in effect shall not exceed three hundred sixty (360) days in any rolling three hundred sixty-five (365)-day period. If the Second Lien Default specified in a Blockage Notice is cured or waived in accordance with the Second Lien Credit Agreement, the Standstill Period shall immediately terminate, and any subsequent exercise of remedies by the Second Lien Agent shall require the delivery of a new Blockage Notice and the commencement of a new Standstill Period in accordance with this Section 3.01(b), subject to the foregoing caps.  
        B. Negotiation Rationale  
          What the First Lien Agent gains.         The 180-day duration is materially longer than the Second Lien Agent’s 90-day opening position and sits at the Market Survey median. It is above Cartwright’s disclosed internal credit-committee floor of 150 days and within the 150-to-180-day range the syndicate has indicated it can accept. At 180 days (six months), the First Lien Agent retains the minimum period Cartwright’s own rationale identifies as necessary to pursue a comprehensive restructuring, orderly asset disposition, or refinancing — the low end of the six-to-nine-month window Cartwright cited, supplemented by the Blockage Notice cap, which permits a second 180-day standstill (up to 360 aggregate days per rolling year) if a default is cured and recurs. The triggers are narrow and objectively verifiable, addressing the First Lien Agent’s stated concern that early-termination triggers must not be subjective or manipulable.  
          What the First Lien Agent concedes.         The First Lien Agent concedes (i) a reduction from its 270-day opening to 180 days, and (ii) the inclusion of three early-termination triggers and a Blockage Notice cap, where its opening position was an absolute standstill with no triggers and no cap. These concessions are supportable because 180 days remains above the syndicate’s floor and at the market median, and because the triggers are drafted to fire only in the specific circumstances in which a standstill serves no legitimate First Lien purpose — namely, when the First Lien Agent has itself ceased enforcement (trigger A), when the First Lien Agent is already liquidating the whole estate (trigger B), or when a plan or sale motion would impair the Second Lien without its consent (trigger C). In each of those scenarios, the standstill is not protecting an active First Lien workout; it is merely immobilizing the Second Lien. The Blockage Notice cap prevents the tactical use of successive Blockage Notices to convert lien subordination into indefinite payment subordination — a concern the Second Lien Agent raised and that the current draft of Section 3.01(b) does not address (neither side’s bracketed proposal included a cap).  
          What the Second Lien Agent gains.         The Second Lien Agent obtains (i) early-termination triggers — its stated firm condition for accepting any standstill longer than 90 days — and (ii) a Blockage Notice cap that prevents perpetual standstill. Trigger A (60-day enforcement inaction) directly addresses Ridgeline’s concern that a 270-day standstill paired with First Lien inaction would permit collateral value to erode while the Second Lien is immobilized. Triggers B and C address Ridgeline’s concern about value destruction in a First Lien-driven liquidation or an impairing plan/sale.  
          What the Second Lien Agent concedes.         The Second Lien Agent concedes an increase from its 90-day opening to 180 days — double its opening position. This concession is supportable because 180 days is the Market Survey median and is the duration the Second Lien Agent has itself indicated it can accept             if         paired with meaningful triggers, which this package provides. The 60-day inaction measuring period in trigger A is an objective, bright-line standard that gives the First Lien Agent a full quarter to commence enforcement before the trigger can fire, preserving the First Lien Agent’s reasonable workout window.  
          Why the compromise falls within each side’s flexibility.         The First Lien Agent has signaled that a standstill in the 150-to-180-day range is acceptable and that a longer standstill (toward 180 days) paired with         “    carefully defined early termination triggers    ”         is something its credit committee would consider, provided the triggers are         “    narrow and objectively verifiable.    ”         The 180-day duration with three objectively verifiable triggers satisfies that description. The Second Lien Agent has signaled that it can accept up to 180 days but only with meaningful early-termination triggers, which it has described as a firm, non-decouplable condition. The package delivers exactly that. The Blockage Notice cap is a 2L-protective element that the First Lien Agent can accept because it does not shorten any individual standstill and because the 360-day aggregate allowance preserves meaningful First Lien protection across a rolling year.  
        C. Market Data  
      The Market Survey (42 deals, 2022–2024) reports: median standstill 180 days; mean 172.9 days; 25th percentile 150 days; 75th percentile 202.5 days; range 90–270 days. Early-termination triggers are included in 32 of 42 deals (76%); 25 of 42 deals pair a standstill of 180 days or longer             with         early-termination triggers — a combination the Survey labels the         “    market standard pairing.    ”         The most common triggers are: liquidation commenced (29 deals), credit bid by 1L (22), filing of involuntary petition (13), DIP financing approved over 2L objection (13), and sale motion filed without 2L consent (9). The compromise triggers map directly onto this set: trigger B captures         “    liquidation commenced    ”         and         “    credit bid by 1L    ”    ; trigger C captures         “    sale motion filed without 2L consent    ”         and the impairing-plan concept; trigger A (enforcement inaction) is a 2L-protective complement not separately tallied in the Survey but consistent with the rationale of the trigger set. The most directly comparable transaction in the Survey, Deal-038 ($470M 1L / $155M 2L / $625M total secured, sponsor-backed industrials — essentially Meridian’s structure), provides a 240-day standstill with a comprehensive trigger set; the compromise’s 180 days with three triggers is more conservative (shorter) than that comparable while remaining at the median. KTM’s own standard-form ICA (Section 3.1) uses a 180-day standstill, confirming that 180 days is KTM’s house standard for this structure.  
        D. Economic Quantification  
              Second Lien time-value compensation during standstill.         Both facilities are floating-rate. During any standstill, interest continues to accrue on the Second Lien Obligations at SOFR + 625 bps (11.58% at current SOFR). Over a 180-day standstill, the Second Lien accrues approximately $8.69 million in interest on its $150 million position ($150M × 11.58% × 180/360), and at the default rate under the Second Lien Credit Agreement (if applicable) the accrual would be higher. This continued accrual substantially addresses the time-value cost of the standstill to the Second Lien, which is the First Lien Agent’s principal affirmative response to the duration concern.  
              Collateral-erosion exposure.         With a 25.78% cushion ($165 million), a TEV decline of 25.78% over the standstill period would reduce Second Lien recovery to zero. Ridgeline’s own illustration: a 15% TEV decline (to ~$544 million) reduces Second Lien theoretical recovery to ~$69 million, or 46 cents on the dollar, before liquidation frictions. At 180 days versus 270 days, the Second Lien bears 90 fewer days of erosion exposure — a meaningful reduction in the probability and magnitude of impairment, while still giving the First Lien a full quarter-plus to pursue a workout.  
              First Lien workout window.         The 180-day period equals six months, the low end of the six-to-nine-month window Cartwright’s rationale identifies as necessary for a restructuring or orderly asset sale; the Blockage Notice cap permits a second 180-day period (up to 360 aggregate days per rolling year) if a default cures and recurs, preserving the First Lien’s ability to re-block without permitting indefinite immobilization.  
      
         V. Provision 2 — Second Lien Purchase Right (Section 5.01)  
       A. Proposed Compromise Language  
          Section 5.01 — Second Lien Purchase Right.         At any time following the occurrence and during the continuance of an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement, or upon the delivery by the First Lien Agent of a notice of acceleration of the First Lien Obligations, the Second Lien Agent (on behalf of the Second Lien Lenders, or one or more Second Lien Lenders, or any designee of the foregoing) shall have the right, but not the obligation (the         “    Second Lien Purchase Right    ”    ), to purchase all (but not less than all) of the First Lien Obligations by paying to the First Lien Agent in immediately available funds a purchase price (the         “    Purchase Price    ”    ) equal to the sum of the following, each determined as of the close of business on the Business Day immediately preceding the purchase date:  
              Outstanding Principal.         One hundred percent (100%) of the aggregate outstanding principal amount of all funded First Lien Obligations, including all amounts outstanding under the First Lien Term Loan A and all drawn amounts under the First Lien Revolver;  
              Accrued Interest at the Non-Default Contract Rate.         All accrued and unpaid interest on the First Lien Obligations, calculated at the non-default contract rate applicable under the First Lien Credit Agreement (i.e., SOFR plus 250 basis points, or 7.83% per annum at a SOFR of 5.33%), through and including the purchase date, calculated on the basis of a 360-day year for the actual number of days elapsed (excluding any default-rate increment, late charges, or other punitive amounts);  
              Fees, Costs, Expenses, and Other Amounts.         All fees (including commitment fees, letter of credit fees, and agency fees), costs, expenses (including reasonable and documented fees and disbursements of counsel), indemnification obligations then due and payable or asserted in writing, breakage costs (including any swap breakage or early termination costs under any hedging arrangements), yield protection amounts, and all other amounts then due and payable under the First Lien Loan Documents, whether or not invoiced; plus  
              Letters of Credit.         The then-outstanding amount of all letters of credit issued under the First Lien Credit Agreement, to the extent not drawn and not cash collateralized, together with an amount sufficient to cash collateralize such letters of credit at one hundred three percent (103%) of the undrawn face amount thereof.  
          Assumption of Unfunded Revolver Commitments — Optional.         As a condition to the purchase, the purchasing Second Lien Secured Parties may, at their sole option, assume all unfunded commitments under the First Lien Revolver (which, as of the date hereof, total $125,000,000 in aggregate commitments, less any amounts drawn thereunder as of the purchase date) by executing and delivering to the First Lien Agent an assumption agreement in form and substance reasonably satisfactory to the First Lien Agent, pursuant to which the purchaser agrees to be bound by the First Lien Credit Agreement as a revolving lender and to fund subsequent draws on terms no less favorable to the Borrower than those set forth in the First Lien Credit Agreement. If the purchasing Second Lien Secured Parties elect not to assume the unfunded Revolver commitments, the unfunded Revolver commitments shall be permanently terminated (or reduced to the drawn amount, as applicable) upon consummation of the purchase, and the First Lien Agent and the remaining First Lien Revolver lenders shall cooperate to effect such termination in a manner that does not impair the Borrower’s rights under any then-drawn Revolver loans. The purchase shall be on a par, non-recourse, as-is, where-is basis, without representation or warranty of any kind by the First Lien Agent or any First Lien Lender (other than customary representations as to the authority of the assigning lender to assign and the enforceability of the transfer).  
        B. Negotiation Rationale  
          What the First Lien Agent gains.         The First Lien Agent obtains its non-negotiable core: a par purchase price for funded debt (100% of outstanding principal), with no fair-market-value alternative and no dealer-poll mechanism. The First Lien Lenders are made whole on principal and on accrued interest at the contract rate, plus all fees, expenses, breakage, and letter-of-credit amounts. The exercise triggers are limited to First Lien Events of Default and First Lien acceleration — the First Lien Agent’s proposed scope — and do not include a Second Lien Event of Default (which the Second Lien Agent had sought). The optional-revolver structure preserves the First Lien Agent’s ability to require an orderly termination of unfunded commitments if the purchaser declines to assume them, addressing the First Lien Agent’s bifurcation concern.  
          What the First Lien Agent concedes.         The First Lien Agent concedes (i) calculation of accrued interest at the non-default contract rate rather than the default rate, and (ii) optional rather than mandatory assumption of unfunded Revolver commitments. Both concessions are supportable. On interest rate: the non-default contract rate is the majority market practice (69% of par deals exclude the default-rate increment), and the First Lien Lenders remain fully made whole on principal and contract-rate interest; the default-rate increment is, in any event, the subject of legitimate dispute as to enforceability in the distressed context, and conceding it removes a contested point without impairing the First Lien’s par recovery. On the revolver: the Second Lien Agent has explained, and the First Lien Agent has acknowledged, that Ridgeline’s fund limited partnership agreement imposes a structural constraint on open-ended revolving-credit commitment obligations — this is a limitation of the fund vehicle, not a negotiating posture. Making assumption optional rather than mandatory accommodates that structural constraint while preserving the option for any purchaser that is not so constrained, and the mandatory-termination fallback prevents the bifurcation the First Lien Agent feared.  
          What the Second Lien Agent gains.         The Second Lien Agent obtains (i) accrued interest at the non-default contract rate rather than the default rate, lowering the purchase price by the default-rate increment; (ii) optional rather than mandatory assumption of the $125 million unfunded Revolver, removing a contingent obligation that Ridgeline’s LPA prohibits it from accepting; and (iii) an acceleration trigger in addition to the First Lien Event of Default trigger. The optional-revolver structure is the Second Lien Agent’s stated deal-breaker resolution: it permits the purchase right to remain exercisable by Ridgeline (which cannot assume the revolver) while preserving the mechanism for purchasers who can.  
          What the Second Lien Agent concedes.         The Second Lien Agent concedes (i) par as the sole purchase-price mechanic, dropping the fair-market-value / dealer-poll alternative it had proposed, and (ii) the narrowing of exercise triggers to First Lien Events of Default and acceleration, dropping the Second Lien Event of Default trigger. The par concession is significant: at full draw, the par purchase price (funded debt, non-default rate, 90-day accrual) is approximately $485.8 million, and the Second Lien’s total capital at risk after exercise would be approximately $635.8 million ($485.8 million purchase price plus its existing $150 million position), approaching the $640 million TEV. This concession is supportable because the First Lien Agent has made par for funded debt non-negotiable, because par is the majority market practice (71%), and because the concessions the Second Lien obtains (non-default rate, optional revolver) materially lower the effective price and remove the LPA-prohibited contingent obligation, preserving the purchase right’s viability.  
          Why the compromise falls within each side’s flexibility.         The First Lien Agent has signaled that par for funded debt is non-negotiable but that there is room on (a) accepting the non-default contract rate for accrued interest and (b) making unfunded Revolver assumption optional rather than mandatory. The compromise adopts both. The Second Lien Agent has signaled that it can accept par for funded debt conditioned on unfunded Revolver assumption being optional, and that it is willing to discuss the non-default contract rate. The compromise delivers both. The result is the precise formulation the Market Survey identifies as the modal outcome for comparable structures.  
        C. Market Data  
      The Market Survey reports: purchase-price mechanic — par in 30 deals (71%), hybrid (lesser of par or FMV) in 8 (19%), FMV in 4 (10%); default-rate interest included in par in only 13 deals (31%, a minority), with 29 deals (69%) using the non-default contract rate; non-default contract-rate interest included in 38 deals (90%, near-universal); unfunded Revolver assumption — mandatory in 10 deals (24%), optional in 26 (62%, the clear majority and the trend, rising to 77% of 2024 deals versus 50% of 2022 deals), silent in 6 (14%); exercise trigger — acceleration of 1L in 42 deals (100%, universal), commencement of enforcement action in 31 (74%); exercise window — median 30 days, mean 34.8 days, range 10–60 days. The most directly comparable transaction, Deal-007 ($475M 1L / $150M 2L / $125M Revolver / non-sponsor industrial — Meridian’s exact structure), provides         “    par for funded debt at non-default contract rate; optional revolver assumption; illustrative par purchase price $484.3M.    ”         The compromise reproduces that formulation precisely.  
        D. Economic Quantification  
              Illustrative par purchase price (funded debt, non-default rate, 90-day accrual, full draw).         $475,000,000 principal + $9,300,000 accrued interest ($475M × 7.83% × 90/360) + approximately $1,500,000 fees and expenses = approximately             $485,800,000    . (This matches the capital structure summary’s base-case figure of $485.80M and the figure both agents have used in their illustrations.)  
              Value of the interest-rate concession (1L to 2L).         The default rate under the First Lien Credit Agreement is the non-default contract rate plus 200 basis points (SOFR + 450 bps, or 9.83% at current SOFR). The difference between default-rate and non-default-rate accrued interest on $475 million is approximately             $2,375,000 for a 90-day accrual         ($475M × 2.00% × 90/360) and approximately             $7,125,000 for a 270-day accrual         ($475M × 2.00% × 270/360). This is the quantified value of the First Lien Agent’s concession on the interest-rate component.  
              Unfunded Revolver.         The First Lien Revolver commitment is $125,000,000. If assumption is mandatory and the purchaser must assume the full unfunded commitment, the purchaser’s contingent funding obligation could reach $125 million. The optional structure removes this contingent obligation for purchasers (such as Ridgeline) whose fund documents prohibit it, while preserving the commitment-fee economics (commitment fee of 0.375% at Pricing Level I, or approximately $469,000 per annum on $125 million) for any purchaser that elects to assume.  
              Second Lien total capital at risk after exercise.         Approximately $485.8 million (purchase price) + $150 million (existing Second Lien position) = approximately             $635.8 million    , approaching the $640 million TEV. This is the economic reality that made the Second Lien seek an FMV alternative; the compromise addresses it not by discounting the price below par (which the First Lien will not accept) but by lowering the price through the non-default rate and removing the LPA-prohibited revolver obligation, keeping the purchase right exercisable.  
      
         VI. Provision 3 — Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness (Section 2.03(c))  
       A. Proposed Compromise Language  
          Section 2.03(c) — Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness.         “    Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness    ”         means indebtedness incurred by the Borrower to refinance, replace, refund, renew, or substitute all or any portion of the Second Lien Obligations, provided that all of the following conditions are satisfied:  
              Principal Amount.         The aggregate principal amount of such refinancing indebtedness shall not exceed one hundred ten percent (110%) of the aggregate principal amount of the Second Lien Obligations being refinanced (i.e., based on the current $150,000,000 Second Lien Term Loan B, a maximum of $165,000,000, representing a permitted increase of $15,000,000); provided that the portion of such refinancing indebtedness exceeding one hundred percent (100%) of the principal amount being refinanced shall be limited to bona fide refinancing-related costs and expenses, including (A) call premiums, make-whole amounts, or prepayment premiums payable in connection with the prepayment of the Second Lien Obligations being refinanced, (B) arranger, underwriting, and placement fees, (C) original issue discount, (D) legal, advisory, rating agency, and filing costs, and (E) other customary transaction costs and expenses incurred in connection with such refinancing;  
              Maturity.         The stated final maturity date of such refinancing indebtedness shall be no earlier than the later of (A) the date that is ninety-one (91) days after the First Lien Maturity Date (i.e., no earlier than September 14, 2030) and (B) the original Second Lien Maturity Date (i.e., no earlier than June 15, 2031);  
              Narrowed Restrictiveness Test.         The terms of such refinancing indebtedness shall not be more restrictive to the Borrower, in any material respect, with respect to (A) financial maintenance covenants, (B) events of default relating to financial covenants, and (C) mandatory prepayment provisions triggered by financial metrics, than the corresponding provisions of the Second Lien Credit Agreement as in effect on the Closing Date;  
              Most-Favored-Nation Right.         If the refinancing indebtedness includes any financial maintenance covenant that is more restrictive to the Borrower than the corresponding financial maintenance covenant in the First Lien Credit Agreement as in effect on the Closing Date, or includes any financial maintenance covenant not contained in the First Lien Credit Agreement, then, at the election of the First Lien Agent (with the consent of the Required Lenders under the First Lien Credit Agreement), such covenant shall be deemed incorporated into the First Lien Credit Agreement for the benefit of the First Lien Lenders, and the Borrower shall effect such amendment to the First Lien Credit Agreement without further consent of the Borrower (to the extent permitted by applicable law and the First Lien Credit Agreement); and  
              Joinder.         The holders of such refinancing indebtedness shall have become party to this Agreement (or a replacement intercreditor agreement on substantially similar terms) by executing and delivering a joinder agreement in form and substance reasonably satisfactory to the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent.  
        B. Negotiation Rationale  
          What the First Lien Agent gains.         The First Lien Agent obtains (i) a restrictiveness test — which its opening position demanded and the Second Lien Agent’s opening position omitted entirely — albeit narrowed to enumerated provisions rather than blanket; (ii) an MFN right ensuring that if a refinancing tightens the Second Lien’s financial maintenance covenants, the First Lien Lenders automatically receive the benefit of those tighter covenants; and (iii) a maturity floor that includes its 91-days-post-1L test. The MFN right is the First Lien Agent’s principal protection against the Second Lien obtaining a covenant package in a refinancing that is more favorable to the Second Lien Lenders than what the First Lien Lenders hold — the precise risk the blanket test was designed to address, now achieved through a more targeted mechanism.  
          What the First Lien Agent concedes.         The First Lien Agent concedes (i) a 10% principal-increase cap ($15 million) rather than its 0% position, and (ii) a narrowed restrictiveness test rather than a blanket         “    no more restrictive in any material respect    ”         test. Both concessions are supportable. On the principal cap: 0% is the minority market position (only 21% of Survey deals), the median is 10%, and 79% of deals that permit an increase limit it to refinancing fees, costs, and premiums — exactly the structure proposed. The $15 million headroom is consumed by bona fide refinancing costs (the Second Lien’s own soft call of 2% in year one equals up to $3 million on $150 million; arranger fees of 200–350 bps equal $3 million–$5.25 million; OID of 1–3% equals $1.5 million–$4.5 million; plus legal and advisory costs), not by incremental enterprise funding, so the increase does not fund operations or distributions that would compress the equity cushion for the First Lien’s benefit. On the restrictiveness test: the narrowed test focuses on the provisions that could trigger cross-defaults or destabilize the capital structure (financial maintenance covenants, financial-covenant events of default, and financial-metric mandatory prepayment triggers), which are the provisions of actual concern to the First Lien; the blanket test’s principal vice — an effectively unbounded, subjective veto over all commercial terms — is removed, while the MFN right preserves the First Lien’s economic protection against covenant tightening.  
          What the Second Lien Agent gains.         The Second Lien Agent obtains (i) a 10% principal-increase cap ($15 million) sufficient to cover customary refinancing costs, which a 0% cap would render impracticable for virtually any refinancing involving non-trivial transaction costs; (ii) a narrowed rather than blanket restrictiveness test, preserving the Second Lien’s ability to negotiate the commercial terms of a replacement facility (other than the enumerated financial provisions) without an effective First Lien veto; and (iii) a maturity floor anchored on the original Second Lien Maturity Date (June 15, 2031), which is the Second Lien’s position and which, as explained below, does not disadvantage the First Lien in any scenario.  
          What the Second Lien Agent concedes.         The Second Lien Agent concedes (i) a reduction from its 20% opening ($30 million) to 10% ($15 million), and (ii) acceptance of a restrictiveness test (narrowed) and an MFN right, where its opening position was no restrictiveness test at all. The 10% concession is supportable because 10% is the market median and because the Second Lien Agent has signaled it can accept 10% if limited to refinancing fees and costs — which this package provides. The restrictiveness-test and MFN concessions are supportable because the narrowed test is limited to enumerated financial provisions (not a blanket veto) and because the MFN is limited to financial maintenance covenants (the 82% majority formulation), preserving the Second Lien’s flexibility on non-financial commercial terms.  
          Why the compromise falls within each side’s flexibility.         The First Lien Agent has signaled openness to a narrowed restrictiveness test paired with an MFN right, and willingness to discuss the principal cap in the overall package context. The Second Lien Agent has signaled it can accept a 10% cap limited to refinancing fees and costs and is open to a narrowed test plus MFN. The compromise delivers the precise combination both sides have indicated they can accept, and it is the exact formulation of the most directly comparable Survey transaction (Deal-007: 10% cap = $15M limited to fees/costs; MFN right on financial covenants; narrowed restrictiveness).  
        C. Market Data  
      The Market Survey reports: principal-increase cap — 0% in 9 deals (21%), 1–10% in 22 (52%), 11–15% in 6 (14%), 16–20% in 5 (12%); median 10%, mean 8.9%; of deals permitting an increase, 79% limit the increase to fees, costs, and premiums. Maturity test — 91 days after 1L maturity in 39 of 42 deals (93%); original-2L-maturity floor also included in 34 deals (81%). Restrictiveness test — blanket in 14 (33%), narrowed in 22 (52%), none in 6 (14%); MFN provision present in 22 deals (52%); critically, 100% of narrowed-restrictiveness deals pair with an MFN provision, and 82% of MFN provisions are limited to financial covenants only. The narrowed-test-plus-MFN combination has grown from 38% of 2022 deals to 65% of 2024 deals, with the 10% cap becoming modal. Deal-007 (Meridian’s comparable) provides the exact compromise formulation.  
        D. Economic Quantification  
              Principal cap.         A 10% cap permits refinancing indebtedness of up to $165,000,000 (a $15,000,000 increase on the current $150,000,000). At the maximum, total secured debt would rise from $625,000,000 to $640,000,000, pushing Total LTV from 97.66% to 100.0%. This is the First Lien Agent’s concern, and it is the reason the increase is limited to bona fide refinancing costs: the $15 million is consumed by transaction costs (call premiums, arranger fees, OID, legal/advisory costs) that would otherwise be paid from the Borrower’s cash (which would itself deplete the cushion), not by incremental enterprise funding.  
              Refinancing-cost illustration.         If the Second Lien is refinanced in year one (on or before July 31, 2026), the soft call is 2% of $150 million = $3,000,000; arranger/underwriting fees of 200–350 bps = $3,000,000–$5,250,000; OID of 1–3% = $1,500,000–$4,500,000; legal, advisory, rating, and filing costs ≈ $1,000,000–$2,000,000. Total ≈ $8,500,000–$14,750,000, within the $15,000,000 cap with modest headroom. If refinanced after year two (after July 31, 2027), the soft call falls away and total costs drop to approximately $5,500,000–$11,750,000, comfortably within the cap.  
              Maturity floor — no economic impact.         The original Second Lien Maturity Date (June 15, 2031) is nine months later than the 91-days-post-1L date (September 14, 2030). Because June 15, 2031 is later, the         “    later of    ”         formulation yields June 15, 2031 as the effective floor, which simultaneously satisfies the First Lien Agent’s 91-day test (93% of deals) and the Second Lien Agent’s original-maturity test (81% of deals). The Second Lien Agent’s position is, in practical effect,             more         restrictive than the First Lien Agent’s, not less; there is no scenario in which the         “    later of    ”         formulation permits a maturity earlier than what the First Lien Agent’s standalone test would permit. This is a clean win-win with no economic cost to either side.  
              MFN — contingent economic impact.         The MFN bites only if a future refinancing tightens the Second Lien’s financial maintenance covenants. The existing Second Lien financial maintenance covenant is a Maximum Total Leverage Ratio of 6.50x; the existing First Lien financial maintenance covenant is a Total First Lien Net Leverage Ratio of 5.50x. If a refinancing tightens the Second Lien’s Total Leverage covenant below 6.50x (or adds a new financial maintenance covenant), the First Lien Lenders would receive the benefit. The economic value is contingent on the terms of a future refinancing and is not presently quantifiable, but the protection is real and is the First Lien’s principal quid pro quo for accepting the narrowed (rather than blanket) test.  
        E. Required Conforming Action — First Lien Credit Agreement Section 6.01(b)  
          This is a critical cross-document point.         Section 6.01(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement permits the Second Lien Obligations         “    in an aggregate principal amount not to exceed $150,000,000 at any time outstanding, so long as such Indebtedness is subject in all respects to the Intercreditor Agreement.    ”         The ICA’s Section 9.10 provides that the ICA governs in the event of a conflict             as among the lien parties    , but the First Lien Credit Agreement’s $150 million indebtedness cap is a covenant by the Borrower to the First Lien Lenders — it is not an intercreditor matter and is not overridden by the ICA as against the Borrower.             Accordingly, the 10% principal-increase cap in the compromise ICA language (permitting up to $165,000,000 of Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness) will not, by itself, permit the Borrower to incur more than $150,000,000 of Second Lien debt.         To give effect to the compromise, the parties must concurrently amend Section 6.01(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement to permit Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness in an aggregate principal amount of up to $165,000,000 (or to add a specific carve-out for Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness as defined in the ICA). This conforming amendment should be identified to both agents as a necessary component of the package and should be documented contemporaneously with the ICA. If this conforming amendment is not made, the 10% cap in the ICA is effectively inert (the First Lien Credit Agreement’s $150 million cap would continue to bind the Borrower), and the Second Lien Agent’s refinancing flexibility would be no greater than under the First Lien Agent’s 0% position — which would defeat the purpose of the compromise on this provision.  
      
         VII. Provision 4 — Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds (Section 4.02)  
       A. Proposed Compromise Language  
          Section 4.02 — Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds.  
              Bifurcated Application.         All Net Cash Proceeds received by the Borrower or any other loan party in respect of any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event shall be applied as follows:  
              Ordinary-Course Sales (Individual Net Cash Proceeds of $20,000,000 or Less).         With respect to any Asset Disposition (other than a Casualty Event) the Net Cash Proceeds of which do not exceed $20,000,000 individually, such Net Cash Proceeds shall be applied in accordance with the reinvestment and mandatory prepayment provisions of Section 2.05(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement, including the $5,000,000 individual threshold, the $15,000,000 annual aggregate threshold, the three hundred sixty-five (365)-day reinvestment period, and the one hundred percent (100%) sweep of non-reinvested proceeds set forth therein. The Borrower shall retain the reinvestment rights granted under Section 2.05(b)(iv) of the First Lien Credit Agreement with respect to such Net Cash Proceeds.  
              Large or Extraordinary Sales and Casualty Events (Individual Net Cash Proceeds Exceeding $20,000,000; All Casualty Events).         With respect to (A) any Asset Disposition the Net Cash Proceeds of which exceed $20,000,000 individually, or (B) any Casualty Event (regardless of amount), one hundred percent (100%) of the Net Cash Proceeds shall be applied to prepay the First Lien Obligations in accordance with the mandatory prepayment provisions of the First Lien Credit Agreement, until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations. Notwithstanding any provision of the First Lien Credit Agreement to the contrary (including the reinvestment rights granted under Section 2.05(b)(iv) thereof), no reinvestment in replacement assets shall be permitted with respect to such Net Cash Proceeds, and no portion of such Net Cash Proceeds shall be available for application to the Second Lien Obligations, for distribution to the Borrower or its equity holders, or for any other purpose, in each case until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations.  
              Anti-Evasion.         No Asset Disposition (and no series of related Asset Dispositions) shall be structured, timed, or otherwise arranged in a manner designed to avoid the $20,000,000 ordinary-course threshold set forth in Section 4.02(a)(i). The First Lien Agent may, in its reasonable discretion, aggregate any Asset Dispositions occurring within a ninety (90)-day period if the First Lien Agent reasonably determines that such Asset Dispositions constitute a single plan or related series of transactions, and in such event the Net Cash Proceeds from all such aggregated Asset Dispositions shall be treated as Net Cash Proceeds from a single Asset Disposition for purposes of determining whether the $20,000,000 threshold has been exceeded. In making such determination, the First Lien Agent shall consider, among other factors, the identity of the acquirer or acquirers, the nature and location of the assets disposed of, and the timing and sequencing of the dispositions.  
              Residual to Second Lien.         After the application of Net Cash Proceeds to the First Lien Obligations in accordance with Section 4.02(a) (including, with respect to ordinary-course sales, after the expiration of the reinvestment period and the application of the 100% sweep of non-reinvested proceeds, and, with respect to large or extraordinary sales and Casualty Events, after the hard 100% sweep), any residual Net Cash Proceeds remaining shall be released to the Second Lien Agent for application to the Second Lien Obligations in accordance with the Second Lien Credit Agreement; provided that no such release shall occur prior to the Discharge of First Lien Obligations except to the extent the First Lien Agent delivers written confirmation to the Second Lien Agent that the First Lien Obligations have been paid in full or that no further application to the First Lien Obligations is required.  
              Reporting.         The Borrower shall deliver to the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent (i) a quarterly certificate of compliance setting forth all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events during the relevant fiscal quarter, the Net Cash Proceeds thereof, and the application of such proceeds pursuant to this Section 4.02; (ii) prompt notice (in any event within five (5) Business Days) of any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event yielding Net Cash Proceeds in excess of the $5,000,000 individual threshold; and (iii) an annual aggregate disposition report containing an itemized list of all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events during the relevant fiscal year and the application of proceeds therefrom.  
        B. Negotiation Rationale  
          What the First Lien Agent gains.         The First Lien Agent obtains its non-negotiable core: a hard 100% sweep to the First Lien Obligations of all Net Cash Proceeds from large or extraordinary Asset Dispositions (those exceeding $20 million) and from all Casualty Events, overriding the reinvestment rights in the First Lien Credit Agreement for those proceeds. This is the protection the First Lien Agent has stated is         “    essential    ”         and         “    not subject to significant compromise,    ”         and it is the universal market standard (100% sweep in all 42 Survey deals; hard sweep for large/extraordinary sales in 90%). The First Lien Agent also obtains robust anti-evasion language with a 90-day aggregation rule (supported by 97% of ordinary-course deals and mirroring the First Lien Credit Agreement’s own existing anti-evasion provision in Section 2.05(b)(iii)), addressing its stated concern that any threshold must be drafted to prevent evasion through sequenced sub-threshold sales.  
          What the First Lien Agent concedes.         The First Lien Agent concedes (i) preservation of reinvestment rights for ordinary-course sales at or below the $20 million threshold, and (ii) release of residual proceeds to the Second Lien after full First Lien satisfaction. Both concessions are supportable. On reinvestment: the First Lien Agent has signaled that reinvestment rights for smaller, ordinary-course asset sales         “    may be acceptable    ”         and that deferring to the First Lien Credit Agreement’s existing reinvestment mechanics for ordinary-course sales below a defined threshold is a         “    workable approach    ”    ; the $20 million threshold is the Survey median for ordinary-course exclusions and is the threshold the parties have discussed informally. On residual: the First Lien Agent has signaled that residual-to-2L is         “    less of a concern    ”         if the hard sweep for significant sales is preserved; the residual is, in any event, economically limited (it arises only after the $475 million First Lien Obligations are paid in full, which requires proceeds plus other paydowns to exceed $475 million against a TEV of $640 million), and it is         “    symbolically important    ”         to the Second Lien.  
          What the Second Lien Agent gains.         The Second Lien Agent obtains (i) preservation of reinvestment rights for ordinary-course sales, respecting the First Lien Credit Agreement’s negotiated reinvestment mechanics (which Meridian’s management has stated are essential to maintaining and modernizing the industrial asset base); (ii) some form of access to residual proceeds after full First Lien satisfaction — its stated minimum position, which it has described as         “    symbolically important even if economically limited    ”         and necessary to demonstrate to its investors and LPs that the intercreditor structure preserves a theoretical waterfall right; and (iii) anti-evasion language that protects against asset-stripping through sequenced sub-threshold sales — a protection the Second Lien Agent itself requested.  
          What the Second Lien Agent concedes.         The Second Lien Agent concedes (i) a hard 100% sweep (with no reinvestment) for large or extraordinary sales and all Casualty Events, and (ii) acceptance of a $20 million ordinary-course threshold (rather than a higher threshold or no threshold). The hard-sweep concession is supportable because the Second Lien Agent has acknowledged that hard sweeps on large or extraordinary sales         “    may be necessary to protect the 1L group’s position.    ”         The $20 million threshold concession is supportable because $20 million is the Survey median and is the threshold the parties have discussed; it represents approximately 22.4% of LTM EBITDA ($89.4 million), a meaningful but bounded operational flexibility for the Borrower.  
          Why the compromise falls within each side’s flexibility.         The First Lien Agent has signaled that a bifurcated approach — deferring to the First Lien Credit Agreement’s reinvestment mechanics for ordinary-course sales below a threshold while imposing a hard sweep for larger dispositions, with anti-evasion language — is a         “    workable approach,    ”         and that residual-to-2L is acceptable if the hard sweep is preserved. The Second Lien Agent has signaled that it can live with the First Lien reinvestment mechanics for ordinary-course sales provided residual flows to the Second Lien, and that it requires anti-evasion language. The compromise delivers all of these elements. The formulation is the exact compromise structure of the most directly comparable Survey transaction (Deal-007: ordinary-course threshold $20 million with anti-evasion at the same level; 365-day reinvestment per the First Lien Credit Agreement; hard sweep for extraordinary sales; 2L residual after full 1L paydown).  
        C. Market Data  
      The Market Survey reports: 1L sweep of 100% of Net Cash Proceeds in all 42 deals (100%, universal); reinvestment right preserved in 34 deals (81%), with a 365-day reinvestment period in 31 of those 34 (91%); hard sweep for large/extraordinary sales in 38 deals (90%); ordinary-course sales excluded in 34 deals (81%), with a median ordinary-course threshold of $20 million (mean $19.7 million, range $10 million–$30 million); anti-evasion clause present in 33 of 34 ordinary-course deals (97%), typically at the same threshold as the ordinary-course exclusion; 2L receives residual after 1L paydown in 33 deals (79%); reporting — quarterly certificate in 38 deals (90%), prompt notice above the individual trigger in 33 (79%), annual aggregate report in 16 (38%). The 2024 trend is the increasing pairing of an ordinary-course reinvestment carve-out with a hard sweep and anti-evasion at $20 million — consistent with the First Lien Credit Agreement’s own terms. Deal-007 (Meridian’s comparable) provides the exact compromise formulation.  
        D. Economic Quantification  
              Ordinary-course threshold.         The $20 million threshold represents approximately 22.4% of LTM EBITDA ($89.4 million), preserving meaningful operational flexibility for the Borrower to manage routine asset turnover (replacement of aging manufacturing equipment, upgrades to production lines) without triggering the hard sweep, while capturing any disposition of real economic significance for the First Lien waterfall.  
              Hard sweep and equity cushion.         With a $165 million equity cushion ($640 million TEV − $475 million First Lien), a single large asset sale swept to the First Lien (e.g., a $50 million facility disposition) directly reduces First Lien exposure and preserves the cushion. If such a sale were instead reinvested or permitted to flow to the Second Lien, the First Lien’s $165 million cushion would be directly eroded — the arithmetic the First Lien Agent’s rationale emphasizes. The hard sweep for sales above $20 million and for all Casualty Events protects this cushion.  
              Residual to 2L — economically limited.         The Second Lien sees residual only if Net Cash Proceeds (plus other First Lien paydowns) exceed the $475 million First Lien Obligations. Given a TEV of $640 million and First Lien debt of $475 million, residual arises only in the scenario where cumulative proceeds are sufficient to pay the First Lien in full — i.e., precisely the scenario in which the Second Lien is entitled to recover. The residual provision is therefore consistent with, and does not impair, the First Lien priority; it merely ensures that value above the First Lien debt is not trapped at the Borrower level but flows to the next-priority secured creditor.  
              Reporting consistency.         The First Lien Credit Agreement already requires a Prepayment Event Notice for any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event yielding Net Cash Proceeds above a $3 million reporting threshold (Section 2.05(b)(v)). The compromise’s $5 million prompt-notice trigger is consistent with (and slightly higher than) the existing First Lien reporting threshold, avoiding a duplicative or more burdensome reporting regime while giving the Second Lien the visibility the Survey majority provides.  
        E. Cross-Document Note — First Lien Credit Agreement Section 2.05(b)  
      The First Lien Credit Agreement’s Section 2.05(b) already contains the $5 million individual threshold, the $15 million annual aggregate threshold, the 365-day reinvestment period, the 100% sweep of non-reinvested proceeds,             and         an anti-evasion provision with a 90-day aggregation rule (Section 2.05(b)(iii)). The compromise ICA Section 4.02(a)(i) expressly             defers to         Section 2.05(b) for ordinary-course sales rather than restating those mechanics, which (i) avoids creating an inconsistency between the ICA and the First Lien Credit Agreement, (ii) avoids the         “    back-door amendment    ”         of the First Lien Credit Agreement through the ICA that the Second Lien Agent objected to, and (iii) ensures that any future amendment to Section 2.05(b) (agreed between the Borrower and the First Lien Lenders) automatically governs the ordinary-course waterfall without requiring a conforming ICA amendment. The ICA’s             own         operative provisions — the hard sweep for sales above $20 million and for Casualty Events (Section 4.02(a)(ii)), the anti-evasion at the $20 million level (Section 4.02(b)), the residual-to-2L waterfall (Section 4.02(c)), and the reporting (Section 4.02(d)) — are ICA-specific and control over any inconsistent First Lien Credit Agreement provision as among the lien parties pursuant to Section 9.10 of the ICA.  
      
         VIII. Package Indivisibility and Cross-Provision Trade-Offs  
      The four compromises are presented as a single, indivisible package because the concessions are mutually offsetting and were calibrated against one another. The specific linkages are as follows:  
             Standstill duration (1L-favorable) ↔ Standstill triggers and Blockage Notice cap (2L-favorable).         The First Lien Agent’s acceptance of 180 days (above its 150-day floor, at the market median) is the consideration for the Second Lien Agent’s acceptance of three early-termination triggers and a Blockage Notice cap. Conversely, the Second Lien Agent’s acceptance of 180 days (double its 90-day opening) is conditioned on the triggers and the cap. Neither element stands alone: a 180-day standstill without triggers is not acceptable to the Second Lien, and triggers without a 180-day duration do not give the First Lien the workout window its rationale requires.  
             Par purchase price (1L-favorable) ↔ Non-default interest rate and optional revolver assumption (2L-favorable).         The Second Lien Agent’s concession of par for funded debt (dropping the FMV alternative) is the consideration for the First Lien Agent’s concessions on the interest rate (non-default rather than default) and the revolver (optional rather than mandatory). The optional-revolver structure is, in addition, a structural necessity for Ridgeline given its LPA constraints — it is not a concession the First Lien Agent can re-trade for additional 1L-favorable terms elsewhere without collapsing the purchase right’s viability for the very party it is designed to benefit.  
             10% refinancing cap and narrowed restrictiveness test (1L-favorable on the test; 2L-favorable on the cap) ↔ MFN right (1L-favorable) and maturity                 “        later of        ”                 formulation (win-win).         The First Lien Agent’s acceptance of a 10% upsize (versus 0%) and a narrowed test (versus blanket) is the consideration for the Second Lien Agent’s acceptance of a restrictiveness test (versus none) and an MFN right. The maturity         “    later of    ”         formulation is a win-win that costs neither side anything but resolves the dispute cleanly. The 10% cap is, in turn, dependent on the conforming amendment to First Lien Credit Agreement Section 6.01(b) described in Section VI.E — without that amendment, the cap is inert and the balance on this provision collapses.  
             Hard sweep for large/extraordinary sales and Casualty Events (1L-favorable) ↔ Ordinary-course reinvestment and residual to 2L (2L-favorable).         The First Lien Agent’s non-negotiable hard sweep is preserved; the Second Lien Agent’s minimum (reinvestment for ordinary-course sales and residual access) is preserved. The anti-evasion and reporting provisions protect both sides and are not contested in principle.  
          Why indivisibility matters.         If the package were unbundled, each agent would have an incentive to accept the provisions favorable to its side (the First Lien Agent: 180-day standstill, par purchase price, restrictiveness test, hard sweep; the Second Lien Agent: early-termination triggers, non-default rate, optional revolver, 10% cap, ordinary-course reinvestment, residual access) while re-litigating the provisions favorable to the other. That cherry-picking would return the parties to the positional impasse and consume the remaining 34 days without resolution. Presenting the package as indivisible — and having Meridian endorse it as a coherent whole to both agents — is the mechanism most likely to produce closure on the timeline the transaction requires. Meridian is uniquely positioned to make this endorsement credible, because both agents recognize that the Borrower needs both lender groups fully committed and closing on time, and that neither side can walk away.  
      
        IX. Cross-Cutting Observations and Required Conforming Actions  
      The following cross-cutting points bear on the package as a whole and should be communicated to both agents together with the compromise language:  
             Conforming amendment to First Lien Credit Agreement Section 6.01(b) (required).         As detailed in Section VI.E, the 10% principal-increase cap in the compromise ICA language will not operate unless Section 6.01(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement is concurrently amended to permit up to $165,000,000 of Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness. This conforming amendment is a necessary component of the package and should be documented contemporaneously with the ICA. This is the single most important cross-document action item.  
             Cross-reference to First Lien Credit Agreement Section 2.05(b) (recommended).         As detailed in Section VII.E, the compromise ICA Section 4.02(a)(i) defers to Section 2.05(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement for ordinary-course sales rather than restating the mechanics. This avoids inconsistency and back-door amendment and should be preserved in the definitive drafting.  
             Maturity floor is a non-issue (win-win).         The         “    later of (91 days post-1L) and (original 2L maturity)    ”         formulation resolves the maturity dispute at no economic cost to either side, because June 15, 2031 is later than September 14, 2030. The Second Lien Agent’s position is, in practical effect, more restrictive than the First Lien Agent’s. This should be presented to both agents as a point already resolved in principle.  
             Financial-covenant headroom context (relevant to the MFN and restrictiveness test).         The First Lien financial maintenance covenant is a Total First Lien Net Leverage Ratio of 5.50x; the Closing Date First Lien leverage is approximately 5.31x, leaving only approximately 0.19x of headroom. The Second Lien financial maintenance covenant is a Maximum Total Leverage Ratio of 6.50x; the Closing Date Total Leverage is approximately 6.99x, which is             above         the 6.50x cap (the covenant is not tested at closing but commences with the first full fiscal quarter ending after the Closing Date). This means the Borrower has very limited covenant headroom at both lien levels, and any tightening of the Second Lien’s Total Leverage covenant in a refinancing (the scenario the MFN addresses) could push the Borrower into default. The MFN right protects the First Lien against the Second Lien obtaining tighter covenants in a refinancing, and the narrowed restrictiveness test protects the Borrower against covenant tightening that would destabilize the capital structure. Both agents should understand that the covenant headroom is thin and that the compromise’s covenant-related provisions are calibrated to that reality.  
             Appraisal going-concern caveat (relevant to the standstill and waterfall).         The $640 million TEV is a going-concern value, not a liquidation or forced-sale value. In the distressed scenarios in which the standstill, the purchase right, and the waterfall operate, realizable value would be materially below $640 million. This caveat supports the First Lien Agent’s concern about collateral erosion during a prolonged standstill             and         the Second Lien Agent’s concern that a prolonged standstill permits value to decline below the second lien’s cushion — it is a reason to set the standstill at the market median (180 days) rather than at either extreme, and to preserve the hard sweep for large sales while permitting ordinary-course reinvestment.

KTM’s own standard-form first lien/second lien ICA (New York law) provides for a 180-day standstill (Section 3.1), a par purchase price (Section 5.2), and a proceeds waterfall that defers to the First Lien Credit Agreement’s mandatory prepayment provisions for non-enforcement proceeds with residual to the Second Lien (Section 4.2). The compromise package is consistent with KTM’s house form, which should give both agents comfort that the compromise reflects a workable, market-standard structure rather than a novel formulation.

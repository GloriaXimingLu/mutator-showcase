       I. Executive Summary  
      This document sets out proposed compromise contract language for the five provisions of the MKPoly Joint Venture Agreement (the         “    JVA    ”    ) that remain contested following two prior rounds of negotiation: (1) Management Committee composition and governance (Section 5.01); (2) capital calls and dilution (Section 4.03); (3) non-competition (Section 11.01); (4) exit valuation methodology (Sections 10.03–10.04); and (5) IP license-back and co-developed IP (Section 12.05; Schedules 12.01, 12.02, and 12.05).  
      For each provision, this document presents (a) ready-to-insert compromise clause language; (b) a summary of each side’s opening and current positions; (c) a negotiation rationale explaining why the proposed language represents a fair middle ground in light of the parties’ respective contributions, risk profiles, and market norms in comparable specialty-chemicals joint ventures; and (d) cross-references to related provisions of the JVA.

           C. Overall balance  
      The package is designed so that each party’s largest concessions are made on the provisions it ranks as lower priority, while each party’s highest-priority concerns are preserved. Meridian’s highest-priority concern — preventing its proprietary coating formulations from reaching its North American competitors through Kastor sublicensing — is preserved by the absolute prohibition on any license of Meridian contributed IP to Kastor, the territorial limit on Kastor’s co-developed IP sublicensing (outside North America only), and the reciprocal Restricted Competitors and 25%-revenue covenants. Kastor’s highest-priority concerns — protective vetoes on Reserved Matters, an independent exit valuation, and a broad co-developed IP license-back — are preserved by the supermajority voting on Reserved Matters, the independent (non-Frazier Whitmore) appraiser, and the non-exclusive perpetual co-developed IP license to both parties with territorial sublicensing. The middle-ground provisions (non-compete duration and capital-call mechanics) are calibrated to market norms reflected in three comparable specialty-chemicals joint-venture transactions, each of which is cited in the rationales below.

           C. Negotiation rationale  
          Board composition (3-2).         Meridian holds a 55% equity interest and contributes the proprietary coating technology that forms the basis of the JV’s product portfolio; a board majority reflecting that equity position is the standard mechanism for majority-held Delaware LLC joint ventures and is consistent with Comparable Transaction #1 (a 60/40 specialty-coatings JV with a 3-2 board) and Comparable Transaction #2 (a 55/45 JV in which the majority partner retained effective control through officer-appointment authority and a casting vote). Kastor has identified board composition numbers as an area of greater flexibility, and accepts the 3-2 structure in exchange for the protective voting rights described below. The 3-2 composition gives Meridian the operational leadership appropriate to its majority equity stake and operator role, while the supermajority mechanism gives Kastor the protective rights appropriate to its near-parity capital commitment.  
          Supermajority on Reserved Matters (4 of 5).         The supermajority threshold of four of five Managers necessarily requires the affirmative vote of at least one Kastor Manager, giving Kastor an effective veto on each Reserved Matter. This addresses Kastor’s core governance concern: that its $43.8M capital commitment — which modestly exceeds Meridian’s $42.9M — warrants protective rights commensurate with its economic stake. The Reserved Matters list is deliberately confined to genuinely existential or structurally significant decisions (amendment, dissolution, asset sales, new members, related-party transactions, business-purpose changes, major capital expenditures and indebtedness, material IP disposition, and appointment/removal of the CEO and CTO). It expressly excludes ordinary-course operating decisions — annual budget approval (except material deviations constituting a business-purpose change), hiring and termination below the C-suite, ordinary customer/supplier contracts, pricing, and product-development priorities — which remain within the CEO’s delegated authority or simple-majority vote. This boundary is the principal Meridian concession on governance: Meridian accepts a Kastor veto on the decisions that most affect a minority partner’s investment, while preserving Meridian’s ability to direct the JV’s day-to-day operations without minority consent. The confinement of the list is the principal Kastor concession: Kastor accepts a narrower set of veto rights than its opening position, in exchange for the certainty that those rights will be meaningful.  
          Tiered deadlock resolution.         The compromise adopts a tiered structure that gives each side its principal deadlock concern while mitigating the other side’s principal objection. Kastor obtains the escalation-and-mediation process it sought (principals’ negotiation, then formal mediation) before any unilateral resolution, ensuring that no party can short-circuit a genuine disagreement. Meridian retains the CEO’s deciding vote on operational (non-Reserved) matters as the final tier, ensuring that the JV is never operationally paralyzed by an unresolved operating dispute — a practical necessity given that the CEO, as a Meridian appointee, is responsible for day-to-day management. For Reserved Matters, where the parties’ interests are most directly adverse and where unilateral resolution by either side would be most disruptive, the final tier is the buy-sell mechanism under Article X rather than a casting vote. This confines the forced-buyout trigger to Reserved-Matter deadlocks that have survived two prior escalation steps, materially reducing the risk — flagged by Meridian — that a party could manufacture deadlocks to trigger an exit at a favorable valuation, while preserving Kastor’s interest in an orderly exit path when the partnership becomes genuinely unworkable on a fundamental issue.  
          Officer appointments.         The CEO is appointed by Meridian (subject to Management Committee confirmation), consistent with Meridian’s operator role and 55% equity position and with Comparable Transaction #2 (majority partner’s sole right to appoint the CEO). The CTO is jointly approved with each Member holding a veto, reflecting that the CTO oversees the JV’s IP portfolio and R&D activities — areas in which both Members have legitimate technical interests (Meridian in coating formulations, Kastor in manufacturing processes). The CFO is appointed by the Management Committee on the CEO’s recommendation, a neutral middle ground. The CEO’s delegated authority for in-budget actions under $1,000,000 ensures that routine operational decisions are not subject to Board-level deadlock.

         IV. Provision 2 — Capital Calls and Dilution (Section 4.03)  
       A. Proposed compromise clause language  
          Section 4.03 — Capital Calls; Failure to Fund; Dilution  
          (a) Capital Calls.         If at any time after the Closing the Management Committee determines that the Company requires additional capital beyond the Initial Capital Contributions and available internal resources, the Management Committee may approve a Capital Call as a Reserved Matter under Section 5.01(e). Upon approval, the Company shall promptly deliver to each Member a written Capital Call Notice specifying (i) the aggregate amount of additional capital required, (ii) each Member’s pro-rata share (based on its then-current Percentage Interest), (iii) the purpose for which the additional capital is required, and (iv) the Funding Deadline.  
          (b) Funding Obligation.         Each Member shall fund its pro-rata share of a Capital Call by wire transfer of immediately available funds to an account designated by the Company within thirty (30) calendar days after receipt of the Capital Call Notice (the         “    Funding Deadline    ”    ). To the extent Kastor’s funding obligations are funded from euro-denominated accounts, Kastor may fund in euros at the European Central Bank reference rate on the date of the Capital Call Notice, with conversion to U.S. dollars handled by the Company’s treasury at the Company’s cost; this accommodation is intended to eliminate foreign-exchange conversion as a basis for delay.  
          (c) Failure to Fund; Cure Period.         If a Member (the         “    Defaulting Member    ”    ) fails to fund its pro-rata share of a Capital Call by the Funding Deadline, the Company shall promptly deliver a written Default Notice to the Defaulting Member specifying the unfunded amount and the consequences of failing to cure. The Defaulting Member shall have a cure period of forty-five (45) calendar days from receipt of the Default Notice (the         “    Cure Period    ”    ) to fund the full amount of its unfunded pro-rata share. During the Cure Period, penalty interest shall accrue automatically on the unfunded amount at a rate per annum equal to the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (SOFR) plus four hundred (400) basis points, compounding monthly, payable to the Company for the account of the Non-Defaulting Member upon funding or upon dilution, as applicable.  
          (d) Optional Bridge Loan.         During the Cure Period, the Non-Defaulting Member may, at its sole election (but not obligation), offer to extend a bridge loan to the Defaulting Member in an amount equal to the Defaulting Member’s unfunded pro-rata share of the Capital Call. If offered and accepted by the Defaulting Member, the bridge loan shall (i) bear interest at the rate set forth in Section 4.03(c), (ii) be secured by a first-priority security interest in the Defaulting Member’s entire membership interest in the Company (with UCC financing statements to be filed promptly upon disbursement), (iii) be repayable in full (principal plus accrued interest) within ninety (90) days of disbursement, and (iv) automatically convert, in the event of non-payment in full by the ninetieth (90th) day, into an additional Capital Contribution by the Non-Defaulting Member, resulting in dilution of the Defaulting Member’s Percentage Interest calculated in accordance with Section 4.03(e). The bridge-loan option shall be available to a Member no more than once in any twenty-four (24)-month period; any subsequent failure to fund within such period shall result in dilution under Section 4.03(e) without the availability of a bridge loan.  
          (e) Dilution.         If the Defaulting Member fails to fund its full pro-rata share (plus accrued penalty interest) within the Cure Period, and no bridge loan under Section 4.03(d) is in place and fully repaid, the Non-Defaulting Member shall have the right (but not the obligation) to fund the entire unfunded amount. Upon such funding, the Percentage Interests of the Members shall be adjusted on a pro-rata basis to reflect the relative aggregate Capital Contributions of the Members (treating the Defaulting Member’s unfunded amount as a Capital Contribution of zero), in accordance with the following formula:  
      New Percentage Interest of each Member = (Aggregate Capital Contributions of such Member) ÷ (Aggregate Capital Contributions of all Members) × 100.  
      The aggregate dilution of a Defaulting Member’s Percentage Interest in any single Fiscal Year shall not exceed fifteen percent (15%) of its then-current Percentage Interest (the         “    Annual Dilution Cap    ”    ). Any dilution that would exceed the Annual Dilution Cap in a given Fiscal Year shall be carried forward and applied in succeeding Fiscal Years until fully applied.  
          (f) Forced Buy-Sell upon Cumulative Dilution.         Notwithstanding the Annual Dilution Cap, if a Member’s Percentage Interest is diluted by an aggregate amount exceeding fifteen percent (15%) in any twenty-four (24)-month period (whether as a result of a single Capital Call Default or a series of Capital Call Defaults), the Non-Defaulting Member shall have the right (but not the obligation), exercisable by written notice within sixty (60) days after such threshold is reached, to initiate the buy-sell procedures set forth in Article X to purchase the Defaulting Member’s remaining membership interest at the Appraised Fair Market Value determined in accordance with Section 10.03. This forced buy-sell right is intended to prevent a Member from indefinitely retaining a substantial equity and governance position while persistently failing to fund its pro-rata share of Capital Calls.  
          (g) Suspension of Voting Rights.         During any period in which a Member is a Defaulting Member (i.e., has failed to fund its pro-rata share and the Cure Period has not expired or the default has not been cured), such Member’s voting rights on the Management Committee shall be suspended with respect to all matters requiring or relating to additional capital expenditure by the Company, including approval of the annual operating budget and any capital expenditure proposals. Such suspension shall not affect the Defaulting Member’s rights with respect to Reserved Matters that do not relate to capital expenditures.  
          (h) No Cap on Governance Consequences.         For the avoidance of doubt, the Annual Dilution Cap in Section 4.03(e) limits only the rate of equity dilution in any single Fiscal Year and does not limit (i) the accrual of penalty interest under Section 4.03(c), (ii) the operation of the forced buy-sell mechanism under Section 4.03(f), or (iii) the suspension of voting rights under Section 4.03(g).

           C. Negotiation rationale  
          Cure period (75 days total).         Meridian’s opening 30-day cure period and Kastor’s opening 90-day cure period reflect legitimate but different concerns: Meridian requires funding certainty to operate the JV, while Kastor, as a Swiss-headquartered multinational with multi-currency treasury operations, requires time to arrange U.S.-dollar funding across internal authorization, foreign-exchange execution, and Swiss regulatory reporting. The compromise of a 30-day initial funding period plus a 45-day cure period (75 days total) sits at the midpoint and is consistent with the 45-to-60-day cure periods observed in Comparable Transactions #1, #2, and #3. The euro-funding accommodation in Section 4.03(b) directly addresses Kastor’s operational concern by allowing Kastor to fund in euros at a locked rate with the Company absorbing the conversion cost — removing foreign-exchange conversion as a basis for delay and thereby justifying a cure period shorter than Kastor’s opening 90-day request. This is a low-cost concession for Meridian (the Company bears only the conversion spread) that meaningfully reduces Kastor’s funding friction.  
          Penalty interest (SOFR + 400 bps).         Penalty interest accruing during the cure period serves two functions: it compensates the Non-Defaulting Member (through the Company) for the time value of delayed capital, and it creates a real economic incentive for the Defaulting Member to fund promptly rather than using the cure period as free financing. SOFR + 400 basis points (currently approximately 8.5% per annum) is meaningfully above a market lending rate (Kastor’s proposed SOFR + 250 bps) but below the punitive SOFR + 500 bps / 10% level Meridian initially sought. The rate is calibrated to make late funding costly without being so punitive as to be uncommercial, and it applies symmetrically to either party.  
          Bridge loan (optional, secured, auto-converting).         The bridge-loan option is the principal structural compromise on this provision. Kastor sought a market-rate loan alternative that would preserve the non-funding Member’s equity position indefinitely; Meridian sought no loan alternative at all, on the ground that a market-rate loan eliminates the incentive to fund (a rational party would borrow at SOFR + 250 bps rather than invest equity expected to generate higher returns). The compromise gives Kastor a genuine loan pathway — preserving equity through a temporary liquidity constraint — while preserving Meridian’s incentive structure through four features: (i) the loan is at the penalty rate (SOFR + 400 bps), not a market rate; (ii) it is secured by a first-priority interest in the borrowing Member’s entire JV interest; (iii) it must be repaid in full within 90 days; and (iv) any unpaid balance automatically converts to equity (dilution) on day 91. The single-use-per-24-months limitation prevents serial use of the bridge to defer funding indefinitely. The net effect is that the bridge is a short-term accommodation for a genuine liquidity constraint, not an escape hatch from capital commitments — and if the borrowing Member cannot repay within 90 days, the end state is the same as dilution, with the lending Member having earned penalty interest in the interim. This structure is consistent with Cordero’s guidance that a         “    short-term bridge that converts to dilution    ”         could work without gutting the incentive structure.  
          Dilution cap (15% per annum) with forced-sale backstop.         This is the most carefully calibrated element. Meridian’s opening position of uncapped immediate dilution provides the strongest incentive to fund but, in Kastor’s view, creates a risk of disproportionate equity loss from a single large Capital Call during a temporary liquidity constraint. Kastor’s opening 10% per annum cap protects against that risk but, in Meridian’s view, would allow a non-funding Member to retain a substantial equity and governance position for years while free-riding on the funding Member’s capital. The compromise adopts a 15% per annum cap — the midpoint between the two positions and the exact figure used in Comparable Transaction #2 (a 55/45 advanced-materials JV) — paired with an automatic buy-sell trigger if cumulative dilution exceeds 15% in any 24-month period. The cap protects Kastor against extreme single-year dilution from a single large Capital Call; the forced-sale backstop prevents the cap from becoming a shield for persistent non-funding, because once a Member is diluted 15% over 24 months, the funding Member may acquire the remaining interest at fair market value. This structure — cap plus forced-sale backstop — is directly modeled on Comparable Transaction #2 and converts the dilution cap from a free-rider protection into a bridge to an orderly exit, satisfying both sides’ core concerns.  
          Suspension of voting rights.         During a default, the Defaulting Member’s voting rights on capital-related matters are suspended. This prevents a non-funding Member from voting on the very capital decisions it has failed to fund, while preserving its rights on non-capital Reserved Matters. This provision is symmetric and protects whichever party is the funding Member.

           C. Negotiation rationale  
          Duration (18 months).         Both parties’ opening positions — 24 months (Meridian) and 12 months (Kastor) — fall within the one-to-two-year safe harbor that Delaware and Texas courts recognize as presumptively reasonable for non-competition covenants ancillary to the sale or disposition of a business interest (as distinct from employment restraints, which receive heightened scrutiny). The 18-month compromise sits at the center of that safe harbor and is the median duration observed across the three comparable specialty-chemicals joint-venture transactions (18 months in Comparable #1, 24 months in Comparable #2, 12 months in Comparable #3). The scaled-back 12-month period for exits occurring within the first 24 months of the JV reflects the reality that, early in the venture’s life, the exiting Member will have had less time to acquire the Company’s goodwill, customer relationships, and confidential information, and a shorter restraint is therefore proportionate; this also mitigates the enforceability risk (noted in the multi-jurisdictional enforceability analysis) that a court could find a full 18-month restraint unsupported where the venture has not yet generated meaningful goodwill. New York law would enforce any duration in the one-to-five-year range in a sale-of-business context, so the 18-month period faces no constraint there. California’s general statutory prohibition on non-competes (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600) is addressed through the LLC-dissociation exception in § 16602.5 and the forum-selection clause channeling enforcement to Delaware or Texas; the narrower the covenant’s scope, the more defensibly it fits within the § 16602.5 exception.  
          Product scope (actual products + substantially similar/directly competitive).         Meridian’s opening scope of         “    all specialty polymer coatings    ”         is broader than necessary to protect the Company’s legitimate interests and risks being narrowed or voided as overbroad, because it would capture product categories the Company may never have entered. Kastor’s opening scope of         “    specific JV product lines only    ”         is narrower than necessary and invites gamesmanship: a departing Member could launch a functionally identical product under a different name or formulation and argue it falls outside the listed product lines. The compromise — products actually produced, marketed, sold, or under active development by the Company, plus products substantially similar to or directly competitive with those products — tracks the Company’s actual business as defined in Schedule 2.04 while providing a protective         “    penumbra    ”         that prevents circumvention through minor product distinctions. This formulation is the one recommended by the multi-jurisdictional enforceability analysis as satisfying the         “    no broader than necessary    ”         standard (Delaware), the         “    reasonable relationship to the value of the business    ”         standard (Texas), and the         “    similar business    ”         requirement of California’s § 16602.5. It is broader than Kastor’s opening position (a Kastor concession) and narrower than Meridian’s opening position (a Meridian concession), and it is anchored to a defined, durable reference (Schedule 2.04) rather than to a fixed product list that could become stale.  
          Geography (United States + Canada + >$500,000-revenue jurisdictions).         Meridian’s         “    all of North America    ”         formulation provides certainty but, if the Company has not yet established sales throughout North America at the time of exit, could be challenged as extending beyond the Company’s actual market reach. Kastor’s         “    territories with active sales at exit    ”         formulation addresses overbreadth but introduces definitional ambiguity (what constitutes an         “    active sale    ”         or a         “    territory    ”    ) and could exclude jurisdictions where the Company is actively developing market entry. The compromise — the United States and Canada (the Company’s operational territory and primary target market) plus any other jurisdiction where the Company generated more than $500,000 in revenue in the 24 months preceding exit — anchors the restriction to the Company’s actual commercial presence (satisfying the California § 16602.5 nexus requirement and the Delaware         “    actual market reach    ”         standard) while providing substantive, predictable coverage. If the Company achieves its projected sales distribution, the practical coverage of this formulation will be essentially coextensive with Meridian’s         “    North America    ”         proposal; if the Company’s footprint is narrower than projected, the restriction scales down accordingly, which is the proportionate outcome. The $500,000 de minimis threshold and 24-month lookback period provide certainty and avoid disputes over incidental sales.  
          Enforceability provisions.         The compromise incorporates the protective drafting measures recommended by the enforceability analysis: (i) express characterization of the covenant as ancillary to the disposition of a membership interest (the predicate for sale-of-business treatment in all four surveyed jurisdictions and for the California § 16602.5 exception); (ii) a blue-pencil/reformation clause authorizing a court to reform an overbroad covenant to the minimum extent necessary (effective in Delaware, Texas, and New York); (iii) a forum-selection clause channeling enforcement to the Delaware Court of Chancery or Harris County, Texas (both favorable jurisdictions, away from California); and (iv) a cross-reference to Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors), which reinforces the product/activity scope by naming the specific competitors with whom the departing Member may not become associated. These provisions do not favor either party; they maximize the enforceability of whatever scope the parties agree to, which is in both parties’ interest.

          Multiple (7.0× baseline, 6.5×–7.5× collar). The compromise adopts a 7.0× baseline — near the 7.1× median and within Kastor’s stated acceptable band of 6.5×–7.5× — with a narrow collar of 6.5×–7.5× within which the Independent Appraiser may adjust based on documented market conditions. This gives Meridian predictability (the outcome will fall within a defined, narrow band, with 7.0× as the default) and gives Kastor both a higher baseline than Meridian’s opening (a Meridian concession) and meaningful upside flexibility (the multiple could reach 7.5× if market conditions warrant). The illustrative analysis in the Frazier Whitmore benchmark workbook shows that, at a base-case projected LTM EBITDA of $12.8M, a 7.0× multiple implies an enterprise value of approximately $89.6M — a modest premium to the $86.7M initial capitalization — while a 7.5× multiple implies approximately $96.0M; both are within the range supported by the comparable data.

This is the principal Kastor gain on this provision and addresses Kastor’s stated top-priority concern: that the JV’s own auditor, which maintains an ongoing commercial relationship with the Company and reports to a Meridian-influenced management structure, should not also serve as the valuation appraiser in a buyout where the Members’ interests are directly adverse. Meridian concedes this point and gives up Frazier Whitmore as appraiser — acknowledging that the independence concern has technical merit, even though Frazier Whitmore’s institutional knowledge of the Company’s financials would otherwise promote efficiency. In exchange, Kastor accepts a simpler selection mechanism than its opening three-appraiser baseball-arbitration process: a single jointly-selected firm, with mutual agreement within 30 days and an AAA fallback if agreement fails. This is the simpler mechanism both principals have indicated they would prefer, and it preserves full independence (the firm has no pre-existing engagement with the Company or either Member) while avoiding the cost, delay, and complexity of a three-appraiser process. Frazier Whitmore’s comparable-transactions data remains available to the Independent Appraiser as market reference, preserving the analytical value of that dataset without the independence concern. The optional baseball-arbitration alternative in Section 10.05 preserves Kastor’s preferred mechanism as an available option by mutual consent, without making it the default.  
          Methodology (EBITDA-only, no DCF as primary).         Both parties agree that trailing-12-month EBITDA should be the foundational metric. EBITDA is calculated from audited financial statements, is verifiable by both parties, and eliminates the most significant source of valuation dispute (the capitalization rate, which is fixed by the agreed multiple). The prohibition on discounted cash flow as the primary method — a Meridian priority — reflects the concern that DCF valuations in a disputed exit are susceptible to manipulation of growth-rate, discount-rate, and terminal-value assumptions. The DCF-vs.-multiple comparison in the Frazier Whitmore benchmark workbook shows that, across three comparable transactions, DCF valuations varied from the EBITDA-multiple valuations by between -7.1% and +9.7%, with a mean absolute variance of 6.5%; the illustrative MKPoly base case shows a DCF premium of approximately 6.3% over the 7.0× multiple valuation. The shoot-out structure — in which the Initiating Member offers to buy or sell and the Responding Member elects to accept the offer or reverse-purchase at the same valuation — is symmetric and self-disciplining: because either party may end up the buyer or the seller at the appraised value, both parties are incentivized to support a fair valuation methodology, and neither party can use the mechanism to force an exit at a distorted price. The default-to-sell rule (if the Responding Member fails to elect) ensures the mechanism does not stall.

The licenses under this Section 12.05(d) are non-exclusive; neither Member’s license shall prevent the other Member from using the same Co-Developed IP or Improvements in its own operations.  
          (e) Territorial Sublicensing of Co-Developed IP.         Notwithstanding Section 12.05(d), each Member shall have the right to sublicense Co-Developed IP and Improvements to third parties, but only within such Member’s respective territory:  
          Meridian may sublicense Co-Developed IP and Improvements within the Americas (North America, Central America, South America, and the Caribbean); and  
          Kastor may sublicense Co-Developed IP and Improvements outside North America (i.e., in Europe, Asia, Africa, Oceania, and the Middle East).  
      For the avoidance of doubt, Kastor shall have no right to sublicense Co-Developed IP or Improvements within North America, and Meridian shall have no right to sublicense Co-Developed IP or Improvements outside the Americas.  
          (f) Restricted Competitors Covenant.         Notwithstanding Section 12.05(e), neither Member shall sublicense Co-Developed IP or Improvements to any Person listed on Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors), regardless of the territory in which the sublicense would be exercised. This prohibition is reciprocal and applies equally to both Members. Schedule 12.05 shall identify, by name, the specific Persons (and their controlled Affiliates, meaning entities in which a listed Person owns, directly or indirectly, fifty percent (50%) or more of the voting equity) to which neither Member may sublicense, and shall include any successor or assign that acquires all or substantially all of the assets or business of any listed Person. Schedule 12.05 may be updated by the Management Committee from time to time in accordance with Section 12.05; no Person shall be removed from Schedule 12.05 without the prior written consent of both Members.  
          (g) Revenue-Based Negative Covenant.         In addition to the Restricted Competitors covenant in Section 12.05(f), neither Member shall sublicense Co-Developed IP or Improvements to any Person that derives more than twenty-five percent (25%) of its consolidated annual revenue from the manufacture, sale, or distribution of specialty polymer coatings in North America. This covenant is reciprocal and operates as a dynamic anti-circumvention mechanism to capture Persons not named on Schedule 12.05, including new market entrants, existing companies that expand into specialty polymer coatings after the date of this Agreement, and entities that restructure to avoid the named-entity list.  
          (h) Audit Right.         Each Member shall have the right, upon reasonable prior written notice and at its own expense, to audit the other Member’s sublicensing records no more than once per calendar year to verify compliance with the territorial restrictions in Section 12.05(e) and the covenants in Sections 12.05(f) and 12.05(g). The audited Member shall cooperate in good faith and provide reasonable access to relevant records, subject to customary confidentiality protections.  
          (i) Improvements to Contributed IP.         The Members acknowledge that, during the term of this Agreement, the Company may develop Improvements to the Contributed IP of either Member using Company resources. The following principles shall apply:  
          During the term of this Agreement, Improvements to a Member’s Contributed IP that are developed by, funded by, or at the direction of the Company shall be owned by the Company.  
          Upon reversion under Section 12.05(a), Improvements to Meridian Contributed IP shall revert to Meridian, and Improvements to Kastor Contributed IP shall revert to Kastor.  
          Upon such reversion, the non-contributing Member shall receive a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to use the Improvements to the other Member’s Contributed IP, subject to the same territorial and sublicensing restrictions set forth in Sections 12.05(d), (e), (f), and (g) as apply to Co-Developed IP (i.e., Kastor may use and sublicense Improvements to Meridian Contributed IP only outside North America and subject to the Restricted Competitors and revenue-based covenants; Meridian may use and sublicense Improvements to Kastor Contributed IP only within the Americas and subject to the same covenants).  
          Classification. An Improvement shall be classified as an Improvement to a Member’s Contributed IP only if it is primarily derivative of, and builds primarily upon, that Member’s underlying Contributed IP. Improvements that build equally upon both Members’ Contributed IP, or that are not primarily derivative of either Member’s Contributed IP, shall be classified as Co-Developed IP and treated in accordance with Sections 12.05(d) through (h). Disputes regarding classification shall be resolved by reference to the Company IP Register maintained by the CTO under Schedule 12.02 and, if necessary, by the dispute resolution procedures in Article XIX.  
          (j) Survival.         The license-back provisions of this Section 12.05 (including the licenses granted, the territorial sublicensing rights, the Restricted Competitors and revenue-based covenants, the audit right, and the treatment of Improvements) shall survive the dissolution of the Company, the termination of this Agreement, and the completion of any buy-sell transaction under Article X, and shall remain in full force and effect in perpetuity.

           C. Negotiation rationale  
          Contributed IP reversion (agreed).         Both sides agree that each Member’s Contributed IP reverts to the contributing Member upon exit or dissolution. This is not contested and is restated for completeness.  
          No license of Meridian Contributed IP to Kastor.         Meridian’s 23 Contributed IP items — independently appraised at $28,600,000 by Frazier Whitmore & Co., LLP, with 17 of the 23 items embedded in JV products — constitute the proprietary coating formulations that are the JV’s core competitive differentiator and the principal commercial justification for the 55/45 equity split. These formulations represent over 15 years of R&D investment and cannot be replicated through independent R&D in any commercially reasonable timeframe. The compromise provides that no license of any kind to Meridian Contributed IP is granted to Kastor after reversion. This is supported by the asymmetry between the parties’ contributed IP: Meridian’s IP is independently appraised at $28.6M and is competitively unique, while Kastor’s 9 items are manufacturing process documentation that is operationally important but not independently cash-valued and not competitively unique. It is also supported by market practice: in Comparable Transactions #1 and #2, no sublicensing of contributed IP was permitted by either party, and in Comparable Transaction #3, contributed IP reverted with no cross-license. Kastor’s minimum position on contributed IP is reversion only (which is satisfied); Kastor’s principal gain on this provision comes from the co-developed IP license-back below, which is Kastor’s actual top priority.  
          Meridian use license to Kastor Contributed IP.         Meridian receives a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual, non-sublicensable license to use Kastor’s 9 Contributed IP items in its own internal operations, including the continued manufacture and sale of products developed during the JV term. This is consistent with Kastor’s own proposal that the non-contributing party receive a use license to contributed IP, and it is commercially necessary: Meridian will need Kastor’s manufacturing process documentation to continue producing MKPoly-legacy products after exit. The license is non-sublicensable, preserving Kastor’s control over its process documentation.  
          Co-Developed IP — non-exclusive license to both parties (Kastor’s top priority).         This is the principal Kastor gain on this provision and addresses Kastor’s highest-priority concern: that its $43.8M capital contribution (of which approximately $41.8M is cash that directly funds the JV’s R&D) should entitle it to meaningful rights in the intellectual property created during the JV’s life. Meridian concedes its opening position of an exclusive co-developed IP license to Meridian only, and accepts that both parties receive a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual, irrevocable license to all Co-Developed IP for their own internal operations worldwide. This ensures that Kastor, having funded the creation of Co-Developed IP, can use the fruits of that investment in its ongoing global chemicals business — the core of Kastor’s participation rationale. The license is non-exclusive, so Meridian retains full rights to use the same Co-Developed IP, preserving the symmetry Kastor sought while not granting Kastor any exclusivity that would restrict Meridian’s use of its own contributed technology as improved by the JV.  
          Territorial sublicensing (Kastor outside North America; Meridian in the Americas).         Kastor’s global business model in many international markets relies on licensing and partnership arrangements with local manufacturers and distributors, so sublicensing rights are commercially essential to the value of Kastor’s co-developed IP license. The compromise grants Kastor sublicensing rights outside North America — the territory where Kastor’s existing distribution network and manufacturing partnerships operate and where Meridian has no significant competitive presence — while prohibiting Kastor sublicensing within North America, where Meridian’s competitive position is most sensitive and where the risk of Meridian’s formulations reaching its competitors is greatest. Meridian receives mirror sublicensing rights in the Americas. This territorial split is directly modeled on Comparable Transaction #2 (a 55/45 advanced-materials JV in which the U.S. partner sublicensed in the Americas and the foreign partner sublicensed in Asia-Pacific) and Comparable Transaction #3 (mutual territorial sublicensing restrictions). It gives Kastor the sublicensing rights it requires for its global business (a Meridian concession) while preserving Meridian’s North American competitive position (a Kastor concession).  
          Restricted Competitors covenant (reciprocal, global).         The territorial sublicensing rights are subject to a reciprocal, global prohibition on sublicensing to any Person listed on Schedule 12.05, regardless of the territory in which the sublicense would be exercised. This addresses Meridian’s core concern — that Kastor could sublicense Co-Developed IP to a North American competitor through a non-North-American intermediary — by making the prohibition global rather than territorial. The reciprocity (the prohibition applies equally to Meridian) addresses Kastor’s symmetry concern. Schedule 12.05 combines Meridian’s North American competitor list (including Axalta, PPG, Sherwin-Williams, RPM International, H.B. Fuller, Hempel, and Carboline, together with the additional North American competitors identified in Meridian’s competitive intelligence, including Polaris Coatings Corporation, Endurance Surface Technologies, Trident Polymer Systems, and others) and Kastor’s global competitor list (including BASF, Akzo Nobel, Jotun, Sika, and Chugoku Marine Paints). The schedule captures controlled affiliates (50% voting-equity threshold) and successors/assigns, and may be updated by the Management Committee to address new competitive threats, with no removal of a listed Person without mutual consent — addressing Meridian’s concern that the list could become stale while preserving Kastor’s interest in a manageable, named-entity list.  
          25%-revenue negative covenant (anti-circumvention).         Because Schedule 12.05 is inherently backward-looking and cannot anticipate future competitors, the compromise adds a reciprocal revenue-based negative covenant: neither party may sublicense Co-Developed IP to any Person deriving more than 25% of consolidated annual revenue from specialty polymer coatings in North America. This dynamic catch-all captures new market entrants, existing companies that expand into specialty polymer coatings, and entities that restructure to avoid the named-entity list — closing the gap Meridian identified in the Restricted Competitors-only approach. The 25% threshold is calibrated to capture entities with material North American coatings exposure while excluding diversified chemicals companies for which coatings are a minor, incidental product category. The covenant is reciprocal, applying equally to Meridian’s sublicensing.  
          Improvements to Contributed IP (the                 “        improvements gap        ”        ).         Both sides flagged the need to address JV-funded improvements to contributed IP — particularly improvements to Meridian’s $28.6M contributed portfolio, which will almost certainly be the subject of JV-funded R&D. The compromise adopts a symmetric structure: improvements to a Member’s Contributed IP revert to that Member upon exit (so a contributor gets back improved versions of its own IP), and the non-contributing Member receives a non-exclusive, perpetual license to those improvements subject to the same territorial and sublicensing restrictions as Co-Developed IP. This ensures that Kastor, having funded improvements to Meridian’s formulations through its capital contributions, can use those improvements outside North America (subject to the competitor covenants) — preserving the value of Kastor’s investment — while Meridian retains its improved formulations and the non-contributing party’s use is confined to the appropriate territory. The         “    primarily derivative    ”         classification test prevents disputes about whether an innovation is an improvement to one party’s contributed IP or independent Co-Developed IP: innovations that build equally on both parties’ IP, or are not primarily derivative of either, default into the Co-Developed IP category. This structure is consistent with the treatment of improvements in Comparable Transaction #2 and addresses the improvements gap both sides identified as requiring express resolution.  
          Survival (perpetuity).         The license-back and covenant provisions survive dissolution, termination, and buyout in perpetuity, reflecting that intellectual property has an indefinite useful life and that the parties’ need to use Co-Developed IP (and to be protected from competitive sublicensing) does not terminate at a fixed date.

          Overall balance assessment  
      The package is balanced in the following sense: each party’s largest concessions fall on the provisions that party ranks as lower priority, while each party’s highest-priority concerns are preserved.  
             Meridian’s highest-priority concern         — preventing its proprietary coating formulations (the $28.6M contributed portfolio) from reaching its North American competitors through Kastor sublicensing — is preserved by three layered protections in Provision 5: (i) no license of any kind of Meridian Contributed IP to Kastor; (ii) Kastor’s co-developed IP sublicensing is confined to outside North America; and (iii) reciprocal Restricted Competitors and 25%-revenue covenants operate globally to block sublicensing to identified and future North American competitors regardless of territory. Meridian’s second-priority concern — management control consistent with its 55% equity position — is preserved in Provision 1 by the 3-2 board, the CEO casting vote on operational matters, and sole CEO appointment, with the supermajority veto confined to a narrow list of existential Reserved Matters.  
             Kastor’s highest-priority concerns         — protective vetoes on Reserved Matters, an independent exit valuation, and a broad co-developed IP license-back — are preserved in Provisions 1, 4, and 5 respectively: the 4-of-5 supermajority requiring at least one Kastor Manager; the independent (non-Frazier Whitmore) appraiser with AAA fallback; and the non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual co-developed IP license to both parties with territorial sublicensing outside North America.  
             The middle-ground provisions         (non-compete duration and capital-call mechanics) are calibrated to the three comparable specialty-chemicals joint-venture transactions and to the multi-jurisdictional enforceability analysis, so that neither party is asked to accept a position outside market norms. The 18-month non-compete is the median comparable duration and sits at the center of the Delaware/Texas safe harbor; the 75-day capital-call cure and the 15% dilution cap with forced-sale backstop are each drawn directly from comparable transaction terms.  
      No single provision in the package gives one party everything it sought, and no provision requires either party to accept a position outside the range supported by market data or by the parties’ own stated flexibility. The principals may adjust any specific number (a threshold, a time period, a multiple) without disturbing the overall structure of the compromise.

       Our Company  
      We are an enterprise software-as-a-service company providing an AI-driven supply chain optimization platform to Fortune 1000 manufacturers and retailers. Our platform, anchored by the Crestline Nexus platform and our proprietary SupplyMind AI engine, combines machine learning, advanced statistical modeling, and real-time data integration to deliver predictive and prescriptive supply chain intelligence, including demand forecasting, inventory optimization, and logistics routing.  
      We were incorporated in the State of Delaware on March 14, 2017 and are headquartered in Austin, Texas. We operate through our wholly-owned subsidiary, Crestline Software UK Ltd., incorporated in England and Wales, which supports our European operations, and we maintain additional offices in San Francisco, California; London, United Kingdom; and Bangalore, India. As of December 31, 2024, we had 1,247 employees.  
      Our intellectual property portfolio includes 47 issued U.S. patents, 12 pending U.S. patent applications, and 8 issued foreign patents covering core algorithmic innovations and data processing methods. We have registered the trademarks         “    Crestline,    ”         “    Crestline Nexus,    ”         and         “    SupplyMind    ”         with the United States Patent and Trademark Office. We achieved SOC 2 Type II certification in August 2024 and maintain compliance with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA).  
        Our Industry and Market Opportunity  
      We operate in the market for AI-driven supply chain optimization software. We estimate that the total addressable market for AI-driven supply chain software will be approximately $28 billion by 2028, with a serviceable addressable market of approximately $9 billion focused on Fortune 1000 manufacturers and retailers. We believe current penetration of this serviceable market remains below 3%, representing significant runway for growth. The market estimates included in this prospectus are based on third-party industry research and our own internal estimates and are subject to significant uncertainty.  
        Our Strengths  
              AI-native, full-stack platform.         The SupplyMind AI engine is purpose-built for supply chain optimization, distinguishing us from legacy vendors retrofitting AI onto deterministic planning tools.  
              Highly recurring revenue model.         Subscription revenue accounted for 90.0% of total revenue in fiscal year 2024, with annual recurring revenue (ARR) of $211.6 million as of December 31, 2024.  
              Strong net retention.         Our dollar-based net retention rate was 132% for fiscal year 2024, reflecting robust upsell and cross-sell dynamics.  
              Robust intellectual property.         Our 47 issued U.S. patents, 12 pending applications, and 8 foreign patents protect our core technology.  
              Blue-chip customer base.         We served 487 customers as of December 31, 2024, including 34 customers contributing ARR in excess of $1.0 million, across manufacturing, retail, logistics, and consumer packaged goods.  
        Our Growth Strategy  
          Expand our enterprise customer base through our direct sales organization.  
          Deepen existing customer relationships through our land-and-expand model, targeting net retention above 130%.  
          Accelerate international go-to-market in EMEA and APAC.  
          Invest in next-generation SupplyMind capabilities, including autonomous supply chain decision-making.  
          Pursue opportunistic acquisitions of complementary technology and talent.

         RISK FACTORS  
      Investing in our Class A common stock involves a high degree of risk. Before making an investment decision, you should carefully consider the risks and uncertainties described below, together with the other information contained in this prospectus, including our consolidated financial statements and the related notes. The occurrence of any of the following risks could materially and adversely affect our business, financial condition, results of operations, and prospects, and the trading price of our Class A common stock could decline, and you could lose all or part of your investment. Additional risks and uncertainties not presently known to us, or that we currently deem immaterial, may also impair our business.  
       Risks Related to Our Business and Industry  
          We have a history of net losses and may not be able to achieve or sustain profitability.  
      We have incurred net losses since inception. We reported a net loss of $24.8 million for the fiscal year ended December 31, 2024 and $27.4 million for the fiscal year ended December 31, 2023. As of December 31, 2024, we had an accumulated deficit of $312.6 million. We expect to continue to incur operating losses and negative operating cash flows for the foreseeable future as we invest in research and development, sales and marketing, and international expansion. We may not achieve or sustain profitability, and our losses could be larger than expected.  
          We may not be able to sustain our historical revenue growth rate.  
      Our revenue grew 45.3% from $128.9 million in fiscal year 2023 to $187.3 million in fiscal year 2024. You should not rely on our historical revenue growth as an indication of future performance. Our revenue growth rate may decline as our revenue base increases, as a result of increased competition, or for other reasons.  
          Our dollar-based net retention rate and other operating metrics may not accurately predict our future results.  
      Our dollar-based net retention rate was 132% for fiscal year 2024. This metric is calculated based on our internal records and may not accurately reflect the metrics that would be derived from a more rigorous or standardized methodology. Any inability to maintain a high net retention rate would adversely affect our growth.  
          We depend on a limited number of customers for a significant portion of our revenue.  
      No single customer accounted for more than 10% of total revenue in fiscal year 2024 or 2023; however, our largest customer, Harmon Industries Inc., represented approximately 8.2% of total revenue ($15.4 million) in fiscal year 2024, and our top 10 customers collectively represented 31.7% of total revenue ($59.4 million). The loss of, or a significant reduction in business from, one or more of these customers could materially and adversely affect our revenue and results of operations.  
          We face intense competition and may lack sufficient financial resources to compete effectively.  
      The market for supply chain optimization software is competitive and includes well-capitalized legacy enterprise resource planning vendors and point-solution providers. Some competitors have greater financial, technical, and marketing resources than we do. We may not be able to compete effectively.  
          If we fail to maintain the security of our platform or our customers’ data, our business and reputation would be harmed.  
      Our platform processes sensitive customer supply chain data. Any security breach, cyberattack, or failure to maintain SOC 2 Type II, GDPR, or CCPA compliance could result in liability, reputational harm, and loss of customers.  
          We are subject to pending patent infringement litigation that could result in significant damages and adversely affect our business.  
      On September 12, 2024, Optivance, Inc. filed a patent infringement lawsuit against us in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Texas (Case No. 6:24-cv-00831), alleging that certain features of our SupplyMind platform infringe U.S. Patent No. 11,284,107, relating to         “    machine-learning-based inventory forecasting.    ”         We believe the claims are without merit and intend to vigorously defend against them. However, litigation is inherently uncertain. Based on the advice of outside patent counsel, we estimate the potential range of loss, in the event of an adverse judgment, to be between $5.0 million and $20.0 million, although we consider an unfavorable outcome to have a low probability. We have not recorded a loss contingency for this matter. An adverse judgment could also trigger an event of default under our term loan if a judgment in excess of $5.0 million remains undischarged for 60 days. See         “    Legal Proceedings    ”         and         “    Description of Indebtedness.    ”  
          Our intellectual property is pledged as collateral under our term loan, which could impair our ability to operate our business in the event of a lender foreclosure.  
      Our obligations under our term loan are secured by a first-priority security interest in substantially all of our assets, including our intellectual property (the 47 issued U.S. patents, 12 pending applications, 8 foreign patents, and our registered trademarks). If we were to default under the term loan and the lender exercised its remedies, the lender could foreclose on our intellectual property, which would severely disrupt our business. See         “    Description of Indebtedness.    ”  
          We depend on key personnel, including our co-founders.  
      Our success depends on the continued services of our co-founders, Arun Ramaswamy, our Chief Executive Officer, and Katerina         “    Kate    ”         Litvak, our Chief Technology Officer. The loss of the services of either co-founder could adversely affect our business. Our term loan also contains a key-person provision that constitutes a change of control if Mr. Ramaswamy ceases to serve as Chief Executive Officer and a successor acceptable to the lender is not appointed within 90 days.  
          Our ability to use our net operating loss carryforwards to offset future taxable income may be subject to limitations.  
      As of December 31, 2024, we had approximately $228.0 million of federal net operating loss carryforwards and approximately $166.0 million of state net operating loss carryforwards. Our ability to use these carryforwards may be limited if we experience an         “    ownership change    ”         as defined in Section 382 of the Internal Revenue Code, including in connection with this offering or other equity transactions. We have not yet completed a Section 382 study. In addition, we maintain a full valuation allowance against our net deferred tax assets.

        Risks Related to Our Indebtedness  
          The terms of our term loan impose operating and financial restrictions on us and may limit our flexibility.  
      Our term loan with Kestridge West Credit Partners contains financial covenants, including a minimum liquidity requirement of $30.0 million and an annual recurring revenue-based leverage ratio not to exceed 2.0x.           We are required to provide our lender with prior notice of this offering.

          A change of control under our term loan could result in acceleration of our indebtedness.  
      Our term loan agreement contains a change of control provision.         Risks Related to Our Common Stock and This Offering  
          The dual-class structure of our common stock and the concentrated voting power of our co-founders will limit your ability to influence corporate matters.  
      Upon the closing of this offering, our amended and restated certificate of incorporation will provide for a dual-class common stock structure consisting of Class A common stock (one vote per share) and Class B common stock (ten votes per share). Our co-founders will hold all 20,800,000 shares of Class B common stock and, together, will control approximately 71.8% of the voting power of our outstanding capital stock. As a result, the co-founders will have the ability to control the outcome of matters submitted to our stockholders for approval, including the election of directors, amendments to our certificate of incorporation, and any merger, consolidation, or sale of substantially all of our assets. This concentration of voting power will limit your ability to influence corporate matters and may adversely affect the market price of our Class A common stock.  
          Our Class B common stock has no time-based or ownership-based sunset provision.  
      Our Class B common stock will convert into Class A common stock only upon (i) transfer to a non-affiliate or (ii) the death or incapacity of the holder. There is no time-based sunset (such as a fixed period after this offering) and no ownership-percentage-based sunset. As a result, the dual-class structure and the concentrated voting power of our co-founders will continue indefinitely. This structure may cause our Class A common stock to be excluded from certain stock market indices, such as the S&P 500 and the FTSE Russell 1000, which have adopted policies restricting companies with multi-class share structures, which could reduce demand for and the market price of our Class A common stock.  
          Because we will be a                 “        controlled company        ”                 under Nasdaq rules, we will be permitted to rely on exemptions from certain corporate governance requirements.  
      Because our co-founders will control more than 50% of the voting power of our outstanding capital stock, we will be a         “    controlled company    ”         under Nasdaq Listing Rule 5615(c). We intend to rely on the exemptions available to controlled companies from the requirement that a majority of our board of directors consist of independent directors, the requirement that our compensation committee be comprised entirely of independent directors, and the requirement that director nominees be selected by independent directors or a nominating committee comprised entirely of independent directors. As a result, you will not have the same protections afforded to stockholders of companies that are subject to these requirements. Our audit committee will remain comprised entirely of independent directors, as the controlled company exemptions do not apply to audit committee independence.  
          Anti-takeover provisions in our charter and bylaws could make it more difficult for a third party to acquire us, even if doing so would benefit our stockholders.  
      Our amended and restated certificate of incorporation and bylaws will contain provisions that may delay, deter, or prevent a change of control or changes in our management that a stockholder might consider favorable, including: a classified (staggered) board of directors; the ability of our board of directors to fill vacancies; removal of directors only for cause and only upon the affirmative vote of the holders of at least 66⅔% of the voting power; the prohibition on stockholder action by written consent (other than by Class B holders acting separately as a class); the inability of stockholders to call special meetings; advance notice requirements for stockholder proposals and nominations; and supermajority voting requirements to amend certain provisions of our charter and bylaws. These provisions could limit the price that investors might be willing to pay in the future for our Class A common stock.  
          Our exclusive forum provisions may limit your ability to obtain a favorable judicial venue.  
      Our amended and restated certificate of incorporation will provide that, unless we consent in writing to the selection of an alternative forum, the Court of Chancery of the State of Delaware will be the sole and exclusive forum for certain state-law claims, and the federal district courts of the United States will be the sole and exclusive forum for claims arising under the Securities Act. These provisions may limit a stockholder’s ability to bring a claim in a judicial forum that it finds favorable for disputes with us or our directors, officers, or stockholders, which may discourage such lawsuits.  
          Our corporate opportunities provision may allocate certain business opportunities to our directors who are affiliated with venture capital or private equity funds.  
      Our amended and restated certificate of incorporation will renounce our interest in certain business opportunities presented to directors who are also partners, members, or employees of investment funds, venture capital funds, or private equity funds, including our directors affiliated with Graymont Ventures and Ridgeline Growth Partners. This means that such directors may pursue business opportunities that might otherwise be available to us, which could reduce the business opportunities available to us.  
          You will experience immediate and substantial dilution in the pro forma net tangible book value per share of the Class A common stock you purchase.  
      The assumed initial public offering price of $24.00 per share (the midpoint of the estimated range) is substantially higher than the pro forma net tangible book value per share of our Class A common stock. As a result, you will experience immediate and substantial dilution of $19.85 per share. See         “    Dilution.    ”  
          Future sales of our Class A common stock, or the perception that such sales may occur, could cause the market price of our Class A common stock to decline.  
      Substantial numbers of shares of our Class A common stock will be eligible for sale in the public market after this offering, including shares issuable upon the exercise of outstanding stock options and the vesting and settlement of outstanding RSUs, subject to lock-up agreements and other restrictions. Sales of a substantial number of shares, or the perception that such sales may occur, could cause the market price of our Class A common stock to decline.  
          We do not intend to pay dividends on our Class A common stock, and you must rely on price appreciation for a return on your investment.  
      We have never declared or paid any cash dividends on our capital stock. We do not intend to pay cash dividends in the foreseeable future. In addition, our term loan agreement prohibits the payment of dividends and stock repurchases without the lender’s consent. Any return on your investment will depend on the future appreciation of our Class A common stock, which may not occur.

        Class A Common Stock  
      Holders of Class A common stock are entitled to one vote per share on all matters submitted to a vote of stockholders, voting together with the holders of Class B common stock as a single class, except as otherwise required by law. Holders of Class A common stock are entitled to receive dividends and distributions when, as, and if declared by our board of directors, on an equal per share basis with holders of Class B common stock.       Holders of Class B common stock are entitled to ten votes per share on all matters submitted to a vote of stockholders, voting together with the holders of Class A common stock as a single class, except as otherwise required by law.       Each share of Class B common stock is convertible at any time at the option of the holder into one share of Class A common stock. Each share of Class B common stock will automatically convert into one share of Class A common stock upon (i) any transfer of such share to a person or entity other than a permitted transferee, or (ii) the death or incapacity of the holder, if the holder is a natural person.         “    Permitted transferee    ”         includes the holder’s spouse, domestic partner, parents, children, grandchildren, and trusts established for the benefit of such persons; entities wholly owned by the holder or permitted transferees; and transfers between the two co-founders.          BUSINESS  
       Our Company  
      Crestline Software, Inc. is an enterprise software-as-a-service company providing an AI-driven supply chain optimization platform to Fortune 1000 manufacturers and retailers. Our platform, anchored by the Crestline Nexus platform and our proprietary SupplyMind AI engine, combines machine learning, advanced statistical modeling, and real-time data integration to deliver predictive and prescriptive supply chain intelligence, including demand forecasting, inventory optimization, and logistics routing. We were incorporated in Delaware on March 14, 2017 and are headquartered in Austin, Texas.  
        Our Products and Technology  
      Our platform consists of the Crestline Nexus platform and the SupplyMind AI engine. The SupplyMind AI engine uses deep learning and reinforcement learning models trained on billions of supply chain events to provide demand sensing, safety stock optimization, network design, and logistics routing. The platform features a multi-tenant, cloud-native architecture with real-time data ingestion from enterprise resource planning, warehouse management, transportation management, and IoT systems, with pre-built connectors for over 100 enterprise data sources.  
        Our Customers  
      We served 487 customers as of December 31, 2024, across manufacturing, retail, logistics, and consumer packaged goods. Our largest customer, Harmon Industries Inc., represented approximately 8.2% of total revenue in fiscal year 2024. Our top 10 customers collectively represented 31.7% of total revenue. No single customer other than Harmon Industries exceeded 5.0% of total revenue. Revenue is well diversified across verticals, with manufacturing representing approximately 38%, retail 24%, logistics 19%, and consumer packaged goods 12% of revenue.  
        Our Intellectual Property

       I. Introduction and Scope  
      This memorandum summarizes the inconsistencies, disclosure risks, and open items that we have identified during our review of the source documents provided in connection with the preparation of the Registration Statement on Form S-1 for the proposed initial public offering of Crestline Software, Inc. (the         “    Company    ”    ). The documents reviewed include: (1) the audited consolidated financial statements for fiscal years 2023 and 2024 prepared by Archer Pryce LLP; (2) the CFO’s IPO readiness memorandum dated February 14, 2025; (3) the amended and restated certificate of incorporation and bylaws; (4) the term loan summary; (5) the underwriting term sheet and form of lock-up agreement; (6) the management (director and officer) questionnaire responses; (7) the equity plan summary and grant records; and (8) the draft roadshow presentation.  
      We have organized the findings into the following categories: (A) financial statement and numeric inconsistencies; (B) term loan and debt inconsistencies; (C) naming and identity inconsistencies; (D) corporate governance and structural issues; (E) related party transaction issues; (F) beneficial ownership and capitalization discrepancies; (G) use of proceeds inconsistencies; (H) professional services margin mischaracterization; (I) litigation and contingency issues; (J) Section 409A and stock-based compensation issues; (K) equity plan record discrepancies; and (L) other disclosure and open items. Each finding identifies the specific documents and figures at issue, the consequence for the S-1, and our recommended next step.  
      The audited consolidated financial statements are the authoritative source for all financial data in the S-1. Where the Company’s internal materials (the CFO memorandum, the roadshow presentation, or the equity plan records) conflict with the audited financial statements, the audited financial statements control, and the internal materials must be corrected. Several findings, however, identify inconsistencies within the audited financial statements themselves or between the audited financial statements and the credit agreement, which must be resolved with Archer Pryce LLP and the lender before filing.  
      This memorandum is privileged and confidential and is prepared in anticipation of litigation and in connection with the SEC registration process. It should not be distributed outside the working group without the consent of the undersigned.

        III. Detailed Findings  
       A. Financial Statement and Numeric Inconsistencies  
          Finding A-1: Fiscal year 2023 subscription revenue is misstated in the CFO memorandum.  
      The CFO’s IPO readiness memorandum (Section 4.1) states that subscription revenue for fiscal year 2023 was $113.4 million and reports subscription revenue year-over-year growth of 48.7%. The audited consolidated statements of operations and Note 3 to the audited financial statements state that subscription revenue for fiscal year 2023 was $116.4 million. The correct year-over-year growth rate, calculated from the audited figures ($168.6 million / $116.4 million − 1), is 44.8%, not 48.7%. The $113.4 million figure is incorrect and must be corrected in all internal materials. The S-1 must use the audited figure of $116.4 million.  
          Finding A-2: Fiscal year 2023 professional services revenue is misstated in the CFO memorandum and appears to confuse revenue with cost of revenue.  
      The CFO memorandum (Section 4.1) states that professional services revenue for fiscal year 2023 was $15.5 million and reports professional services year-over-year growth of 20.6%. The audited financial statements (Note 3) state that professional services revenue for fiscal year 2023 was $12.5 million. Notably, $15.5 million is the figure reported in Note 4 as professional services cost of revenue for fiscal year 2023, suggesting that the CFO memorandum inadvertently substituted the cost of revenue figure for the revenue figure. The correct year-over-year growth rate, calculated from the audited figures ($18.7 million / $12.5 million − 1), is 49.6%, not 20.6%. The S-1 must use the audited figure of $12.5 million.  
          Finding A-3: The Adjusted EBITDA figure for fiscal year 2024 does not reconcile to its stated add-backs.  
      The CFO memorandum (Section 5.2) and the roadshow presentation (Slides 3, 23, 34, and 35) state Adjusted EBITDA of $22.8 million for fiscal year 2024, with an Adjusted EBITDA margin of 12.2%. However, the stated add-backs do not sum to $22.8 million. Using the figures stated in the reconciliation (net loss of $(24.8) million plus depreciation and amortization of $8.7 million plus stock-based compensation of $38.2 million plus interest expense of $3.1 million less interest income of $6.2 million plus income tax provision of $0.3 million plus restructuring charges of $1.2 million), the sum is $20.5 million, not $22.8 million. The $2.3 million discrepancy is unexplained. The roadshow presentation’s own notes acknowledge that the figure is         “    erroneous.    ”         The correct Adjusted EBITDA margin is 10.9% ($20.5 million / $187.3 million), not 12.2%. The S-1 must present an Adjusted EBITDA reconciliation that foots to the most directly comparable GAAP measure; presenting a non-GAAP measure that does not reconcile would violate Regulation G and Item 10(e) of Regulation S-K and would likely result in an SEC comment letter. We have presented the reconciling figure of $20.5 million in the draft S-1.  
          Finding A-4: The Adjusted EBITDA reconciliation for fiscal year 2023 uses component figures that differ from the audited financial statements.  
      The CFO memorandum’s Adjusted EBITDA reconciliation for fiscal year 2023 uses the following figures, each of which differs from the audited financial statements: stock-based compensation of $29.1 million (audited: $26.4 million per Note 12(d)); interest expense of $2.8 million (audited: $3.1 million); interest income of $4.6 million (audited: $5.8 million); income tax provision of $0.2 million (audited: $0.1 million); and restructuring charges of $0.6 million (audited: $0, as no restructuring occurred in fiscal year 2023 and the restructuring accrual was $0 as of December 31, 2023 per Note 9). Using the audited figures, Adjusted EBITDA for fiscal year 2023 is $3.5 million, not the $7.6 million stated in the CFO memorandum. The S-1 must use the audited component figures.  
          Finding A-5: The depreciation and amortization add-back uses an inconsistent definition across the two years.  
      The CFO memorandum’s Adjusted EBITDA reconciliation uses depreciation and amortization of $8.7 million for fiscal year 2024 (the figure from the consolidated statement of cash flows, which includes property and equipment depreciation, intangible asset amortization, and other amortization) but $6.9 million for fiscal year 2023 (the figure from Note 5, which reflects only property and equipment depreciation). The cash flow statement reports total depreciation and amortization of $7.1 million for fiscal year 2023. The S-1 must use a consistent definition of depreciation and amortization across all periods presented.  
          Finding A-6: The restructuring charge description is inconsistent as to headcount and timing.  
      The CFO memorandum (Section 5.3) describes the fiscal year 2024 restructuring as         “    a workforce reduction of approximately 30 positions in our professional services organization in the second quarter of 2024.    ”         Note 9 to the audited financial statements describes the restructuring as         “    a workforce reduction affecting approximately 46 employees    ”         implemented         “    during the third quarter of 2024.    ”         The headcount (30 versus 46) and the timing (second quarter versus third quarter) are inconsistent. The S-1 must use the audited description (approximately 46 employees, third quarter of 2024).  
          Finding A-7: The CFO memorandum’s condensed balance sheet misstates several current asset and current liability components.  
      The CFO memorandum (Section 6.1) presents a condensed balance sheet as of December 31, 2024 in which the following components differ from the audited consolidated balance sheet: accounts receivable, net is stated as $28.2 million (audited: $29.8 million, understated by $1.6 million); prepaid expenses and other current assets is stated as $13.2 million (audited: $11.6 million, overstated by $1.6 million); accounts payable is stated as $9.8 million (audited: $8.2 million, overstated by $1.6 million); and accrued expenses and other current liabilities is stated as $14.7 million (audited: $12.1 million, overstated by $2.6 million). In addition, the CFO memorandum omits the current portion of operating lease liabilities ($4.2 million) from current liabilities. The total current assets ($178.4 million) and total current liabilities ($72.8 million) match the audited balance sheet only because the component errors offset each other. The S-1 must present the balance sheet exactly as reported in the audited financial statements.  
        B. Term Loan and Debt Inconsistencies  
          Finding B-1: The date the term loan facility was entered is inconsistent.  
      The Company’s internal term loan summary states that the Term Loan and Security Agreement is         “    Dated as of December 15, 2022    ”         and that the closing date of the credit agreement was December 15, 2022, with an initial draw of $40.0 million made on the closing date. Note 8 to the audited financial statements states that         “    In June 2023, the Company entered into a term loan facility with Kestridge West Credit Partners providing for borrowings of up to $50.0 million.    ”         The date of the facility (December 2022 versus June 2023) and the amount and timing of the initial draw are inconsistent. This must be reconciled with the credit agreement and the auditor before the S-1 is filed, as the description of indebtedness in the S-1 must be accurate.  
          Finding B-2: The term loan balance does not reconcile between the cash flow statement and the balance sheet.  
      The audited consolidated statement of cash flows reports proceeds from term loan borrowings of $15.0 million in fiscal year 2023 and a repayment of $5.0 million in fiscal year 2024, with no borrowings in fiscal year 2024. The audited consolidated balance sheet reports a term loan balance of $40.0 million as of both December 31, 2023 and December 31, 2024. These figures do not reconcile. If $15.0 million was borrowed in fiscal year 2023 (and the facility was entered in 2023 per Note 8), the December 31, 2023 balance should reflect the cumulative draws, not necessarily $40.0 million. More significantly, if the balance was $40.0 million at December 31, 2023 and $5.0 million was repaid in fiscal year 2024 with no additional borrowings, the December 31, 2024 balance should be $35.0 million, not $40.0 million. The cash flow statement, balance sheet, and Note 8 cannot all be correct simultaneously. This is a material inconsistency in the audited financial statements that must be resolved with Archer Pryce LLP before filing.  
          Finding B-3: The amortization schedule is inconsistent between the credit agreement summary and the audited financial statements.  
      The term loan summary (Section 3) describes level quarterly amortization of 1.25% of the drawn amount, or $500,000 per quarter ($2.0 million per year), with the remaining balance due as a bullet payment at maturity. Note 8 to the audited financial statements reports actual and scheduled principal repayments of $5.0 million in fiscal year 2024, $5.0 million in 2025, $10.0 million in 2026, and $25.0 million in 2027. These schedules do not match: level amortization of $2.0 million per year is inconsistent with the $5.0 million / $5.0 million / $10.0 million / $25.0 million schedule reflected in the audited financial statements. The S-1 must reflect the amortization schedule set forth in the credit agreement, and the audited financial statements must be consistent with that schedule.  
          Finding B-4: The current portion of the term loan is not classified as a current liability.  
      Note 8 to the audited financial statements reports that $5.0 million of principal is due in fiscal year 2025 (within 12 months of December 31, 2024). However, the audited consolidated balance sheet presents the entire $40.0 million term loan balance as non-current, with no current portion of the term loan included in current liabilities. Under generally accepted accounting principles, the portion of long-term debt due within 12 months should be classified as current. This classification should be reviewed with the auditor.  
          Finding B-5: The lender is referred to by two different names.  
      The audited financial statements, the CFO memorandum, and the term loan summary refer to the lender as         “    Kestridge West Credit Partners.    ”         The roadshow presentation (Slides 20, 24, and 27) refers to the lender as         “    Silicon Valley Credit Partners.    ”         The term loan summary abbreviates the lender as         “    SVCP,    ”         which corresponds to         “    Silicon Valley Credit Partners    ”         rather than         “    Kestridge West Credit Partners    ”         (which would abbreviate as         “    KWCP    ”    ). The correct legal name of the lender must be confirmed and used consistently throughout the S-1 and all offering materials.

        D. Corporate Governance and Structural Issues  
          Finding D-1: The size of the board of directors is inconsistent (six versus seven members), and the director status of the Chief Technology Officer is unclear.  
      The amended and restated certificate of incorporation (Article V, Section 5.2) provides that the initial board of directors shall consist of seven members, and Section 5.3 lists seven directors, including the Chief Technology Officer as a Class III director (Class I: Priya Venkatesh and Thomas J. Moreno; Class II: Dr. Helen Park and James Kwon; Class III: Arun Ramaswamy, Katerina         “    Kate    ”         Litvak, and Adaora Obi). The management questionnaire responses (Section 6) state that the board of directors consists of six members and that         “    Katerina Litvanova, Michael D. Hartwell, and Sandra Y. Chen are executive officers but do not serve on the Board.    ”         The underwriting term sheet (Schedule 1) lists five non-employee directors plus the Chief Executive Officer, implying a six-member board. The roadshow presentation is internally inconsistent: Slide 7 lists five non-employee directors (implying six total), while Slide 30 states         “    3 of 7 total directors.    ”         The director status of the Chief Technology Officer, and consequently the size of the board, must be resolved before the S-1 is filed, because it affects the classified board structure, the independence analysis, the controlled company analysis, and the signature block (which, under Section 302 of the Securities Act, must include a majority of the board of directors). If the Chief Technology Officer is a director, the board has seven members and the charter is correct; if she is not, the charter must be corrected. We have presented a seven-member board in the draft S-1 consistent with the charter, but this must be confirmed.  
          Finding D-2: The Company has not adopted a formal board resolution electing to rely on the controlled company exemption.  
      Following the closing of this offering, the co-founders will hold approximately 71.8% of the voting power of the outstanding capital stock, and the Company will be a         “    controlled company    ”         under Nasdaq Listing Rule 5615(c). The management questionnaire responses (Open Item 1) confirm that no board resolution is on record regarding reliance on the controlled company exemption, and that the director responses are         “    supportive but noncommittal    ”         or defer to counsel. Only three of the directors are independent (whether the board is six or seven members), which is not a majority. The board must adopt a formal resolution electing to rely on the controlled company exemption before the S-1 is filed, and the S-1 must disclose the exemption and the resulting reduced governance protections. The audit committee will remain fully independent, as the controlled company exemptions do not apply to audit committee independence.  
          Finding D-3: The dual-class structure has no sunset provision.  
      The amended and restated certificate of incorporation (Article IV, Section 4.3) provides that Class B common stock will automatically convert into Class A common stock only upon (i) transfer to a non-permitted transferee or (ii) the death or incapacity of the holder. There is no time-based sunset (for example, a fixed period after the offering) and no ownership-percentage-based sunset. As a result, the dual-class structure and the co-founders’ concentrated voting power will continue indefinitely. This is below current market norms for recent IPOs, many of which include a sunset provision, and may cause the Company’s Class A common stock to be excluded from certain stock market indices (including the S&P 500 and the FTSE Russell 1000), which have adopted policies restricting companies with multi-class share structures. The S-1 must disclose the absence of a sunset as a risk factor. The board should consider whether to adopt a sunset provision before the offering.  
          Finding D-4: The charter and bylaws contain multiple anti-takeover provisions that must be disclosed.  
      The amended and restated certificate of incorporation and bylaws contain provisions that may delay, deter, or prevent a change of control, including: a classified (staggered) board of directors (Article V, Section 5.3); removal of directors only for cause and only upon the affirmative vote of at least 66⅔% of the voting power (Article V, Section 5.4); vacancies filled solely by the board of directors (Article V, Section 5.5); prohibition on stockholder action by written consent, except for Class B holders acting separately as a class (Article VI, Section 6.1); special meetings callable only by the Chairman, the Chief Executive Officer, or the board of directors (Article VI, Section 6.2); advance notice requirements for stockholder proposals and nominations (bylaws, Article II, Section 2.8); and supermajority (66⅔%) voting requirements to amend certain provisions of the charter (Article IX) and bylaws (bylaws, Article VIII). These provisions, combined with the dual-class structure, entrench the co-founders’ control. The S-1 must disclose these provisions as risk factors and in the description of capital stock.  
          Finding D-5: The exclusive forum provisions must be disclosed.  
      The amended and restated certificate of incorporation (Article X) provides for the exclusive jurisdiction of the Court of Chancery of the State of Delaware for certain state-law claims and the exclusive jurisdiction of the federal district courts for claims arising under the Securities Act. The federal forum provision for Securities Act claims is enforceable under             Salzberg v. Sciabacucchi         (Del. 2020) but limits the ability of stockholders to bring Securities Act claims in state court, which would otherwise be concurrently available under             Cyan Inc. v. Beaver County Employees Retirement Fund         (2018). The S-1 must disclose these provisions as risk factors.  
          Finding D-6: The corporate opportunities renunciation benefits the venture-capital-affiliated directors and must be disclosed.  
      The amended and restated certificate of incorporation (Article XI) renounces the Company’s interest in business opportunities presented to directors who are partners, members, or employees of investment funds, venture capital funds, or private equity funds, and specifically names Graymont Ventures and Ridgeline Growth Partners and their affiliates and portfolio companies. Two directors (Priya Venkatesh of Graymont Ventures and James Kwon of Ridgeline Growth Partners) benefit from this provision. The renunciation is permissible under Section 122(17) of the DGCL but must be disclosed in the S-1, as it may allocate business opportunities away from the Company.  
          Finding D-7: The General Counsel is a former associate of the issuer’s counsel.  
      Sandra Y. Chen, the Company’s General Counsel and Corporate Secretary, was formerly an associate at Thorncroft & Wexler LLP, which serves as the Company’s outside counsel in connection with this offering. Ms. Chen has disclosed that she has no ongoing financial interest in the firm. This relationship should be disclosed in the S-1 under         “    Certain Relationships and Related Party Transactions    ”         and evaluated for any conflict-of-interest considerations. We have included this disclosure in the draft S-1.  
        E. Related Party Transaction Issues  
          Finding E-1: The Vantara Logistics related party transaction is inconsistently disclosed.  
      In April 2023, the Company entered into a SaaS license agreement with Vantara Logistics, Inc., a portfolio company of Graymont Ventures, providing for annual subscription fees of $1.2 million over a three-year term (total contract value $3.6 million). Priya Venkatesh, a director of the Company, is the Managing Partner of Graymont Ventures and serves on the board of directors of Vantara Logistics. Ms. Venkatesh disclosed this transaction in her director questionnaire, including that it was reviewed and approved by the Audit Committee with her recusal. However, the transaction was not identified as a related party transaction in the CFO’s questionnaire response, in the CFO’s IPO readiness memorandum (which lists Vantara as a standard customer contributing to ARR), or in the roadshow presentation (Slide 14), which presents Vantara as a customer case study without any disclosure of the Graymont relationship. This transaction must be disclosed in the S-1 under Item 404 of Regulation S-K with full detail, including the Graymont affiliation, Ms. Venkatesh’s dual role, the Audit Committee approval, and Ms. Venkatesh’s recusal. All investor-facing materials must be updated to identify Vantara as a related party. We have included this disclosure in the draft S-1.  
          Finding E-2: The Vantara agreement may require notification to the term loan lender.  
      The term loan summary (Section 7.2) states that the credit agreement requires the Company to notify the lender of any affiliate transaction exceeding $500,000 in value. The Vantara SaaS agreement has an annual contract value of $1.2 million, which exceeds this threshold. It should be confirmed whether the required notification to Kestridge West Credit Partners was provided in connection with the Vantara agreement, and if not, whether a curative notice should be delivered. This should be disclosed in the S-1 if there is a potential covenant compliance issue.

        I. Litigation and Contingency Issues  
          Finding I-1: The Optivance patent litigation must be disclosed, and its interaction with the term loan covenants must be addressed.  
      The Company is a defendant in             Optivance, Inc. v. Crestline Software, Inc.    , Case No. 6:24-cv-00831 (E.D. Tex.), filed September 12, 2024, alleging infringement of U.S. Patent No. 11,284,107. Management estimates the potential range of loss, in the event of an adverse judgment, to be between $5.0 million and $20.0 million, with a low probability of an unfavorable outcome, and has not recorded a loss contingency. This matter is disclosed in Note 14 to the audited financial statements and must be disclosed in the S-1 under         “    Legal Proceedings    ”         (Item 103 of Regulation S-K) and as a risk factor. In addition, the term loan summary (Section 8.1(h)) provides that one or more judgments for the payment of money in excess of $5.0 million in the aggregate that remain undischarged for 60 days constitute an event of default. Because the estimated exposure range ($5.0 million to $20.0 million) overlaps the $5.0 million judgment threshold, an adverse judgment at the lower end or above could trigger an event of default under the term loan if not bonded, appealed, or stayed. This interaction between the litigation exposure and the debt covenant must be disclosed in the S-1 risk factors. We have included this disclosure in the draft S-1.  
          Finding I-2: The Company’s intellectual property is pledged as collateral for the term loan.  
      The term loan summary (Section 4) states that the Company’s obligations under the term loan are secured by a first-priority security interest in substantially all of the Company’s assets, including its intellectual property (the 47 issued U.S. patents, 12 pending applications, 8 foreign patents, and the registered trademarks). The Optivance litigation concerns the Company’s SupplyMind platform, which is protected by this intellectual property. If the lender were to foreclose on the collateral following an event of default, the Company’s ability to operate its business could be severely impaired. This must be disclosed in the S-1 risk factors. We have included this disclosure in the draft S-1.  
        J. Section 409A and Stock-Based Compensation Issues  
          Finding J-1: First-quarter 2023 option grants were priced using a 409A valuation that was subsequently superseded.  
      During the three months ended March 31, 2023, the Company granted 2,400,000 stock options (grant OPT-2023-Q1-001) at an exercise price of $9.50 per share, based on the 409A valuation dated September 30, 2022, which determined the fair value of the common stock to be $9.50 per share. A subsequent 409A valuation dated March 31, 2023 determined the fair value to be $14.20 per share, an increase of 49.5%. The grants were made before the updated valuation was obtained. Note 12(e) to the audited financial statements discloses this fact pattern. While the Board considered the September 30, 2022 valuation to be the most recent available at the time of grant, the timing of the grants relative to the subsequent increase in the determined fair value creates a potential Section 409A compliance consideration and a         “    cheap stock    ”         issue for stock-based compensation measurement. The S-1 should disclose the timing of the 409A valuations relative to the grants, and the Company should confirm with its valuation specialist and tax counsel that the grants comply with Section 409A.  
          Finding J-2: The most recent 409A valuation is preliminary and was used for fiscal year 2024 RSU grants.  
      The 409A valuations tab indicates that the December 31, 2024 valuation, which determined a fair value of $18.50 per share, is preliminary, with the final report expected in February 2025. The fiscal year 2024 RSU grants (including the named executive officer grants in March 2024) used a grant date fair value of $18.50 per share, described as an interpolation from the December 31, 2023 valuation ($16.80) to a Board-approved fair value of $18.50. The S-1’s stock-based compensation disclosure must use the appropriate valuation, and the final December 31, 2024 valuation report must be completed and Board-approved before the S-1 is filed. The use of an interpolated value for grant pricing should be reviewed for Section 409A compliance.  
          Finding J-3: The 409A valuation records describe the Series A, B, and C financing terms in a manner inconsistent with the audited financial statements.  
      The 409A valuations tab describes the contemporaneous financing events as follows: Series A         “    closed July 2018 at $2.50/share ($25M round, $50M post-money)    ”    ; Series B         “    closed February 2020 at $6.00/share ($60M round, $180M post-money)    ”    ; and Series C         “    closed November 2021 at $12.00/share ($120M round, $720M post-money).    ”         The audited financial statements (Note 10) and the CFO memorandum describe the financings as follows: Series A in October 2018, 8,000,000 shares at $1.50 per share, $12.0 million aggregate; Series B in April 2020, 9,500,000 shares at $4.00 per share, $38.0 million aggregate; and Series C in November 2021, 7,916,667 shares at $12.00 per share, $95.0 million aggregate. The Series A and Series B descriptions in the 409A valuations tab (dates, per-share prices, and aggregate amounts) are materially different from the audited financial statements, and the Series C aggregate amount ($120 million versus $95.0 million) is inconsistent (7,916,667 shares at $12.00 per share equals $95.0 million, not $120 million). Only the Series D description is consistent ($24.00 per share, $1.92 billion post-money). Because the 409A valuations use the preferred financing events as inputs to the option pricing model and probability-weighted expected return method, the use of incorrect financing inputs could affect the reliability of the common stock valuations used for stock-based compensation. This must be investigated and reconciled. Either the 409A valuations tab contains documentation errors that must be corrected, or the valuations were based on incorrect financing data, in which case the valuations and the related stock-based compensation expense may need to be reviewed with the valuation specialist and the auditor.

        L. Other Disclosure and Open Items  
          Finding L-1: The Company’s net operating loss carryforwards may be limited by a Section 382 ownership change.  
      Note 13 to the audited financial statements discloses approximately $228.0 million of federal net operating loss carryforwards and approximately $166.0 million of state net operating loss carryforwards as of December 31, 2024, with a full valuation allowance. The Company has not completed a Section 382 study. An initial public offering or other equity transaction could trigger an         “    ownership change    ”         under Section 382 of the Internal Revenue Code, which would limit the Company’s ability to use its net operating loss carryforwards to offset future taxable income. The S-1 must disclose this risk as a risk factor. We have included this disclosure in the draft S-1.  
          Finding L-2: The 30-day public offering notice to the lender must be delivered and disclosed.  
      The term loan summary (Section 5.4) and Note 8 to the audited financial statements describe the requirement under Section 7.14 of the credit agreement that the Company provide Kestridge West Credit Partners with written notice not less than 30 days prior to the consummation of any initial public offering, including the anticipated date, gross proceeds, use of proceeds, and a pro forma capitalization table. Failure to deliver this notice constitutes an event of default, subject to a 10-business-day cure period. The notice must be delivered at least 35 days before the expected pricing date, and the S-1 must disclose this notification requirement and the lender’s potential remedies. We have included this disclosure in the draft S-1.  
          Finding L-3: The change of control analysis should be confirmed against the credit agreement.  
      The term loan summary (Section 5.3) concludes that the offering will not constitute a change of control because the         “    existing stockholders    ”         will retain more than 50% of the aggregate voting power. The analysis equates the         “    existing stockholders    ”         with the co-founders’ Class B voting power (71.8%). However, the credit agreement defines         “    existing stockholders    ”         as the stockholders as of the closing date of the credit agreement (described as December 15, 2022 in the term loan summary, although Note 8 describes the facility as entered in June 2023; see Finding B-1). The Series D preferred stock was issued in June 2023, after the December 15, 2022 closing date described in the term loan summary, and therefore Series D holders may not be         “    existing stockholders    ”         under the credit agreement. The analysis should identify the         “    existing stockholders    ”         as of the closing date of the credit agreement and calculate their aggregate post-offering voting power, rather than relying solely on the co-founders’ Class B voting power. The conclusion (no change of control) is likely still correct because the co-founders alone hold 71.8% of the voting power, but the analysis should be confirmed against the credit agreement and the lender’s interpretation, particularly in light of the inconsistency regarding the closing date of the credit agreement (Finding B-1).  
          Finding L-4: Director ages must be confirmed.  
      The management questionnaire (Open Item 4) notes that ages for the non-employee directors (Priya Venkatesh, Thomas J. Moreno, Dr. Helen Park, James Kwon, and Adaora Obi) were not provided in all questionnaire responses. Item 401 of Regulation S-K requires disclosure of the age of each director and executive officer. The ages must be confirmed and included in the S-1.  
          Finding L-5: The filing date of the S-1 is inconsistent.  
      Note 17 to the audited financial statements states that         “    In February 2025, the Company filed a Registration Statement on Form S-1 with the Securities and Exchange Commission.    ”         The CFO memorandum (dated February 14, 2025) states that the Company         “    anticipate[s] filing the initial Form S-1 in March 2025,    ”         and the underwriting term sheet is dated         “    March 2025.    ”         The auditor’s report is dated “March __, 2025.” The actual filing date of the S-1 must be confirmed, and the subsequent events note and the auditor’s report must be updated to reflect the correct date.  
          Finding L-6: The dilution sensitivity for the over-allotment option does not reconcile.  
      The CFO memorandum (Section 8) states that, if the over-allotment option is exercised in full, the pro forma net tangible book value per share would be approximately $4.36 and the dilution to new investors would be approximately $19.64 per share. However, based on the additional net proceeds from the over-allotment option of approximately $50.5 million (2,250,000 shares at $24.00 per share, less the 6.5% underwriting discount), the pro forma net tangible book value per share would be approximately $4.54 (($92.9 million + $331.8 million + $50.5 million) / 104,666,667 shares), and the dilution to new investors would be approximately $19.46 per share ($24.00 − $4.54). The $4.36 figure in the CFO memorandum does not reconcile to the stated over-allotment net proceeds. The S-1 dilution table must present the correct figures. We have presented the reconciled figures in the draft S-1.  
          Finding L-7: The roadshow presentation’s customer concentration statement is imprecise.  
      The roadshow presentation (Slide 13) states that         “    No single customer exceeds ~8% of total revenue.    ”         The audited financial statements (Note 3) and the CFO memorandum state that the largest customer, Harmon Industries Inc., represented approximately 8.2% of total revenue in fiscal year 2024. Because 8.2% exceeds 8%, the         “    ~8%    ”         statement is imprecise. The S-1 must state the customer concentration precisely (8.2%).  
          Finding L-8: The emerging growth company status and accommodations must be disclosed.  
      The underwriting term sheet (Section 10) and the roadshow presentation (Slide 2) note that the Company qualifies as an         “    emerging growth company    ”         as defined in the JOBS Act, based on fiscal year 2024 revenue of $187.3 million (below the $1.235 billion threshold) and the fact that this is the Company’s first sale of common equity under a registered offering. The S-1 must disclose the Company’s emerging growth company status and the reduced disclosure and reporting requirements it will use, including the presentation of only two years of audited financial statements, reduced executive compensation disclosure, and the exemption from the requirement to obtain an auditor attestation on internal control over financial reporting under Section 404(b) of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act. The S-1 must also disclose whether the Company has elected to use the extended transition period for complying with new or revised accounting standards. The draft S-1 states that the Company has elected not to use the extended transition period; this election should be confirmed with management.

       I. Executive Summary  
      You asked us to review the draft Settlement Agreement and General Release of All Claims dated May 5, 2025 (the         “    Draft    ”    ), prepared by Engel & Associates, P.C. on behalf of Marcus R. Delano, against Greenleaf’s employment records and the company policies and plan documents referenced in the Draft. A redlined markup reflecting our recommended revisions accompanies this memorandum as              settlement-agreement-markup.docx    .  
      The Draft is a $525,000 settlement of Mr. Delano’s retaliation, whistleblower, and age-discrimination claims arising from his November 8, 2024 internal cold-chain complaint, his December 3, 2024 Oregon OSHA complaint (No. OR-OSHA-2024-11872), and his March 14, 2025 termination. We identified             twenty-five (25) distinct issues    , ranging from defects that would render the age-discrimination release invalid to a material misstatement in the equity valuation to a significant piece of pretext evidence in Greenleaf’s own records.  
      The most important conclusions are:  
             The ADEA/age-discrimination release is likely invalid as drafted.         Mr. Delano is 58 years old (DOB 07/14/1966) and is protected by the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (    “    OWBPA    ”    ). The Draft gives him only             14 days         to consider the agreement (Sections 5(c), 16) and is             entirely silent on the required 7-day revocation period    . OWBPA requires at least             21 days         to consider and a             7-day revocation period    . As written, Mr. Delano could sign the agreement and still sue Greenleaf for age discrimination. This is the single most important defect and has been redlined.  
             The RSU valuation in the Draft is wrong and contains a false statement.         Section 3(d) values 5,000 accelerated RSUs at             $10.00/share ($50,000)    , stating this is         “    based on the most recent independent valuation conducted by Ridgepoint Valuation Services.    ”         Greenleaf’s equity records show the most recent 409A valuation is dated             January 15, 2025 at $12.50/share    . The correct value is             $62,500    , and the correct total settlement value is             $537,500, not $525,000         — a             $12,500 understatement    . The Draft’s statement that $10.00 is the         “    most recent    ”         valuation is false. This has been redlined.  
             Greenleaf’s own records contain powerful pretext evidence.         The employment summary shows that             Raj Mehta was hired as Senior Director of Supply Chain & Logistics on March 20, 2025 — six days after Mr. Delano’s March 14, 2025 termination — at $185,000.         The termination was cited as         “    elimination of position due to restructuring.    ”         Hiring a replacement within days directly contradicts the stated rationale and substantially strengthens Mr. Delano’s retaliation and age-discrimination claims. This strongly supports the decision to settle, but it also makes the non-disparagement and reference provisions (which encode the         “    restructuring    ”         narrative) fragile.  
             The Draft is missing several provisions essential to protect Greenleaf    , including any governing-law/venue/dispute-resolution clause, a return-of-property clause, a confidential-information/trade-secret clause, a cooperation clause, and any address of Greenleaf’s EPLI insurance. These have been added in the redline.  
             Several provisions are unenforceable or unlawful as drafted    , including the non-compete’s coverage of             California         (void under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600) and the confidentiality/non-disparagement clauses’ failure to carve out the right to file with or cooperate with government agencies (EEOC, OSHA, SEC, NLRB, Oregon BOLI).  
      We recommend that Greenleaf             not execute the Draft in its current form    . The accompanying redline addresses the defects we identified; the items below that require factual follow-up (the 2024 bonus, EPLI consent-to-settle, Compensation Committee approval) should be resolved before execution.

        IV. Issues and Risks  
      Each issue below is rated             Critical    ,             High    ,             Medium    , or             Lower         based on its potential effect on the enforceability of the release, Greenleaf’s financial exposure, or Greenleaf’s litigation risk.  
       Issue 1 — ADEA/OWBPA release is likely invalid: 14-day consideration period and no revocation period. [Critical]  
          The problem.         Mr. Delano is 58 years old (DOB 07/14/1966; age 58 as of the report date of May 10, 2025). His age-discrimination claim under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (    “    ADEA    ”    ) is the core of the settlement. For a release of ADEA claims to be         “    knowing and voluntary    ”         under the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (    “    OWBPA    ”    ), 29 U.S.C. § 626(f), the agreement must (a) give the individual             at least 21 days         to consider the agreement (45 days only if the program is part of a group exit-incentive or other termination program — this is an individual settlement, so 21 days applies), and (b) allow             at least 7 days to revoke         after signing, during which the agreement is not effective or enforceable.  
      The Draft provides only             fourteen (14) calendar days         to consider (Sections 5(c) and 16) and is             entirely silent on any revocation period    . Neither requirement is satisfied.  
          Consequence.         The ADEA release is almost certainly invalid. Mr. Delano could execute the agreement, accept the $525,000, and thereafter file an ADEA charge and lawsuit, arguing the waiver was not knowing and voluntary. Greenleaf would have paid the consideration and received no enforceable release of the age claim — the most valuable claim in the settlement. This is the single greatest risk in the Draft.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Section 5(c) and Section 16 revised to             21 days    ; new Section 5(g) adds the             7-day revocation period         with revocation mechanics; the Effective Date (Section 1(b)) is redefined as the eighth day after the last signature (i.e., after the revocation period expires without revocation). These changes protect Greenleaf by making the ADEA release enforceable.

        Issue 3 — RSU acceleration is unconditional; the plan requires Compensation Committee approval. [High]  
          The problem.         Section 3(d) states that         “    Greenleaf shall cause the immediate accelerated vesting    ”         of 5,000 RSUs, with no condition. The Equity Details (Sub-Table A) state that the         “    Compensation Committee has discretion to approve accelerated vesting in connection with separation agreements    ”         under the 2021 Equity Incentive Plan, and the Forfeiture Provision provides that unvested RSUs are forfeited upon termination unless an exception (death, disability, Change of Control) applies — none of which is present here. Acceleration is therefore not automatic; it requires Compensation Committee action.  
          Consequence.         If the Compensation Committee declines to approve the acceleration, Greenleaf cannot deliver the shares and would be in breach of the agreement as drafted, exposing Greenleaf to a breach-of-contract claim (and potentially reviving the released claims if the breach is material). The Draft gives Greenleaf no protection.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Section 3(d) revised to make accelerated vesting             subject to Compensation Committee approval         as an express condition precedent, with Greenleaf’s obligation to seek approval promptly, and a             cash fallback of $62,500         (at Mr. Delano’s election) if approval is not obtained within 30 days.             Action item:         obtain Compensation Committee approval (or document the process) before or promptly after execution.  
        Issue 4 — Greenleaf’s records contain strong pretext evidence: a replacement was hired six days after termination. [Critical — litigation risk]  
          The problem.         The Employment History states that Mr. Delano was terminated on             March 14, 2025    , with the stated reason         “    Elimination of position due to restructuring of supply chain division.    ”         The same record states:         “        Replacement Hire: Raj Mehta, Senior Director of Supply Chain & Logistics, hired March 20, 2025, salary $185,000.    ”         A replacement was therefore hired             six days         after the termination, in the same functional area (supply chain and logistics), at a salary ($185,000) below Mr. Delano’s $218,000 base.  
          Consequence.         This is powerful evidence that the         “    elimination of position    ”         rationale is pretextual. It directly supports Mr. Delano’s retaliation claim under ORS 654.062(5) (he complained internally on November 8, 2024 and to Oregon OSHA on December 3, 2024, and was placed on leave February 10, 2025 and terminated March 14, 2025) and his ADEA claim (he was replaced by a lower-paid individual; the timing is suspect). The close temporal proximity between protected activity and adverse action, combined with a near-immediate replacement, makes this a strong plaintiff case. Two practical implications:  
              The settlement is well-advised.         Greenleaf’s litigation exposure (back pay, front pay, compensatory and (under ORS 659A) punitive damages, attorney fees, and Oregon OSHA penalties) likely exceeds the $537,500 corrected value. Settling is the right call.  
              The                 “        restructuring        ”                 narrative is fragile.         Sections 7 (non-disparagement) and 11 (reference) encode the         “    restructuring/elimination of position    ”         story. If the Raj Mehta hire surfaces — through the pending Oregon OSHA investigation, public records, LinkedIn, or a prospective-employer reference check — the narrative collapses and Greenleaf’s good-faith position is undermined. Greenleaf should ensure the release is airtight and should not rely on the non-disparagement/reference provisions to suppress facts that are already in the company’s own records.  
          Redline / recommendation.         No redline can redact this evidence. We recommend: (i) confirm the release covers all claims arising from the replacement hire and the termination rationale; (ii) preserve litigation hold / document-retention for the OSHA investigation; (iii) consider whether the reference provision (Section 11) should avoid affirmatively stating a         “    reorganization    ”         reason that the records contradict (the redline softens the reference to         “    neutral, factual    ”         and states the position         “    was eliminated as part of a reorganization    ”         — Greenleaf should satisfy itself that this statement is defensible, or further limit the reference to dates and title only).  
        Issue 5 — No governing law, venue, or dispute-resolution provision. [High]  
          The problem.         The Draft contains no choice-of-law clause, no forum-selection/venue clause, no jury waiver, and no arbitration clause. Disputes about enforcement (e.g., alleged breach of the non-compete, confidentiality, or non-disparagement provisions) could therefore be brought in an uncertain forum under uncertain law.  
          Consequence.         Greenleaf loses predictability and home-court advantage, and may face a jury in an employment-friendly forum on a breach claim. This is a significant gap for a company that is imposing post-termination restrictive covenants.  
          Redline / recommendation.         New Section 23 added:             Oregon law         governs; exclusive venue in             Multnomah County, Oregon         state court or the             U.S. District Court for the District of Oregon    ; mutual             jury-trial waiver    ; and fee-shifting to the prevailing party. (If Greenleaf prefers arbitration, we can substitute an arbitration clause; confirm preference.)  
        Issue 6 — Non-compete covers California, where non-competes are void. [High]  
          The problem.         Section 9 restricts Mr. Delano from working for an organic-food competitor         “    within the states of Oregon, Washington, Idaho,             and California    .    ”         California Business and Professions Code § 16600 voids contractual restraints on the practice of a lawful profession, trade, or business, subject only to narrow exceptions (sale of a business; certain partner/LLC member departures) that do not apply here. A non-compete covering California is unenforceable as to California.  
          Consequence.         The California portion is void; including it invites a challenge to the entire non-compete and creates a severability dispute. It also signals overreach that a court may hold against Greenleaf when evaluating the reasonableness of the remaining geographic scope.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Section 9 revised to remove California from the restricted territory and to add an express carve-out stating the non-compete does not apply in California to the extent prohibited by Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600. The severability clause (Section 17) is retained.  
        Issue 7 — Confidentiality and non-disparagement lack government-agency / whistleblower carve-outs. [High]  
          The problem.         Section 6 (confidentiality) permits disclosure only to spouse, counsel, tax advisor, or         “    as required by applicable law, regulation, court order, subpoena, or other compulsory legal process.    ”         Section 7 (non-disparagement) is broadly worded. Section 15(a)(ii) states Mr. Delano         “    agrees not to file any… charge, or proceeding in the future with respect to any claim released herein, except as may be required by law or to enforce the terms of this Agreement.    ”         Read together, these provisions could be construed to prohibit Mr. Delano from             voluntarily         filing a charge with, or cooperating in an investigation by, the EEOC, Oregon OSHA, the NLRB, the SEC, or the Oregon Bureau of Labor and Industries, and from receiving a whistleblower award.  
      Such restrictions are unenforceable and, in some respects, unlawful: federal anti-retalization and whistleblower statutes protect the right to report to government agencies and to receive awards (e.g., SEC/Dodd-Frank; OSHA); the EEOC’s position is that agreements cannot bar charges or EEOC cooperation; and the NLRB has scrutinized overbroad confidentiality/non-disparagement clauses. The point is acute here because the             Oregon OSHA investigation (Complaint No. OR-OSHA-2024-11872) is pending and unresolved    , and Mr. Delano cannot waive the government’s right to investigate.  
          Consequence.         Without carve-outs, these provisions are vulnerable to challenge, and Greenleaf risks an enforcement action or a public-policy defense that could invalidate portions of the agreement. The provisions also create a trap for Mr. Delano that a court is unlikely to enforce.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Carve-outs added: a new paragraph at the end of Section 4 (right to file, cooperate, and receive awards; OSHA investigation unaffected); a new exception (v) in Section 6; a carve-out in Section 7; and a cross-reference in Section 15(a)(ii). The carve-outs preserve the confidentiality of the settlement terms while protecting lawful government reporting.  
        Issue 8 — 2024 performance bonus was never determined or paid; potential unpaid-wage claim. [High]  
          The problem.         The Compensation History shows a 2024 bonus target of             20% of $218,000 = $43,600    , with the actual bonus recorded as         “        Not determined — termination    ”         and         “    2024 bonus not determined prior to termination.    ”         Historically, bonuses were paid in March of the following year (e.g., 2023 bonus paid March 2024). Mr. Delano was placed on administrative leave February 10, 2025 and terminated March 14, 2025 — before any 2024 bonus was determined or paid.  
      Section 2 of the Draft states that Mr. Delano         “    confirms that he has received all wages, salary, and compensation earned through the Separation Date, including payment for all accrued but unused paid time off (    ‘    PTO    ’    ), and that no further wages, salary, commissions, bonuses, or benefits of any kind are owed,    ”         and that         “    the terms of this Section 2 are not in dispute.    ”         If the 2024 bonus was an             earned wage         under Greenleaf’s bonus plan, this representation is false, and the release of wage claims may not fully resolve the exposure.  
          Consequence.         Under Oregon wage statutes (ORS 653.045; ORS 652.610), unpaid wages can carry             penalty wages of up to 30 days’ wages         plus the employee’s attorney fees and costs. A $43,600 unpaid bonus could therefore generate a substantially larger liability if litigated. The blanket         “    all compensation received    ”         representation also creates a mutual-mistake/fraud risk.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Section 2 revised to acknowledge that the Section 3 payments include, by mutual agreement, any unpaid 2024 incentive compensation and PTO, and to remove the         “    not in dispute    ”         language; Section 3(b) clarified that back pay includes unpaid 2024 incentive compensation.             Action item:         HR should confirm, before execution, whether the 2024 bonus was earned/vested under the bonus plan (e.g., whether the plan requires employment at the payment date) and document the basis for treating it as satisfied by the back-pay component.  
        Issue 9 — Effective Date is fixed at May 5, 2025, which is inconsistent with the consideration and revocation periods. [High]  
          The problem.         The Preamble states the agreement is         “    entered into as of May 5, 2025 (    ‘    Effective Date    ’    ),    ”         and Section 1(b) defines         “    Effective Date    ”         as         “    May 5, 2025, the date first set forth above.    ”         But Mr. Delano has up to 21 days to sign (after the redline) and 7 days to revoke, so the agreement cannot be effective on May 5, 2025. All payment timing is keyed to the Effective Date (Section 3 payments are due         “    within fourteen (14) calendar days of the Effective Date    ”    ), and the back-pay period runs         “    through the Effective Date.    ”  
          Consequence.         The fixed Effective Date creates internal inconsistency: payment deadlines and the back-pay period are tied to a date that precedes actual effectiveness. This invites disputes about when payments are due and the scope of the back-pay period.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Section 1(b) redefined so the Effective Date is the             eighth (8th) calendar day after the last Party signs    , provided Mr. Delano has not revoked during the 7-day Revocation Period; the Preamble updated to reference the defined Effective Date rather than a fixed date. This aligns payment timing with OWBPA mechanics.  
        Issue 10 — Entire Agreement clause would supersede the Equity Plan and RSU Grant Agreement. [Medium]  
          The problem.         Section 18 states the agreement         “    supersedes all prior and contemporaneous… agreements… including but not limited to any prior offer letters, employment agreements, memoranda of understanding, or term sheets.    ”         This sweep could be read to supersede the             Greenleaf 2021 Equity Incentive Plan         and the             RSU Grant Agreement dated July 1, 2022         — the very instruments that govern the RSU acceleration in Section 3(d) — and any prior confidentiality, invention-assignment, or restrictive-covenant agreements whose survival Greenleaf may want to preserve.  
          Consequence.         If the Plan and RSU Grant are superseded, the legal basis for the accelerated vesting (and the related tax/forfeiture terms) becomes muddled, and surviving confidentiality/IP obligations could be extinguished inadvertently.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Section 18 revised to carve out the Equity Plan, the RSU Grant Agreement, and any prior confidentiality, invention-assignment, or restrictive-covenant agreements, each of which remains in effect except as expressly modified by the settlement.  
        Issue 11 — EPLI insurance is not addressed; consent-to-settle and coverage allocation. [High]  
          The problem.         The Employment Records show Greenleaf maintains employment practices liability insurance with             Timberline Insurance Group, Policy No. TIG-EPL-2024-00391    . The Draft is silent on insurance. Many EPLI policies contain a             consent-to-settle         clause (and some a         “    hammer    ”         or         “    cooperation    ”         clause), and most require an             allocation         between covered claims (e.g., discrimination, retaliation) and uncovered claims (e.g., certain wage or common-law claims). Settling without tendering the claim or obtaining the insurer’s consent can jeopardize coverage.  
          Consequence.         Greenleaf may fund a $537,500 settlement that its insurer would have covered (in whole or part), or may breach a policy condition, losing coverage entirely. The allocation of the payment among the covered/uncovered claim categories also affects recovery.  
          Redline / recommendation.         New Section 22 added: Greenleaf is responsible for determining coverage and obtaining any required insurer consents; the payments are             absolute and unconditional         and not conditioned on insurance proceeds (protecting Mr. Delano); Greenleaf will provide reasonable cooperation to its insurer.             Action item (urgent):         GC should (i) confirm whether the Delano claim was tendered to Timberline; (ii) review the consent-to-settle and allocation provisions of Policy TIG-EPL-2024-00391; and (iii) obtain any required insurer consent before execution.  
        Issue 12 — Reference provision mandates a         “    positive    ”         reference and specific praise. [Medium]  
          The problem.         Section 11 requires Greenleaf to provide a         “    positive, neutral, or at minimum non-negative    ”         reference and to state that Mr. Delano         “    was a valued member of the Greenleaf team who made significant contributions.    ”         Mandating specific positive statements creates two risks: (i)             misrepresentation exposure         to prospective employers who rely on mandated praise that may not be fully accurate; and (ii)             enforceability problems         — courts are reluctant to compel specific positive speech. The provision also directs all inquiries to             Leanne Foss, HR Director    , who is the same person who received Mr. Delano’s November 8, 2024 internal complaint and is named as a Releasee — a potential conflict.  
          Consequence.         Greenleaf could be contractually obligated to make statements it cannot stand behind, and the         “    positive    ”         mandate is difficult to enforce and police across multiple executives.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Section 11 revised to require a         “        neutral, factual    ”         reference confirming dates of employment, final title, and a statement that Mr. Delano         “    was employed in good standing and that his position was eliminated as part of a reorganization.    ”         Greenleaf should satisfy itself that the         “    good standing    ”    /    “    reorganization    ”         statements are defensible in light of the replacement-hire evidence (Issue 4); if not, the reference should be limited to dates and title only.  
        Issue 13 — Non-disparagement is perpetual. [Medium]  
          The problem.         Section 7 provides that the non-disparagement obligations         “    shall continue in perpetuity.    ”         A perpetual non-disparagement clause is unusually broad and vulnerable to challenge as overbroad; most enforceable provisions run for a defined term (commonly 2–3 years).  
          Consequence.         A court may refuse to enforce a perpetual restriction or may blue-pencil it, undermining the provision’s deterrent value.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Section 7 revised to a             three (3) year         term following the Effective Date, with the government-agency carve-out (Issue 7).  
        Issue 14 — No return-of-company-property clause. [Medium]  
          The problem.         The Draft contains no representation that Mr. Delano has returned all company property and no covenant to return or destroy remaining materials. As a former VP of Supply Chain Operations, Mr. Delano had access to cold-chain protocols, supplier/vendor relationships, pricing, customer lists, and other confidential data.  
          Consequence.         Without this provision, Greenleaf lacks a contractual hook to compel return/destruction and to support a conversion or trade-secret claim if materials are retained.  
          Redline / recommendation.         New Section 19 added: representation of return of all property; covenant to delete/destroy remaining information and certify within 10 days; and a direction that Greenleaf provide 401(k) balance/rollover information (addressing Issue 19).  
        Issue 15 — No confidential-information / trade-secret protection clause (with DTSA notice). [Medium]  
          The problem.         The Draft imposes a non-compete and non-solicit but has no standalone confidentiality-of-company-information covenant, and it omits the             Defend Trade Secrets Act immunity notice         (18 U.S.C. § 1833(b)), which is good practice and supports the enforceability of trade-secret protections.  
          Consequence.         Greenleaf’s protection of its confidential information rests only on the non-compete/non-solicit (themselves vulnerable) and any prior agreements (which Section 18, as originally drafted, might have superseded). Omitting the DTSA notice is a missed protection.  
          Redline / recommendation.         New Section 20 added: confidentiality of company information; survival; and the DTSA immunity notice preserving the right to report trade-secret violations to government officials/attorneys and in sealed court filings, and to receive whistleblower awards.  
        Issue 16 — No cooperation clause. [Medium]  
          The problem.         The Oregon OSHA investigation is pending. The Draft contains no cooperation clause obligating Mr. Delano to assist Greenleaf in the OSHA matter or any future investigation, audit, or litigation within his former scope.  
          Consequence.         Greenleaf may lose access to a key witness with institutional knowledge of the cold-chain protocols and the supply-chain reorganization, to its detriment in the OSHA investigation.  
          Redline / recommendation.         New Section 21 added: reasonable cooperation (including with the OSHA investigation) with expense reimbursement and reasonable compensation for substantial time, with carve-outs for matters in which Mr. Delano is adverse to Greenleaf and for privileged information.  
        Issue 17 — Back-pay amount ($125,000) substantially exceeds salary for the stated period. [Lower]  
          The problem.           The problem.           The problem.         Even with California removed (Issue 6), the non-compete is imposed             at separation    , not at hire. ORS 653.295 imposes requirements on non-competition agreements (e.g., a written agreement provided to the employee at the time of, or within two weeks of, the offer; a salary above the statutory threshold; an 18-month maximum). There is ambiguity whether ORS 653.295 applies to a non-compete contained in a             settlement         agreement (as opposed to an employment agreement), and the notice-at-hire requirement likely cannot be satisfied post-termination. Separately, imposing an 18-month non-compete on an employee Greenleaf terminated after he raised food-safety concerns risks being portrayed as retaliatory and may attract scrutiny.  
          Consequence.         The non-compete’s enforceability in Oregon is uncertain. The 18-month duration and the salary threshold ($218,000 exceeds the ORS 653.295 threshold) are satisfied, but the notice/formality issue and the retaliatory optics are real.  
          Redline / recommendation.         Section 9 revised to add an acknowledgment that the covenant is entered in connection with the settlement, is supported by the Section 3 consideration, that Mr. Delano’s salary exceeded the ORS 653.295 threshold, and that the duration does not exceed the 18-month statutory maximum.             Action item:         confirm enforceability with Oregon employment counsel before relying on the non-compete, and consider whether the business need for the non-compete justifies the retaliatory optics (the non-solicitation in Section 10 may suffice to protect Greenleaf’s customer/employee relationships).

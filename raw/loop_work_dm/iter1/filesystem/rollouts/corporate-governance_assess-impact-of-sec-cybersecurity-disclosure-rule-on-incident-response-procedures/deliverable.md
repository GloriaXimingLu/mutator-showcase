       I. Executive Summary  
      This memorandum analyzes the Securities and Exchange Commission (    “    SEC    ”    ) cybersecurity disclosure compliance posture of Vantage Industrial Technologies, Inc. (    “    Vantage    ”         or the         “    Company    ”    ) in light of the ransomware and data-exfiltration incident detected on November 18, 2024 (Incident INC-2024-0047), as documented in the Company’s incident status report dated December 5, 2024 (Day 17), the Thorngate Forensic Solutions, Inc. interim report dated December 1, 2024, the Cybersecurity Incident Response Plan (    “    CIRP    ”    ), the Form 10-K Item 1C disclosure for fiscal year 2023, the Audit Committee Charter, the customer contract excerpts, the Ridgeline cyber insurance policy summary, and the General Counsel’s December 3, 2024 email to the Audit Committee Chair.  
          The central finding is that, as of Day 17, the Company has conducted no materiality determination and has filed no Form 8-K disclosing the incident, despite the presence of strong, readily apparent materiality indicators.         The SEC’s cybersecurity disclosure rules—Item 1.05 of Form 8-K and Item 106 of Regulation S-K, adopted in the SEC’s final rules on         “    Cybersecurity Risk Management, Strategy, Governance, and Incident Disclosure    ”         (effective September 5, 2023, as cited in the Company’s own Form 10-K)—require a registrant to determine whether a cybersecurity incident is material         “    without unreasonable delay    ”         after discovery and, upon a materiality determination, to file an Item 1.05 Form 8-K within four business days. The SEC has stated that materiality should generally be determined within a matter of days, not weeks. Seventeen calendar days (approximately eleven business days) have elapsed with no determination and no filing, and no U.S. Attorney General notification authorizing delay has been received. This is the single most urgent compliance gap and creates material exposure to SEC enforcement, shareholder litigation, and disclosure-controls findings.  
      The incident itself presents powerful materiality indicators on both quantitative and qualitative grounds: encryption of 347 of approximately 2,100 networked endpoints; disabling of the core ERP finance, procurement, and order-management modules for 17+ days with manual workarounds; exfiltration of approximately 83 GB of data including unencrypted customer ACH routing and account numbers, procurement-contact personally identifiable information (    “    PII    ”    ), and contract pricing data for approximately 4,200 customer records (including at least six of the Company’s top ten customers, which represent 38% of revenue, or approximately $710.6 million of $1.87 billion in projected FY2024 revenue); an estimated $4.5 million in costs (a floor that excludes customer claims, regulatory penalties, litigation, liquidated damages, and potential insurance-coverage loss); a pending $425 million acquisition (Kessler Precision Systems GmbH) whose counterparty and advisor have not been notified; and a cascade of breached customer-contract notification deadlines, an insurance late-notice problem, and unaddressed state and (likely) EU/GDPR breach-notification obligations.  
      Beyond the Item 1.05 disclosure failure, the record reveals a series of governance, risk-management, and process gaps that are themselves disclosable under Item 106 of Regulation S-K and that contradict specific representations in the Company’s existing Form 10-K Item 1C disclosure—most notably the representation that the Company         “    has implemented … multi-factor authentication for access to … remote access connections,    ”         which the forensic record shows was not true for third-party contractor VPN access (the very vector used in the attack). The Audit Committee Charter (last amended March 15, 2021) contains no cybersecurity oversight provisions; the CIRP escalation matrix stops at the Chief Technology Officer and treats the General Counsel, CEO, and Board as discretionary         “    optional    ”         contacts; the CIRP has never been tested; and the CIRP expressly places external/regulatory communications         “    outside the scope    ”         of the plan.  
      This memorandum sets out 27 numbered findings, each with the supporting record evidence (stated verbatim where the record states a precise figure, name, date, or identifier), the compliance consequence, and a specific recommendation. Findings are assigned a priority rating of             Critical    ,             High    , or             Medium    . A consolidated materiality assessment and a prioritized remediation plan appear in Sections VII and VIII.

        III. Applicable SEC Framework  
      The controlling framework is the SEC’s cybersecurity disclosure rules, which the Company’s own 10-K Item 1C identifies as         “    Item 106 of Regulation S-K, as adopted by the Securities and Exchange Commission (the         ‘    SEC    ’    ) in its final rules on         ‘    Cybersecurity Risk Management, Strategy, Governance, and Incident Disclosure,    ’         effective September 5, 2023.    ”         That rulemaking established two complementary disclosure regimes:  
             Item 106 of Regulation S-K (annual report disclosure).         Requires, in the annual report on Form 10-K, disclosure of (a) the registrant’s processes for assessing, identifying, and managing material risks from cybersecurity threats, including risks associated with third-party service providers and integration with the registrant’s overall risk management system; and (b) the board of directors’ oversight of cybersecurity risks (including which committee or subcommittee oversees and the processes by which the board is informed) and management’s role in assessing and managing such risks (including relevant management positions, reporting lines, and the processes by which management is informed).  
             Item 1.05 of Form 8-K (current report disclosure of material cybersecurity incidents).         Requires a registrant, upon determining that a cybersecurity incident is material, to file a Form 8-K within four business days describing the material aspects of the             nature, scope, and timing         of the incident and the             material impact or reasonably likely material impact         on the registrant, including its financial condition and results of operations. The materiality determination must be made         “    without unreasonable delay    ”         after discovery of the incident, with delay permitted only as necessary to (i) prevent unauthorized access to systems and (ii) determine materiality. The SEC has stated that materiality should generally be determined within a matter of days, not weeks. A limited exception permits delayed disclosure only where the U.S. Attorney General notifies the SEC that disclosure would pose a substantial risk to national security or public safety.  
             Materiality standard.         Materiality is assessed under the established federal-securities standard (information is material if there is a substantial likelihood a reasonable investor would consider it important), considering both quantitative and qualitative factors. For cybersecurity incidents, qualitative factors—such as harm to reputation, customer relationships, competitive position, and the potential for regulatory or litigation exposure—can render an incident material even where the quantified financial impact is modest.  
             Disclosure controls and internal control over financial reporting (        “        ICFR        ”        ).         The timeliness and rigor of the materiality-determination process is itself a function of the Company’s disclosure controls and procedures; a failure to escalate and evaluate a known incident promptly implicates disclosure-controls effectiveness and, where the incident affects financial reporting systems, potentially ICFR.  
      The analysis below audits the record against each of these elements.  
      
        IV. Findings — SEC Current-Report (Item 1.05) Disclosure Gaps  
       Finding 1 — No Form 8-K filed; no materiality determination conducted as of Day 17 [CRITICAL]  
          Description.         As of the December 5, 2024 Status Report (Day 17), the Company has filed no Form 8-K or other public disclosure regarding the incident and has conducted no materiality determination. The Status Report states verbatim:         “    No Form 8-K or other public disclosure has been filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission regarding this incident. No formal materiality determination has been conducted.    ”         The GC Email to the Audit Committee Chair (December 3, 2024) likewise proposes only to         “    discuss this matter further at the next scheduled Audit Committee meeting on January 22, 2025, or sooner if you believe a special session is warranted    ”    —a timeline incompatible with the four-business-day Item 1.05 regime.  
          Evidence.         Status Report §1 (Executive Summary), §5.4 (    “    Regulatory Filings    ”    ), and §7 (Open Item 5:         “    No formal materiality determination has been conducted for SEC disclosure purposes. Legal department and outside counsel (Hollister Marsh LLP) are evaluating disclosure obligations.    ”    ); GC Email,         “    Proposed Next Steps,    ”         item 4.  
          Consequence.         The Company is exposed to SEC enforcement risk (including under Exchange Act Section 13(a) and Rules 13a-1/13a-11 and the antifraud provisions where any misleading omission is coupled with a duty to update), to shareholder class and derivative action risk, and to a finding that its disclosure controls and procedures are ineffective. Every additional business day without a determination compounds the         “    without unreasonable delay    ”         exposure.  
          Recommendation.         Convene a materiality-determination meeting of the disclosure committee (General Counsel, CFO, CTO, CISO, and outside securities counsel)             immediately    —no later than December 9, 2024. Document the determination and its basis contemporaneously. If material (as the analysis in Section VII strongly indicates), file the Item 1.05 Form 8-K within four business days of the determination, addressing nature, scope, timing, and material/reasonably likely material impact. If the determination is that the incident is not yet material, document the reasoning with equal rigor and re-evaluate as the investigation matures, filing promptly upon any change in determination.  
        Finding 2 — Materiality determination delayed beyond the         “    without unreasonable delay    ”         standard [CRITICAL]  
          Description.         The incident was discovered on November 18, 2024, at approximately 2:17 a.m. EST. As of December 5, 2024, approximately 17 calendar days (roughly 11 business days, accounting for the November 28 Thanksgiving holiday) have elapsed with no materiality determination. The SEC’s standard requires the determination to be made without unreasonable delay after discovery, generally within days, not weeks.  
          Evidence.         Status Report §2 (timeline: detection 2:17 a.m. EST November 18, 2024; IRT activation 5:00 a.m.); Status Report §5.4 and §7 (no determination as of December 5, 2024).  
          Consequence.         The elapsed time already exceeds the outer bound of what the SEC has described as acceptable for a materiality determination. The delay is difficult to justify given that the key materiality facts—endpoint count, ERP module outage, data exfiltration volume, and customer-banking-data exposure—were known by the December 1, 2024 Thorngate interim report at the latest, and the core operational impact was known on Day 0.  
          Recommendation.         Treat the materiality determination as overdue. Do not allow the Thorngate 60-day investigation timeline (preliminary findings due December 20, 2024) to govern the disclosure clock; the SEC regime does not permit a registrant to wait for a completed forensic investigation before determining materiality. Make the determination on the information already available and update via subsequent filings (e.g., the next Form 10-Q) as facts develop, which the rules expressly contemplate.  
        Finding 3 — Strong, readily apparent materiality indicators are present [CRITICAL]  
          Description.         The record contains multiple materiality indicators, both quantitative and qualitative, that should have triggered a prompt determination:  
              Quantitative:         Estimated $4.5 million in costs ($1.4 million incident response; $2.8 million business disruption; $0.3 million customer-notification preparation), representing approximately 1.45% of projected FY2024 EBITDA of approximately $310 million. This figure is expressly described as a floor—    “    Final costs may be materially higher than the estimates    ”    —and excludes customer claims, regulatory penalties, litigation costs, liquidated damages, and reputational impact.  
              Operational scope:         347 of approximately 2,100 networked endpoints encrypted; core ERP finance, procurement, and order-management modules disabled since November 18, 2024, with manual workarounds in place for 17+ days; 58 endpoints still unrestored as of December 5, 2024 (estimated completion December 18, 2024).  
              Data sensitivity:         Approximately 83 GB exfiltrated, including customer ACH routing numbers and account numbers (stored unencrypted/plaintext), procurement-contact PII, and contract pricing data for approximately 4,200 customer records.  
              Customer concentration:         At least six of the Company’s top ten customers (representing 38% of revenue, or approximately $710.6 million of $1.87 billion in projected FY2024 revenue) are believed to have records in the exfiltrated dataset; named affected accounts include Harmon Defense Solutions, Crestfield Aerospace, and Nexagen Manufacturing.  
              Geographic/regulatory reach:         Affected records span at least 38 U.S. states plus Germany, Mexico, Canada, and the United Kingdom, implicating multiple state breach-notification regimes and likely the EU GDPR.  
              Strategic transaction:         A pending $425 million acquisition (Kessler Precision Systems GmbH) may be affected; neither the counterparty nor the Company’s advisor has been notified.  
              Contractual exposure:         Customer-contract notification deadlines (48 hours) have been breached, triggering liquidated damages, termination, indemnification, payment-suspension, and audit rights.  
          Evidence.         Status Report §§1, 3, 4, 5.3; Thorngate Interim Report §§1, 4.2, 4.4, 4.5; Customer Contract Excerpts §§1–3 and Comparative Summary Table.  
          Consequence.         The qualitative factors alone—exfiltration of unencrypted customer banking data for top-revenue customers, breached customer contracts with termination rights, and a potentially affected $425 million acquisition—are sufficient to support a materiality finding even setting aside the quantified costs. The absence of a determination is not a function of insufficient information; it is a process failure.  
          Recommendation.         Proceed on the working assumption that the incident is material (see Section VII) and prepare the Item 1.05 disclosure accordingly, while completing the formal determination to confirm. The disclosure should address each materiality dimension above.  
        Finding 4 — No basis to invoke the limited disclosure-delay exception [HIGH]  
          Description.         Item 1.05 permits delayed disclosure only where the U.S. Attorney General notifies the SEC that disclosure would pose a substantial risk to national security or public safety. The Company contacted the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (    “    IC3    ”    ) on November 20, 2024, but the Status Report confirms:         “    As of the date of this report, no request from law enforcement to delay any public disclosure related to this incident has been received.    ”  
          Evidence.         Status Report §1 (    “    no request from law enforcement to delay any public disclosure … has been received    ”    ); Status Report §5.1.  
          Consequence.         No lawful basis exists to delay the Item 1.05 disclosure on national-security or public-safety grounds. (Separately, the involvement of a defense contractor customer—Harmon Defense Solutions—may raise distinct DoD reporting obligations; see Finding 25, but those do not supply an SEC disclosure-delay basis.)  
          Recommendation.         Do not treat the FBI IC3 contact or any pending law-enforcement coordination as a basis to defer the SEC filing. If the Company wishes to seek a delay, it must be pursued through the formal AG-to-SEC notification channel, which has not occurred.  
        Finding 5 — Disclosure controls and procedures deficiency; potential ICFR implication [HIGH]  
          Description.         The fact that 17 days elapsed without a materiality determination indicates that the Company’s disclosure controls and procedures did not timely surface a known, significant cybersecurity event to the disclosure decision-makers. The Audit Committee Charter (§V.A.6) requires the Committee to review the adequacy and effectiveness of disclosure controls and procedures; the incident response and disclosure processes did not function as designed. Because the incident disabled the ERP finance module and required manual financial workarounds, there is also a potential ICFR implication (a significant deficiency or material weakness cannot be ruled out while data-integrity validation is ongoing and manual processes substitute for automated controls).  
          Evidence.         Status Report §3 (ERP finance module disabled; manual workarounds since November 18, 2024); Thorngate Interim Report §6.4 (    “    ERP modules are partially operational … Data integrity validation is currently ongoing … certain ERP functions may produce results that require manual verification    ”    ); Audit Committee Charter §V.A.6.  
          Consequence.         A disclosure-controls deficiency must be evaluated for disclosure in the Company’s next periodic report and in management’s assessment of disclosure controls. An ICFR deficiency, if it rises to a material weakness, would require disclosure and a remediation plan, and would be relevant to the FY2024 audit (Greystone & Associates LLP), which has not been notified (Finding 23).  
          Recommendation.         (a) Document and remediate the disclosure-controls gap (escalation triggers, timing, and decision rights). (b) Direct management and the independent auditor to assess ICFR impact, particularly around the ERP finance module, period-end close, and manual workaround controls. (c) Reflect any conclusion in the next Form 10-Q and the FY2024 Form 10-K.  
      
         V. Findings — Governance and Risk-Management Gaps (Item 106 of Regulation S-K)  
       Finding 6 — Audit Committee Charter contains no cybersecurity oversight provisions [HIGH]  
          Description.         The Audit Committee Charter, last amended and restated March 15, 2021, predates the SEC’s September 5, 2023 cybersecurity rules and contains no cybersecurity oversight provisions. Its         “    Duties and Responsibilities    ”         (§V) cover financial reporting oversight, independent-auditor oversight, internal-audit oversight, compliance and (financial) risk management, related-party transactions, and other responsibilities—but none address cybersecurity risk oversight. By contrast, the 10-K Item 1C states that         “    the Board of Directors … acting primarily through its Audit Committee … oversees the Company’s cybersecurity risk management program    ”         and that         “    [t]he Audit Committee receives quarterly updates on the Company’s cybersecurity posture.    ”  
          Evidence.         Audit Committee Charter §§I, V (no cybersecurity provisions; amendment history noting March 15, 2021 as last amendment); 10-K Item 1C (    “    Governance — Board of Directors Oversight    ”    ).  
          Consequence.         There is a mismatch between the Company’s disclosed governance (Item 106(b) requires disclosure of which committee oversees cybersecurity and the processes by which the board is informed) and the charter that actually governs the Committee. This is a documentation gap that weakens the Company’s Item 106 disclosure and its defense to any governance-related claim. The charter should be amended to reflect the cybersecurity oversight role the Company already claims to perform.  
          Recommendation.         Amend the Audit Committee Charter to add explicit cybersecurity oversight responsibilities, including: receipt of periodic cybersecurity posture reports; oversight of the cybersecurity risk management program and the CIRP; receipt of prompt notice of significant cybersecurity incidents; and authority to engage independent cybersecurity advisors. Have the Board approve the amendment and reflect it in the FY2024 10-K Item 1C.  
        Finding 7 — CIRP escalation matrix stops at the CTO; Board, Audit Committee, General Counsel, and CEO are only discretionary         “    optional    ”         contacts [CRITICAL]  
          Description.         The CIRP’s mandatory escalation matrix (§10.1) escalates only to the Chief Technology Officer: Tier 1 → CISO (awareness only); Tier 2 → CISO → CTO (Lauren Hessler) within 4 hours of IRT activation; Tier 3 → CISO → CTO within 2 hours of IRT activation. The General Counsel, the CEO, and the Board/Audit Committee appear only in         “    Appendix B — Notification Matrix (Optional Contacts),    ”         each expressly stated to be at the discretion of the Incident Commander or the CTO. The 10-K Item 1C, by contrast, represents that         “    in the event of a significant cybersecurity incident, the Company’s processes provide for escalation to the Board of Directors and, as appropriate, the Audit Committee, to ensure that the Board is informed in a timely manner.    ”  
          Evidence.         CIRP §10.1 (Escalation Matrix) and §10.2 (    “    Any such further escalation is at the CTO’s sole discretion, and this Plan does not prescribe specific triggers, recipients, or timelines for escalation beyond the CTO    ”    ); CIRP Appendix B (General Counsel Priya Raghavan, Associate General Counsel Daniel Ito, outside counsel Hollister Marsh LLP, CEO Marcus Ellsworth—all discretionary); 10-K Item 1C (Governance).  
          Consequence.         The CIRP’s escalation design is the root cause of the delayed Board, General Counsel, and CEO notifications documented in the Status Report (Findings 8–9). It also contradicts the 10-K’s representation of timely Board escalation, creating a disclosure-accuracy issue under Item 106(b). A Tier 3 incident—the highest severity—does not mandatorily reach the disclosure officer (General Counsel), the CEO, or the Board, which is untenable for an SEC registrant.  
          Recommendation.         Revise the CIRP escalation matrix to make notification of the General Counsel, the CEO, and the Audit Committee Chair (or the full Audit Committee) mandatory for Tier 3 incidents and for any incident with potential disclosure, customer-notification, regulatory, or materiality implications, with defined timeframes (e.g., General Counsel within hours; Audit Committee Chair within 24 hours; full Board/Audit Committee within 48–72 hours or on a special-meeting basis). Align the revised CIRP with the 10-K Item 1C governance disclosure.  
        Finding 8 — Audit Committee Chair not notified until Day 15; no special meeting called [HIGH]  
          Description.         The first Board-level notification occurred on December 3, 2024 (Day 15), by email from the General Counsel to the Audit Committee Chair. The next regularly scheduled Audit Committee meeting is January 22, 2025, and no special meeting has been called. The 10-K Item 1C represents that escalation to the Board/Audit Committee occurs         “    in a timely manner.    ”  
          Evidence.         Status Report §2 (December 3, 2024 entry:         “    Audit Committee Chair Dr. Helen Ostrowski received an email summary … This communication represented the first Board-level notification    ”    ); Status Report §5.5 (    “    The next regularly scheduled meeting of the Audit Committee is January 22, 2025. No special meeting of the Audit Committee has been called.    ”    ); GC Email (December 3, 2024, 4:47 a.m.); 10-K Item 1C (Governance).  
          Consequence.         A 15-day delay in Board-level notice of a Tier 3 incident involving exfiltrated customer banking data, breached customer contracts, and a potentially affected $425 million acquisition is inconsistent with the Company’s disclosed governance and with the board-oversight expectations of Item 106(b). It also deprived the Audit Committee of the opportunity to oversee the materiality determination and disclosure process at the time those decisions should have been made.  
          Recommendation.         Call a special meeting of the Audit Committee (and, as appropriate, the full Board) promptly to review the incident, the materiality determination, the disclosure plan, and the remediation status. Establish a written escalation standard requiring Board/Audit-Committee notice within a defined short window for Tier 3 or potentially material incidents.  
        Finding 9 — General Counsel (the disclosure officer) not notified until Day 2, and only upon return from travel [HIGH]  
          Description.         The General Counsel, Priya Raghavan, learned of the incident on November 20, 2024 (Day 2), only upon returning from international travel; she was not informed on November 18 or 19. The Associate General Counsel, Daniel Ito, learned of the incident on November 19         “    through informal communication from an IT colleague.    ”         The CIRP does not mandate General Counsel notification (it is an         “    optional    ”         contact). The General Counsel is the Company’s disclosure officer and the person who engaged outside securities counsel (Hollister Marsh LLP) on November 20.  
          Evidence.         Status Report §2 (November 19 entry:         “    Associate General Counsel Daniel Ito learned of the incident … through informal communication from an IT colleague. General Counsel Priya Raghavan was traveling internationally … and was not informed    ”    ; November 20 entry:         “    General Counsel Priya Raghavan learned of the incident upon returning from international travel    ”    ); CIRP Appendix B (General Counsel listed as optional, discretionary contact).  
          Consequence.         The two-day delay in notifying the disclosure officer delayed the engagement of outside securities counsel and the commencement of the materiality analysis, directly contributing to the Item 1.05 disclosure gap. Reliance on the General Counsel’s physical availability—and on informal IT-to-lawyer communication—is an inadequate escalation design for an SEC registrant.  
          Recommendation.         Make General Counsel notification mandatory and immediate for any Tier 2/Tier 3 incident (revising the CIRP per Finding 7), with a designated backup (the Associate General Counsel) empowered to act and to engage outside counsel when the General Counsel is unavailable. Implement an out-of-office escalation protocol so that travel does not delay disclosure-critical notifications.  
        Finding 10 — CIRP has never been tested; no substantive review since June 2022 [HIGH]  
          Description.         The CIRP expressly states:         “    No tabletop exercises or simulation-based tests of this Plan have been conducted.    ”         The Plan was originally issued June 15, 2022 (Version 1.0); the only subsequent revision (Version 1.0.1, August 14, 2023) was an administrative update to vendor contact information with         “    no substantive changes to Plan content, procedures, governance structure, classification framework, escalation matrix, or communication protocols.    ”         The 10-K Item 1C describes the CIRP as establishing         “    a structured incident response framework    ”         and represents the program as adaptive and periodically reviewed.  
          Evidence.         CIRP §1.4 (    “    No tabletop exercises or simulation-based tests of this Plan have been conducted    ”    ); CIRP §4.3 (    “    As of the date of this Plan, no tabletop exercises have been scheduled    ”    ); CIRP Document Version History; 10-K Item 1C (Risk Management and Strategy).  
          Consequence.         An untested plan is a risk-management deficiency disclosable under Item 106(a), and the gap between the 10-K’s description of a functioning, adaptive program and an untested, substantively unreviewed plan is a disclosure-accuracy concern. The escalation and notification failures documented in this incident are consistent with a plan that has never been exercised.  
          Recommendation.         Schedule and conduct a tabletop exercise promptly (and on a recurring basis), with scenarios that include disclosure-materiality triggers and Board/General-Counsel escalation. Conduct a substantive (not merely administrative) CIRP review and update the plan to address the gaps identified in this memorandum. Reflect the testing cadence in the FY2024 10-K Item 1C.  
        Finding 11 — CIRP places external and regulatory communications         “    outside the scope    ”         of the plan [HIGH]  
          Description.         CIRP §9.3 states:         “    External communications regarding cybersecurity incidents—including communications to customers, regulators, media, or the public—are outside the scope of this Plan,    ”         and defers to the corporate communications team         “    at the discretion    ”         of the Incident Commander. The plan therefore provides no framework, triggers, or timelines for the very disclosures now required: the SEC Item 1.05 Form 8-K, state breach notifications, GDPR supervisory-authority notifications, and customer-contract notifications.  
          Evidence.         CIRP §9.3 (External Communications); CIRP §9.2 (vendor/third-party communications only).  
          Consequence.         The incident response plan is structurally disconnected from the Company’s disclosure obligations. This contributed to the absence of any disclosure process being triggered by the technical response, and it leaves customer, regulator, and SEC notification timing to ad hoc discretion rather than defined procedure.  
          Recommendation.         Integrate disclosure and notification workflows into the CIRP (or a companion disclosure sub-plan), with defined triggers, owners, and timelines for: SEC Item 1.05 filing; state attorney general / data-protection authority notifications; GDPR supervisory-authority notification (72 hours); customer-contract notifications; and independent-auditor notification. Assign the General Counsel as the disclosure-process owner.  
        Finding 12 — CIRP severity classification is purely technical and contains no materiality/disclosure lens [MEDIUM]  
          Description.         CIRP §3.2 classifies incidents by         “    solely    ”         technical criteria (endpoint count, downtime, data exfiltration, ransomware on production systems, privileged-credential compromise, perimeter breach, APT). The Tier 3 classification here was based on         “    >100 endpoints    ”         and         “    >4 hours    ”         critical-system downtime. The plan contains no mechanism to flag disclosure materiality, customer-notification triggers, or regulatory-reporting obligations, and expressly states severity is         “    determined by the Incident Commander based solely on the technical criteria.    ”  
          Evidence.         CIRP §3.2 (    “    Severity classification is determined by the Incident Commander based solely on the technical criteria set forth above    ”    ); Status Report §2 (Tier 3 based on endpoint count and downtime).  
          Consequence.         Technical severity and disclosure materiality are different concepts; a technically         “    Tier 1    ”         incident could be disclosure-material (e.g., exfiltration of a small volume of highly sensitive data), and the plan provides no bridge between the two. This risks both over- and under-escalation for disclosure purposes.  
          Recommendation.         Add a parallel         “    disclosure-materiality assessment    ”         step to the classification process, triggered for any incident involving data exfiltration, customer data, regulated data, or potential business/financial impact, with mandatory General Counsel involvement. Decouple the technical severity tier from the disclosure decision.  
        Finding 13 — 10-K Item 1C MFA representation overstates actual coverage; potential misstatement and insurance-warranty breach [CRITICAL]  
          Description.         The 10-K Item 1C states the Company         “    has implemented access management controls, including role-based access controls, privileged access management, and multi-factor authentication for access to critical information systems, remote access connections, and administrative accounts.    ”         The Thorngate Interim Report and the Status Report establish that the compromised third-party maintenance contractor VPN credential—the attack vector—was             not         protected by MFA, and that         “    MFA was not universally required for third-party contractor VPN access    ”         prior to November 20, 2024 (MFA was enforced on VPN connections only after the incident). The Ridgeline Policy Application (dated October 15, 2023) likewise warranted that the Company         “    employs multi-factor authentication for all remote access to Computer Systems.    ”  
          Evidence.         10-K Item 1C (Access Management Controls); Thorngate Interim Report §6.2 (    “    Multi-factor authentication (    ‘    MFA    ’    ) has been enforced on all VPN connections as of November 20, 2024. Prior to this date, MFA was not universally required for third-party contractor VPN access    ”    ); Status Report §3 (Attack Vector:         “    the compromised credential was not protected by multi-factor authentication (    ‘    MFA    ’    )    ”    ); Ridgeline Policy Summary §9 (Policy Application Warranty: MFA for         “    all remote access    ”    ).  
          Consequence.           Recommendation.         (a) Correct the MFA representation in the FY2024 10-K Item 1C to accurately describe the scope of MFA coverage (including the historical gap for third-party VPN and the remediation). (b) Immediately assess the insurance-warranty and exclusion exposure with coverage counsel; the MFA gap is the single largest threat to the $2.0 million of coverage above the SIR and potentially to the entire policy. (c) Complete and validate MFA deployment on all remote access (including all third-party accounts) and document the remediation.

        Finding 19 — Insurance late notice: Ridgeline notified at ~73 hours vs. 72-hour requirement; rights reserved [HIGH]  
          Description.         Ridgeline Insurance Group was notified on November 21, 2024,         “    approximately 73 hours after initial detection,    ”         against a policy requirement of notice         “    in no event later than seventy-two (72) hours after Discovery.    ”         Ridgeline         “    has reserved rights regarding the timeliness of notice but has not denied coverage at this time.    ”         The policy’s         “    Discovery    ”         definition is broad—Discovery occurs when any qualifying officer (CISO, CIO, CTO, General Counsel, CFO, or any VP-level-or-above officer) becomes aware; the CISO became aware at IRT activation (5:00 a.m. EST, November 18, 2024), so the 72-hour clock began no later than then.           Evidence.         Status Report §1 and §5.2 (notified November 21, 2024; ~73 hours; reserved rights); Ridgeline Policy Summary §5.1 (72-hour notice; Discovery definition) and §5.3 (consequences of late notice; notice-prejudice vs. strict compliance); §7.5 (Ohio law; AAA arbitration, Columbus).

         VII. Materiality Assessment  
      Applying the federal-securities materiality standard to the facts known as of December 5–6, 2024:  
          Quantitative factors. A reasonable estimate of total reasonably likely exposure substantially exceeds the $4.5 million figure.  
          Qualitative factors (decisive here).         The qualitative indicators strongly support materiality:  
          Exfiltration of             unencrypted customer banking data         (ACH routing and account numbers) for approximately 4,200 records, creating direct fraud risk.  
          Affected customers include             at least six of the top ten customers (38% of revenue)    , with breached 48-hour notification deadlines triggering liquidated damages, termination, payment-suspension, audit, and future-business-exclusion rights.  
              Core ERP systems (finance, procurement, order management) disabled for 17+ days         with manual workarounds, implicating operations, financial reporting, and ICFR.  
              Pending $425 million acquisition         potentially affected.  
              Multi-jurisdiction regulatory exposure         (SEC, 38+ U.S. states, EU/GDPR, and possibly DoD).  
              Reputational and competitive harm         from exfiltration of contract pricing data and customer PII.  
              Insurance coverage uncertainty         that could materially increase net cost.  
          Conclusion.         The incident is             likely material         under the SEC’s standard. The materiality determination is overdue.           Non-issue noted.         The FY2023 10-K Item 1C statement that the Company         “    has not experienced any cybersecurity incidents that have materially affected … the Company’s business strategy, results of operations, or financial condition    ”         was accurate as of the February 28, 2024 filing date (the incident occurred in November 2024) and is not a misstatement; it must, however, be updated in the FY2024 10-K (Finding 17).

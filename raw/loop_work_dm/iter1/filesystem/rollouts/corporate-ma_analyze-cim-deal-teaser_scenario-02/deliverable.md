        1. Executive Summary  
          Recommendation: Proceed to submit a non-binding Indication of Interest, but anchor valuation on a buyer-adjusted EBITDA that is materially below the seller’s $17.1 million figure, and condition the bid on a structured earnout and a defined diligence gate.         CES is a genuine, well-positioned Pacific Northwest environmental services platform that fits Fund III’s sector and geographic focus, and Thornfield’s GreenWorks and Allied Waste experience is directly relevant. However, the sell-side materials contain a pattern of internal inconsistencies, aggressive EBITDA adjustments, a customer-concentration contract that expires before close, and a growth thesis that is almost entirely dependent on a two-year-old service line. These are diligence-resolvable in part, but several items (data integrity across the CIM and data room, the PNR contract expiration, owner transition, and PFAS execution) are material to price and structure and should be reflected in the IOI.  
          Valuation view.         The CIM implies an enterprise value of             $136.8 million–$171.0 million         (8.0x–10.0x FY2024 Adjusted EBITDA of $17.1 million) and pushes the         “    higher end,    ”         with a forward view of $157.6 million–$197.0 million on FY2025E Adjusted EBITDA of $19.7 million. After correcting the EBITDA bridge for the issues identified in this memo,             buyer-defensible FY2024 Adjusted EBITDA is approximately $14.1 million–$15.2 million         (central case ~$14.6 million), implying an EV range of roughly             $113 million–$152 million at 8.0x–10.0x    , with a central case near             $126 million–$137 million (≈9.0x)    . I recommend an IOI enterprise-value range of approximately             $115 million–$140 million         on a cash-free, debt-free basis with normalized net working capital, with the upper portion delivered through an earnout tied to (i) PFAS revenue achievement against the $14.0 million FY2025E target and (ii) renewal of the Pacific Northwest Refining Co. (    “    PNR    ”    ) master service agreement. Note that even the lower end of the seller’s range exceeds Fund III’s stated $30 million–$100 million platform EV target; fund-fit and any co-invest requirement should be confirmed with the IC before the IOI is submitted.  
          Top risks (detailed in Sections 6–15):  
              Sell-side financials do not reconcile across documents.         The CIM’s condensed income statement and balance sheet differ materially from the Financial Exhibits workbook — net income differs by $1.7 million ($5.0M CIM vs. $6.7M exhibits), total assets by $3.9 million, and the exhibits’ own income statement does not internally tie (Operating Income + D&A ≠ Reported EBITDA in every year). The exhibits also reveal $2.5 million of goodwill/intangibles and a below-the-line classification of the DEQ settlement that the CIM does not surface.  
              Adjusted EBITDA is 29% adjustments ($5.0M of $17.1M), and several add-backs are overstated.         The related-party lease add-back contradicts the CIM’s own market comparable; the DEQ settlement appears to be double-counted; regulatory costs are treated as fully non-recurring against sector norms; and the owner-compensation normalization ignores transition overlap and a real replacement-CEO cost.  
              The largest customer’s contract expires before close.         PNR (21.4% of FY2024 revenue, $18.7 million) operates under an MSA expiring March 31, 2025 — six to nine months before the expected Q4 2025 close.  
              The growth thesis is PFAS-dependent and aggressive.         PFAS is projected to grow 125.8% in FY2025 (~3x the achievable company-level rate per sector data and Thornfield’s GreenWorks analog) and drives ~64% of incremental FY2024–FY2026 revenue; contracted PFAS backlog is not disclosed.  
              Free cash flow is negligible on a normalized basis.         Reported FCF is only $2.0–$2.8 million against $12.1 million EBITDA, and if maintenance capex is normalized to the sector 5–7% range (vs. the CIM’s 3.4%), FCF falls to roughly $0–$1 million. A buyer is paying a high FCF multiple for EBITDA that does not currently convert to cash.  
              Key-person and capacity risk.         Founder/CEO/68% owner Randall Oakes (age 61) exits within 18–24 months; PE headcount just fell from three to two (January 2025), constraining the remediation and PFAS sign-off capacity that the growth plan requires.  
              Data integrity.         The CIM and the Customer Summary & Backlog Report disagree on the identity of five of the top ten customers, the segments served by three of the top five, and the relationship start dates of four of the top five — despite identical dollar amounts and subtotals.

        4. Company and Investment Thesis (Balanced View)  
          Genuine strengths (to be preserved in the IC narrative):  
              Integrated, 16-year platform.         Three complementary segments — Environmental Remediation ($48.2M, 55.2%), Industrial Cleaning ($27.8M, 31.8%), Emergency Response ($11.3M, 12.9%) — with cross-selling and a single-source value proposition for industrial customers.  
              Real regulatory moat.         Oregon DEQ Contractor License ENV-2009-04821; hazardous waste transporter permits in all four states (HWT-OR-2009-3847, HWT-WA-2015-6192, HWT-ID-2018-2204, HWT-CA-2019-8831); EPA Generator ID ORD-093124785; licensed PEs required for remediation sign-off. These credentials take years to assemble and are a genuine barrier to entry.  
              Reported economics are within sector norms.         Reported FY2024 EBITDA margin of 13.9% sits inside the 10–15% sector range, indicating a reasonably well-managed operation (not a turnaround).  
              Genuine growth, even discounting PFAS.         Core industrial cleaning has grown ~11–15% annually; the 12.5% headline CAGR is real, even if PFAS-flattered (see Finding R-4).  
              Safety and qualifications.         EMR of 0.87 (better than industry average); ISNetworld and Avetta qualifications required for refinery access.  
              Backlog and retention.         $42.6 million contracted backlog; stated 90% MSA renewal rate; no top-10 customer lost in five years (subject to verification — see Finding C-5).  
              Sector tailwinds and fund fit.         PFAS regulation, state enforcement, and infrastructure spend are durable demand drivers; CES fits Fund III’s industrial/environmental services focus, and Thornfield’s GreenWorks and Allied Waste experience provides direct operating playbooks.  
          The thesis depends on three things that the sell-side materials do not yet substantiate:         (i) PFAS revenue scaling to $14.0 million in FY2025 from a $6.2 million base with no disclosed contracted backlog; (ii) renewal of the PNR MSA (21.4% of revenue) that expires before close; and (iii) a clean transition of founder-led customer relationships and PE sign-off capacity. The findings below quantify the risk in each.

         7. Findings — Quality of Earnings and Adjusted EBITDA  
      The seller’s FY2024 Adjusted EBITDA bridge adds $5.0 million (29% of the $17.1 million total) to reported EBITDA of $12.1 million. Each adjustment is scrutinized below.  
       Q-1. Aggregate adjustments are aggressive and several are overstated (HIGH)  
      $5.0 million of add-backs on $12.1 million of reported EBITDA is high for this sector (the Lindgren memo flags adjusted margins above 18–20% as warranting scrutiny; sector adjusted range is 15–18%). At least four of the six adjustments are overstated or misclassified (Q-2 through Q-5). A buyer-defensible Adjusted EBITDA is approximately             $14.1 million–$15.2 million         (central ~$14.6 million), roughly $1.9 million–$3.0 million below the seller’s $17.1 million. At 8.0x–10.0x, that is             $15 million–$30 million of enterprise value         at stake in the bridge alone.  
        Q-2. Owner-compensation normalization ($1.9M) overstates near-term savings (HIGH)  
      The CIM adds back $1.9 million, computed as Randall Oakes’s total FY2024 compensation of $2.4 million (salary, bonus, benefits, and         “    personal expenses allocated to the Company    ”    ) less an assumed replacement-CEO cost of $0.5 million. Problems: (a) the $2.4 million explicitly includes             personal expenses allocated to the Company         — these are a true add-back, but the replacement-CEO salary of $0.5 million is a real, ongoing cash cost that persists; (b) Oakes intends to remain for 18–24 months post-close in a transitional/advisory capacity, so the buyer will likely pay             both         Oakes (transition compensation)             and         a new CEO during the overlap, eroding the near-term saving; (c) the $0.5 million replacement assumption is unverified.             Defensible run-rate add-back:         approximately $1.0 million–$1.4 million (personal expenses plus excess owner cash comp above a market CEO package), a reduction of ~$0.5 million–$0.9 million.             Action:         QoE to split the $2.4 million into true personal/non-business expenses vs. compensation; confirm replacement-CEO cost; model transition overlap into year-one projections.  
        Q-3. Related-party lease add-back ($0.8M) contradicts the CIM’s own market comparable (HIGH)  
      CES leases its HQ at 4200 Industrial Way from Mr. Oakes personally at $1.4 million/year; the CIM normalizes to a $0.6 million         “    market    ”         rate and adds back $0.8 million.             But the CIM’s own Section IV.C states that                 “        specialized environmental staging facilities commanding rates of $1.0 million to $1.2 million annually for properties of comparable acreage and infrastructure.        ”         The HQ is precisely such a facility — a purpose-built environmental operations center with a permitted wash rack, secondary containment, roll-off staging, and soil stockpile areas (Section VIII.A). Using generic         “    industrial yard space    ”         at $0.6 million to justify the add-back is inconsistent with the CIM’s own comparable for the property’s actual specialized use.             Defensible add-back:         approximately $0.2 million–$0.4 million (the excess of $1.4 million over a $1.0–$1.2 million specialized-facility rate), a reduction of ~$0.4 million–$0.6 million. Further, post-close the buyer must either renegotiate the lease with a departing Oakes (who may seek to monetize the real estate) or purchase the property at fair market value — in which case the rent add-back is irrelevant and replaced by owned-asset depreciation.             Action:         obtain an independent MAI appraisal of the HQ as a specialized environmental facility; structure the real estate as a separate purchase or a market lease as a closing condition (see Finding RE-1).  
        Q-4. DEQ settlement appears double-counted in the $1.2M regulatory add-back (HIGH)  
      The CIM adds back $1.2 million for the August 14, 2024 Oregon DEQ matter ($875,000 settlement + $325,000 legal fees). The Exhibits, however, show         “    Other Income / (Expense)    ”         of $(875) in FY2024 — i.e., the $875,000 settlement is classified             below operating income    , where it reduces pre-tax income but             does not reduce EBITDA    . If the settlement is below the line, it was never subtracted from EBITDA, so adding it back inflates Adjusted EBITDA by $875,000. Only the $325,000 of legal fees (presumably in operating expenses) would be a legitimate EBITDA add-back.             Consequence:         potential $875,000 overstatement of Adjusted EBITDA — at 8.0x–10.0x, $7.0 million–$8.8 million of enterprise value.             Action:         QoE to confirm the income-statement classification of the $875,000 settlement and the $325,000 legal fees against the audited financials; recompute the add-back on the correct basis.  
        Q-5. Regulatory costs are treated as fully non-recurring against sector norms (HIGH)  
      Independent of the classification issue (Q-4), treating the full $1.2 million as         “    one-time    ”         conflicts with sector reality. Per the Lindgren memo and Thornfield portfolio data, recurring regulatory and compliance costs for a company of CES’s size run             $200,000–$500,000 per year         (GreenWorks: a $380K         “    one-time    ”         add-back proved recurring at ~$275K/year; Allied: ~$310K/year recurring with a $840K spike year). QoE best practice is to normalize to an ongoing run-rate and add back only the excess. If a $300,000 run-rate is appropriate, the defensible add-back is the excess over $300,000 — not the full $1.2 million. Combined with Q-4, the defensible regulatory add-back is likely             $0.3 million–$0.7 million    , a reduction of ~$0.5 million–$0.9 million versus the seller’s $1.2 million.             Action:         request CES’s complete regulatory enforcement history for the past 10 years; establish an ongoing regulatory cost run-rate; add back only the excess.  
        Q-6. Phantom-unit SBC ($0.3M) is a real cash cost at closing, not a run-rate add-back (HIGH)  
      The CIM adds back $0.3 million of non-cash phantom-unit stock-based compensation and states that         “    all outstanding phantom units are expected to be settled in cash at closing.    ”         Non-cash SBC is a standard EBITDA add-back, but here the units convert to             cash at closing         — a real use of cash that reduces net proceeds to the buyer. The total cash settlement liability is             not disclosed    .             Consequence:         (a) the $0.3 million should be removed from run-rate Adjusted EBITDA (it is real compensation, just non-cash until settlement); (b) the total settlement amount is a net-debt-like item / transaction expense that must be quantified and deducted in the equity bridge.             Action:         request the phantom-unit plan document, outstanding unit count, strike/value formula, and the estimated cash settlement at closing; model it as a closing use of funds.  
        Q-7. Adjusted EBITDA CAGR of 20.8% is inflated by non-recurring add-backs (MEDIUM)  
      The CIM (Appendix B) states Adjusted EBITDA grew at a 20.8% CAGR (FY2021 $9.7M → FY2024 $17.1M),         “    outpacing revenue growth.    ”         Reported EBITDA CAGR is 17.8% ($7.4M → $12.1M). The gap is driven by add-backs that grew from $2.3 million (FY2021) to $5.0 million (FY2024), with the $1.2 million regulatory add-back appearing only in FY2024. On a like-for-like basis (excluding one-time regulatory and relocation items), margin expansion is more modest.

       1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY  
      This memorandum reports the findings of our review of the Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (Phase I ESA) and supporting environmental documents produced in connection with Cornerstone Industrial Partners LLC’s proposed acquisition of the approximately 14.7-acre former manufacturing/warehouse complex at 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo, Ohio (the         “    Property    ”    ) from Breckenridge Manufacturing Co. (    “    Seller    ”         or         “    Breckenridge    ”    ). The controlling transaction documents — the Asset Purchase Agreement (APA) dated September 1, 2024 and the Great Lakes Capital Bank (    “    Lender    ”    ) environmental due diligence requirements letter dated September 10, 2024 — both unambiguously identify the Toledo Property as the subject of the proposed acquisition.  
      We reviewed eight (8) documents.             Three of those eight documents do not pertain to the Property at all         — they concern a different property located at 2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, Ohio (Montgomery County), owned by Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc. This is addressed as Issue 1 and is the single most important structural finding in the production: a portion of the environmental due-diligence record relates to a different transaction and must not be relied upon for the Toledo acquisition.  
      For the Toledo Property itself, the Greenfield Environmental Consultants Inc. Phase I ESA (dated October 25, 2024, ASTM E1527-21) identified two recognized environmental conditions (RECs): (i) former trichloroethylene (TCE) vapor degreasing operations (1961–1992) with an abandoned, leaking waste-TCE drum; and (ii) former hexavalent chromium/nickel electroplating operations (1965–2004) with visible staining and an in-ground sump containing liquid with a sheen. In our review, the ESA             under-classified the environmental condition of the Property in at least three material respects    : it failed to identify the off-site tetrachloroethylene (PCE) groundwater plume migrating onto the Property from the adjacent former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility as a REC (contrary to the Lender’s express requirement); it failed to classify two unregistered, undocumented underground storage tanks (USTs) as RECs; and it failed to classify the former outdoor drum storage area (the location of a documented 2003 Ohio EPA Notice of Violation) as a REC.  
      The combined environmental profile — 30+ years of TCE degreasing, 39 years of hexavalent chromium electroplating with no hazardous waste manifests for any year before 2004, an off-site chlorinated-solvent plume in a state-designated groundwater management zone (GMZ), and two unaccounted-for USTs — triggers the Lender’s enhanced (    “    REC    ”    ) due-diligence requirements (Phase II ESA, ≥$5,000,000 pollution legal liability insurance, possible remediation escrow, possible remediation plan, and a vapor intrusion assessment) and raises serious questions about the adequacy of the Seller’s indemnity package (a $1,750,000 cap and a 3-year survival period) and the accuracy of the Seller’s environmental representations (Sections 4.12 and 4.13 of the APA).  
      We identified             thirty-four (34) distinct issues    , rated Critical (5), High (8), Moderate (10), Low (7), and Non-Issue/Informational (4) for the Toledo Property, plus an additional nine (9) issues within the misfiled Dayton-property documents (flagged separately so they are not erroneously applied to the Toledo acquisition). A prioritized recommendations section appears at Part 10.

        4. DETAILED ISSUE ANALYSIS — TOLEDO PROPERTY  
       CRITICAL ISSUES  
       Issue 1 — Three of eight produced documents pertain to a different property (Dayton/Valtec), not the Toledo acquisition  
          Severity: CRITICAL  
          Description and evidence.         The proposed acquisition is unambiguously the Toledo Property. The APA (dated September 1, 2024) defines the         “    Property    ”         as 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo, Lucas County, Ohio 43611 (Lucas County Parcel Nos. 18-04562-001, -002, -003), sold by Breckenridge Manufacturing Co. to Cornerstone for $8,750,000, with a $6,125,000 loan from Great Lakes Capital Bank and a December 15, 2024 closing date. The Lender letter (September 10, 2024) confirms the same Property, loan number GLCB-2024-08173, and parties.  
      Three of the eight documents in the production do not concern this Property. They concern             2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, Ohio 45414 (Montgomery County)    , owned by             Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc.    , a different seller, a different city and county, a different purchase price ($4,200,000), and a different environmental consultant (Terravista Environmental Consulting Inc.):         - The Terravista Phase I ESA is dated             April 18, 2025         — after the Toledo closing date of December 15, 2024 — and is prepared to the older             ASTM E1527-13         standard, not the E1527-21 standard required by the APA and the Lender.         - The Redland Environmental Services UST Closure Report (November 12, 2004) is prepared         “    for Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc.    ”         at         “    2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, Ohio 45414.    ”         - The GeoSearch database search summary (March 28, 2025) lists the         “    Target Property    ”         as         “    2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, Ohio 45414,    ”         Tax Parcel ID R72-04410-0025, prepared for Terravista.  
          Consequence.         Reliance on the Dayton documents for the Toledo acquisition would be inappropriate and potentially misleading: the two properties have different histories, different contaminants, different regulatory statuses, and different sellers. Conflating them could cause the Buyer to (a) credit a regulatory closure (the Dayton 2011 VAP NFA letter) that does not exist for the Toledo Property, (b) rely on an ESA prepared to the wrong ASTM standard, or (c) misallocate remediation responsibilities between two unrelated sellers. The presence of these documents also raises a document-production/quality-control concern: either the wrong files were produced for this transaction, or Cornerstone is pursuing a separate, parallel acquisition of the Dayton property that has not been disclosed in the Toledo transaction documents.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Confirm in writing with Cornerstone’s deal team whether the Dayton documents were produced in error or pertain to a separate transaction. (b) Segregate the Dayton documents from the Toledo due-diligence file and mark them         “    DO NOT RELY — DIFFERENT PROPERTY.    ”         (c) Do not cite the Dayton UST closure report, the Dayton VAP NFA letter, or the Dayton GeoSearch summary in any analysis of the Toledo Property. (d) The Dayton documents are nevertheless reviewed separately in Part 9 of this memorandum because they were produced and contain their own issues, but that review is expressly limited to the Dayton property.  
      
        Issue 2 — Off-site PCE groundwater plume (Lake Erie Dry Cleaning) migrating onto the Property; Property within a state-designated Groundwater Management Zone; not classified as a REC by the ESA  
          Severity: CRITICAL  
          Description and evidence.         The former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility at 4900 Lakeshore Industrial Drive is located immediately north of and hydraulically upgradient from the Property, approximately 120 feet from the northern property boundary. It is listed on the Ohio EPA Master Sites List under VAP Identification No. NW-0923 with a confirmed tetrachloroethylene (PCE) groundwater contamination plume. The documented facts are:  
          A 2014 investigation identified PCE at             87 µg/L         at monitoring well             MW-7    , located approximately             120 feet north         of the Property boundary. The applicable Ohio generic numerical groundwater standard for PCE is             5 µg/L         (OAC § 3745-300-08, Table 1). The detected concentration             exceeds the standard by a factor of approximately 17.4    .  
          The documented plume migration direction is             south-southeast — toward the Property         — consistent with regional groundwater flow.  
          A Covenant Not to Sue was issued in 2018 under the Ohio VAP for the             dry cleaner parcel only    ; it             does not address off-site migration         of the PCE plume onto the Property.  
          An environmental covenant recorded under Ohio Rev. Code § 5301.82 restricts groundwater use and requires a sub-slab vapor mitigation system             on the dry cleaner parcel only    .  
          The Ohio EPA response (September 28, 2024) expressly states:         “        no investigation of PCE concentrations in groundwater beneath the Subject Property has been conducted, and the lateral and vertical extent of PCE plume migration onto or beneath the Subject Property has not been delineated.    ”  
          The Property is located within a             designated Groundwater Management Zone (GMZ)         established by Ohio EPA in connection with this plume. The GMZ         “    may impose restrictions on new groundwater withdrawals, new well installations, and certain land uses.    ”  
          The Greenfield ESA did not classify this condition as a REC.         The ESA describes the plume in detail in its database review (§ 5.1.1) and EDR appendix narrative, but the Findings section (§ 9.0) lists only REC-1 (TCE) and REC-2 (electroplating). The PCE plume appears in neither the REC, CREC, HREC, nor de minimis categories. This is a direct failure to comply with the Lender’s express instruction:         “    The Phase I ESA must evaluate whether any off-site contamination, including groundwater plumes, may be migrating toward or impacting the subject Property,             and any such conditions must be identified as recognized environmental conditions    ”         (Lender letter § 2).  
          Consequence.         This is, in our judgment, the most significant environmental condition affecting the Property, and it was not carried forward as a REC. The consequences are multiple:  
              Lender compliance.         The ESA, as written, does not satisfy the Lender’s § 2 requirement. The Lender’s environmental counsel (Whitfield & Crane LLP) is independently reviewing the ESA for AAI compliance and may reject it or require supplementation.  
              Vapor intrusion.         PCE is a volatile organic compound (VOC). A chlorinated-solvent plume beneath an occupied or occupiable structure presents a vapor intrusion risk. The Property has been used for warehousing since December 2019; warehouse workers and future occupants may be exposed. The Lender’s § 3.B.5 vapor intrusion assessment is triggered.  
              CERCLA contiguous property owner defense.         The Property is receiving (or is threatened with receiving) contamination from an adjacent property. The contiguous property owner defense under CERCLA § 107(q), 42 U.S.C. § 9607(q), requires, among other things, that the owner conducted all appropriate inquiries, is not the source of the contamination, provides cooperation and assistance to parties conducting cleanup, and takes reasonable steps to prevent or reduce exposure. The ESA’s failure to identify the plume as a REC undermines the Buyer’s ability to demonstrate that it conducted all appropriate inquiries and took reasonable steps. The GMZ may also impose land-use restrictions with which the Buyer must comply as a continuing obligation.  
              Regulatory/land-use restrictions.         The GMZ may restrict groundwater withdrawals, well installations, and certain land uses on the Property — a potential institutional control that affects redevelopment (the Buyer’s planned use is warehousing/light industrial).  
              Remediation cost exposure.         If PCE has migrated beneath the Property, the Buyer could face investigation, monitoring, and possible remediation costs, and could be drawn into the ongoing Lake Erie Dry Cleaning VAP matter.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Require the environmental consultant to issue a supplemented or amended ESA that expressly evaluates the off-site PCE plume and classifies it as a REC (or, at minimum, a CREC/vapor intrusion condition) consistent with the Lender’s requirement. (b) Commission a Phase II ESA that includes groundwater monitoring wells on the Property (downgradient of MW-7) to delineate whether PCE has migrated beneath the Property, plus a vapor intrusion assessment (sub-slab soil gas and/or indoor air) for occupied structures. (c) Request the complete Lake Erie Dry Cleaning VAP file (VAP ID NW-0923) and the GMZ establishment documentation from Ohio EPA to understand the full extent and any use restrictions. (d) Evaluate whether the GMZ constitutes an         “    institutional control    ”         or         “    activity and use limitation    ”         that must be disclosed and that may breach Seller’s representation in APA § 4.12(e). (e) Confirm the Buyer’s eligibility for, and document the conditions of, the contiguous property owner defense.  
      
        Issue 3 — REC-1: Former TCE vapor degreasing operations (1961–1992) and abandoned, leaking waste-TCE drum  
          Severity: CRITICAL  
          Description and evidence.         Trichloroethylene (TCE) was used as a vapor degreasing solvent in the northwest corner of the main manufacturing building from 1961 through approximately 1992 — a period of more than 30 years. The evidence of a release includes:  
          A photoionization detector (PID) reading of             42.3 ppm         was recorded at a crack in the floor slab of the former degreasing area. Background PID readings elsewhere in the building were generally             less than 0.5 ppm    . The 42.3 ppm reading is indicative of VOC vapors emanating from beneath the slab.  
          Visible dark staining penetrates the concrete floor across an approximately 4,000-square-foot area; interior walls show staining up to approximately three feet above floor level.  
              No closure documentation, decommissioning records, or soil/groundwater confirmation sampling         were located for the degreasing area following the reported cessation of TCE use in 1992.  
          An abandoned             55-gallon drum labeled                 “        WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE        ”         was observed on the ground surface behind the maintenance/utility building. The drum is approximately half full, with a rusted bottom and evidence of past leakage. Stained soil (approximately 3 ft × 3 ft, dark and oily, vegetation absent) was observed beneath the drum. Seller’s principal stated the drum had been         “    there for years    ”         and was         “    probably from the early ’90s.    ”  
          A             March 14, 1988 chemical spill         involving an         “    unknown quantity of degreasing solvent    ”         from an overturned drum was documented in City of Toledo Fire Department records. Cleanup was performed by Breckenridge employees; no follow-up inspection was conducted. (Notably, this spill does             not         appear in the federal ERNS database or the Ohio EPA Spills database — see Issue 13.)  
          Consequence.         TCE is a chlorinated solvent and a known human carcinogen. It is highly mobile in groundwater and persists in the environment. The combination of 30+ years of use, an elevated PID reading at a floor crack, undocumented closure, a leaking waste drum with stained soil, and a documented 1988 spill strongly indicates a release of chlorinated solvents to soil and likely to groundwater in the northwest portion of the main building and behind the maintenance building. Vapor intrusion into occupied warehouse space is a significant concern. Remediation of chlorinated solvent contamination (soil and groundwater) is typically expensive and time-consuming, and may exceed the Seller’s $1,750,000 indemnity cap (Issue 6).  
          Recommendation.         (a) Phase II ESA: advance soil borings through the floor slab in the former degreasing area and behind the maintenance building; install groundwater monitoring wells; analyze for VOCs including TCE and its degradation products (cis-1,2-dichloroethylene, vinyl chloride). (b) Immediately characterize and properly dispose of the abandoned waste-TCE drum and the stained soil beneath it; do not close until the drum is removed. (c) Conduct a vapor intrusion assessment for the former degreasing area and any occupied structures, per Lender § 3.B.5. (d) Preserve the 1988 Fire Department spill record as evidence relevant to Seller’s § 4.12(a) representation.  
      
        Issue 4 — REC-2: Former hexavalent chromium/nickel electroplating operations (1965–2004)  
          Severity: CRITICAL  
          Description and evidence.         Electroplating operations using hexavalent chromium and nickel plating solutions were conducted from 1965 through 2004 in the south wing of the main manufacturing building — approximately 39 years. The evidence includes:  
          Prominent             blue-green staining         on the concrete floor and lower walls (to approximately four feet), consistent with chromium residues; the concrete shows pitting, etching, and chemical degradation from prolonged exposure to acidic/alkaline plating solutions.  
          An             in-ground concrete sump         (approximately 3 ft × 3 ft × 3 ft deep) in the center of the former electroplating room contains approximately             four inches of liquid with a visible iridescent sheen         and a greenish tint. The source and composition were not determined (no sampling was performed, consistent with Phase I scope).  
              Hazardous waste disposal documentation exists only for the final year of operations (2004):         six manifests documenting disposal of             14,200 pounds         of waste under RCRA codes             D007 (chromium)         and             F006 (electroplating sludge)    , transported by Tri-County Environmental Hauling Inc.         No manifests, biennial reports, or disposal records exist for any of the preceding 39 years (1965–2003).         The Ohio EPA response confirms this:         “    Ohio EPA has no hazardous waste manifests, biennial reports, or annual hazardous waste generation reports on file for this facility for any reporting year other than 2004.    ”  
          A             2003 Ohio EPA compliance inspection         (July 17, 2003) documented improper storage of hazardous waste — specifically,         “    multiple rusted and visibly leaking 55-gallon drums in an outdoor storage area located on the east side    ”         containing electroplating sludge and spent chromium-bearing wastewater, with staining on the ground beneath and around the drums. A Notice of Violation (File No. NW-2003-0471) was issued August 12, 2003 and resolved through a Director’s Final Findings and Orders (consent order) (see Issues 9 and 11).  
          Consequence.         Hexavalent chromium is a potent toxin and known carcinogen; nickel compounds are also regulated. The 39-year operating history, visible chromium residues, liquid waste with sheen in the sump, the near-total absence of hazardous waste disposal documentation (raising the question of how 39 years of chromium-bearing waste was managed), and the documented 2003 regulatory violation collectively indicate a likely release of chromium and related metals to the subsurface beneath and around the former electroplating room. The absence of disposal records for 1965–2003 is itself a significant compliance and liability concern: it suggests either undocumented on-site disposal, undocumented off-site shipment, or recordkeeping failures, any of which could expose the Buyer to successor liability or regulatory scrutiny. Remediation of metals-contaminated soil and groundwater, plus sump liquid characterization and disposal, is likely to be costly.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Phase II ESA: characterize the sump liquid (metals, VOCs, pH, other parameters); advance soil borings through and beneath the floor slab in the former electroplating room; install groundwater monitoring wells; analyze for total and hexavalent chromium, nickel, and other plating-related metals. (b) Investigate the 1965–2003 waste management history; request any remaining corporate records (see Issue 13) and interview former employees if locatable. (c) Obtain and review the 2003 consent order and compliance certification (see Issue 11). (d) Budget for sump liquid characterization and disposal as an immediate post-closing (or pre-closing) action.  
      
         HIGH ISSUES  
       Issue 5 — Lender         “    REC    ”         due-diligence requirements triggered (Phase II, PLL insurance ≥$5M, possible escrow, possible remediation plan, vapor intrusion assessment)  
          Severity: HIGH  
          Description and evidence.         The Lender letter § 3.B provides that if the Phase I ESA identifies one or more RECs, the Lender         “    will             not         fund the Loan based solely on the Phase I ESA.    ”         The Borrower must then satisfy             all         of the following before closing:         1.             Phase II ESA         (ASTM E1903-19) with quantified remediation cost estimates (best-case, most-likely, worst-case) prepared by a licensed professional engineer.         2.             Pollution Legal Liability (PLL) insurance         from a carrier rated A- or better, with limits of not less than the greater of (x) the worst-case remediation cost estimate or (y)             $5,000,000    , term of not less than             10 years    , with the Bank as additional insured and loss payee. The Lender states this is         “        non-negotiable    ”         and that it         “    will not rely solely on seller indemnification provisions.    ”         3.             Remediation cost escrow         at Great Lakes Capital Bank equal to             125% of the most-likely remediation cost estimate    , if that estimate exceeds             $250,000    .         4.             Remediation plan         (by a licensed environmental professional, submitted to/approved by Ohio EPA where applicable, completed within 36 months of closing), if estimated costs exceed             $500,000         under either the most-likely or worst-case scenario.         5.             Vapor intrusion assessment         (per Ohio EPA/U.S. EPA guidance) and, if warranted, a vapor mitigation plan implemented prior to or concurrently with closing, if VOCs are identified in soil or groundwater at or beneath the Property.  
      The Greenfield ESA identified two RECs (TCE and hexavalent chromium). Our review identifies at least three additional conditions that should have been classified as RECs (the off-site PCE plume (Issue 2), two unregistered USTs (Issue 8), and the former drum storage area (Issue 9)). The Lender’s § 3.B requirements are therefore triggered, and the scope of the required Phase II and cost estimates is broader than the ESA’s two-REC findings would suggest.  
          Consequence.         The Borrower faces substantial additional cost and time before closing: a Phase II ESA with quantified cost estimates; a ≥$5,000,000, 10-year PLL policy (premiums can be significant); a possible escrow at 125% of most-likely remediation costs; a possible Ohio-EPA-approved remediation plan; and a vapor intrusion assessment/mitigation plan. The Lender also reserves the right (§ 3.C) to decline the loan if the worst-case remediation cost exceeds 50% of appraised value, if the Property is on the NPL, if there is an active RCRA corrective action order, or if unresolved data gaps prevent adequate risk characterization (see Issue 13).

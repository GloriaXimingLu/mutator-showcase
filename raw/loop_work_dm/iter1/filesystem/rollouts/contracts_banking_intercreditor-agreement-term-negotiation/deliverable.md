       I. Introduction and Scope of Engagement  
      You have asked Kesswick, Tanaka & Moreau LLP (    “    KTM    ”    ) to prepare proposed compromise language for the four contested provisions of the Intercreditor Agreement (the         “    ICA    ”    ) to be entered into among Cartwright National Bank, N.A. (as First Lien Agent, the         “    1L Agent    ”    ), Ridgeline Capital Partners, LP (as Second Lien Agent, the         “    2L Agent    ”    ), and Meridian Industrial Holdings, Inc. (the         “    Borrower    ”    ), dated as of July 31, 2025.       The four contested provisions are: (1) the Standstill Period (Section 3.01(b)); (2) the Second Lien Purchase Right (Section 5.01); (3) Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness (Section 2.03(c)); and (4) the Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds (Section 4.02). For each provision, this memorandum sets out (a) a concise statement of the compromise reached, (b) the full contractual drafting proposed to replace the bracketed language in the current working draft of the ICA, and (c) a negotiation rationale explaining why the compromise falls within the flexibility each side has signaled, supported by the KTM market survey of 42 first lien/second lien intercreditor agreements closed between January 2022 and December 2024 (the         “    Market Survey    ”    ) and by quantified economic analysis.  
          This memorandum presents the four compromises as a single, indivisible package.         As you instructed, and as both agents have independently urged, the concessions on each provision are linked to concessions on the others. The package is calibrated so that, taken as a whole, it preserves the structural protections the 1L Agent’s syndicate has identified as essential while preserving the bargained-for economic protections the 2L Agent’s lender group requires given the thin equity cushion. Unbundling the package — accepting some provisions and rejecting others — would upset that balance and is likely to reopen issues that the package resolves. The proposed language should therefore be offered to Cartwright (through Aldgate & Pryor LLP) and to Ridgeline (through Sternfeld Yates & Carroll LLP) only on the basis that all four provisions are accepted together.  
          Urgency.         As of the date of this memorandum, 34 days remain until the July 31, 2025 drop-dead date for closing, and the existing senior secured facility being refinanced matures on August 15, 2025 — only 15 days after the drop-dead date. There is no realistic prospect of an extension of the existing facility. The compromise package below is designed to be credible and closeable within that window without a further round of positional bargaining.

        IV. The Single Indivisible Package — Why the Four Provisions Are Linked  
      Both agents have asked that the open issues be resolved as a package, and you have so instructed. The linkage is not merely procedural; it is substantive. The four provisions allocate distress risk between the lien levels, and the allocation is only coherent when the provisions are read together:  
             Standstill and Purchase Right are linked.         The standstill gives the 1L Agent a protected period to work out the credit; the purchase right gives the 2L Agent a mechanism to acquire the senior position and control the workout if the 1L Agent does not. A longer standstill increases the value of the purchase right to the 2L Agent (because it extends the period during which the 2L Agent may be blocked from remedies and therefore may need to buy the senior position to regain control). The compromise pairs a 180-day standstill (longer than the 2L Agent wanted) with early termination triggers and a non-default-rate, optional-revolver purchase price (more favorable to the 2L Agent than the 1L Agent’s opening). The 2L Agent accepts the longer standstill because the triggers and the purchase-right terms preserve its exit; the 1L Agent accepts the triggers and the purchase-right concessions because it gets the 180-day period and par for funded debt.  
             Purchase Right and Refinancing are linked.         The purchase right is the 2L Agent’s distress exit; the refinancing permission is its going-concern exit. Both must remain viable. Capping refinancing principal at 0% (the 1L Agent’s opening) would make a market refinancing impracticable, channeling the 2L Agent toward the purchase right as its only exit and increasing the likelihood that the purchase right is exercised in a manner the 1L Agent finds disruptive. Allowing a 10% cap limited to transaction costs keeps the refinancing exit viable, reducing pressure on the purchase right.  
             Refinancing and Proceeds Waterfall are linked.         The restrictiveness test and MFN right on refinancing protect the 1L Agent against covenant loosening that could destabilize the capital structure; the hard sweep on extraordinary asset sales protects the 1L Agent against collateral leakage. Together they bound the 1L Agent’s downside on the two paths by which the 2L position can change (refinancing or asset sales) without the 1L Agent’s consent.  
             Standstill and Proceeds Waterfall are linked.         The 2L Agent’s concern that a long standstill permits collateral value erosion is partially addressed by the proceeds waterfall: the hard sweep on extraordinary sales ensures that large asset sales during any workout reduce the 1L balance (benefiting the 2L Agent’s residual position) rather than leaking to the Borrower, and the residual-to-2L right ensures the 2L Agent captures value once the 1L is satisfied.  
      For these reasons, the package should be presented and accepted as a whole. The remainder of this memorandum sets out the compromise, the contractual drafting, and the rationale for each provision.  
        V. Compromise Clause 1 — Standstill Period (Section 3.01(b))  
       A. The Compromise  
      A             180-day         Standstill Period, measured from delivery of the Blockage Notice, with             four narrow, objectively verifiable early termination triggers    : (A) the 1L Agent has commenced an Enforcement Action and then ceased active prosecution for 60 consecutive days; (B) the 1L Agent has consummated or commenced a court-supervised process to dispose of substantially all of the Collateral; (C) a Section 363 sale motion seeking to sell substantially all of the Collateral free and clear is filed without the 2L Agent’s consent; and (D) a DIP Financing order is entered over the 2L Agent’s objection. The package also introduces a             Blockage Notice cap    : no more than two Blockage Notices in any rolling 12-month period, and aggregate standstill days not to exceed 360 in any rolling 12-month period.  
        B. Proposed Contractual Drafting (replacing the bracketed Section 3.01(b))  
      Following the occurrence and during the continuance of a Second Lien Default, the Second Lien Agent shall not exercise any rights or remedies with respect to the Collateral for a period of one hundred eighty (180) days following delivery by the First Lien Agent to the Second Lien Agent of a written notice (a         “        Blockage Notice    ”    ) specifying the Second Lien Default giving rise thereto (the         “        Standstill Period    ”    ). The Standstill Period shall terminate prior to the expiration of such one hundred eighty (180)-day period upon the occurrence of any of the following Early Termination Events, each of which shall be objectively verifiable:  
          (A)         the First Lien Agent shall have commenced an Enforcement Action with respect to the Collateral and shall thereafter have ceased to take any material steps to prosecute such Enforcement Action for a period of sixty (60) consecutive days;  
          (B)         the First Lien Agent shall have consummated, or shall have commenced a court-supervised process to dispose of, substantially all of the Collateral;  
          (C)         a motion shall have been filed under Section 363 of the Bankruptcy Code seeking authority to sell substantially all of the Collateral free and clear of Liens without the consent of the Second Lien Agent; or  
          (D)         an order shall have been entered approving DIP Financing over the objection of the Second Lien Agent.  
      Upon the occurrence of any Early Termination Event, the Second Lien Agent may exercise any and all rights and remedies available to it under the Second Lien Loan Documents and applicable law with respect to the Collateral, subject to Section 3.03 and the priority and subordination provisions of this Agreement. The First Lien Agent shall not deliver more than two (2) Blockage Notices during any rolling twelve (12)-month period, and the aggregate number of days during which one or more Standstill Periods are in effect during any rolling twelve (12)-month period shall not exceed three hundred sixty (360) days. The Standstill Period shall be tolled, and shall not run, during the pendency of any Insolvency Proceeding with respect to the Borrower or any loan party. If the Second Lien Default specified in a Blockage Notice is cured or waived in accordance with the Second Lien Loan Documents, the Standstill Period shall immediately terminate, and any subsequent exercise of remedies by the Second Lien Agent shall require delivery of a new Blockage Notice and the commencement of a new Standstill Period.  
      The existing Section 3.02 (Permitted Actions During Standstill) and Section 3.03 (Actions Following Expiration of Standstill) remain in effect unchanged.  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Falls within the 1L Agent’s flexibility.         In his without-prejudice email of June 26, 2025, Thomas Buchanan III disclosed that Cartwright’s credit committee has approved a minimum standstill of 150 days as a hard floor, and that         “    the syndicate could live with something in the 150- to 180-day range.    ”         He further stated that         “    if a longer standstill — closer to 180 days — were paired with carefully defined early termination triggers, that is something the committee would be willing to consider,    ”         provided the triggers are         “    narrow and objectively verifiable.    ”         The proposed 180-day period is at the top of the range the 1L Agent identified as acceptable, and each of the four triggers is objectively verifiable (each turns on an observable event — a court filing, a consummated disposition, an entered order, or a 60-day cessation of documented enforcement activity) rather than on a subjective judgment. The package deliberately omits the trigger categories the 1L Agent’s position letter expressly rejected — early termination upon a decline in collateral value, upon a third-party foreclosure, upon the 1L Agent’s failure to commence enforcement within a specified period of the default, and upon the filing of an involuntary petition — and instead adopts the trigger categories that the Market Survey shows are the most common and that the 2L Agent itself identified. The Blockage Notice cap addresses the 1L Agent’s concern about indefinite blocking only indirectly (it limits, rather than expands, the 1L Agent’s own blocking power) and is therefore not a concession the 1L Agent need resist.  
          Falls within the 2L Agent’s flexibility.         In her without-prejudice email of June 26, 2025, Naomi Oduya stated that         “    Ridgeline can accept up to a 180-day standstill, but only if the ICA includes meaningful early termination triggers. This is a firm condition and not something we can decouple from the duration.    ”         The proposed 180-day period is exactly the 2L Agent’s stated ceiling, and the four triggers are drawn from the categories the 2L Agent identified (cessation of active enforcement; disposition of substantially all collateral) and from the Market Survey’s most common trigger types. The Blockage Notice cap directly addresses the 2L Agent’s stated concern that an extended standstill could be         “    deployed tactically to indefinitely block Second Lien enforcement    ”         and could         “    convert the standstill from a temporary pause on 2L enforcement into a de facto preclusion of Second Lien rights.    ”  
          Market support.         The Market Survey of 42 broadly syndicated 1L/2L ICAs closed January 2022–December 2024 shows a median standstill of 180 days (mean 172.9; range 90–270; 25th percentile 150; 75th percentile 202.5). Seventy-six percent (76%) of deals (32 of 42) include early termination triggers, and 25 of 42 deals pair a standstill of 180 days or longer with early termination triggers — making the         “    180 days + triggers    ”         combination the clear market-standard pairing. The most common trigger types in the survey are: liquidation commenced (29 deals), credit bid by 1L (22 deals), filing of involuntary petition (13 deals), DIP financing approved (13 deals), and sale motion filed without 2L consent (9 deals). The proposed triggers (B), (C), and (D) map directly onto         “    liquidation commenced,    ”         “    sale motion without 2L consent,    ”         and         “    DIP financing approved.    ”         Trigger (A) (cessation of active enforcement) is not separately tabulated in the survey but is the specific trigger the 2L Agent requested and is objectively verifiable. Deal-007 in the survey — a non-sponsor industrial credit with a $475,000,000 1L facility, $150,000,000 2L facility, and $625,000,000 total secured debt, structurally comparable to Meridian — used a 150-day standstill; the proposed 180 days is modestly more protective of the 1L Agent than that comparable. Deal-038 ($470,000,000 1L / $155,000,000 2L / $625,000,000 total, sponsor-backed industrials) used a 240-day standstill with a comprehensive trigger set, confirming that high-leverage industrial deals at this size routinely pair extended standstills with triggers.  
          Reconciliation of the competing market surveys.         The 2L Agent’s position letter cites an internal survey of 37 sponsor-backed ICAs reporting a median standstill of 120 days and a mode of 90 days. The KTM Market Survey (42 deals, including both sponsor-backed and non-sponsor transactions across all sectors) reports a median of 180 days. Both the 1L Agent’s counsel and the engagement materials acknowledge the 180-day median from the KTM survey. The discrepancy is largely a function of sample composition: the 2L Agent’s survey is restricted to sponsor-backed deals and reports a lower central tendency, while the KTM survey is broader. The proposed 180 days is consistent with the KTM median and is within the 2L Agent’s expressly stated 180-day ceiling, so it is defensible against either dataset. We recommend the parties rely on the KTM Market Survey as the common reference because it is the dataset both agents have already acknowledged in the negotiation.  
          Economic quantification.         The 180-day period is 90 days shorter than the 1L Agent’s 270-day opening and 90 days longer than the 2L Agent’s 90-day opening; it sits at the midpoint of the two opening positions and at the market median. The economic significance of the duration is asymmetric. For the 2L Agent, the floating-rate structure (SOFR + 625 bps, currently 11.58% all-in) means interest continues to accrue on the $150,000,000 Second Lien Term Loan B during the standstill at the applicable rate (including default-rate interest under the 2L Credit Agreement, which adds 200 bps), so the 2L Agent is compensated for the passage of time — approximately $4,345,000 of interest accrues on the 2L facility over 180 days at the non-default rate ($150M × 11.58% × 180/360), and more at the default rate. For the 1L Agent, the 180-day period provides approximately six months to pursue a restructuring, orderly asset sale, or refinancing — within the six-to-nine-month range the 1L Agent’s position letter identifies as the minimum reasonably necessary for a business of Meridian’s size and complexity. The early termination triggers cap the 1L Agent’s downside: if the 1L Agent itself ceases active enforcement (trigger A) or moves to dispose of substantially all collateral (trigger B), the standstill has served its purpose and the 2L Agent’s remedies are restored. The Blockage Notice cap (2 per rolling 12 months; 360 aggregate days) ensures the standstill cannot be used serially to block 2L remedies indefinitely — a protection the 2L Agent specifically requested and one that has no economic cost to the 1L Agent so long as the 1L Agent is genuinely pursuing a workout.  
         VI. Compromise Clause 2 — Second Lien Purchase Right (Section 5.01)  
       A. The Compromise  
      A             par         purchase price for funded First Lien Obligations (100% of outstanding principal plus accrued interest plus fees and expenses), with accrued interest calculated at the             non-default contract rate         (SOFR + 250 bps),             not         the default rate. Assumption of unfunded Revolver commitments is             optional         at the 2L purchaser’s election (not mandatory); if the 2L Agent does not elect to assume, the unfunded Revolver commitments terminate upon consummation of the purchase. The exercise trigger is an             Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement         (occurring and continuing). The FMV/dealer-poll alternative is dropped.  
        B. Proposed Contractual Drafting (replacing the bracketed Section 5.01)  
      At any time following the occurrence and during the continuance of an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement, the Second Lien Agent (on behalf of the Second Lien Lenders) shall have the right (but not the obligation) to purchase all (but not less than all) of the First Lien Obligations (the         “        Second Lien Purchase Right    ”    ) by paying to the First Lien Agent in immediately available funds a purchase price (the         “        Purchase Price    ”    ) equal to the sum of: (i) one hundred percent (100%) of the aggregate outstanding principal amount of all funded First Lien Obligations (including all amounts outstanding under the First Lien Term Loan A and all drawn amounts under the First Lien Revolver); plus (ii) all accrued and unpaid interest thereon, calculated at the non-default contract rate applicable under the First Lien Credit Agreement (i.e., SOFR plus 250 basis points), through and including the date of purchase; plus (iii) all fees, costs, expenses (including reasonable and documented fees and disbursements of counsel), indemnification obligations, breakage costs, yield protection amounts, and all other amounts then due and payable or owing under the First Lien Loan Documents, whether or not invoiced. As a condition to the purchase, the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) may, at its sole election, assume all unfunded commitments under the First Lien Revolver by executing and delivering to the First Lien Agent an assumption agreement in form and substance reasonably satisfactory to the First Lien Agent, pursuant to which the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) shall irrevocably commit to fund any subsequent draws by the Borrower on terms no less favorable to the Borrower than those set forth in the First Lien Credit Agreement (including the same interest rate, commitment fee, borrowing base provisions, maturity, and conditions to borrowing). If the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) does not elect to assume the unfunded commitments under the First Lien Revolver, such unfunded commitments shall terminate upon consummation of the purchase, and the First Lien Revolver lenders shall have no further obligation to fund advances thereunder; provided that the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent shall cooperate in good faith to arrange for the continuation or replacement of revolving credit liquidity for the Borrower, it being understood that nothing herein shall obligate the Second Lien Agent or any Second Lien Lender to assume any unfunded revolving credit commitment. The purchase shall be on a par, non-recourse, as-is, where-is basis, without representation or warranty of any kind by the First Lien Agent or any First Lien Lender (other than representations as to the authority of the assigning lender to assign and the enforceability of the transfer).  
      The existing Section 5.02 (Mechanics of Purchase) and Section 5.03 (Effect of Purchase) remain in effect unchanged.  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Falls within the 1L Agent’s flexibility.         The 1L Agent’s counsel stated in his June 26 email that         “    par purchase price for funded debt is non-negotiable. The syndicate will not accept any formulation that permits purchase below par.    ”         The compromise delivers par for funded debt and drops the FMV/dealer-poll alternative the 2L Agent had sought — a significant concession by the 2L Agent that the 1L Agent’s email acknowledged as         “    a significant concession on our part.    ”         On the two subsidiary points where the 1L Agent signaled flexibility, the compromise tracks the 1L Agent’s own signals: (a) the 1L Agent’s email stated that         “    there may be room to accept accrued interest calculated at the non-default contract rate — SOFR plus 250 basis points,    ”         and the compromise adopts the non-default rate; and (b) the 1L Agent’s email stated that on unfunded Revolver assumption         “    there may be room to soften this requirement, potentially making assumption of unfunded Revolver commitments optional rather than mandatory,    ”         and the compromise makes assumption optional. The exercise trigger (1L Event of Default) is the 1L Agent’s own proposed trigger.  
          Falls within the 2L Agent’s flexibility.         The 2L Agent’s counsel stated in her June 26 email that         “    Ridgeline acknowledges that par for funded debt may be acceptable as the Purchase Price for the 1L Obligations,    ”         but that this acceptance is         “    conditioned on the unfunded revolver assumption being optional, not mandatory,    ”         because Ridgeline’s fund limited partnership agreement (the Amended and Restated Agreement of Limited Partnership, dated as of March 1, 2021)         “    contains structural constraints that prevent the fund from taking on open-ended revolving credit commitment obligations.    ”         The compromise makes unfunded Revolver assumption optional, directly resolving the 2L Agent’s stated deal-breaker. The 2L Agent’s email also expressed willingness to         “    discuss whether accrued interest at the non-default contract rate (rather than the default rate the 1L side insists upon) could be part of the overall package    ”    ; the compromise adopts the non-default rate. The 2L Agent’s broader exercise triggers (2L Event of Default, or acceleration) are not adopted; the rationale is set out below.  
          Market support.         The Market Survey shows that 71% of deals (30 of 42) use a par purchase price mechanic (19% hybrid lesser-of-par-or-FMV; 10% pure FMV), confirming that par is the market majority and that the 2L Agent’s concession to par is consistent with market practice. On the interest-rate component, 69% of deals (29 of 42) exclude default-rate interest from the par calculation and use the non-default contract rate, while 90% of deals (38 of 42) include the non-default contract rate — making the non-default rate the clear market majority and the default rate a minority position (31%). On unfunded Revolver assumption, 62% of deals (26 of 42) make assumption optional, 24% mandatory, and 14% silent, with a clear trend toward optional assumption (77% of 2024 deals vs. 50% of 2022 deals). Deal-007 in the survey — the deal most structurally comparable to Meridian ($475M 1L / $150M 2L / $125M Revolver) — used         “    par for funded debt at non-default rate; optional revolver assumption,    ”         with an illustrative par purchase price of $484.3M, which is essentially identical to the compromise proposed here. On exercise triggers, 100% of deals include         “    acceleration of 1L    ”         (which flows from a 1L Event of Default), 74% include         “    commencement of enforcement action,    ”         45% include         “    payment default continuing beyond cure,    ”         and 38% include         “    bankruptcy filing    ”    ; a 1L Event of Default trigger is therefore squarely within the market range.  
          Why the 2L Agent’s broader triggers are not adopted.         The 2L Agent’s opening proposed that the purchase right be exercisable upon an Event of Default under either the First Lien or the Second Lien Credit Agreement, or upon acceleration. Permitting exercise upon a 2L-only Event of Default would allow the 2L Agent to acquire the senior position on the occurrence of a default that does not itself impair the 1L position — a result the 1L Agent is unlikely to accept and that is not well-supported by the survey (the survey’s triggers are uniformly tied to 1L acceleration, 1L enforcement, or bankruptcy, not to 2L-only defaults). Because the purchase right exists to allow the 2L Agent to protect its position by acquiring control of the senior debt in distress, tying the trigger to a 1L Event of Default (which is the scenario in which the senior position is itself in distress and the 2L Agent’s need to acquire it is genuine) is both market-standard and defensible to both sides. Acceleration of the 1L Obligations is subsumed within a continuing 1L Event of Default.  
          Economic quantification.         Using the capital structure summary’s illustrative purchase-price model (SOFR at 5.33%; 1L all-in non-default rate 7.83%; default rate SOFR + 450 bps = 9.83%; estimated fees and expenses $1,500,000):  
              Base case — non-default rate, 90 days, full draw:         $475,000,000 principal + $9,300,000 accrued interest (90 days) + $1,500,000 fees =             $485,800,000    .  
              Base case — default rate, 90 days, full draw:         $475,000,000 + $11,670,000 + $1,500,000 =             $488,170,000    .  
              Difference (default-rate increment), 90 days:         approximately             $2,370,000         (200 bps × $475,000,000 × 90/360). Over a 180-day period the difference is approximately $4,750,000; over 270 days approximately $7,125,000.  
      The non-default rate therefore saves the 2L Agent approximately $2.37 million per 90 days of accrual relative to the 1L Agent’s opening default-rate position — a meaningful but not windfall amount, and one that aligns with the market majority (69% of deals). The optional (rather than mandatory) Revolver assumption relieves the 2L Agent of a contingent obligation of up to $125,000,000 — the precise obligation Ridgeline’s fund LPA structurally prohibits it from assuming. If the 2L Agent elects not to assume, the unfunded Revolver commitments terminate on consummation of the purchase; the good-faught cooperation clause preserves the Borrower’s ability to arrange replacement revolving liquidity without imposing any obligation on the 2L Agent. The par purchase price of approximately $485.8 million (funded debt, 90-day non-default accrual) is within the range the 2L Agent’s counsel identified as acceptable and is the figure both agents have used in their illustrative calculations, which should facilitate agreement.  
         VII. Compromise Clause 3 — Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness (Section 2.03(c))  
       A. The Compromise  
      Three sub-issues, each resolved as follows:             (A) Principal cap:         10% (a maximum $15,000,000 increase on the current $150,000,000 Second Lien Term Loan B, i.e., up to $165,000,000), limited solely to refinancing-related fees, costs, premiums, and expenses.             (B) Maturity floor:         both tests — no earlier than 91 days after the First Lien Maturity Date (September 14, 2030) AND no earlier than the original Second Lien Maturity Date (June 15, 2031); because June 15, 2031 is later, the binding floor is June 15, 2031.             (C) Restrictiveness test:         a narrowed test limited to financial maintenance covenants (with a bright-line 6.50x total leverage floor), paired with an MFN right giving the 1L Lenders the benefit of any more favorable financial maintenance covenant in the refinancing documentation.  
        B. Proposed Contractual Drafting (replacing the bracketed Section 2.03(c))  
      “        Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness    ”         means indebtedness incurred to refinance, replace, or substitute the Second Lien Obligations, provided that:  
          (i)         the aggregate principal amount of such refinancing indebtedness shall not exceed one hundred ten percent (110%) of the aggregate principal amount of the Second Lien Obligations being refinanced (i.e., based on the current $150,000,000 Second Lien Term Loan B, a maximum of $165,000,000, reflecting a $15,000,000 permitted increase), provided that any such increase shall be limited solely to the payment of refinancing-related fees, costs, premiums, and expenses, including call premiums or make-whole amounts payable to the existing Second Lien Lenders, arranger and underwriting fees, original issue discount, and legal, advisory, rating agency, and other transaction costs incurred in connection with such refinancing;  
          (ii)         the stated final maturity date of such refinancing indebtedness shall be no earlier than the later of (x) the date that is ninety-one (91) days after the First Lien Maturity Date (i.e., no earlier than September 14, 2030) and (y) the Second Lien Maturity Date (i.e., no earlier than June 15, 2031);  
          (iii)         the financial maintenance covenants contained in such refinancing indebtedness shall not be more restrictive to the Borrower than the financial maintenance covenants contained in the Second Lien Credit Agreement as in effect on the Closing Date (for the avoidance of doubt and consistent with Section 7.08(d) of the Second Lien Credit Agreement, any total leverage maintenance covenant set at a level lower than 6.50 to 1.00 shall be deemed to be more restrictive);  
          (iv)         if any financial maintenance covenant contained in such refinancing indebtedness is more favorable to the lenders than the corresponding financial maintenance covenant in the First Lien Credit Agreement as in effect on the Closing Date, the First Lien Lenders shall automatically receive the benefit of such more favorable covenant (the         “        MFN Right    ”    ), and the First Lien Credit Agreement shall be deemed amended to incorporate such more favorable covenant without further action by any party; and  
          (v)         the holders of such refinancing indebtedness shall have become party to this Agreement (or a replacement intercreditor agreement on substantially similar terms) by executing and delivering a joinder agreement in form and substance reasonably satisfactory to the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent.  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Sub-issue (A) — Principal cap. Falls within the 1L Agent’s flexibility.         The 1L Agent’s opening position was a 0% cap. The 1L Agent’s counsel stated in his June 26 email that he was         “    not in a position to signal specific flexibility on this point today    ”         but noted the syndicate’s         “    willingness to discuss it further in the context of the overall package,    ”         and that         “    [t]his will inevitably be part of the broader trade.    ”         The compromise’s 10% cap is offered precisely as part of that broader trade: the 1L Agent receives, elsewhere in the package, a 180-day standstill (above its 150-day floor), par for funded debt, a narrowed restrictiveness test reinforced by an MFN right, and a hard sweep on extraordinary asset sales. In exchange, the 1L Agent accepts a 10% refinancing cap that is (a) the market median, (b) half of the 2L Agent’s opening 20% ask, and (c) confined to transaction costs rather than new money to the Borrower. The 1L Agent’s principal concern — that an upsize compresses the 25.78% equity cushion and increases Total LTV — is addressed by the limitation of the increase to fees, costs, premiums, and expenses: the increase covers the friction costs of refinancing (which are paid out to existing lenders, arrangers, and advisers) rather than incremental debt that remains outstanding against the Borrower’s enterprise value. A 10% increase to $165,000,000 would, if fully retained as outstanding debt, raise total secured debt to $640,000,000 and Total LTV to 100%; however, because the increase is limited to transaction costs that are largely paid out at closing, the incremental debt that remains outstanding is materially less than $15,000,000, and the 1L Agent retains the restrictiveness test and MFN right to protect against any covenant loosening that could accompany the refinancing.  
          Falls within the 2L Agent’s flexibility.         The 2L Agent’s counsel stated in her June 26 email that         “    Ridgeline can accept a 10% principal increase cap — that is, $15M — provided the increase is limited to refinancing-related fees and costs (e.g., OID, arrangement fees, and transaction expenses).    ”         The compromise adopts exactly that position: a 10% cap ($15,000,000) limited to refinancing-related fees, costs, premiums, and expenses. This is a meaningful move from the 2L Agent’s opening 20% ($30,000,000) position.  
          Market support.         The Market Survey shows a median principal increase cap of 10% (mean 8.9%); 52% of deals fall in the 1–10% range, 21% use 0%, 14% use 11–15%, and 12% use 16–20%. Critically, of the deals that permit any increase, 79% (26 of 33) limit the increase to fees, costs, and premiums only — exactly the limitation the compromise adopts. Deal-007 (the Meridian-comparable deal) used a 10% cap ($15M) limited to fees/costs with an MFN right on financial covenants — identical in structure to the compromise. The trend is toward the 10%-cap-limited-to-fees formulation.  
          Economic quantification.         The $15,000,000 headroom is calibrated to the customary costs of refinancing a $150,000,000 second lien term loan, as the 2L Agent’s position letter itemized: (i) call premium — the 2L Credit Agreement provides for a prepayment premium of 2.00% in year one (on or before July 31, 2026), 1.00% in year two (after July 31, 2026 but on or before July 31, 2027), and par thereafter, so the maximum call premium is 2.00% × $150,000,000 = $3,000,000; (ii) arranger/underwriting fees of 200–350 bps of the committed amount = $3,000,000–$5,250,000; (iii) original issue discount of 1–3% = $1,500,000–$4,500,000; and (iv) legal, advisory, rating agency, and filing costs of approximately $1,000,000–$2,000,000. The aggregate of these customary costs is approximately $8,500,000–$14,750,000, which fits within the $15,000,000 cap. A 0% cap (the 1L Agent’s opening) would render any refinancing with non-trivial transaction costs impracticable — the 2L Agent’s point that it would         “    prohibit any refinancing that involves non-trivial transaction costs, which is to say, virtually every refinancing transaction in the market.    ”         The 10% cap therefore makes the refinancing exit viable without permitting a material upsize of junior secured debt.  
          Sub-issue (B) — Maturity floor. This is a non-issue and both tests are satisfied.         The 1L Agent proposes a floor of 91 days after the First Lien Maturity Date (September 14, 2030). The 2L Agent proposes a floor of the original Second Lien Maturity Date (June 15, 2031). June 15, 2031 is 274 days after the First Lien Maturity Date of June 15, 2030 — i.e., 183 days later than the 1L Agent’s 91-day floor. The 2L Agent’s proposed floor is therefore more restrictive (later) than the 1L Agent’s, and satisfies the 1L Agent’s minimum. The compromise adopts both tests (the later of the two controls), which gives the 1L Agent its 91-day-post-1L test (used in 93% of survey deals) and the 2L Agent its original-2L-maturity floor (used in 81% of survey deals). There is no scenario in which the 2L Agent’s position disadvantages the 1L Agent, because the 2L Agent’s floor is later. The compromise simply codifies both protections, with the binding floor being June 15, 2031.  
          Sub-issue (C) — Restrictiveness test + MFN. Falls within the 1L Agent’s flexibility.         The 1L Agent’s counsel stated in his June 26 email that         “    Cartwright is open to a narrowed restrictiveness test — one that would not require a blanket comparison but would instead focus on specifically enumerated provisions — provided it is combined with a most-favored-nation right for the benefit of the 1L lenders.    ”         The compromise adopts exactly that: a narrowed test limited to financial maintenance covenants (an enumerated, objective category), paired with an MFN right. The 1L Agent’s concern that a replacement 2L facility could introduce tighter covenants that trigger cross-defaults or destabilize the capital structure is addressed by the narrowed test (which prohibits financial maintenance covenants more restrictive than the existing 2L covenants, with a bright-line 6.50x total leverage floor drawn directly from Section 7.08(d) of the 2L Credit Agreement). The 1L Agent’s concern that a refinancing could introduce covenants more favorable to lenders that the 1L Agent does not share is addressed by the MFN right (which gives the 1L Lenders the automatic benefit of any more favorable financial maintenance covenant).  
          Falls within the 2L Agent’s flexibility.         The 2L Agent’s opening position was no restrictiveness test at all, on the ground that the ICA should not regulate the bilateral commercial terms of the 2L facility. The 2L Agent’s counsel stated in her June 26 email that Ridgeline is         “    open to discussing whether a narrowed restrictiveness test paired with an MFN right could work.    ”         The compromise addresses the 2L Agent’s principal objection to the 1L Agent’s opening blanket test — that it would give the 1L Agent         “    an effective veto over the commercial terms of the Second Lien facility    ”         because the standard is         “    inherently subjective    ”         — by (a) narrowing the test to financial maintenance covenants only (leaving pricing, OID, fees, affirmative covenants, negative covenants other than financial maintenance, events of default, and other commercial terms to the 2L Agent and the Borrower to negotiate bilaterally), and (b) making the financial-covenant test objective via the 6.50x bright line, so that no subjective 1L Agent judgment is required to determine compliance. The MFN right is limited to financial maintenance covenants, consistent with the 2L Agent’s framing of the existing 2L financial covenant (Total Leverage ≤ 6.5x) as the relevant benchmark.  
          Market support.         The Market Survey shows that 52% of deals use a narrowed restrictiveness test (33% blanket; 14% none). Every single deal that uses a narrowed test (22 of 22, 100%) pairs it with an MFN provision, and 82% of MFN provisions (18 of 22) are limited to financial covenants only. The trend is strongly toward the narrowed-test-plus-MFN combination: 38% of 2022 deals used it, rising to 65% of 2024 deals. The compromise is therefore the modal and trending market structure. The 6.50x bright line is drawn directly from Section 7.08(d) of the 2L Credit Agreement, which already deems any refinancing with a total leverage covenant below 6.50x to be more restrictive — so the compromise aligns the ICA with the 2L Credit Agreement’s own internal standard, reducing the risk of inconsistency between the two documents. A WAL (weighted-average life) requirement is not adopted; only 29% of survey deals include a WAL test, making it a minority position, and the maturity floor (sub-issue B) already provides the temporal protection a WAL test would offer.  
         VIII. Compromise Clause 4 — Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds (Section 4.02)  
       A. The Compromise  
      A             bifurcated         proceeds waterfall. For             ordinary-course         Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events with Net Cash Proceeds at or below             $20,000,000         individually (and not constituting an         “    Extraordinary Disposition    ”    ), the ICA defers to the First Lien Credit Agreement’s existing reinvestment and mandatory prepayment mechanics (the 365-day reinvestment period, the $5,000,000 individual and $15,000,000 annual aggregate prepayment triggers, and the 100% sweep of non-reinvested proceeds), preserving the Borrower’s reinvestment rights. For Asset Dispositions or Casualty Events with Net Cash Proceeds             exceeding $20,000,000         individually, or any             “        Extraordinary Disposition        ”         (sale of substantially all assets, sale of a material business unit/segment/subsidiary, or casualty/condemnation of a material facility), a             hard 100% sweep to the First Lien Obligations    , overriding reinvestment rights, until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations. Robust             anti-evasion         language (90-day aggregation, considering acquirer identity, asset nature/location, and timing/sequencing) prevents structuring sales below the $20,000,000 threshold.             Residual proceeds         flow to the Second Lien Obligations after the Discharge of First Lien Obligations (per the Section 4.01 waterfall), not back to the Borrower.             Reporting    : quarterly compliance certificate, prompt (5-Business-Day) notice of any event generating Net Cash Proceeds above $5,000,000, and an annual aggregate disposition report.  
        B. Proposed Contractual Drafting (replacing the bracketed Section 4.02)  
      All Net Cash Proceeds received by the Borrower or any other loan party in respect of any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event shall be applied as follows:  
          (a) Ordinary Course Dispositions.         With respect to any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event that is not an Extraordinary Disposition and the Net Cash Proceeds of which do not exceed $20,000,000 individually (an         “        Ordinary Course Disposition    ”    ), the Net Cash Proceeds shall be applied in accordance with the mandatory prepayment and reinvestment provisions of Section 2.05(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement, including the Borrower’s right to reinvest such Net Cash Proceeds in Permitted Reinvestment Assets during the 365-day reinvestment period set forth therein and the mandatory prepayment sweep of 100% of any Net Cash Proceeds not so reinvested, subject to the $5,000,000 individual threshold and the $15,000,000 annual aggregate threshold set forth in Section 2.05(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement. The First Lien Lenders shall receive the full benefit of their bargain under the First Lien Credit Agreement with respect to Ordinary Course Dispositions.  
          (b) Extraordinary Dispositions and Large Sales.         With respect to (i) any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event the Net Cash Proceeds of which exceed $20,000,000 individually, or (ii) any Extraordinary Disposition (each, a         “        Hard Sweep Event    ”    ), one hundred percent (100%) of the Net Cash Proceeds shall be applied to prepay the First Lien Obligations in accordance with the waterfall set forth in Section 4.01, and the reinvestment rights granted to the Borrower under Section 2.05(b)(iv) of the First Lien Credit Agreement shall be superseded by this Section 4.02(b) with respect to such Net Cash Proceeds; no reinvestment in replacement assets shall be permitted with respect to any such Net Cash Proceeds. No portion of such Net Cash Proceeds shall be available for prepayment of the Second Lien Obligations, for reinvestment by the Borrower, or for distribution to the Borrower or any other person, until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations shall have occurred.  
          (c) Definition of Extraordinary Disposition.         “        Extraordinary Disposition    ”         means (i) any Asset Disposition involving the sale, transfer, or other disposition of substantially all of the assets of the Borrower and its subsidiaries taken as a whole, (ii) any Asset Disposition involving the sale, transfer, or other disposition of any material business unit, operating segment, or subsidiary of the Borrower, or (iii) any Casualty Event affecting a material manufacturing facility, distribution center, or other material asset of the Borrower, in each case regardless of the amount of Net Cash Proceeds generated thereby.  
          (d) Anti-Evasion.         No Asset Disposition (and no series of related Asset Dispositions) shall be structured, timed, or otherwise arranged in a manner designed to avoid the $20,000,000 threshold set forth in Section 4.02(b). The First Lien Agent may, in its reasonable discretion, aggregate any Asset Dispositions occurring within a 90-day period if the First Lien Agent reasonably determines that such Asset Dispositions constitute a single plan or related series of transactions, and, in such event, the Net Cash Proceeds from all such aggregated Asset Dispositions shall be treated as Net Cash Proceeds from a single Asset Disposition for purposes of determining whether the $20,000,000 threshold has been exceeded. In making such determination, the First Lien Agent shall consider, among other factors, the identity of the acquirer or acquirers, the nature and location of the assets disposed of, and the timing and sequencing of the dispositions.  
          (e) Residual Proceeds.         After the Discharge of First Lien Obligations, any Net Cash Proceeds remaining after application in accordance with this Section 4.02 (including, with respect to Ordinary Course Dispositions, after expiration of the applicable reinvestment period and application of the mandatory prepayment sweep) shall be applied to the Second Lien Obligations in accordance with the waterfall set forth in Section 4.01, and shall not be distributed to the Borrower or any other person until the Second Lien Obligations have been paid in full.  
          (f) Reporting.         The Borrower shall (i) deliver to the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent a quarterly certificate of compliance setting forth all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events occurring during such fiscal quarter and the application of Net Cash Proceeds therefrom, (ii) deliver prompt written notice (and in any event within five (5) Business Days) of any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event generating Net Cash Proceeds in excess of $5,000,000, and (iii) deliver an annual aggregate disposition report setting forth an itemized list of all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events during such fiscal year and the application of Net Cash Proceeds therefrom.  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Falls within the 1L Agent’s flexibility.         The 1L Agent’s counsel stated in his June 26 email that         “    a hard sweep of proceeds from large and/or extraordinary asset sales is essential to the syndicate and is not subject to significant compromise. That is a firm requirement,    ”         but that         “    reinvestment rights for smaller, ordinary-course asset sales may be acceptable. The syndicate recognizes that the Borrower’s business requires routine asset turnover, and it would be counterproductive to strip out all reinvestment flexibility.    ”         He further proposed         “    a workable approach … for the ICA to defer to the 1L Credit Agreement’s existing reinvestment mechanics for ordinary-course sales below a defined threshold, while imposing a hard 100% sweep to 1L obligations for larger or extraordinary dispositions,    ”         and flagged that         “    any threshold distinguishing ordinary-course from extraordinary sales must be carefully drafted to prevent evasion — specifically, to guard against a large disposition being structured as a series of smaller transactions. Anti-evasion language will be essential.    ”         Finally, he stated that         “    the question of residual proceeds flowing to 2L after full 1L satisfaction … is less of a concern if the hard sweep for significant sales is preserved.    ”         The compromise implements each of these signals precisely: a hard 100% sweep on sales above $20,000,000 and on Extraordinary Dispositions; deferral to the 1L Credit Agreement’s reinvestment mechanics for ordinary-course sales at or below $20,000,000; robust anti-evasion language (90-day aggregation, mirroring the anti-evasion provision already in Section 2.05(b)(iii) of the 1L Credit Agreement); and residual proceeds to the 2L only after the Discharge of First Lien Obligations. The 1L Agent’s opening blanket 100% sweep (overriding all reinvestment) is softened only for ordinary-course sales below the threshold — the category the 1L Agent expressly conceded.  
          Falls within the 2L Agent’s flexibility.         The 2L Agent’s counsel stated in her June 26 email that         “    Ridgeline understands that hard sweeps on large or extraordinary asset sales may be necessary to protect the 1L group’s position,    ”         and that the 2L Agent’s         “    primary concern is that some form of 2L access to residual asset sale proceeds be preserved in the ICA,    ”         characterizing this as         “    symbolically important even if economically limited.    ”         She stated that         “    Ridgeline could live with the 1L reinvestment mechanics applying for ordinary-course sales … provided that the residual after 1L application flows to the 2L rather than back to the Borrower,    ”         and flagged the need for         “    anti-evasion language to protect against asset-stripping through a series of sub-threshold sales structured to stay below the $20M ordinary-course threshold.    ”         The compromise delivers each of these: the 2L Agent accepts the hard sweep on large and extraordinary sales (its acknowledged necessity); the 1L reinvestment mechanics are preserved for ordinary-course sales (protecting the Borrower’s operational flexibility, which the 2L Agent’s position letter emphasized); residual proceeds flow to the 2L after the 1L is satisfied rather than back to the Borrower (the 2L Agent’s minimum); and anti-evasion language protects against sub-threshold structuring (the 2L Agent’s flagged concern). The $20,000,000 ordinary-course threshold is the figure discussed informally among the parties and is the market median.  
          Market support.         The Market Survey shows that a 100% sweep to the 1L is universal (100% of 42 deals), so the compromise’s hard sweep on large/extraordinary sales is unimpeachable. Eighty-one percent (81%) of deals preserve a reinvestment right (34 of 42), and 90% include a hard sweep for large/extraordinary sales (38 of 42) — the bifurcated structure the compromise adopts is therefore the dominant market model. Seventy-nine percent (79%) of deals provide 2L access to residual proceeds (33 of 42), confirming that the residual-to-2L right is market-standard and not a 1L concession of concern. The ordinary-course threshold has a median of $20,000,000 (mean $19,700,000; range $10,000,000–$30,000,000), and 81% of deals exclude ordinary-course sales from the hard sweep; 97% of deals with an ordinary-course exclusion include an anti-evasion clause (33 of 34), typically at the same threshold. The reinvestment period is 365 days in 91% of deals that preserve reinvestment (31 of 34), matching the 1L Credit Agreement’s existing 365-day period. Deal-007 (the Meridian-comparable deal) used an ordinary-course threshold of $20,000,000 with an anti-evasion clause at the same level, a 365-day reinvestment period, a hard sweep for extraordinary sales, and 2L residual after full 1L paydown — structurally identical to the compromise. On reporting, 90% of deals require a quarterly compliance certificate, 79% require event-driven notice, and 38% require an annual aggregate report; the compromise adopts all three, matching the comprehensive reporting package in Deal-007.  
          Consistency with the First Lien Credit Agreement.         The compromise is designed to defer to, rather than override, the First Lien Credit Agreement’s existing Section 2.05(b) mechanics for ordinary-course sales. Section 2.05(b) of the 1L Credit Agreement already provides: a $5,000,000 individual threshold and a $15,000,000 annual aggregate threshold for the mandatory prepayment trigger; a 100% sweep of Net Cash Proceeds (net of reinvested amounts); a 365-day reinvestment period (with a Reinvestment Notice due within 10 Business Days); an anti-evasion provision permitting 90-day aggregation based on acquirer identity, asset nature/location, and timing/sequencing; and a $3,000,000 reporting threshold for Prepayment Event Notices. The compromise’s ordinary-course carve-out incorporates these mechanics by reference, so the First Lien Lenders receive exactly the bargain they struck in the 1L Credit Agreement for routine asset turnover. The ICA’s hard sweep applies only above the $20,000,000 threshold or to Extraordinary Dispositions — the category the 1L Agent identified as requiring a firm sweep. This avoids the legal and commercial concern, raised by both the 2L Agent and the Borrower, that a blanket ICA sweep would         “    amend the First Lien Credit Agreement through the back door of the intercreditor agreement.    ”  
          Economic quantification.         The $20,000,000 ordinary-course threshold represents approximately 22.4% of LTM EBITDA ($89,400,000), a level that accommodates the Borrower’s routine asset turnover (replacement of aging manufacturing equipment, upgrades to production lines, and responses to shifts in customer demand) without triggering the hard sweep — the operational flexibility the Borrower’s CEO, Sandra Koh-Wexler, has emphasized is essential to a precision industrial components manufacturer whose customers require continuity of supply. For the 1L Agent, the hard sweep on sales above $20,000,000 and on Extraordinary Dispositions protects the recovery margin: with enterprise value of $640,000,000 and first lien debt of $475,000,000, the margin of collateral coverage is $165,000,000, and any large asset sale that bypassed the 1L waterfall would erode that margin. The anti-evasion provision ensures the $20,000,000 threshold cannot be gamed by sequencing a large disposition as a series of sub-threshold sales — the specific risk both agents flagged. The residual-to-2L right has limited economic value in the near term (it bites only after the 1L is paid in full, which in the current capital structure requires substantial proceeds), but it preserves the 2L Agent’s ability to demonstrate to its investors and LPs that the intercreditor structure maintains a waterfall right after full 1L satisfaction — the         “    symbolic    ”         importance the 2L Agent identified.  
         IX. Cross-Cutting Observations and Package Balance  
      The four compromises are designed to be accepted or rejected together. The following observations summarize the overall balance and the principal interdependencies.  
          What the 1L Agent gains across the package.         (1) A 180-day standstill — 30 days above Cartwright’s disclosed 150-day credit-committee floor and 90 days above Ridgeline’s opening 90-day position — with triggers that are objectively verifiable and that omit the categories the 1L Agent’s position letter rejected. (2) Par for funded debt in the purchase right — the 1L Agent’s stated non-negotiable — with the FMV alternative dropped. (3) A refinancing principal cap of 10% (half the 2L Agent’s opening 20%) confined to transaction costs, plus a narrowed restrictiveness test reinforced by an MFN right that gives the 1L Lenders the benefit of any tighter financial covenant in a replacement 2L facility. (4) A hard 100% sweep on all large and extraordinary asset sales, with anti-evasion protection. Across the package, the 1L Agent’s core structural protections — lien priority, payment priority, a meaningful standstill, par recovery on a purchase, control over covenant loosening in a refinancing, and control over large asset-sale proceeds — are preserved or strengthened.  
          What the 1L Agent concedes across the package.         (1) Early termination triggers (which its opening position categorically refused), narrowed to four objectively verifiable events and paired with a Blockage Notice cap. (2) Accrued interest at the non-default rate rather than the default rate in the purchase price (a difference of approximately $2.37 million per 90 days of accrual). (3) Optional rather than mandatory unfunded Revolver assumption (relieving the 2L Agent of a contingent obligation its fund LPA structurally prohibits). (4) A 10% refinancing cap (vs. its opening 0%), limited to transaction costs. (5) A narrowed rather than blanket restrictiveness test. (6) Preservation of reinvestment rights for ordinary-course asset sales at or below $20,000,000, and residual proceeds to the 2L after the 1L is paid in full. Each concession is one the 1L Agent’s counsel expressly signaled as available in his June 26 email, and each is supported by the Market Survey majority practice.  
          What the 2L Agent gains across the package.         (1) Meaningful early termination triggers (its firm condition for accepting any standstill above 90 days), plus a Blockage Notice cap preventing indefinite blocking. (2) Accrued interest at the non-default rate and optional (not mandatory) unfunded Revolver assumption — resolving its stated deal-breaker arising from the Ridgeline fund LPA’s prohibition on open-ended revolving commitments. (3) A 10% refinancing cap sufficient to cover customary transaction costs, keeping the refinancing exit viable. (4) Freedom from a subjective blanket restrictiveness veto, with the financial-covenant test made objective via the 6.50x bright line. (5) Preservation of reinvestment rights for ordinary-course asset turnover and a residual proceeds right after 1L satisfaction. Across the package, the 2L Agent’s core economic protections — a viable purchase right, a viable refinancing exit, protection against indefinite enforcement blocking, and a residual waterfall right — are preserved.  
          What the 2L Agent concedes across the package.         (1) A 180-day standstill (double its opening 90-day position), though with triggers and a Blockage Notice cap. (2) Par for funded debt and the dropping of the FMV/dealer-poll alternative (a significant concession the 2L Agent’s counsel acknowledged). (3) A 10% cap (half its opening 20%), limited to transaction costs. (4) A narrowed restrictiveness test plus an MFN right (vs. its opening position of no test at all). (5) A hard 100% sweep on large and extraordinary asset sales (vs. its opening position of following the 1L reinvestment mechanics for all sales). (6) A 1L-Event-of-Default trigger for the purchase right (vs. its opening broader triggers including a 2L-only default).

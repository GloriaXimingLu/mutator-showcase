        IV. Findings and Reduction Calculations  
       Finding 1 — Unapproved Timekeeper: Nina Barros (Section 4.2)  
          Reduction: $23,275.00 (full write-off of 49.0 hours × $475).  
      Nina Barros, a fourth-year mid-level associate, is not on the Approved Staffing Plan. The Plan is explicit that         “    James Pettersson is the only mid-level associate assigned to this matter. No other mid-level associate is included on the approved staffing roster,    ”         and that any addition requires         “    prior written approval    ”         under Section 4.2.  
      The record shows the Firm attempted to obtain approval but did not succeed. On April 28, 2025, Karen Cho emailed Rachel Dominguez requesting Barros’s addition at         “    $475/hr    ”         for an estimated         “    40–50 hours per month.    ”         Pinnacle’s Designated In-House Counsel did             not         approve the request. Rachel Dominguez’s May 1, 2025 reply states verbatim:             “        Before we can move forward on adding Nina Barros, though, let me review this with Sandra and get back to you. I also want to check where things stand against the current budget and staffing plan on our end. I’ll try to circle back with you early next week.        ”         No subsequent written approval appears anywhere in the production. The Firm’s own Rate Schedule concedes the point, listing Barros’s status as         “    Pending Approval — Added May 2025    ”         with the note         “    Requested via email April 28, 2025; client response pending.    ”  
      Notwithstanding the absence of approval, Barros billed 49.0 hours ($23,275.00) across fifteen entries from May 1 through May 30, performing interrogatory research, deposition-transcript review, and motion-related research — precisely the work the April 28 email said she would do. The June 1 Case Status Report asserts Barros’s         “    involvement this month was consistent with the request submitted to Ms. Dominguez on April 28, 2025,    ”         but a             request         is not an             approval    . Section 4.2 is unambiguous:             “        Staffing changes submitted after the fact, or where the new timekeeper has already commenced work prior to receiving written approval, are subject to reduction or rejection of all fees attributable to the unapproved timekeeper, at Pinnacle’s sole discretion.        ”  
      The Firm also did not invoke the exigent-circumstances exception in Section 4.2. That exception requires notice         “    within twenty-four (24) hours    ”         and a retroactive approval request explaining the exigency. The third document production and the May deposition schedule were foreseeable — the Firm knew of both when it wrote the April 28 email — and the Firm gave no 24-hour notice. The exception is therefore unavailable.  
          Recommendation.         Write off all $23,275.00 attributable to Barros. If Pinnacle wishes to retain Barros prospectively, it should require a complete Section 4.2 staffing-change request (name, role, rate, reason, anticipated hours, and budget impact) and issue written approval before she bills further.

        Finding 8 — Monthly Budget Overage Without Pre-Approval (Section 7.3)  
          No additional dollar reduction (subsumed by the reductions above); violation noted.  
      The Approved Staffing Plan sets a monthly fee range of $95,000.00 to $165,000.00 and, per Section 7.3, a maximum payable without pre-approval of 115% of the high end, i.e., $165,000 × 1.15 =             $189,750.00    . Section 7.3 requires the Firm to notify the Designated In-House Counsel and obtain written pre-approval before monthly fees exceed that threshold.  
      Both the invoice’s stated fee total ($193,372.50) and the line-item total ($215,680.00) exceed the $189,750.00 threshold — by $3,622.50 and $25,930.00 respectively. No pre-approval was sought or given; the April 28 / May 1 correspondence addressed only the Barros staffing request, not a budget overage, and the June 1 Case Status Report contains no Section 7.3 overage notification. The Firm therefore violated the Section 7.3 notice and pre-approval requirement.  
      Section 7.3 permits Pinnacle to         “    reduce the invoice to the approved budget ceiling plus fifteen percent (15%)    ”         — i.e., to $189,750.00 — and provides that this remedy         “    does not limit Pinnacle’s right to apply other reductions.    ”         Because the substantive reductions in Findings 1–7 already bring the payable fee amount to $140,786.88, well below $189,750.00, the budget cap does not operate as an             additional         dollar reduction here. The violation is nonetheless recorded: the Firm exceeded the approved range and the 115% threshold without the required pre-approval, and Pinnacle retains the right to treat any future unapproved overage under Section 7.3.  
          Recommendation.         No additional reduction is required on this invoice, but Pinnacle should (i) notify the Firm in writing that the Section 7.3 pre-approval requirement was not satisfied, and (ii) require a Section 7.3 overage notification (projected total, reason, driving tasks/timekeepers, and revised estimate) before approving any future month that may exceed $189,750.00.

        Finding 10 — Over-Lawyering / Redundant Senior Review (Section 6.2) — Observation  
      Section 6.2 prohibits         “    over-lawyering    ”         —         “    multiple levels of attorney review of the same work product beyond what is reasonably necessary for quality control    ”         — and provides that         “    [a] single round of review by the responsible supervising attorney is presumptively reasonable; additional rounds of review or involvement of additional senior timekeepers require justification.    ”         The invoice reflects a recurring pattern of three-level review that exceeds the presumptively reasonable single supervisory round:  
              Interrogatory responses:         drafted by Pettersson (Entries 2, 6, 11), reviewed and revised by Cho (Entries 3, 7, 12), and then re-edited by Hargrove on May 27 (Entry 92:         “    Review and edit interrogatory responses prepared by J. Pettersson    ”    ). That is a third review level, performed by the lead partner, with no stated justification.  
              Motion to compel:         drafted by Pettersson (Entries 19, 29), reviewed by Cho (Entries 20, 30, 35), and reviewed again by Hargrove (Entries 32, 43). Hargrove’s Entry 43 cite-checking (Finding 6(a)) is particularly difficult to justify given that the approved junior associate was already cite-checking the same brief (Entry 47).  
              Deposition outlines (Liu and Krenshaw):         prepared by Pettersson, reviewed by Cho, and then conferenced with Hargrove, repeatedly.  
      No separate dollar reduction is assigned here because the redundant senior review is captured within the block-billed entries (Finding 4) and, in the case of Entry 43, the task-appropriateness reduction (Finding 6(a)).             Recommendation.         On resubmission, the Firm should either justify or remove the redundant senior review time. Going forward, Pinnacle should enforce the single-round-of-supervisory-review presumption and require justification for any additional senior review.  
        Finding 11 — Insufficient Time Narrative (Section 9.2) — Observation  
      Section 9.2 requires narratives         “    sufficiently detailed to allow Pinnacle to assess the nature, necessity, and reasonableness of the work performed    ”         and rejects generic descriptions such as         “    review documents.    ”         Most entries in this invoice are adequately described (Tate’s document-review entries, for example, specify Bates ranges). The principal exception is Entry 79 (Webb):             “        Review third batch of MedCore production documents for relevance and privilege        ”         — a generic description that does not identify the documents reviewed or the issues addressed, and that is indistinguishable from the routine first-level review the contract attorney was performing concurrently. This entry is reduced under Finding 6(b) for task appropriateness; if the Firm resubmits it, the narrative must be brought into Section 9.2 compliance.             Recommendation.         Reject any resubmitted entry that does not meet Section 9.2’s specificity standard.  
        Finding 12 — Billing Increments (Section 5.5) — Observation (Compliant)  
      Section 5.5 requires time in increments         “    of no greater than one-tenth (0.1) of an hour    ”         and rejects quarter-hour (0.25) increments. All 117 entries are recorded in 0.5-hour multiples, which are valid 0.1-increment multiples; no quarter-hour (0.25) entries were found, so no Section 5.5 rounding reduction applies. Pinnacle should note, however, that the uniform 0.5-hour granularity is coarse and may overstate time on short tasks; the Firm should record time in true 0.1-hour increments going forward.             No reduction.  
        Finding 13 — Partner Hours Exceed the Approved Monthly Range — Observation  
      The Approved Staffing Plan projects 15–25 hours/month for David Hargrove. He billed 38.5 hours in May — 13.5 hours (54%) above the high end of the approved range. The overage is nonetheless a flag: it helped drive the invoice over the Section 7.3 threshold, and a portion of the partner time was spent on tasks more appropriately delegated (document review, Entry 9; cite-checking, Entry 43).             No separate reduction; noted for budget-management follow-up.

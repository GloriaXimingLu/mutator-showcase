       I. Introduction and Scope  
      This memorandum catalogs the exhibits produced by Respondent Darren Kwan in response to Petitioner Melinda Torres-Kwan’s First Request for Production of Documents (Nos. 1–22), summarizes the financial figures reported in those exhibits, flags inconsistencies and omissions disclosed by cross-referencing the exhibits against one another, and recommends follow-up discovery. The production was transmitted by cover email from Respondent’s counsel, Raymond Fossett of Hargrove Fossett & Yates LLP, and consists of seven document sets (PROD-001 through PROD-007) together with a privilege log identifying four withheld items (PRIV-001 through PRIV-004).  
      The central takeaway of this review is that the Respondent’s Sworn Financial Declaration (PROD-001) is materially incomplete and, in several respects, directly contradicted by the very records Respondent simultaneously produced. The most significant discrepancies are: (1) an entirely undisclosed real property — a rental condominium at 1035 Elm Street, Unit C, titled solely in Respondent’s name and acquired during the marriage; (2) a $85,000 wire transfer moved out of Respondent’s brokerage account two weeks after the asserted date of separation to an account at an institution that appears nowhere in the Declaration; (3) an income presentation that substitutes cash distributions ($174,000) for actual pass-through K-1 income ($367,200), understating Respondent’s 2023 income by roughly $193,000 on that line alone; and (4) a set of recurring, unexplained transfers totaling approximately $14,600 to an unidentified individual (    “    Kiera M.    ”    ). Each of these, and the additional findings below, warrants targeted follow-up discovery.

        V. Flagged Inconsistencies, Omissions, and Deficiencies  
      Each finding below states the discrepancy, the supporting evidence (with exhibit and paragraph/Bates references), the consequence for Petitioner’s position, and the recommended follow-up (consolidated in Part VII).  
       A. Undisclosed real property and related income, expenses, and liabilities  
          Finding 1 — Undisclosed rental condominium at 1035 Elm Street, Unit C.         The 2023 tax return (PROD-002, Schedule E, Part I) reports rental real estate at 1035 Elm Street, Unit C, Fairview, Columbia 20147 — a single-family residential condominium rented 365 days with zero personal-use days, generating $25,200 in rents and $2,940 net income — and expressly states         “    Property titled in the name of Darren Kwan.    ”         The Meridian mortgage statements (PROD-005) confirm a loan (7842-0163-ELM) on that same property, solely in Respondent’s name, originated October 8, 2019, with a current principal balance of $241,281.19. The Sworn Financial Declaration, however, discloses only the marital residence at 418 Birchwood Lane and affirmatively represents in ¶ 23 that         “    Respondent does not hold any ownership interest, whether sole or joint, in any other real property.    ”         The Elm Street condominium is therefore an entirely omitted marital asset (acquired during the marriage, October 2019) and an omitted liability (the ~$241,281 mortgage).             Consequence:         A material marital asset and its encumbrance are absent from the Declaration, understating both the marital estate and Respondent’s liabilities and skewing any equitable-distribution analysis.             Follow-up:         Interrogatory demanding identification of all real property interests; request deed, title, and purchase documents for 1035 Elm Street; request the complete Schedule E and Form 4562 (depreciation) for the property.  
          Finding 2 — Undisclosed rental income.         The Declaration’s         “    Other Income    ”         section (¶ 19) states         “    None    ”         and expressly excludes         “    rental income.    ”         The tax return reports $25,200 in gross rents ($2,100/month) and $2,940 net rental income from 1035 Elm Street, and the FCB checking statements (PROD-004) show recurring         “    ACH Deposit — Tenant Rent Payment    ”         of $2,100.00 in April, May, June, and July 2024.             Consequence:         Income available for support and for characterization of the estate is understated; the rental stream also confirms the property is an active income-producing asset.             Follow-up:         Interrogatory on all rental income; request rent rolls, lease(s), and tenant payment records.  
          Finding 3 — Undisclosed Elm Street mortgage liability and mortgage expense.         The ~$241,281 Elm Street mortgage is omitted from the Declaration’s liability schedule (¶¶ 43–48, total $249,000), and the $1,680/month PITI is omitted from the monthly expense schedule (¶ 49). The FCB checking statements confirm Respondent pays both Meridian mortgages from his individual checking each month — $1,842.00 (Birchwood) and $1,680.00 (Elm Street).             Consequence:         Liabilities and monthly expenses are both understated.             Follow-up:         Request all Meridian loan documents for both properties (see Finding 5).  
        B. Income understatement and mischaracterization  
          Finding 4 — K-1 distributions reported as K-1 income.         The Declaration (¶¶ 15–16) reports         “    K-1 Income / Distributions    ”         of $174,000 and a total annual income of $359,000. The Schedule K-1 (PROD-002) shows Box 1 ordinary business income of $367,200 and Box 19a cash distributions of $174,000. The $174,000 figure used in the Declaration is the             distribution    , not the             income    ; the actual pass-through ordinary business income allocated to Respondent is $367,200 — a $193,200 difference. Because Respondent is the 60% managing member who, with the CEO, determines distributions, the pass-through income (not merely the cash distributed) is the figure relevant to a support analysis; the retained earnings at the entity level (Respondent’s 60% share = $193,200) remain his economic interest.             Consequence:         Respondent’s income is understated by approximately $193,200 on this line alone, materially affecting child-support and alimony calculations.             Follow-up:         Interrogatory requiring Respondent to state total 2023 and 2024 K-1 ordinary business income separately from distributions; request Form 1065 and entity financial statements.  
          Finding 5 —                 “        Other income: None        ”                 contradicted by the tax return.         Declaration ¶ 19 represents that Respondent has no interest, dividend, rental, royalty, or other income. The tax return reports taxable interest ($1,840, Schedule B, from Fairview Community Bank), ordinary dividends ($6,310, Schedule B, from the Crestline account ending 9956), net capital gain ($8,450, Schedule D, from Crestline), K-1 interest income ($1,240, K-1 Box 5), and net rental income ($2,940).             Consequence:         Multiple income sources are omitted.             Follow-up:         Interrogatory requiring a complete income accounting reconciled to the 2023 return and 2024 year-to-date records.  
          Finding 6 — Vehicle allowance not disclosed.         The W-2 (PROD-002, Box 14) reports a $9,600 vehicle allowance ($800/month employer stipend) from NovaBridge, not reflected anywhere in the Declaration’s income or expense sections.             Consequence:         Additional compensation omitted.             Follow-up:         Interrogatory on all non-W-2-box-1 compensation and benefits.  
          Finding 7 — Expense reimbursements not disclosed; possible personal items.         NovaBridge reimbursed Respondent $12,662 in H1 2024 (PROD-007), deposited to his personal checking (PROD-004). The Declaration does not disclose these reimbursements as income or cash flow. At least one reimbursed item is categorized         “    Technology Equipment — Personal Devices    ”         (tablet, smartphone, accessories, $2,150, 05/17/2024), suggesting personal-use items run through the business.             Consequence:         Cash flow understated; potential commingling of personal and business expenses.             Follow-up:         Request underlying receipts for all reimbursed items; interrogatory on personal-use items.  
          Finding 8 — Aggregate income understatement.         Combining the above, Respondent’s 2023 income attributable to him (W-2 $185,000 + K-1 ordinary business income $367,200 + K-1 interest $1,240 + net rental $2,940 + vehicle allowance $9,600, plus his share of joint investment income) is on the order of $565,000–$583,000, versus the $359,000 declared — an understatement of roughly $200,000+.             Consequence:         The income figure driving support is materially low.             Follow-up:         Retain a forensic accountant to reconstruct actual income.  
        C. Post-separation asset transfers and dissipation concerns  
          Finding 9 — $85,000 wire transfer to an undisclosed institution/account.         On June 15, 2024 — two weeks after the asserted June 1, 2024 separation date — Respondent initiated an $85,000 outgoing wire from the Crestline brokerage account (PROD-003) to         “    Harborstone Financial Group, account ending 2291    ”         (reference WT-2024-061524-0087), authorized by recorded telephone call. No account at Harborstone Financial Group appears in the Declaration’s account list (¶¶ 24–33), and ¶ 33 affirmatively states Respondent maintains no other financial accounts. The account essentially funded the wire by liquidating securities (35 shares of Crestline Strategic Equity Fund sold 06/10 for $3,199) and drawing down cash; the account fell from $468,695 (05/31) to $385,125 (06/30), a decline of $83,570 almost entirely attributable to the wire.             Consequence:         A substantial marital asset was moved post-separation to an undisclosed account, raising dissipation and asset-concealment concerns; the recipient and current disposition of the $85,000 are unknown.             Follow-up:         Subpoena Harborstone Financial Group for account ending 2291 (ownership, statements, current balance, subsequent transfers); interrogatory on the purpose, recipient, and authorization of the wire; request Crestline’s wire authorization records and recorded call.  
          Finding 10 — Brokerage value reported on a post-transfer date, not the date of separation.         The Declaration reports the Crestline balance as $387,450.00 (¶ 29), which is the Aug. 1, 2024 valuation — i.e.,             after         the $85,000 wire. ¶ 56 represents that all balances are         “    as of the date of separation … on or about June 1, 2024.    ”         The May 31, 2024 closing value was $468,695.00; using the post-transfer figure understates this marital asset by approximately $81,000 and conceals the outflow.             Consequence:         The marital estate is understated and the transfer obscured.             Follow-up:         Demand date-of-separation balances for every account; see Finding 9.  
          Finding 11 — Anomalous $14,644.60 401(k) contribution on the valuation date.         The FCB checking statement (PROD-004) shows a $14,644.60 ACH to         “    Pinnacle Retirement Services 401(k) — Early Month    ”         on 08/01/2024 — roughly ten times the regular $1,437.50 monthly contribution — on the very date used for the Declaration’s account balances. This single transaction reduces the checking balance to exactly $8,215.00 (the figure reported in the Declaration).             Consequence:         A large, timed shift of liquid cash into a retirement account on the cutoff date may be designed to reduce apparent liquid assets or to favorably recharacterize funds; at minimum it is unexplained.             Follow-up:         Interrogatory on the purpose and authorization of the 08/01/2024 contribution; request Pinnacle 401(k) statements showing the posting and any catch-up basis.  
          Finding 12 — Recurring unexplained transfers to                 “        Kiera M.        ”         The FCB checking statements (PROD-004) show recurring Venmo transfers to         “    Kiera M.    ”         totaling $14,600 over March–July 2024 ($2,800; $3,100; $2,500; $3,200; $3,000; average ~$2,920/month). No such transfers, and no person by that name, appear in the Declaration, and the amounts are not reflected in the monthly expense schedule.             Consequence:         Substantial recurring transfers to an unidentified third party are undisclosed and may constitute dissipation of marital funds or support to an undisclosed dependent/paramour.             Follow-up:         Interrogatory identifying         “    Kiera M.    ”         and the purpose of each transfer; subpoena Venmo records for Darren Kwan’s account.  
        D. Mortgage documentation mismatch  
          Finding 13 — Produced mortgage statements are for the undisclosed property, not the marital residence.         The cover letter describes PROD-005 as mortgage statements responsive to Request Nos. 9–10, and the Declaration’s only disclosed mortgage is the $218,400 joint mortgage on 418 Birchwood Lane (¶¶ 22, 44). The statements actually produced, however, are for 1035 Elm Street, Unit C (loan 7842-0163-ELM, sole borrower Darren Kwan, balance $241,281.19) — the property omitted from the Declaration.

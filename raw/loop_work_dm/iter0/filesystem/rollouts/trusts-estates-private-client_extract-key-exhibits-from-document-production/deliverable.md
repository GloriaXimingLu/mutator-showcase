       I. Purpose and Scope  
      This memorandum catalogs the exhibits produced by Respondent Darren Kwan in response to Petitioner Melinda Torres-Kwan’s First Request for Production of Documents (Nos. 1–22), summarizes the financial figures set forth in those exhibits and in Respondent’s Sworn Financial Declaration, flags the material inconsistencies, omissions, and discrepancies identified across the production, and recommends follow-up discovery. The production was transmitted by cover email from Respondent’s counsel, Raymond Fossett of Hargrove Fossett & Yates LLP, and consists of seven (7) document sets (PROD-001 through PROD-007) plus a summary privilege log.  
      This review is preliminary. Several categories of clearly relevant documents were not produced (joint account statements, the marital-residence mortgage file, NovaBridge entity-level records and Operating Agreement, and a formal privilege log), and a number of figures in the Sworn Financial Declaration cannot be reconciled to the underlying records. The findings below should be read as a roadmap for targeted follow-up discovery, not as final conclusions of fact.  
      
        II. Case Background  
      The parties were married on June 14, 2009, and have two minor children (Ethan, b. 2011; Sofia, b. 2014). Petitioner filed her Petition for Dissolution on September 3, 2024. Respondent is the Co-Founder and Chief Technology Officer of NovaBridge Solutions LLC, a Delaware LLC (formed January 15, 2016) taxed as a partnership, in which he holds a 60% membership interest; the remaining 40% is held by Jared Ostrowski (CEO). Petitioner is a high school art teacher with the Fairview Regional School District. The date of separation is contested on the face of Respondent’s own documents (see Section V.C).

         V. Flagged Inconsistencies, Omissions, and Discrepancies  
      Each finding below is rated for severity (HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW) based on its likely impact on equitable distribution, support, or the integrity of the production.  
       A. [HIGH] Undisclosed real property — 1035 Elm Street, Unit C — and its mortgage and rental income  
      The Sworn Financial Declaration, ¶23, states:         “    Respondent does not hold any ownership interest, whether sole or joint, in any other real property.    ”         The 2023 tax return (PROD-002, Schedule E, Part I) and the produced mortgage statements (PROD-005) directly contradict this. Schedule E reports a rental condominium at 1035 Elm Street, Unit C, Fairview, Columbia 20147,         “    titled in the name of Darren Kwan,    ”         rented 365 days, generating $25,200 in rents ($2,100/mo) and $2,940 of net rental income in 2023. The Meridian Home Lending mortgage statements (PROD-005) confirm a 30-year fixed mortgage (Loan No. 7842-0163-ELM, originated Oct. 8, 2019, original amount $279,000, 4.875%) on that property with a current principal balance of approximately $241,281 as of Aug. 1, 2024, and a monthly PITI of $1,680. The FCB checking statements (PROD-004) confirm both the $2,100/mo rent deposits (    “    ACH Deposit — Tenant Rent Payment,    ”         Apr.–Jul. 2024) and the $1,680/mo mortgage payment (    “    ACH Payment — Meridian Home Lending    ”    ).  
      This single omission affects three sections of the Declaration at once: (1)             Assets         — the Elm Street condominium is not listed; (2)             Liabilities         — the ~$241,281 Elm Street mortgage is not listed (the Declaration’s liability schedule shows only the $218,400 Birchwood mortgage); and (3)             Income         — ¶19 expressly denies any rental income. The property was acquired during the marriage (mortgage originated Oct. 2019) and is serviced with marital earnings, so it is presumptively marital despite sole title.             Consequence:         the marital estate is missing an entire real-property asset and its associated debt, and Respondent’s income is understated by the omitted net rental income.             Recommendation:         demand an appraisal and full title/chain-of-title for 1035 Elm Street, Unit C; obtain the complete mortgage payment history; and seek sanctions/fees for the sworn misstatement if not cured.  
        B. [HIGH] Income understatement — K-1 ordinary business income vs. distributions, and omitted investment/rental income  
      As detailed in Section IV.A, the Declaration reports $174,000 of         “    K-1 Income / Distributions    ”         but the tax return shows $367,200 of K-1 ordinary business income (Schedule K-1, Box 1; Schedule E, Part II). The $193,200 difference is income Respondent was taxed on but retained in NovaBridge (increasing his capital account). The Declaration also omits $1,840 of interest, $6,310 of dividends, $8,450 of capital gains, and $2,940 of net rental income — all reported on the joint return — and ¶19 expressly represents that none of these sources exist.             Consequence:         Respondent’s declared 2023 income ($359,000) understates his documented taxable income (~$571,740) by roughly $212,740, which directly affects child-support and maintenance calculations under the Columbia support guidelines.             Recommendation:         use the tax-return figures (not the Declaration) as the income baseline; obtain 2024 year-to-date K-1 information and NovaBridge interim financials; and consider a forensic accountant to reconstruct cash flow available for support, including retained earnings Respondent could cause the entity to distribute.  
        C. [HIGH] Conflicting date of separation — June 1, 2024 vs. August 1, 2024  
      The Sworn Financial Declaration (¶5 and ¶56) identifies the date of separation as         “    on or about June 1, 2024    ”         and states that all asset values are         “    as of the date of separation.    ”         Respondent’s own counsel, however, describes the withheld privileged communications in terms of         “    the parties’ separation on August 1, 2024    ”         (PRIV-001) and         “    post-separation August 1, 2024    ”         (PRIV-002). The Crestline interim valuation was generated         “    at the request of the account holder’s legal counsel    ”         as of Aug. 1, 2024 — the same date counsel uses for separation.             Consequence:         the date of separation is a foundational fact that drives (i) characterization of assets/income as marital or separate, (ii) the valuation date for equitable distribution, and (iii) the window for dissipation claims. A two-month shift materially changes the analysis of the June 15, 2024 $85,000 wire transfer, the Venmo payments, the condo lease, and the brokerage decline (see V.D, V.E, V.I).             Recommendation:         serve a targeted interrogatory requiring Respondent to state under oath the precise date of separation and the factual basis for it; reconcile the conflict with the privilege-log descriptions; and consider whether the Aug. 1 date (which post-dates the $85,000 transfer and most of the questioned spending) is the more accurate — in which case those transactions occurred during the marriage and are marital dissipation.  
        D. [HIGH] $85,000 outgoing wire transfer to an undisclosed account (Harborstone Financial Group, acct. 2291)  
      The Crestline statements (PROD-003) show an $85,000 outgoing wire transfer on June 15, 2024 to         “    Harborstone Financial Group,    ”         recipient account ending 2291 (Ref. WT-2024-061524-0087), authorized by the account holder. No account at Harborstone Financial Group is listed anywhere in the Sworn Financial Declaration, which purports to be a         “    complete list of all bank accounts, investment accounts, brokerage accounts, and retirement accounts    ”         (¶24, ¶33). This single transfer accounts for almost the entire decline in the Crestline account from $472,450 (Jan. 1, 2024) to $387,450 (Aug. 1, 2024). The transfer occurred two weeks after the Declaration’s stated separation date (June 1) and six weeks before counsel’s stated separation date (Aug. 1).             Consequence:         this is either an undisclosed account (an asset omission) or a transfer/dissipation of marital funds to a third-party institution, and it is the largest single movement of money in the production.             Recommendation:         immediately subpoena Harborstone Financial Group for all records relating to account ending 2291 (ownership, statements, beneficiaries, subsequent transfers); serve an interrogatory on the purpose and destination of the wire; and evaluate a dissipation/waste claim and a request for an accounting or injunction.  
        E. [HIGH] Recurring Venmo transfers to an unidentified third party (    “    Kiera M.    ”    )  
      The FCB checking statements (PROD-004) show recurring Venmo payments to         “    Kiera M.    ”         in escalating amounts: $2,800 (3/15), $3,100 (4/22), $2,500 (5/10), $3,200 (6/18), and $3,000 (7/14) — totaling $14,600 over five months, or roughly $33,000+ annualized. No such payee or expense appears anywhere in the Sworn Financial Declaration’s monthly expense budget, and         “    Kiera M.    ”         is not identified in any produced document.             Consequence:         recurring transfers of this size to an unidentified recipient are a classic indicator of dissipation of marital assets (e.g., support of a paramour, a hidden account, or an undisclosed obligation) and are wholly absent from the sworn expense schedule.             Recommendation:         serve an interrogatory requiring Respondent to identify         “    Kiera M.,    ”         state the purpose of each transfer, and produce any related communications; subpoena Venmo/PayPal records for Respondent’s account; and consider a dissipation claim for the amounts transferred during the marriage.

        H. [HIGH] NovaBridge interest unvalued; self-approved expense reimbursements;         “    personal devices    ”         reimbursement; undisclosed entity-level debt

The Declaration gives no value for the interest and does not mention the capital account or retained earnings. A 60% interest in an entity generating $612,000 of ordinary business income on $4,280,000 of gross revenue is likely worth substantially more than tax-basis book capital once goodwill and earning capacity are considered.

             “        Personal Devices        ”                 reimbursement mirroring a personal credit-card charge.           Consequence:         the largest marital asset is unvalued and its associated debt and reimbursement practices are undisclosed or inadequately controlled; the self-approval structure and the         “    personal devices    ”         pattern raise dissipation/conversion concerns.             Recommendation:         press for immediate production of the NovaBridge Operating Agreement (Request Nos. 16–22), entity-level financial statements, general ledger, bank statements, and the complete 2023 and 2024 K-1 packages; retain a business valuator; obtain all expense reports for 2023 and H2 2024; and subpoena NovaBridge bank records to trace reimbursement deposits.

## V. Flagged Inconsistencies, Omissions, and Discrepancies

Each finding below is rated for severity (HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW) based on its likely impact on equitable distribution, support, or the integrity of the production.

### A. [HIGH] Undisclosed real property — 1035 Elm Street, Unit C — and its mortgage and rental income

The Sworn Financial Declaration, ¶23, states: "Respondent does not hold any ownership interest, whether sole or joint, in any other real property." The 2023 tax return (PROD-002, Schedule E, Part I) and the produced mortgage statements (PROD-005) directly contradict this. Schedule E reports a rental condominium at 1035 Elm Street, Unit C, Fairview, Columbia 20147, "titled in the name of Darren Kwan," rented 365 days, generating $25,200 in rents ($2,100/mo) and $2,940 of net rental income in 2023. The Meridian Home Lending mortgage statements (PROD-005) confirm a 30-year fixed mortgage (Loan No. 7842-0163-ELM, originated Oct. 8, 2019, original amount $279,000, 4.875%) on that property with a current principal balance of approximately $241,281 as of Aug. 1, 2024, and a monthly PITI of $1,680. The FCB checking statements (PROD-004) confirm both the $2,100/mo rent deposits ("ACH Deposit — Tenant Rent Payment," Apr.–Jul. 2024) and the $1,680/mo mortgage payment ("ACH Payment — Meridian Home Lending").

This single omission affects three sections of the Declaration at once: (1) **Assets** — the Elm Street condominium is not listed; (2) **Liabilities** — the ~$241,281 Elm Street mortgage is not listed (the Declaration's liability schedule shows only the $218,400 Birchwood mortgage); and (3) **Income** — ¶19 expressly denies any rental income. The property was acquired during the marriage (mortgage originated Oct. 2019) and is serviced with marital earnings, so it is presumptively marital despite sole title. **Consequence:** the marital estate is missing an entire real-property asset and its associated debt, and Respondent's income is understated by the omitted net rental income. **Recommendation:** demand an appraisal and full title/chain-of-title for 1035 Elm Street, Unit C; obtain the complete mortgage payment history; and seek sanctions/fees for the sworn misstatement if not cured.

### B. [HIGH] Income understatement — K-1 ordinary business income vs. distributions, and omitted investment/rental income

As detailed in Section IV.A, the Declaration reports $174,000 of "K-1 Income / Distributions" but the tax return shows $367,200 of K-1 ordinary business income (Schedule K-1, Box 1; Schedule E, Part II). The $193,200 difference is income Respondent was taxed on but retained in NovaBridge (increasing his capital account). The Declaration also omits $1,840 of interest, $6,310 of dividends, $8,450 of capital gains, and $2,940 of net rental income — all reported on the joint return — and ¶19 expressly represents that none of these sources exist. **Consequence:** Respondent's declared 2023 income ($359,000) understates his documented taxable income (~$571,740) by roughly $212,740, which directly affects child-support and maintenance calculations under the Columbia support guidelines. **Recommendation:** use the tax-return figures (not the Declaration) as the income baseline; obtain 2024 year-to-date K-1 information and NovaBridge interim financials; and consider a forensic accountant to reconstruct cash flow available for support, including retained earnings Respondent could cause the entity to distribute.

### C. [HIGH] Conflicting date of separation — June 1, 2024 vs. August 1, 2024

A two-month shift materially changes the analysis of the June 15, 2024 $85,000 wire transfer, the Venmo payments, the condo lease, and the brokerage decline (see V.D, V.E, V.I). **Recommendation:** serve a targeted interrogatory requiring Respondent to state under oath the precise date of separation and the factual basis for it; reconcile the conflict with the privilege-log descriptions; and consider whether the Aug. 1 date (which post-dates the $85,000 transfer and most of the questioned spending) is the more accurate — in which case those transactions occurred during the marriage and are marital dissipation.

### H. [HIGH] NovaBridge interest unvalued; self-approved expense reimbursements; "personal devices" reimbursement; undisclosed entity-level debt

Several interrelated problems surround the NovaBridge interest, which is likely the single most valuable marital asset and is left entirely unvalued in the Declaration (¶37: "has not been formally appraised"; no value stated):

1. **No valuation; capital account and retained earnings ignored.** The K-1 (PROD-002) shows an ending tax-basis capital account of $675,800 and 2023 retained earnings at the entity level of $322,000 (Respondent's 60% share: $193,200). The Declaration gives no value for the interest and does not mention the capital account or retained earnings. A 60% interest in an entity generating $612,000 of ordinary business income on $4,280,000 of gross revenue is likely worth substantially more than tax-basis book capital once goodwill and earning capacity are considered.

2. **Undisclosed entity-level recourse debt.** The K-1 reports Respondent's share of partnership recourse liabilities of $36,400. This debt is not disclosed anywhere in the Declaration's liability schedule.

3. **Self-approved expense reimbursements with no co-member review.** The expense report (PROD-007) states that under §5.3 of the NovaBridge Operating Agreement, a managing member holding 60% or more "shall" approve his own expense reimbursements "without requirement of separate board or member approval," and that Jared Ostrowski's review is "N/A — Review not required." Respondent thus controls his own reimbursements with no oversight, creating a mechanism by which personal spending could be run through the company as "reimbursements" — a potential vehicle for disguised compensation or dissipation.

4. **"Personal Devices" reimbursement mirroring a personal credit-card charge.** The Q2 expense report lists a 5/17/2024 "TechVault Solutions — Technology Equipment — Personal Devices — Tablet, smartphone, and accessories" reimbursement of $2,150. The personal credit-card statement (PROD-006) shows a 5/17/2024 charge to "Pinnacle Electronics — Personal Devices" of $2,149.50 — the same date, essentially the same amount, and the same "Personal Devices" description, but a different vendor. The FCB checking account shows a $2,150 "NovaBridge Solutions LLC Expense Reimb." deposit on 5/20/2024. ### I. [MEDIUM] Valuation-date / balance-date inconsistencies — declared balances match Aug. 1, 2024, not the June 1, 2024 separation date

### L. [LOW] Tax-return arithmetic and missing schedules

1. **Self-employment tax appears overstated.** The return reports self-employment tax of $51,960 (and a one-half deduction of $25,980 at Line 10) on $367,200 of net SE earnings (K-1 Box 14a). The standard SE-tax computation (92.35% × $367,200 = ~$339,109; 12.4% × $160,200 SS wage base + 2.9% × $339,109) yields approximately $29,699, with a one-half deduction of ~$14,849. The reported figures would overstate the AGI adjustment and understate AGI by roughly $11,100. This is the parties' joint return, so any error affects both, but it should be verified.

2. **Form 8995 (QBI deduction) referenced but not produced; deduction not reflected.** The schedule index states Form 8995 was filed, but no QBI deduction appears in the taxable-income computation (Line 15 = AGI − itemized deductions only). At this income level the QBI deduction is subject to W-2 wage/UBIA limitations; the treatment is unclear from the excerpts.

3. **Complete return not produced.** Only excerpts of the 47-page return were produced; Schedule A, B, D, SE, and Form 8995 were not produced (some are summarized in an index). **Recommendation:** obtain the complete 2023 return with all schedules and worksheets, and the 2022 return, directly from the preparer (Archer & Linden CPAs) by subpoena if necessary; verify the SE-tax and QBI computations.

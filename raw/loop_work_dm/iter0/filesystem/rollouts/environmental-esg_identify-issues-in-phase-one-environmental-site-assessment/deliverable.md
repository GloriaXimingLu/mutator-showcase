       I. Executive Summary  
      This memorandum reports our review of the Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (    “    Phase I ESA    ”    ) and supporting environmental documents prepared in connection with Cornerstone Industrial Partners LLC’s (    “    Cornerstone    ”         or         “    Buyer    ”    ) proposed acquisition of the approximately 14.7-acre former manufacturing/warehouse complex located at 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo, Lucas County, Ohio 43611 (the         “    Property    ”    ) from Breckenridge Manufacturing Co. (    “    Breckenridge    ”         or         “    Seller    ”    ) for a purchase price of $8,750,000, to be financed in part by a $6,125,000 acquisition loan from Great Lakes Capital Bank (the         “    Lender    ”    ). The transaction is governed by the Asset Purchase Agreement dated September 1, 2024 (the         “    Purchase Agreement    ”    ), with a scheduled Closing Date of December 15, 2024 and an Environmental Due Diligence Period that expires at 5:00 p.m. Eastern Time on             October 31, 2024    .  
      The Phase I ESA was prepared by Greenfield Environmental Consultants Inc. (    “    Greenfield    ”    ) and is dated October 25, 2024 (site reconnaissance October 2, 2024), conforming to ASTM E1527-21. Greenfield identified             two recognized environmental conditions (        “        RECs        ”        )         — (1) former trichloroethylene (    “    TCE    ”    ) vapor-degreasing operations, an abandoned leaking waste-TCE drum, and a documented 1988 solvent spill; and (2) former hexavalent-chromium and nickel electroplating operations with visible contamination, an in-ground sump containing liquid with a sheen, and a 39-year gap in hazardous-waste disposal records — together with one historical recognized environmental condition (    “    HREC    ”    ) (a former farmhouse heating-oil underground storage tank (    “    UST    ”    )), one de minimis condition (railroad herbicide), and two data gaps.  
      Our independent review, conducted against the controlling frameworks of ASTM E1527-21, the All Appropriate Inquiries (    “    AAI    ”    ) rule at 40 C.F.R. Part 312, the CERCLA bona fide prospective purchaser (    “    BFPP    ”    ) and contiguous property owner defenses, the representations, termination, and indemnification provisions of the Purchase Agreement, and the Lender’s environmental due-diligence requirements,             identifies several material conditions that the Phase I ESA either omitted, misclassified, or left unresolved, and several transactional exposures that the ESA does not address.         The most significant of these are:  
             A confirmed off-site tetrachloroethylene (        “        PCE        ”        ) groundwater plume         from the former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility immediately north of the Property, documented at 87 µg/L (17.4 times the Ohio groundwater standard of 5 µg/L) at a monitoring well only 120 feet from the Property boundary and migrating south-southeast — directly toward the Property. The Phase I ESA identified this plume in its records-review section and its own database appendix rates it                 “        HIGH,        ”                 yet it did not classify it as a REC.         VI. Detailed Issue Analysis  
       Issue 1 — Off-Site PCE Groundwater Plume (Lake Erie Dry Cleaning): Omitted/Misclassified REC  
          Severity: CRITICAL  
          Description and evidence.         The former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility at 4900 Lakeshore Industrial Drive is located immediately north of and hydraulically upgradient from the Property, approximately 120 feet from the northern Property boundary. Both the EDR database report and the Ohio EPA response letter confirm the following:  
          The dry-cleaner site is a confirmed release site on the Ohio EPA Master Sites List (VAP ID NW-0923) with a documented PCE groundwater contamination plume.  
          A 2014 investigation detected PCE at             87 µg/L         at monitoring well MW-7, located approximately 120 feet north of the Property boundary. The applicable Ohio generic numerical groundwater standard for PCE is             5 µg/L         (Ohio Admin. Code § 3745-300-08, Table 1). The detected concentration exceeds the standard by a factor of approximately             17.4    .  
          The documented plume migration direction is             south-southeast — toward the Subject Property         — consistent with regional groundwater flow.  
          The Property is located within a             state-designated Groundwater Management Zone (        “        GMZ        ”        )         established in connection with this plume.  
          A Covenant Not to Sue was issued under the Ohio VAP in 2018, but it             applies exclusively to the dry-cleaner parcel         and         “    does not address, release, or otherwise cover off-site migration of the PCE groundwater plume onto adjacent properties, including the Subject Property.    ”  
          An environmental covenant recorded against the dry-cleaner parcel under Ohio Rev. Code § 5301.82 restricts groundwater use and             requires maintenance of an active sub-slab vapor mitigation system         in occupied structures on that parcel — confirming that PCE vapor intrusion is a recognized concern in the immediate vicinity.  
          Ohio EPA confirms that             no investigation of PCE concentrations in groundwater beneath the Subject Property has been conducted    , and the lateral and vertical extent of plume migration onto or beneath the Property             has not been delineated    .  
      The Greenfield Phase I ESA recites these facts in its records-review section (§5.1.1) and in the narrative summary of its EDR appendix, where it describes the dry-cleaner listing as         “    the most significant nearby listing    ”         and states that the plume is         “    migrating in a south-southeast direction — toward the Subject Property.    ”         The EDR database summary independently rates the potential impact on the Property as             “        HIGH.        ”         Nevertheless, the ESA’s Findings and Conclusions (§9)             do not classify the off-site PCE plume as a REC    , do not list it among the         “    Additional Findings    ”         (§9.5), and do not address it in the data-gap analysis. The ESA’s conclusions identify only the two on-site RECs (TCE and electroplating).  
          Why this is a deficiency and a Critical issue.         Under ASTM E1527-21 §3.2.78, a REC includes         “    the presence or likely presence of any hazardous substances … in, on, or at a property … due to a release to the environment.    ”         A confirmed off-site plume at 17.4 times the groundwater standard, located 120 feet upgradient and documented to be migrating toward the Property, supports a conclusion that hazardous substances are         “    likely … at    ”         the Property due to an off-site release. The Lender’s requirements letter (Section 2) expressly directs that         “    the Phase I ESA must evaluate whether any off-site contamination, including groundwater plumes, may be migrating toward or impacting the subject Property, and             any such conditions must be identified as recognized environmental conditions.    ”         The ESA’s failure to do so is a direct non-conformance with the Lender’s requirement and an internally inconsistent treatment of its own record.  
          Consequences.         (1)             CERCLA defense risk.         Buyer’s ability to assert the BFPP defense (CERCLA §107(r)) and the contiguous property owner defense (§107(q)) depends on AAI and, for the contiguous-property defense, on taking         “    reasonable steps    ”         to stop a continuing release and prevent exposure. If Buyer relies on a Phase I ESA that fails to identify a known migrating plume as a REC, Buyer may lack the inquiry basis and the         “    reasonable steps    ”         record needed to defend against liability for the PCE on the Property. The Lender expressly warns that AAI failure exposes both Borrower and the Bank to CERCLA liability without available defenses. (2)             Lender acceptance risk.         The Lender’s environmental counsel will review the Phase I ESA for AAI compliance and for the required off-site plume evaluation; an ESA that omits the plume from its REC conclusions is likely to be rejected or require supplementation, jeopardizing the November 15, 2024 delivery deadline. (3)             Vapor-intrusion and groundwater liability.         If the plume has migrated beneath the Property’s buildings (the main manufacturing building occupies the central/northern portion of the site), Buyer may inherit vapor-intrusion exposure in occupied warehouse space and groundwater liability that the Seller’s VAP covenant does not cover. (4)             GMZ restrictions.         The GMZ designation may restrict new groundwater withdrawals, well installations, and certain land uses on the Property.  
          Recommendation.         Direct Greenfield to issue a supplement classifying the off-site PCE plume as a REC (or, at minimum, a CREC/vapor-intrusion condition requiring investigation), and to evaluate the contiguous-property-owner framework. The Phase II ESA must include groundwater monitoring wells on the Property (hydraulically downgradient of MW-7) to delineate whether the plume has migrated onto/beneath the Property, plus sub-slab/indoor-air vapor-intrusion sampling in occupied structures. Buyer should obtain the dry-cleaner’s 2014 investigation report and any subsequent monitoring data from Ohio EPA, and should confirm the GMZ’s restrictions with Ohio EPA’s Division of Drinking and Ground Waters. Buyer should also evaluate whether the dry cleaner (or its successors) is a viable third-party responsible party for any on-Property plume impacts, and should not assume the Seller’s VAP covenant provides any protection for off-site migration.  
      
        Issue 2 — REC-1: Former TCE Vapor-Degreasing Operations, Abandoned Leaking Waste-TCE Drum, and 1988 Solvent Spill  
          Severity: CRITICAL  
          Description and evidence.         Greenfield identified this as REC No. 1, and we concur with the classification. The supporting record is strong:  
          TCE was used as a vapor-degreasing solvent in the northwest corner of the main manufacturing building from 1961 through approximately 1992 — more than 30 years. TCE is a known human carcinogen and a highly mobile chlorinated solvent that persists in groundwater and produces vapor-intrusion risks.  
          A photoionization detector (    “    PID    ”    ) reading of             42.3 ppm         was recorded at a crack in the floor slab of the former degreasing area, against background readings of less than 0.5 ppm elsewhere in the building — strong evidence of residual VOC vapors emanating from beneath the slab.  
              No closure documentation, decommissioning records, or soil/groundwater confirmation sampling         were located for the degreasing area following the reported cessation of TCE use in 1992.  
          An abandoned 55-gallon drum labeled             “        WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE        ”         was observed behind the maintenance/utility building, approximately half full, with a rusted bottom and evidence of past leakage; stained soil (approximately 3 ft × 3 ft) with absent vegetation was observed beneath the drum.  
          A             March 14, 1988 Toledo Fire Department incident report         documents a         “    chemical spill    ”         at the Property involving an         “    unknown quantity of degreasing solvent    ”         from an overturned drum, cleaned up by Breckenridge employees with no follow-up inspection. The EDR database summary confirms this spill does             not         appear in ERNS or the Ohio spills database — i.e., it was never reported to state/federal environmental authorities and has no regulatory closure.  
          Seller (Gerald Breckenridge III), when confronted with the fire-department record, stated he was         “    not aware of any spills    ”         and called the incident         “    a minor incident    ”         and         “    nothing to worry about.    ”  
          Consequences.         TCE contamination in soil and groundwater beneath and around the main building is plausible and may be extensive given the 30-year use period, the elevated PID reading, the undocumented closure, the leaking drum, and the unreported 1988 spill. Chlorinated-solvent remediation is typically among the most expensive categories of remediation (source-area treatment, groundwater pump-and-treat or in-situ treatment, and long-term monitoring), and vapor-intrusion mitigation in occupied structures may be required. This condition alone could produce remediation costs approaching or exceeding the $500,000 §6.3(b) termination threshold and the $1,750,000 indemnification cap. The Lender’s vapor-intrusion assessment is triggered (VOCs in soil/groundwater are likely). Seller’s denial of the 1988 spill, in the face of a contemporaneous fire-department record, is relevant to the fraud/intentional-misrepresentation analysis (Issue 5) and to the reliability of Seller’s         “    to Seller’s knowledge    ”         representations.  
          Recommendation.         Proceed with the Phase II ESA recommended by Greenfield: soil borings through the floor slab in the former degreasing area; soil and groundwater sampling for VOCs (TCE and degradation products cis-1,2-DCE and vinyl chloride); soil borings around the abandoned drum with drum characterization and proper removal/disposal; and sub-slab/indoor-air vapor-intrusion sampling. Preserve the fire-department record and Seller’s denial as evidence relevant to the §4.12(a)         “    to Seller’s knowledge    ”         representation and the fraud exception. Do not allow the drum to remain in place through Closing.  
      
        Issue 3 — REC-2: Former Hexavalent-Chromium/Nickel Electroplating, In-Ground Sump Liquid, 39-Year Manifest Gap, and 2003 NOV  
          Severity: CRITICAL  
          Description and evidence.         Greenfield identified this as REC No. 2, and we concur. The record is, if anything, more troubling than the ESA’s narrative conveys:  
          Electroplating using hexavalent chromium and nickel was conducted in the south wing of the main building from 1965 through 2004 — approximately 39 years. Hexavalent chromium is a potent carcinogen and is regulated under RCRA toxicity characteristic D007.  
          Visible blue-green staining on floors and walls is consistent with chromium residues; the concrete exhibits pitting, etching, and chemical degradation.  
          An in-ground concrete sump in the former electroplating room contains approximately four inches of liquid with a visible iridescent sheen and a greenish tint; the source and composition are undetermined (no sampling was performed, consistent with Phase I scope).  
              Hazardous-waste disposal documentation exists for only the final year of operations (2004):         six manifests documenting 14,200 pounds of waste under RCRA codes D007 (chromium) and F006 (electroplating sludge), transported by Tri-County Environmental Hauling Inc.         No manifests, biennial reports, or disposal records exist for 1965 through 2003         — a 39-year gap confirmed independently by the Ohio EPA response letter, which states that Ohio EPA         “    has no hazardous waste manifests, biennial reports, or annual hazardous waste generation reports on file for this facility for any reporting year other than 2004.    ”  
          A             2003 Ohio EPA Compliance Evaluation Inspection         (July 17, 2003) documented: (1) improper storage of hazardous waste in rusted and visibly leaking 55-gallon drums in an             outdoor storage area on the east side         of the Property, containing electroplating sludge and spent chromium-bearing wastewater, with staining on the ground beneath and around the drums; (2) failure to label containers with accumulation-start dates; and (3) failure to maintain hazardous-waste determination records. This resulted in an             NOV (File No. NW-2003-0471)         and a             Director’s Final Findings and Orders (consent order)         requiring a facility-wide waste audit, proper disposal of accumulated waste, a written waste-management plan, and a compliance certification within 120 days.  
          Consequences.         The combination of 39 years of chromium operations, visible contamination, an undetermined sump liquid, a near-total absence of waste-disposal documentation, and a documented regulatory violation for leaking drums with ground staining indicates a strong potential for hexavalent-chromium and nickel releases to soil and groundwater beneath and around the south wing and the east-side drum-storage area. The 39-year manifest gap raises the distinct possibility that hazardous wastes were not lawfully managed and disposed of for most of the facility’s operating life. Remediation of metals (particularly hexavalent chromium, which is mobile in groundwater under certain conditions) can be costly and may require soil excavation, groundwater treatment, and long-term monitoring. This condition, like Issue 2, could exceed the §6.3(b) $500,000 threshold and the indemnification cap. The 2003 NOV is also a direct breach of Seller’s §4.12(b) compliance representation and should have been disclosed on Schedule 4.12(c) (see Issue 5).  
          Recommendation.         Proceed with the Phase II ESA: characterization sampling of the sump liquid (metals, VOCs, pH, and other parameters); soil borings through and beneath the floor slab in the former electroplating room for metals (including hexavalent chromium and total chromium); and groundwater monitoring wells for metals analysis. Obtain the archived 2003 consent order and any compliance certification from Ohio EPA Central Office (Division of Hazardous Waste Management) — the Ohio EPA response indicates a separate records request is required and may take 30–45 business days, so this should be initiated immediately. Confirm whether the consent-order corrective actions were actually completed; if the compliance certification was never submitted, Seller’s §4.12(b) and §4.12(c) representations are further undermined.  
      
        Issue 4 — Undocumented 2,000-Gallon Waste-Solvent UST (East Side): Potential Underclassified REC  
          Severity: HIGH  
          Description and evidence.         The 1987 Sanborn fire-insurance map depicts a             “        2,000-gal. waste solvent tank (underground)        ”         on the east side of the Property, in the area between the main building and the eastern property boundary — the same general vicinity as the outdoor drum-storage area visible on the 1990 and 2000 aerial photographs and the curbed area with stressed vegetation observed during the 2024 reconnaissance.

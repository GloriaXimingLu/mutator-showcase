# MKPoly Coatings, LLC — Joint Venture Agreement

## Compromise Provisions for the Five Contested Sections

### Draft for Principals' Review — August 11, 2025 Meeting

**Prepared for:** Elaine Cordero, Chief Executive Officer, Meridian Advanced Materials, LLC; and Dr. Henrik Laufer, Chief Strategy Officer, Kastor Chemical Industries AG

**Working draft:** MKPoly JVA v.4 (redline dated July 18, 2025)

**Purpose:** This document sets out proposed compromise contract language for the five contested provisions remaining in the MKPoly Joint Venture Agreement, together with a negotiation rationale for each and a summary concession matrix. Each provision is drafted to be self-contained and independently evaluable, so that the principals may consider the five terms separately or as a package at the August 11 meeting. The clause language is formatted for direct insertion into the JVA at the section cross-references indicated.

### B. The compromise approach

The provisions below apply a "fallback ladder" methodology. For each term, the draft identifies a landing point that (i) sits within the range bracketed by the parties' opening positions, (ii) is supported by the comparable-transaction data and enforceability analysis in the record, and (iii) gives each side its core objective while requiring a reciprocal concession on a point the other side values more. The balance of concessions is calibrated so that each side concedes more heavily on the items it ranks as lower priority and holds more carefully on the items it ranks as higher priority, producing a package that is balanced as a whole rather than provision by provision.

### C. Landing points at a glance

1. **Governance (Art. IV):** Five-member Management Committee (3 Meridian / 2 Kastor); supermajority (4 of 5) veto on a narrow list of nine Reserved Matters; tiered deadlock resolution (principals → mediation → CEO casting vote on ordinary matters / buy-sell on Reserved Matters); CEO and CFO appointed by Meridian, CTO by Kastor.
2. **Capital Calls (Art. V):** 75-day total funding window (15-day notice + 60-day cure); penalty interest during cure; a member-loan alternative at SOFR + 400 bps with mandatory 12-month repayment and automatic equity conversion on default; pro-rata dilution capped at 15% per annum with a forced-sale backstop if cumulative dilution reaches 15% in any 24-month period.
3. **Non-Compete (Art. XIV):** 18-month post-exit duration; product scope limited to JV products plus substantially similar or directly competitive products; geographic scope covering the United States, Canada, and any jurisdiction where MKPoly generated more than $500,000 in revenue in the preceding 24 months; explicit carve-out for each party's pre-existing non-competing businesses.
4. **Exit Valuation (Art. X):** Trailing 12-month EBITDA × 7.0× (the "Agreed Multiple"); an independent appraiser that is not the JV auditor and has no pre-existing engagement with either party; mutual selection with AAA fallback; baseball arbitration for disputed determinations; a 6.5×–7.5× collar; trigger events expanded to include deadlock and involuntary transfer.
5. **IP License-Back (Art. XII):** Contributed IP reverts to the contributing party, with a reciprocal 24-month transitional license for legacy products; non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual co-developed IP license to both parties on a territorial basis (Meridian: North America; Kastor: rest of world); sublicensing permitted within each territory except to Schedule 12.05 Restricted Competitors (a global, reciprocal prohibition) and subject to a 25% North American revenue negative covenant; improvements to contributed IP revert to the contributor with a restricted license to the other party.

### D. Overall balance

The package is designed so that the concessions net out across the five terms. On the two provisions each side treats as most important — IP license-back and governance for Meridian; IP license-back and appraiser independence for Kastor — the landing points preserve each side's non-negotiable core (no competitor access to Meridian's contributed formulations; a genuine supermajority veto and independent valuation) while requiring real movement on the dimensions each side had flagged as flexible (board composition for Kastor; the valuation multiple and non-compete scope for Meridian). On the three remaining provisions, both sides move substantially from their opening positions toward a market-supported midpoint. The concession matrix in Part II sets out the trade-offs in detail.

## III. Provision 1 — Management Committee Composition and Governance

**JVA v.4 location:** Article IV, §§ 4.01 (Establishment; Composition), 4.02 (Meetings; Quorum), 4.03 (Voting; Reserved Matters), 4.04 (Deadlock Resolution), 4.05 (Officers).

### A. Proposed compromise clause language

The following language is drafted to replace the corresponding subsections of Article IV of JVA v.4.

**Section 4.01 — Establishment; Composition**

**(b) Composition.** The Management Committee shall consist of five (5) individuals (each, a "**Manager**"), of whom three (3) shall be appointed by Meridian (the "**Meridian Designees**") and two (2) shall be appointed by Kastor (the "**Kastor Designees**"). Each Member shall designate its initial appointees to the Management Committee by written notice to the other Member and the Company prior to or at the Closing. The composition of the Management Committee is intended to reflect Meridian's fifty-five percent (55%) Percentage Interest while preserving meaningful protective rights for Kastor as the forty-five percent (45%) Member through the Reserved Matters and deadlock provisions of this Article IV.

**Section 4.02 — Meetings; Quorum**

**(b) Quorum.** A quorum for the transaction of business at any meeting of the Management Committee shall consist of four (4) Managers, of whom at least two (2) must be Meridian Designees and at least one (1) must be a Kastor Designee. No business shall be transacted at any meeting of the Management Committee unless a quorum is present. If a quorum is not present at any duly called meeting, the Managers present may adjourn such meeting to a later date not less than five (5) nor more than fifteen (15) Business Days thereafter, upon written notice to all Managers.

**Section 4.03 — Voting; Reserved Matters**

**(a) Ordinary Matters.** Each Manager shall have one (1) vote. Except as provided in Section 4.03(b), all actions of the Management Committee shall be decided by a simple majority vote of the Managers present at a duly constituted meeting at which a quorum is present.

**(b) Reserved Matters.** The following matters (the "**Reserved Matters**") shall require the affirmative vote of at least four (4) of the five (5) Managers (a "**Supermajority Vote**"), which shall include the affirmative vote of at least one (1) Kastor Designee:

> (i) Any amendment, modification, or restatement of this Agreement or the Certificate of Formation;
>
> (ii) The voluntary dissolution, winding up, or filing of a petition under any federal or state bankruptcy, insolvency, or receivership statute by or against the Company;
>
> (iii) The sale, transfer, lease, or other disposition of all or substantially all of the assets of the Company in a single transaction or a series of related transactions;
>
> (iv) The admission of any new Member to the Company or the issuance of any new Membership Interests to any Person other than the existing Members pro-rata;
>
> (v) The entry into, material amendment of, or termination of any Related-Party Transaction;
>
> (vi) Any material change to the nature or scope of the Business Purpose as set forth in Section 2.04 and Schedule 2.04;
>
> (vii) The authorization of any Capital Call in an aggregate amount exceeding Five Million Dollars ($5,000,000) in any Fiscal Year;
>
> (viii) The incurrence of indebtedness (including guarantees or liens on Company assets securing indebtedness) in an amount exceeding Seven Million Five Hundred Thousand Dollars ($7,500,000), whether in a single transaction or a series of related transactions;
>
> (ix) The acquisition, disposition, lease, or encumbrance of any material asset of the Company (including any Intellectual Property) having a value exceeding Three Million Dollars ($3,000,000) in a single transaction or a series of related transactions, in each case not contemplated by the approved annual operating budget.

For the avoidance of doubt, the following matters are **not** Reserved Matters and shall be decided by simple majority vote under Section 4.03(a) or handled within the CEO's delegated authority under Section 4.05: approval of the annual operating budget; hiring, compensation, and termination of personnel below the C-suite level; entry into customer, supplier, or distribution contracts in the ordinary course of business; pricing and product-development priorities; and the initiation or settlement of litigation involving claims of Two Million Dollars ($2,000,000) or less.

**Section 4.04 — Deadlock Resolution**

In the event that the Management Committee is unable to reach the required vote on any matter after two (2) consecutive duly noticed meetings at which such matter is considered and voted upon (a "**Deadlock**"), the following procedure shall apply:

**(a) Escalation to Principals.** The matter shall be referred to the Chief Executive Officer of Meridian and the Chief Strategy Officer of Kastor (or their respective designees) for good-faith negotiation for a period of fifteen (15) Business Days (the "**Escalation Period**"). During the Escalation Period, the status quo shall be maintained, and neither party shall take unilateral action on the deadlocked matter.

**(b) Mediation.** If the Deadlock is not resolved during the Escalation Period, either Member may initiate non-binding mediation administered by the American Arbitration Association before a single mediator with experience in the specialty chemicals industry. The mediation shall be completed within thirty (30) calendar days of the appointment of the mediator.

**(c) Resolution Following Mediation.** If the Deadlock persists after the mediation period:

> (i) **Ordinary Matters.** For any matter that is not a Reserved Matter, the Chief Executive Officer of the Company (who shall be a Meridian appointee) shall hold a casting vote to resolve the Deadlock, ensuring that the Company is not operationally paralyzed by an unresolved disagreement.

> (ii) **Reserved Matters.** For any Reserved Matter, the Chief Executive Officer shall **not** hold a casting vote. Either Member may, by written notice to the other Member within thirty (30) calendar days following the conclusion of the mediation, elect to initiate the buyout procedure set forth in Article X, and such Deadlock shall constitute a Triggering Event for purposes of Article X. If both Members deliver such a notice, the Member with the higher Percentage Interest shall be deemed the Initiating Member.

**Section 4.05 — Officers**

**(b) Appointment of Officers.**

> (i) The Chief Executive Officer ("**CEO**") and the Chief Financial Officer ("**CFO**") shall be appointed by Meridian, and Meridian shall have the sole right to appoint and replace the CEO and CFO.

> (ii) The Chief Technology Officer ("**CTO**") shall be appointed by Kastor, and Kastor shall have the sole right to appoint and replace the CTO.

> (iii) The Chief Operating Officer ("**COO**") and all other officers shall be appointed by the Management Committee by simple majority vote under Section 4.03(a). All officers and senior management below the C-suite level shall be appointed by the CEO.

**(c) Removal of C-Suite Officers.** The removal of the CEO, CFO, or CTO prior to the expiration of any applicable employment agreement shall be a Reserved Matter requiring a Supermajority Vote under Section 4.03(b). Each Member retains the right to remove its own appointee to any such office at any time for cause.

**(d) Delegated Authority.** The CEO shall have authority to act on behalf of the Company without Management Committee approval for any action or expenditure that (i) is within the approved annual operating budget and (ii) does not involve a single-transaction expenditure exceeding One Million Dollars ($1,000,000). Actions outside the approved annual operating budget, or exceeding the One Million Dollar ($1,000,000) single-transaction threshold, shall require Management Committee approval under Section 4.03.

### B. Summary of the parties' positions

**Meridian's opening position.** A five-member Management Committee (3 Meridian, 2 Kastor); simple majority voting on all matters; a Meridian-appointed CEO holding a casting vote to resolve all deadlocks; the CEO and CFO appointed by Meridian. Meridian's rationale is that its 55% equity interest and its contribution of the JV's core technology justify proportionate governance control, with the CEO casting vote providing an efficient deadlock mechanism.

**Kastor's opening position.** A six-member Management Committee (3 Meridian, 3 Kastor); simple majority on ordinary matters; supermajority (5 of 6) on a list of twelve Reserved Matters; deadlock resolved through principals' escalation, mediation, and ultimately a forced buyout; no CEO casting vote on Reserved Matters. Kastor's rationale is that its total contribution ($43.8M) slightly exceeds Meridian's ($42.9M), and that the disparity between its near-parity economic contribution and its 45% minority equity position requires governance protections — principally a supermajority veto on material corporate actions.

### C. Negotiation rationale

**Board composition (3 Meridian / 2 Kastor).** The compromise adopts Meridian's proposed 3-2 composition, which directly reflects the 55/45 equity split and is the structure used in the most directly comparable majority-held specialty-coatings joint venture in the Frazier Whitmore dataset (a 60/40 transaction with a 3-2 board). Kastor identified board seat count as an area of greater flexibility, and the 3-2 structure is offered in exchange for the supermajority veto described below. Meridian gains a board majority consistent with its equity; Kastor concedes the equal-representation structure it preferred but does so in exchange for the protective voting threshold that it identified as its single most important governance term.

**Supermajority veto on nine narrow Reserved Matters.** The compromise grants Kastor a Supermajority Vote (4 of 5, necessarily including at least one Kastor Designee) on a defined list of nine Reserved Matters. The list is limited to genuinely existential decisions — amendment of the agreement, dissolution, sale of substantially all assets, admission of new members, related-party transactions, changes to the business purpose, large capital calls, material indebtedness, and material asset dispositions — and expressly excludes operational matters (annual budget approval, sub-C-suite hiring, ordinary-course contracts, pricing, and product-development priorities), which remain within the CEO's delegated authority or simple-majority voting. This is the standard protective-rights framework for a minority partner in a majority-held Delaware LLC and mirrors the Reserved Matters lists in the comparable transactions. Kastor gains the protective veto that addresses its core concern about decisions that could adversely affect its near-parity investment; Meridian concedes the veto but confines it to the existential category, preserving its ability to direct day-to-day operations without minority consent.

**Tiered deadlock resolution.** The compromise adopts a tiered mechanism that gives Kastor the escalation process it sought (principals' negotiation, then mediation) while preserving Meridian's ultimate tiebreak on operational matters. On ordinary matters, the CEO casting vote resolves a deadlock that survives escalation and mediation, ensuring the Company is never operationally paralyzed. On Reserved Matters, there is no CEO casting vote — the matter instead proceeds to the buy-sell mechanism under Article X. This structure directly addresses Kastor's firm requirement that the CEO (a Meridian appointee) not hold a casting vote on the very matters the supermajority threshold is designed to protect, while addressing Meridian's concern that a forced-buyout trigger on ordinary operating disagreements would create perverse incentives. Confining the buyout trigger to Reserved Matters deadlocks that survive two prior escalation steps significantly reduces the risk of manufactured deadlocks.

**Officer appointments.** Meridian appoints the CEO and CFO, preserving operational and financial leadership aligned with its majority position and its role as operator. Kastor appoints the CTO, giving it a meaningful voice in the technical direction of the JV — a genuine concession to Kastor that recognizes its manufacturing and process expertise and its capital funding of the JV's R&D. The COO and other officers are appointed by simple majority. Removal of any C-suite officer is a Reserved Matter, which protects Kastor's CTO appointee from unilateral removal and gives both parties a say in the removal of the CEO and CFO, while Meridian retains the sole right to appoint and replace the CEO and CFO. This balances Meridian's need for operational control with Kastor's need for a protected technical leadership role.

## IV. Provision 2 — Capital Calls and Dilution

**JVA v.4 location:** Article V, § 5.01 (Capital Calls). Conforming definitions in Article I ("Cure Period," "Funding Deadline," "Annual Dilution Cap").

### A. Proposed compromise clause language

The following language is drafted to replace Section 5.01 of JVA v.4 and the related defined terms in Article I.

**Section 5.01 — Capital Calls**

**(a) Approval.** If at any time after the Closing the Management Committee determines that the Company requires additional capital beyond the Initial Capital Contributions and any available internal resources, the Management Committee may approve a Capital Call. Any Capital Call in an aggregate amount exceeding Five Million Dollars ($5,000,000) in any Fiscal Year shall require a Supermajority Vote as a Reserved Matter under Section 4.03(b)(vii); Capital Calls within that threshold may be approved by simple majority vote.

**(b) Notice.** Upon approval of a Capital Call, the Company shall promptly deliver to each Member a written Capital Call Notice specifying (i) the aggregate amount of additional capital required, (ii) each Member's pro-rata share, (iii) the purpose for which the additional capital is required, and (iv) the Funding Deadline. The Capital Call Notice shall be delivered not less than fifteen (15) calendar days before the Funding Deadline.

**(c) Funding; Funding Deadline.** Each Member shall fund its pro-rata share of the Capital Call by wire transfer of immediately available funds to an account designated by the Company within sixty (60) calendar days after receipt of the Capital Call Notice (the "**Funding Deadline**"). For purposes of accommodating multi-currency treasury operations, a Member may satisfy its funding obligation in Euros at the European Central Bank reference rate published on the date of the Capital Call Notice, with conversion to U.S. Dollars handled by the Company at the Company's cost; the Member's Capital Account shall be credited in the U.S. Dollar equivalent so determined.

**(d) Failure to Fund; Penalty Interest.** If a Member fails to fund its pro-rata share by the Funding Deadline (a "**Defaulting Member**"), penalty interest shall accrue automatically on the unfunded amount at a rate equal to the greater of (i) the Secured Overnight Financing Rate ("**SOFR**") plus five hundred (500) basis points per annum, or (ii) ten percent (10%) per annum, from the Funding Deadline until the date the unfunded amount (together with accrued penalty interest) is funded or converted to equity under subsection (f). Penalty interest shall be payable to the Company for the benefit of the Non-Defaulting Member upon funding or upon dilution, as applicable.

**(e) Cure Loan Alternative.** At any time during the period beginning on the Funding Deadline and ending on the date that is thirty (30) calendar days after the Funding Deadline (the "**Cure Window**"), the Non-Defaulting Member may, at its election, advance the Defaulting Member's unfunded share as a loan (the "**Cure Loan**"). If the Non-Defaulting Member elects to make a Cure Loan:

> (i) The Cure Loan shall bear interest at SOFR plus four hundred (400) basis points per annum, compounding quarterly;

> (ii) The Cure Loan shall be repayable in full (principal plus accrued interest) within twelve (12) months of disbursement, in a single lump sum, with no extensions or refinancing;

> (iii) The Defaulting Member shall grant to the Non-Defaulting Member a first-priority security interest in its entire Membership Interest as collateral for the Cure Loan, and the Non-Defaulting Member shall file UCC financing statements promptly upon disbursement;

> (iv) If the Defaulting Member fails to repay the Cure Loan in full within the twelve (12)-month repayment period, the outstanding balance (principal plus accrued interest) shall automatically convert to an additional Capital Contribution of the Non-Defaulting Member, and the Percentage Interests shall be adjusted under subsection (f) as if the Non-Defaulting Member had funded the unfunded amount as additional capital;

> (v) The Cure Loan alternative shall be available to any Member no more than once in any twenty-four (24)-month period; and

> (vi) If a Cure Loan is made, no dilution shall occur until and unless the Cure Loan is not repaid and converts to equity under subsection (e)(iv).

**(f) Dilution.** If the Defaulting Member does not fund its unfunded share (together with accrued penalty interest) by the end of the Cure Window and no Cure Loan is in effect, or if a Cure Loan converts to equity under subsection (e)(iv), the Non-Defaulting Member may fund the unfunded amount as an additional Capital Contribution, and the Percentage Interests of the Members shall be adjusted on a pro-rata basis to reflect the additional capital contributed by the Non-Defaulting Member relative to the aggregate Capital Contributions of both Members. All governance rights, distribution entitlements, and allocation rights shall be automatically adjusted to reflect the revised Percentage Interests.

**(g) Annual Dilution Cap; Forced-Sale Backstop.** Notwithstanding subsection (f), in no event shall a Defaulting Member's Percentage Interest be diluted by more than fifteen percent (15%) of such Member's then-current Percentage Interest in any single Fiscal Year (the "**Annual Dilution Cap**"). If the adjustment that would otherwise result from subsection (f) would exceed the Annual Dilution Cap, the excess shall be treated as a Cure Loan under subsection (e) until the next Fiscal Year. If a Member's cumulative dilution reaches fifteen percent (15%) of its then-current Percentage Interest in any twenty-four (24)-month period, the Non-Defaulting Member may, by written notice, require the diluted Member to sell all (but not less than all) of its Membership Interest to the Non-Defaulting Member at the Fair Market Value determined in accordance with Section 10.03 (the "**Forced-Sale Right**"). The diluted Member shall comply with the buyout procedures of Article X. The Annual Dilution Cap and the Forced-Sale Right together ensure that a Member cannot indefinitely retain its equity position while declining to fund its pro-rata share of Capital Calls.

### B. Summary of the parties' positions

**Meridian's opening position.** A 30-day funding period; immediate, uncapped, pro-rata dilution upon failure to fund; no loan alternative; no dilution cap. Meridian's rationale is that uncapped dilution is the strongest incentive for prompt funding and that a cap or market-rate loan would allow a non-funding partner to free-ride on the funding partner's capital while retaining governance rights disproportionate to its economic stake.

**Kastor's opening position.** A 90-day cure period; dilution capped at 10% per annum; a loan alternative at a market rate (SOFR + 250 bps) available before dilution. Kastor's rationale is that its multi-currency treasury operations and Swiss regulatory requirements can cause short-term funding delays unrelated to financial capacity, and that temporary liquidity timing constraints should not permanently destroy a member's equity position.

### C. Negotiation rationale

**Funding window (75 days total).** The compromise provides a 15-day notice period plus a 60-day funding period, for a total of 75 days from the Capital Call Notice to the dilution trigger. This sits at the midpoint between Meridian's 30-day opening and Kastor's 90-day request, and it falls within the 45–60 day cure range observed in the comparable transactions. The 75-day window gives Kastor reasonable time to arrange U.S. Dollar funding, including foreign-exchange conversion and any regulatory filings, while preserving the urgency of the funding obligation. The Euro-funding accommodation in subsection (c) further addresses Kastor's operational concern by allowing it to fund in Euros at a locked reference rate with conversion handled by the Company, effectively removing the foreign-exchange conversion burden that was the principal justification for a 90-day window. Meridian concedes a longer window than it preferred; Kastor concedes a shorter window than it preferred, but gains the Euro-funding accommodation that materially eases its treasury burden.

**Penalty interest.** Penalty interest at SOFR + 500 bps (or 10%, whichever is greater) accrues on unfunded amounts during the cure period. This compensates the Company and the funding member for the time value of delayed capital and imposes a meaningful economic cost on late funding, preserving the incentive to fund promptly that Meridian requires. The rate is above market, but it is a penalty for non-performance rather than a loan rate, and it applies only to the period of non-funding.

**Cure Loan alternative.** The compromise provides a loan alternative — which Kastor identified as essential — but structures its terms so that dilution remains the economically rational choice for a member acting in good faith. The loan bears interest at SOFR + 400 bps (above Kastor's requested market rate of SOFR + 250 bps but below a purely punitive rate), must be repaid in full within 12 months, is secured by the borrowing member's entire JV interest, automatically converts to equity (dilution) on default, and may be used only once in any 24-month period. This gives Kastor a genuine bridge for temporary liquidity constraints (its core concern) while ensuring that persistent or repeated non-funding still results in dilution (Meridian's core concern). The automatic conversion on default means the end state is the same as dilution if the borrower does not repay, and the single-use limitation prevents serial use of the loan to defer funding indefinitely.

**Dilution cap and forced-sale backstop (15%).** This is the most contested element. Meridian's opening position was uncapped dilution, with a stated floor of a 20% annual cap; Kastor's opening position was a 10% annual cap. The compromise lands at 15% per annum — the midpoint — and pairs it with a forced-sale backstop drawn directly from the comparable-transaction data: in the most directly comparable 55/45 joint venture in the Frazier Whitmore dataset, dilution was capped at 15% per annum with an automatic forced-sale mechanism triggered when cumulative dilution exceeded 15% in any 24-month period. The forced-sale backstop is the key to making a 15% cap acceptable to Meridian: it converts the cap from a shield for indefinite free-riding into a bridge to exit. A member that consistently declines to fund cannot retain its equity position behind the cap indefinitely — once cumulative dilution reaches 15% in 24 months, the funding member may require the diluted member to sell its interest at fair market value. Kastor gains a dilution cap that protects against punitive single-event dilution (its concern); Meridian gains the assurance that a serial non-funder is bought out rather than protected forever (its concern). Both sides move from their opening positions: Kastor from 10% to 15%, Meridian from uncapped/20% to 15%.

### B. Summary of the parties' positions

**Meridian's opening position.** A two-year post-exit non-compete covering all specialty polymer coatings across all of North America. Meridian's rationale is that its contributed IP and customer relationships require meaningful protection against immediate post-exit competition by a former partner that has had access to proprietary formulations, customer lists, and commercial strategy.

**Kastor's opening position.** A one-year post-exit non-compete limited to the specific JV product lines and confined to territories where MKPoly had active sales at exit. Kastor's rationale is that its broader global chemicals business includes many product lines that do not compete with MKPoly, and that an overbroad non-compete would unreasonably restrain its pre-existing operations and create enforceability risk.

### C. Negotiation rationale

**Duration (18 months).** The compromise lands at 18 months — the midpoint between Meridian's two years and Kastor's one year. Eighteen months sits squarely within the one-to-two-year safe harbor that courts in Delaware and Texas (the two jurisdictions most likely to govern MKPoly, given its Delaware formation and Houston operational headquarters) recognize as presumptively reasonable for non-competes ancillary to the sale or disposition of a business interest. The multi-jurisdiction enforceability analysis in the record confirms that 18 months is well within the permissible range in all four relevant jurisdictions (Delaware, Texas, New York, and, under the LLC dissociation exception, California). Meridian concedes six months from its opening position; Kastor concedes six months from its opening position — an even split.

**Product scope (JV products plus substantially similar or directly competitive products).** The compromise rejects both extremes. Meridian's "all specialty polymer coatings" formulation is broader than the JV's actual business and would capture product categories MKPoly may never have entered, creating enforceability risk under the "no broader than necessary" standard applied in Delaware and Texas. Kastor's "specific JV product lines only" formulation is too narrow and would invite gamesmanship — a departing member could immediately launch a functionally identical product under a different name. The compromise defines the Restricted Business by reference to the products MKPoly actually developed, manufactured, or sold at exit (tethered to Schedule 2.04), plus a "substantially similar or directly competitive" penumbra that prevents circumvention while remaining tied to the JV's actual business. This formulation is supported by the enforceability analysis, which recommends exactly this structure, and it is narrower than Meridian's opening position (excluding unrelated coatings categories) while broader than Kastor's (preventing adjacent-product end-runs). The explicit carve-out for each party's pre-existing non-competing business lines directly addresses Kastor's concern about its diversified global chemicals operations.

**Geographic scope (United States, Canada, and jurisdictions exceeding a $500,000 revenue threshold in 24 months).** The compromise adopts the hybrid geographic formulation recommended in the enforceability analysis. It is anchored to MKPoly's actual commercial presence (satisfying the nexus requirements of Delaware, Texas, and California's LLC dissociation exception) rather than to arbitrary political boundaries, but it provides substantive North American coverage: if MKPoly achieves its projected sales distribution, the practical coverage will be essentially coextensive with Meridian's "North America" proposal. The $500,000 de minimis threshold eliminates jurisdictions where MKPoly had only incidental sales (addressing Kastor's overbreadth concern), and the 24-month lookback provides certainty. Meridian concedes the categorical "all of North America" formulation in favor of a footprint-tied definition; Kastor concedes the narrow "active sales territories only" formulation in favor of a broader, threshold-based North American scope.

**Enforceability protections.** The compromise incorporates a Delaware choice-of-law provision, a mandatory forum-selection clause channeling enforcement to the Delaware Court of Chancery or Harris County, Texas, a severability and reformation authorization, and a cross-reference to Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors). These provisions maximize enforceability in the primary governing jurisdictions and mitigate the California public-policy risk by channeling enforcement away from California courts. The non-compete is expressly characterized as ancillary to a member's exit or dissociation, which is the predicate for the more deferential sale-of-business standard in all four jurisdictions and for California's LLC dissociation exception.

## VI. Provision 4 — Exit Valuation Methodology

**JVA v.4 location:** Article X, § 10.01 (Triggering Events), § 10.03 (Valuation), § 10.04 (Payment Terms).

### A. Proposed compromise clause language

The following language is drafted to replace Section 10.01 and Section 10.03 of JVA v.4. Section 10.04 (Payment Terms) is retained as currently drafted.

**Section 10.01 — Triggering Events**

A buyout or dissolution procedure under this Article X shall be initiated upon the occurrence of one of the following events (each, a "**Triggering Event**"):

**(a)** A Change of Control of either Member;

**(b)** A Material Breach by either Member that remains uncured after the applicable cure period set forth in Section 11.02;

**(c)** A Deadlock on a Reserved Matter that has not been resolved through the escalation and mediation procedures set forth in Section 4.04(a) and (b); or

**(d)** An Involuntary Transfer of all or any portion of a Member's Membership Interest (by operation of law, bankruptcy, judicial order, or otherwise).

For purposes of this Section 10.01, the following shall **not** constitute a Change of Control or an Involuntary Transfer: (i) a reorganization of a Member's corporate structure (including a transfer of a Member's Membership Interest to a wholly owned Affiliate) that does not change the ultimate beneficial ownership or control of such Member's interest; or (ii) a merger, consolidation, or similar transaction involving a Member's parent company, provided that the surviving entity assumes all of the Member's obligations under this Agreement.

**Section 10.03 — Valuation**

**(a) Methodology.** The Fair Market Value of the Company shall be determined by multiplying the Company's trailing twelve (12) month EBITDA (calculated in accordance with GAAP as consistently applied in the Company's audited financial statements) by 7.0 (the "**Agreed Multiple**"). EBITDA shall be the primary and foundational valuation metric. No discounted cash flow analysis, comparable-company analysis, or other forward-looking methodology shall be used as the primary valuation methodology; such methodologies may be used by the Independent Appraiser only as supplementary cross-checks. No minority discount or marketability discount shall be applied to the Fair Market Value of a Member's Membership Interest.

**(b) Independent Appraiser.** The Fair Market Value shall be determined by a single, jointly selected, nationally recognized independent accounting or valuation firm (the "**Independent Appraiser**"). The Independent Appraiser shall not be the Company's then-current auditor (currently Frazier Whitmore & Co., LLP) and shall not have provided material services to the Company or to either Member or their respective Affiliates during the preceding twenty-four (24) months.

**(c) Selection of the Independent Appraiser.** The Members shall use good-faith efforts to mutually agree on a single Independent Appraiser within thirty (30) calendar days following the occurrence of the applicable Triggering Event. If the Members are unable to agree within such thirty (30)-day period, the Independent Appraiser shall be appointed by the American Arbitration Association upon the application of either Member. The Independent Appraiser shall determine the trailing twelve (12) month EBITDA (resolving any disputes as to normalization adjustments in accordance with GAAP as applied in the Company's most recent audited financial statements) and shall confirm the resulting Fair Market Value by applying the Agreed Multiple, together with a written valuation report setting forth the determination and the bases therefor.

**(d) Baseball Arbitration.** If either Member disputes the Independent Appraiser's determination of Fair Market Value, the following procedure shall apply:

> (i) Each Member shall, within twenty (20) calendar days of receipt of the Independent Appraiser's report, submit to the Independent Appraiser a single sealed final valuation number (each, a "**Final Offer**");

> (ii) The Independent Appraiser shall select the Final Offer that is closest to its own determination of Fair Market Value, and the selected Final Offer shall be the Fair Market Value for all purposes under this Agreement; and

> (iii) The Independent Appraiser's selection shall be final and binding upon the Members, absent fraud or manifest error.

**(e) Multiple Collar.** Notwithstanding anything to the contrary, the EBITDA multiple applied in any determination of Fair Market Value under this Section 10.03 (whether by the Independent Appraiser or through baseball arbitration) shall not be less than 6.5× or greater than 7.5× (the "**Collar**"). The Independent Appraiser may adjust the Agreed Multiple within the Collar based on the Company's growth profile, margin trajectory, and comparable transaction data at the time of the Triggering Event, provided that any such adjustment is supported by a written explanation. Any Final Offer that applies a multiple outside the Collar shall be non-compliant and shall be excluded from the baseball arbitration.

**(f) Member Interest Valuation.** The Fair Market Value of a Member's Membership Interest shall equal the Fair Market Value of the Company multiplied by such Member's Percentage Interest.

**(g) Costs.** The costs and expenses of the Independent Appraiser shall be borne equally by the Members, except that the cost of any baseball arbitration proceeding initiated by a Member whose Final Offer is not selected shall be borne by that Member.

### B. Summary of the parties' positions

**Meridian's opening position.** A 6.5× trailing 12-month EBITDA multiple; the JV's own auditor (Frazier Whitmore & Co., LLP) serving as the sole valuation appraiser; trigger events limited to change of control and material breach. Meridian's rationale is that using the existing auditor reduces cost and leverages institutional knowledge, and that 6.5× is a conservative, defensible multiple for a development-stage venture.

**Kastor's opening position.** An independent Big Four appraiser (not the JV auditor) selected through a three-appraiser baseball-arbitration mechanism; a market-determined EBITDA multiple (with flexibility within a 6.5×–7.5× range); trigger events expanded to include deadlock and involuntary transfer. Kastor's rationale is that the JV auditor has an inherent conflict of interest in a buyout scenario and that the integrity of the buyout mechanism requires genuine appraiser independence.

### C. Negotiation rationale

**Appraiser independence.** The compromise adopts Kastor's core requirement: the valuation appraiser is an independent firm that is not the JV auditor and has no pre-existing engagement with either party. This directly addresses the independence concern Kastor identified as non-negotiable — an entity that serves as the JV's auditor maintains an ongoing commercial relationship with the Company and, through a Meridian-controlled management structure, a primary reporting relationship to one party, creating an inherent conflict in a buyout where the parties' interests are directly adverse. Meridian concedes this point, recognizing that the independence concern has technical merit and that the integrity of the buyout mechanism depends on it. In exchange, the compromise adopts a simplified selection mechanism (mutual agreement on a single firm within 30 days, with AAA appointment as fallback) rather than the more complex three-appraiser process Kastor originally proposed — a simplification Meridian sought and Kastor indicated it would accept, provided the core independence requirement is maintained.

**EBITDA multiple (7.0×).** The compromise sets the Agreed Multiple at 7.0×, which is near the 7.1× median of the Frazier Whitmore comparable-transaction dataset (range 6.2×–8.1×) and within the 6.5×–7.5× band Kastor identified as acceptable. Meridian moves from 6.5× to 7.0× — a meaningful concession that brings the multiple to the market center, supported by independent data and defensible to Meridian's board. Kastor, which had flexibility on the specific number, obtains a multiple near the median rather than at the conservative end. The 7.0× figure is also the multiple at which the Frazier Whitmore illustrative analysis shows the JV's base-case enterprise value modestly exceeding its initial capitalization, reflecting a fair going-concern valuation. The compromise permits the Independent Appraiser to adjust the multiple within a 6.5×–7.5× collar based on the Company's performance and market conditions at exit, giving Kastor a degree of market-responsive flexibility while bounding the outcome to the comparable range — addressing Meridian's requirement that the process not produce outlier results.

**EBITDA-only methodology.** Both parties' positions favor a trailing 12-month EBITDA methodology, and the compromise codifies EBITDA as the primary and foundational metric, expressly prohibiting discounted cash flow or other forward-looking methods as the primary methodology. EBITDA is calculated from audited financial statements, making it objective and verifiable, and it captures the value of Meridian's contributed IP (which flows through to revenue and margins) without requiring a separate, contentious IP valuation at exit. This addresses Meridian's concern about projection-based manipulation in a disputed exit and reflects the market preference for EBITDA confirmed in the comparable transactions.

**Trigger events.** The compromise expands the trigger events to include deadlock (on a Reserved Matter, after exhaustion of the escalation and mediation process) and involuntary transfer, as Kastor sought. Deadlock as a trigger is consistent with the tiered deadlock resolution in Section 4.04 and provides a terminal step for Reserved Matter deadlocks that cannot be resolved — without it, the JV would face indefinite paralysis. Involuntary transfer as a trigger protects both parties against being forced to operate the JV with an unknown, unchosen counterparty (such as a bankruptcy trustee or creditor). Meridian concedes these additional triggers, which cost little in practice and are consistent with the comparable transactions. The compromise includes carve-outs for intra-group restructurings that do not change ultimate beneficial ownership, protecting both parties from inadvertently triggering a buyout through ordinary corporate reorganizations.

**Baseball arbitration and collar.** The compromise retains Kastor's preferred baseball-arbitration mechanism for disputed determinations, which incentivizes both parties to submit reasonable figures because the appraiser selects only the closer submission. The 6.5×–7.5× collar bounds the range of possible outcomes to the comparable-transaction data, preventing outlier lowball or inflation results — a guardrail Meridian requires and that is consistent with the range both parties identified as acceptable.

### B. Summary of the parties' positions

**Meridian's opening position.** An exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to co-developed IP for Meridian only; no Kastor license to co-developed IP; no sublicensing by Kastor; no post-exit license of Meridian's contributed IP to Kastor. Meridian's rationale is that its 23 contributed IP items (independently appraised at $28.6M, including the proprietary CR-7 and CR-9 coating formulations embedded in JV products) are the JV's core competitive differentiator, and that any structure permitting Kastor to sublicense co-developed IP — or improvements derived from Meridian's contributed IP — to Meridian's North American competitors would destroy the competitive moat these formulations provide.

**Kastor's opening position.** A non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to co-developed IP for both parties; territorial sublicensing rights (Meridian: North America; Kastor: rest of world); improvements to contributed IP treated as co-developed IP; a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to use the other party's contributed IP in its own operations (without sublicensing). Kastor's rationale is that its $43.8M contribution (including €38M in cash that directly funds the JV's R&D) entitles it to meaningful rights to use the intellectual property created during the JV's life, and that without sublicensing rights its license would be commercially unworkable for a global company that operates through manufacturing and distribution partners.

**Common ground.** Both parties agree that contributed IP reverts to the contributing party upon exit or dissolution.

### C. Negotiation rationale

This is the most complex of the five provisions and the one on which each side places its highest priority. The compromise is built around a single organizing principle: Kastor receives a broad, commercially meaningful license to co-developed IP with territorial sublicensing outside North America (its core objective), while Meridian's contributed formulations are protected by layered restrictions that ensure no pathway — direct or indirect — exists for those formulations to reach Meridian's North American competitors (its core objective).

**Co-developed IP license to both parties.** The compromise grants each party a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual, irrevocable license to all co-developed IP — the structure Kastor requires and that Meridian accepts for internal use. The license is non-exclusive (Meridian retains full rights to use the same co-developed IP), royalty-free (reflecting that both parties have already funded the IP's development through their capital contributions), and perpetual (reflecting the indefinite useful life of intellectual property). Meridian concedes its opening position of an exclusive license to Meridian only; Kastor obtains the bilateral license it identified as foundational to its participation.

**Territorial sublicensing with layered competitor protections.** The compromise permits each party to sublicense co-developed IP within its respective territory (Meridian: North America; Kastor: rest of world), giving Kastor the sublicensing rights it needs to commercialize JV technology through manufacturing and distribution partners outside North America. Three overlapping mechanisms protect Meridian's competitive position: (i) a global, reciprocal prohibition on sublicensing to any entity on Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors) or their affiliates or successors, regardless of territory; (ii) a 25% North American revenue negative covenant that captures any sublicensee with material North American specialty coatings exposure, even if not named on Schedule 12.05 — a dynamic anti-circumvention mechanism that addresses the risk of the Restricted Competitors list becoming stale as new entrants emerge; and (iii) a reciprocal structure, so that the same restrictions bind Meridian, addressing Kastor's symmetry concern. The restrictions apply regardless of where the sublicense would be exercised, closing the loophole by which a competitor could receive a sublicense nominally outside North America and then use it in its North American operations. Meridian concedes sublicensing by Kastor outside North America (its opening position was no sublicensing at all); Kastor concedes the Restricted Competitors and 25% revenue restrictions (its opening position was unrestricted sublicensing within its territory). The audit right gives Meridian a practical enforcement mechanism.

**Contributed IP: reversion with a reciprocal transitional license.** Contributed IP reverts to the contributing party (agreed). The compromise adds a reciprocal, 24-month, non-sublicensable transitional license limited to Legacy Products — the products the JV actively commercialized in the 12 months before exit. This addresses the legitimate commercial concern that an abrupt reversion could disrupt customer supply of JV-legacy products, and it does so symmetrically (both parties receive the same limited license to the other's contributed IP for legacy products). The transitional license is heavily restricted: it is non-sublicensable (so Kastor cannot pass Meridian's formulations to any third party, let alone a competitor), time-limited (24 months, not perpetual), and purpose-limited (legacy products only, not new or improved products). This structure respects Meridian's requirement that Kastor not obtain a perpetual license to Meridian's contributed formulations, while giving both parties a workable transition period. Kastor concedes that it does not receive a perpetual license to Meridian's contributed IP (its opening position sought one for internal use); Meridian concedes a limited transitional license (its opening position sought no license at all), but one so restricted that it creates no competitive risk.

**Improvements to contributed IP.** Both parties flagged the treatment of JV-funded improvements to contributed IP as a central unresolved issue, particularly given that Meridian's $28.6M contributed IP will almost certainly be the subject of JV-funded R&D and improvements. The compromise adopts a "primarily derivative" classification test: an improvement that builds primarily on one party's contributed IP is treated as an improvement to that party's IP (reverting to the contributor on exit), while improvements that build equally on both parties' IP or are independent are treated as co-developed IP. The non-contributing party receives a non-exclusive, perpetual license to improvements on the other's contributed IP, with sublicensing permitted within its territory subject to the same Restricted Competitors and 25% revenue restrictions that govern co-developed IP. This balances the parties' interests: Meridian retains ownership and reversion of improvements to its core formulations (so an improved CR-7 formulation remains Meridian's property), while Kastor obtains a meaningful license to use and sublicense (outside North America, to non-competitors) the improvements that its capital helped fund — capturing a return on its R&D investment without gaining the ability to place improved Meridian formulations in the hands of North American competitors. Kastor concedes that improvements to Meridian's IP revert to Meridian (its opening position treated all improvements as co-developed IP); Meridian concedes that Kastor receives a license with sublicensing rights to those improvements outside North America (its opening position granted Kastor no license to co-developed IP at all).

**Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors).** The compromise provides for a mutually agreed initial list of specifically identified competitors (and their affiliates and successors), limited to entities competing in the North American specialty polymer coatings market, with annual updates by mutual agreement (or Management Committee resolution as a Reserved Matter if the parties cannot agree). This addresses Kastor's requirement that the list be limited to named entities (not broad categories) and updated periodically, while giving Meridian a defined, enforceable set of protected competitors. The list is to be finalized by the parties prior to execution, drawing on both the North American competitor analysis and the broader industry list currently in the v.4 draft.

For purposes of accommodating multi-currency treasury operations, a Member may satisfy its funding obligation in Euros at the European Central Bank reference rate published on the date of the Capital Call Notice, with conversion to U.S. Dollars handled by the Company at the Company’s cost; the Member’s Capital Account shall be credited in the U.S. Dollar equivalent so determined.  
          (d) Failure to Fund; Penalty Interest.         If a Member fails to fund its pro-rata share by the Funding Deadline (a         “        Defaulting Member    ”    ), penalty interest shall accrue automatically on the unfunded amount at a rate equal to the greater of (i) the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (    “        SOFR    ”    ) plus five hundred (500) basis points per annum, or (ii) ten percent (10%) per annum, from the Funding Deadline until the date the unfunded amount (together with accrued penalty interest) is funded or converted to equity under subsection (f). Penalty interest shall be payable to the Company for the benefit of the Non-Defaulting Member upon funding or upon dilution, as applicable.  
          (e) Cure Loan Alternative.         At any time during the period beginning on the Funding Deadline and ending on the date that is thirty (30) calendar days after the Funding Deadline (the         “        Cure Window    ”    ), the Non-Defaulting Member may, at its election, advance the Defaulting Member’s unfunded share as a loan (the         “        Cure Loan    ”    ). If the Non-Defaulting Member elects to make a Cure Loan:  
          The Cure Loan shall bear interest at SOFR plus four hundred (400) basis points per annum, compounding quarterly;  
          The Cure Loan shall be repayable in full (principal plus accrued interest) within twelve (12) months of disbursement, in a single lump sum, with no extensions or refinancing;  
          The Defaulting Member shall grant to the Non-Defaulting Member a first-priority security interest in its entire Membership Interest as collateral for the Cure Loan, and the Non-Defaulting Member shall file UCC financing statements promptly upon disbursement;  
          If the Defaulting Member fails to repay the Cure Loan in full within the twelve (12)-month repayment period, the outstanding balance (principal plus accrued interest) shall automatically convert to an additional Capital Contribution of the Non-Defaulting Member, and the Percentage Interests shall be adjusted under subsection (f) as if the Non-Defaulting Member had funded the unfunded amount as additional capital;  
          The Cure Loan alternative shall be available to any Member no more than once in any twenty-four (24)-month period; and  
          If a Cure Loan is made, no dilution shall occur until and unless the Cure Loan is not repaid and converts to equity under subsection (e)(iv).  
          (f) Dilution.         If the Defaulting Member does not fund its unfunded share (together with accrued penalty interest) by the end of the Cure Window and no Cure Loan is in effect, or if a Cure Loan converts to equity under subsection (e)(iv), the Non-Defaulting Member may fund the unfunded amount as an additional Capital Contribution, and the Percentage Interests of the Members shall be adjusted on a pro-rata basis to reflect the additional capital contributed by the Non-Defaulting Member relative to the aggregate Capital Contributions of both Members. All governance rights, distribution entitlements, and allocation rights shall be automatically adjusted to reflect the revised Percentage Interests.  
          (g) Annual Dilution Cap; Forced-Sale Backstop.         Notwithstanding subsection (f), in no event shall a Defaulting Member’s Percentage Interest be diluted by more than fifteen percent (15%) of such Member’s then-current Percentage Interest in any single Fiscal Year (the         “        Annual Dilution Cap    ”    ). If the adjustment that would otherwise result from subsection (f) would exceed the Annual Dilution Cap, the excess shall be treated as a Cure Loan under subsection (e) until the next Fiscal Year. If a Member’s cumulative dilution reaches fifteen percent (15%) of its then-current Percentage Interest in any twenty-four (24)-month period, the Non-Defaulting Member may, by written notice, require the diluted Member to sell all (but not less than all) of its Membership Interest to the Non-Defaulting Member at the Fair Market Value determined in accordance with Section 10.03 (the         “        Forced-Sale Right    ”    ). The diluted Member shall comply with the buyout procedures of Article X. The Annual Dilution Cap and the Forced-Sale Right together ensure that a Member cannot indefinitely retain its equity position while declining to fund its pro-rata share of Capital Calls.  
        B. Summary of the parties’ positions  
          Meridian’s opening position.         A 30-day funding period; immediate, uncapped, pro-rata dilution upon failure to fund; no loan alternative; no dilution cap. Meridian’s rationale is that uncapped dilution is the strongest incentive for prompt funding and that a cap or market-rate loan would allow a non-funding partner to free-ride on the funding partner’s capital while retaining governance rights disproportionate to its economic stake.  
          Kastor’s opening position.         A 90-day cure period; dilution capped at 10% per annum; a loan alternative at a market rate (SOFR + 250 bps) available before dilution. Kastor’s rationale is that its multi-currency treasury operations and Swiss regulatory requirements can cause short-term funding delays unrelated to financial capacity, and that temporary liquidity timing constraints should not permanently destroy a member’s equity position.  
        C. Negotiation rationale  
          Funding window (75 days total).         The compromise provides a 15-day notice period plus a 60-day funding period, for a total of 75 days from the Capital Call Notice to the dilution trigger. This sits at the midpoint between Meridian’s 30-day opening and Kastor’s 90-day request, and it falls within the 45–60 day cure range observed in the comparable transactions. The 75-day window gives Kastor reasonable time to arrange U.S. Dollar funding, including foreign-exchange conversion and any regulatory filings, while preserving the urgency of the funding obligation. The Euro-funding accommodation in subsection (c) further addresses Kastor’s operational concern by allowing it to fund in Euros at a locked reference rate with conversion handled by the Company, effectively removing the foreign-exchange conversion burden that was the principal justification for a 90-day window. Meridian concedes a longer window than it preferred; Kastor concedes a shorter window than it preferred, but gains the Euro-funding accommodation that materially eases its treasury burden.  
          Penalty interest.         Penalty interest at SOFR + 500 bps (or 10%, whichever is greater) accrues on unfunded amounts during the cure period. This compensates the Company and the funding member for the time value of delayed capital and imposes a meaningful economic cost on late funding, preserving the incentive to fund promptly that Meridian requires. The rate is above market, but it is a penalty for non-performance rather than a loan rate, and it applies only to the period of non-funding.  
          Cure Loan alternative.         The compromise provides a loan alternative — which Kastor identified as essential — but structures its terms so that dilution remains the economically rational choice for a member acting in good faith. The loan bears interest at SOFR + 400 bps (above Kastor’s requested market rate of SOFR + 250 bps but below a purely punitive rate), must be repaid in full within 12 months, is secured by the borrowing member’s entire JV interest, automatically converts to equity (dilution) on default, and may be used only once in any 24-month period. This gives Kastor a genuine bridge for temporary liquidity constraints (its core concern) while ensuring that persistent or repeated non-funding still results in dilution (Meridian’s core concern). The automatic conversion on default means the end state is the same as dilution if the borrower does not repay, and the single-use limitation prevents serial use of the loan to defer funding indefinitely.  
          Dilution cap and forced-sale backstop (15%).         This is the most contested element. Meridian’s opening position was uncapped dilution, with a stated floor of a 20% annual cap; Kastor’s opening position was a 10% annual cap. The compromise lands at 15% per annum — the midpoint — and pairs it with a forced-sale backstop drawn directly from the comparable-transaction data: in the most directly comparable 55/45 joint venture in the Frazier Whitmore dataset, dilution was capped at 15% per annum with an automatic forced-sale mechanism triggered when cumulative dilution exceeded 15% in any 24-month period. The forced-sale backstop is the key to making a 15% cap acceptable to Meridian: it converts the cap from a shield for indefinite free-riding into a bridge to exit. A member that consistently declines to fund cannot retain its equity position behind the cap indefinitely — once cumulative dilution reaches 15% in 24 months, the funding member may require the diluted member to sell its interest at fair market value. Kastor gains a dilution cap that protects against punitive single-event dilution (its concern); Meridian gains the assurance that a serial non-funder is bought out rather than protected forever (its concern). Both sides move from their opening positions: Kastor from 10% to 15%, Meridian from uncapped/20% to 15%.

        B. Summary of the parties’ positions  
          Meridian’s opening position.         A two-year post-exit non-compete covering all specialty polymer coatings across all of North America. Meridian’s rationale is that its contributed IP and customer relationships require meaningful protection against immediate post-exit competition by a former partner that has had access to proprietary formulations, customer lists, and commercial strategy.  
          Kastor’s opening position.         A one-year post-exit non-compete limited to the specific JV product lines and confined to territories where MKPoly had active sales at exit. Kastor’s rationale is that its broader global chemicals business includes many product lines that do not compete with MKPoly, and that an overbroad non-compete would unreasonably restrain its pre-existing operations and create enforceability risk.  
        C. Negotiation rationale  
          Duration (18 months).         The compromise lands at 18 months — the midpoint between Meridian’s two years and Kastor’s one year. Eighteen months sits squarely within the one-to-two-year safe harbor that courts in Delaware and Texas (the two jurisdictions most likely to govern MKPoly, given its Delaware formation and Houston operational headquarters) recognize as presumptively reasonable for non-competes ancillary to the sale or disposition of a business interest. The multi-jurisdiction enforceability analysis in the record confirms that 18 months is well within the permissible range in all four relevant jurisdictions (Delaware, Texas, New York, and, under the LLC dissociation exception, California). Meridian concedes six months from its opening position; Kastor concedes six months from its opening position — an even split.  
          Product scope (JV products plus substantially similar or directly competitive products).         The compromise rejects both extremes. Meridian’s         “    all specialty polymer coatings    ”         formulation is broader than the JV’s actual business and would capture product categories MKPoly may never have entered, creating enforceability risk under the         “    no broader than necessary    ”         standard applied in Delaware and Texas. Kastor’s         “    specific JV product lines only    ”         formulation is too narrow and would invite gamesmanship — a departing member could immediately launch a functionally identical product under a different name. The compromise defines the Restricted Business by reference to the products MKPoly actually developed, manufactured, or sold at exit (tethered to Schedule 2.04), plus a         “    substantially similar or directly competitive    ”         penumbra that prevents circumvention while remaining tied to the JV’s actual business. This formulation is supported by the enforceability analysis, which recommends exactly this structure, and it is narrower than Meridian’s opening position (excluding unrelated coatings categories) while broader than Kastor’s (preventing adjacent-product end-runs). The explicit carve-out for each party’s pre-existing non-competing business lines directly addresses Kastor’s concern about its diversified global chemicals operations.  
          Geographic scope (United States, Canada, and jurisdictions exceeding a $500,000 revenue threshold in 24 months).         The compromise adopts the hybrid geographic formulation recommended in the enforceability analysis. It is anchored to MKPoly’s actual commercial presence (satisfying the nexus requirements of Delaware, Texas, and California’s LLC dissociation exception) rather than to arbitrary political boundaries, but it provides substantive North American coverage: if MKPoly achieves its projected sales distribution, the practical coverage will be essentially coextensive with Meridian’s         “    North America    ”         proposal. The $500,000 de minimis threshold eliminates jurisdictions where MKPoly had only incidental sales (addressing Kastor’s overbreadth concern), and the 24-month lookback provides certainty. Meridian concedes the categorical         “    all of North America    ”         formulation in favor of a footprint-tied definition; Kastor concedes the narrow         “    active sales territories only    ”         formulation in favor of a broader, threshold-based North American scope.  
          Enforceability protections.         The compromise incorporates a Delaware choice-of-law provision, a mandatory forum-selection clause channeling enforcement to the Delaware Court of Chancery or Harris County, Texas, a severability and reformation authorization, and a cross-reference to Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors). These provisions maximize enforceability in the primary governing jurisdictions and mitigate the California public-policy risk by channeling enforcement away from California courts. The non-compete is expressly characterized as ancillary to a member’s exit or dissociation, which is the predicate for the more deferential sale-of-business standard in all four jurisdictions and for California’s LLC dissociation exception.

          JVA v.4 location:         Article X, § 10.01 (Triggering Events), § 10.03 (Valuation), § 10.04 (Payment Terms).  
       A. Proposed compromise clause language  
      The following language is drafted to replace Section 10.01 and Section 10.03 of JVA v.4. Section 10.04 (Payment Terms) is retained as currently drafted.  
          Section 10.01 — Triggering Events  
      A buyout or dissolution procedure under this Article X shall be initiated upon the occurrence of one of the following events (each, a         “        Triggering Event    ”    ):  
          (a)         A Change of Control of either Member;  
          (b)         A Material Breach by either Member that remains uncured after the applicable cure period set forth in Section 11.02;  
          (c)         A Deadlock on a Reserved Matter that has not been resolved through the escalation and mediation procedures set forth in Section 4.04(a) and (b); or

          Section 10.03 — Valuation  
          (a) Methodology.         The Fair Market Value of the Company shall be determined by multiplying the Company’s trailing twelve (12) month EBITDA (calculated in accordance with GAAP as consistently applied in the Company’s audited financial statements) by 7.0 (the         “        Agreed Multiple    ”    ). EBITDA shall be the primary and foundational valuation metric. No discounted cash flow analysis, comparable-company analysis, or other forward-looking methodology shall be used as the primary valuation methodology; such methodologies may be used by the Independent Appraiser only as supplementary cross-checks. No minority discount or marketability discount shall be applied to the Fair Market Value of a Member’s Membership Interest.  
          (b) Independent Appraiser.         The Fair Market Value shall be determined by a single, jointly selected, nationally recognized independent accounting or valuation firm (the         “        Independent Appraiser    ”    ). The Independent Appraiser shall not be the Company’s then-current auditor (currently Frazier Whitmore & Co., LLP) and shall not have provided material services to the Company or to either Member or their respective Affiliates during the preceding twenty-four (24) months.  
          (c) Selection of the Independent Appraiser.         The Members shall use good-faith efforts to mutually agree on a single Independent Appraiser within thirty (30) calendar days following the occurrence of the applicable Triggering Event. If the Members are unable to agree within such thirty (30)-day period, the Independent Appraiser shall be appointed by the American Arbitration Association upon the application of either Member. The Independent Appraiser shall determine the trailing twelve (12) month EBITDA (resolving any disputes as to normalization adjustments in accordance with GAAP as applied in the Company’s most recent audited financial statements) and shall confirm the resulting Fair Market Value by applying the Agreed Multiple, together with a written valuation report setting forth the determination and the bases therefor.  
          (d) Baseball Arbitration.         If either Member disputes the Independent Appraiser’s determination of Fair Market Value, the following procedure shall apply:  
          Each Member shall, within twenty (20) calendar days of receipt of the Independent Appraiser’s report, submit to the Independent Appraiser a single sealed final valuation number (each, a         “        Final Offer    ”    );  
          The Independent Appraiser shall select the Final Offer that is closest to its own determination of Fair Market Value, and the selected Final Offer shall be the Fair Market Value for all purposes under this Agreement; and  
          The Independent Appraiser’s selection shall be final and binding upon the Members, absent fraud or manifest error.  
          (e) Multiple Collar.         Notwithstanding anything to the contrary, the EBITDA multiple applied in any determination of Fair Market Value under this Section 10.03 (whether by the Independent Appraiser or through baseball arbitration) shall not be less than 6.5× or greater than 7.5× (the         “        Collar    ”    ). The Independent Appraiser may adjust the Agreed Multiple within the Collar based on the Company’s growth profile, margin trajectory, and comparable transaction data at the time of the Triggering Event, provided that any such adjustment is supported by a written explanation. Any Final Offer that applies a multiple outside the Collar shall be non-compliant and shall be excluded from the baseball arbitration.  
          (f) Member Interest Valuation.         The Fair Market Value of a Member’s Membership Interest shall equal the Fair Market Value of the Company multiplied by such Member’s Percentage Interest.  
          (g) Costs.         The costs and expenses of the Independent Appraiser shall be borne equally by the Members, except that the cost of any baseball arbitration proceeding initiated by a Member whose Final Offer is not selected shall be borne by that Member.  
        B. Summary of the parties’ positions  
          Meridian’s opening position.         A 6.5× trailing 12-month EBITDA multiple; the JV’s own auditor (Frazier Whitmore & Co., LLP) serving as the sole valuation appraiser; trigger events limited to change of control and material breach. Meridian’s rationale is that using the existing auditor reduces cost and leverages institutional knowledge, and that 6.5× is a conservative, defensible multiple for a development-stage venture.  
          Kastor’s opening position.         An independent Big Four appraiser (not the JV auditor) selected through a three-appraiser baseball-arbitration mechanism; a market-determined EBITDA multiple (with flexibility within a 6.5×–7.5× range); trigger events expanded to include deadlock and involuntary transfer. Kastor’s rationale is that the JV auditor has an inherent conflict of interest in a buyout scenario and that the integrity of the buyout mechanism requires genuine appraiser independence.  
        C. Negotiation rationale  
          Appraiser independence.         The compromise adopts Kastor’s core requirement: the valuation appraiser is an independent firm that is not the JV auditor and has no pre-existing engagement with either party. This directly addresses the independence concern Kastor identified as non-negotiable — an entity that serves as the JV’s auditor maintains an ongoing commercial relationship with the Company and, through a Meridian-controlled management structure, a primary reporting relationship to one party, creating an inherent conflict in a buyout where the parties’ interests are directly adverse. Meridian concedes this point, recognizing that the independence concern has technical merit and that the integrity of the buyout mechanism depends on it. In exchange, the compromise adopts a simplified selection mechanism (mutual agreement on a single firm within 30 days, with AAA appointment as fallback) rather than the more complex three-appraiser process Kastor originally proposed — a simplification Meridian sought and Kastor indicated it would accept, provided the core independence requirement is maintained.  
          EBITDA multiple (7.0×).         The compromise sets the Agreed Multiple at 7.0×, which is near the 7.1× median of the Frazier Whitmore comparable-transaction dataset (range 6.2×–8.1×) and within the 6.5×–7.5× band Kastor identified as acceptable. Meridian moves from 6.5× to 7.0× — a meaningful concession that brings the multiple to the market center, supported by independent data and defensible to Meridian’s board. Kastor, which had flexibility on the specific number, obtains a multiple near the median rather than at the conservative end. The 7.0× figure is also the multiple at which the Frazier Whitmore illustrative analysis shows the JV’s base-case enterprise value modestly exceeding its initial capitalization, reflecting a fair going-concern valuation. The compromise permits the Independent Appraiser to adjust the multiple within a 6.5×–7.5× collar based on the Company’s performance and market conditions at exit, giving Kastor a degree of market-responsive flexibility while bounding the outcome to the comparable range — addressing Meridian’s requirement that the process not produce outlier results.  
          EBITDA-only methodology.         Both parties’ positions favor a trailing 12-month EBITDA methodology, and the compromise codifies EBITDA as the primary and foundational metric, expressly prohibiting discounted cash flow or other forward-looking methods as the primary methodology. EBITDA is calculated from audited financial statements, making it objective and verifiable, and it captures the value of Meridian’s contributed IP (which flows through to revenue and margins) without requiring a separate, contentious IP valuation at exit. This addresses Meridian’s concern about projection-based manipulation in a disputed exit and reflects the market preference for EBITDA confirmed in the comparable transactions.  
          Trigger events.         The compromise expands the trigger events to include deadlock (on a Reserved Matter, after exhaustion of the escalation and mediation process) and involuntary transfer, as Kastor sought. Deadlock as a trigger is consistent with the tiered deadlock resolution in Section 4.04 and provides a terminal step for Reserved Matter deadlocks that cannot be resolved — without it, the JV would face indefinite paralysis. Involuntary transfer as a trigger protects both parties against being forced to operate the JV with an unknown, unchosen counterparty (such as a bankruptcy trustee or creditor). Meridian concedes these additional triggers, which cost little in practice and are consistent with the comparable transactions. The compromise includes carve-outs for intra-group restructurings that do not change ultimate beneficial ownership, protecting both parties from inadvertently triggering a buyout through ordinary corporate reorganizations.  
          Baseball arbitration and collar.         The compromise retains Kastor’s preferred baseball-arbitration mechanism for disputed determinations, which incentivizes both parties to submit reasonable figures because the appraiser selects only the closer submission. The 6.5×–7.5× collar bounds the range of possible outcomes to the comparable-transaction data, preventing outlier lowball or inflation results — a guardrail Meridian requires and that is consistent with the range both parties identified as acceptable.

        B. Summary of the parties’ positions  
          Meridian’s opening position.         An exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to co-developed IP for Meridian only; no Kastor license to co-developed IP; no sublicensing by Kastor; no post-exit license of Meridian’s contributed IP to Kastor. Meridian’s rationale is that its 23 contributed IP items (independently appraised at $28.6M, including the proprietary CR-7 and CR-9 coating formulations embedded in JV products) are the JV’s core competitive differentiator, and that any structure permitting Kastor to sublicense co-developed IP — or improvements derived from Meridian’s contributed IP — to Meridian’s North American competitors would destroy the competitive moat these formulations provide.  
          Kastor’s opening position.         A non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to co-developed IP for both parties; territorial sublicensing rights (Meridian: North America; Kastor: rest of world); improvements to contributed IP treated as co-developed IP; a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to use the other party’s contributed IP in its own operations (without sublicensing). Kastor’s rationale is that its $43.8M contribution (including €38M in cash that directly funds the JV’s R&D) entitles it to meaningful rights to use the intellectual property created during the JV’s life, and that without sublicensing rights its license would be commercially unworkable for a global company that operates through manufacturing and distribution partners.  
          Common ground.         Both parties agree that contributed IP reverts to the contributing party upon exit or dissolution.  
        C. Negotiation rationale  
      This is the most complex of the five provisions and the one on which each side places its highest priority. The compromise is built around a single organizing principle: Kastor receives a broad, commercially meaningful license to co-developed IP with territorial sublicensing outside North America (its core objective), while Meridian’s contributed formulations are protected by layered restrictions that ensure no pathway — direct or indirect — exists for those formulations to reach Meridian’s North American competitors (its core objective).  
          Co-developed IP license to both parties.         The compromise grants each party a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual, irrevocable license to all co-developed IP — the structure Kastor requires and that Meridian accepts for internal use. The license is non-exclusive (Meridian retains full rights to use the same co-developed IP), royalty-free (reflecting that both parties have already funded the IP’s development through their capital contributions), and perpetual (reflecting the indefinite useful life of intellectual property). Meridian concedes its opening position of an exclusive license to Meridian only; Kastor obtains the bilateral license it identified as foundational to its participation.  
          Territorial sublicensing with layered competitor protections.         The compromise permits each party to sublicense co-developed IP within its respective territory (Meridian: North America; Kastor: rest of world), giving Kastor the sublicensing rights it needs to commercialize JV technology through manufacturing and distribution partners outside North America. Three overlapping mechanisms protect Meridian’s competitive position: (i) a global, reciprocal prohibition on sublicensing to any entity on Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors) or their affiliates or successors, regardless of territory; (ii) a 25% North American revenue negative covenant that captures any sublicensee with material North American specialty coatings exposure, even if not named on Schedule 12.05 — a dynamic anti-circumvention mechanism that addresses the risk of the Restricted Competitors list becoming stale as new entrants emerge; and (iii) a reciprocal structure, so that the same restrictions bind Meridian, addressing Kastor’s symmetry concern. The restrictions apply regardless of where the sublicense would be exercised, closing the loophole by which a competitor could receive a sublicense nominally outside North America and then use it in its North American operations. Meridian concedes sublicensing by Kastor outside North America (its opening position was no sublicensing at all); Kastor concedes the Restricted Competitors and 25% revenue restrictions (its opening position was unrestricted sublicensing within its territory). The audit right gives Meridian a practical enforcement mechanism.  
          Contributed IP: reversion with a reciprocal transitional license.         Contributed IP reverts to the contributing party (agreed). The compromise adds a reciprocal, 24-month, non-sublicensable transitional license limited to Legacy Products — the products the JV actively commercialized in the 12 months before exit. This addresses the legitimate commercial concern that an abrupt reversion could disrupt customer supply of JV-legacy products, and it does so symmetrically (both parties receive the same limited license to the other’s contributed IP for legacy products). The transitional license is heavily restricted: it is non-sublicensable (so Kastor cannot pass Meridian’s formulations to any third party, let alone a competitor), time-limited (24 months, not perpetual), and purpose-limited (legacy products only, not new or improved products). This structure respects Meridian’s requirement that Kastor not obtain a perpetual license to Meridian’s contributed formulations, while giving both parties a workable transition period. Kastor concedes that it does not receive a perpetual license to Meridian’s contributed IP (its opening position sought one for internal use); Meridian concedes a limited transitional license (its opening position sought no license at all), but one so restricted that it creates no competitive risk.  
          Improvements to contributed IP.         Both parties flagged the treatment of JV-funded improvements to contributed IP as a central unresolved issue, particularly given that Meridian’s $28.6M contributed IP will almost certainly be the subject of JV-funded R&D and improvements. The compromise adopts a         “    primarily derivative    ”         classification test: an improvement that builds primarily on one party’s contributed IP is treated as an improvement to that party’s IP (reverting to the contributor on exit), while improvements that build equally on both parties’ IP or are independent are treated as co-developed IP. The non-contributing party receives a non-exclusive, perpetual license to improvements on the other’s contributed IP, with sublicensing permitted within its territory subject to the same Restricted Competitors and 25% revenue restrictions that govern co-developed IP. This balances the parties’ interests: Meridian retains ownership and reversion of improvements to its core formulations (so an improved CR-7 formulation remains Meridian’s property), while Kastor obtains a meaningful license to use and sublicense (outside North America, to non-competitors) the improvements that its capital helped fund — capturing a return on its R&D investment without gaining the ability to place improved Meridian formulations in the hands of North American competitors. Kastor concedes that improvements to Meridian’s IP revert to Meridian (its opening position treated all improvements as co-developed IP); Meridian concedes that Kastor receives a license with sublicensing rights to those improvements outside North America (its opening position granted Kastor no license to co-developed IP at all).  
          Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors).         The compromise provides for a mutually agreed initial list of specifically identified competitors (and their affiliates and successors), limited to entities competing in the North American specialty polymer coatings market, with annual updates by mutual agreement (or Management Committee resolution as a Reserved Matter if the parties cannot agree). This addresses Kastor’s requirement that the list be limited to named entities (not broad categories) and updated periodically, while giving Meridian a defined, enforceable set of protected competitors. The list is to be finalized by the parties prior to execution, drawing on both the North American competitor analysis and the broader industry list currently in the v.4 draft.

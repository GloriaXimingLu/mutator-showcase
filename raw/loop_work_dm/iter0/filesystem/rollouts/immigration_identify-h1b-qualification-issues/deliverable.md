       I. Executive Summary  
      This memorandum reports the results of a cross-reference review of the H-1B petition package assembled for Rajesh Venkataraman, to be filed by Brightfield Analytics Inc. for the position of Senior Machine Learning Engineer (SOC 15-2051, Data Scientists). Ten documents were examined and compared against one another and against the governing statutory and regulatory framework (INA § 101(a)(15)(H)(i)(b), § 214(i); 8 CFR 214.2(h)(4); INA § 212(n); 20 CFR 655, Subparts H and I).  
      The package is, on the whole, internally coherent on its core         “    macro    ”         data points: the LCA case number, SOC code, prevailing wage figure ($113,734), offered wage ($128,000), worksite address, NAICS code, FEIN, attorney of record, and validity/employment period (October 1, 2025 – September 30, 2028) are consistent across the support letter, certified LCA, prevailing wage determination (PWD), and posting notice. The petitioner’s revenue ($41.2M) and headcount (187) are likewise consistent across the LCA, support letter, and organizational chart.  
      However, the cross-reference review identified             seventeen (17) issues         of varying severity, including             one (1) critical         qualification-path problem,             six (6) high-severity         problems that are likely to draw a Request for Evidence (RFE) or create audit exposure if not corrected,             six (6) medium-severity         items, and             four (4) low-severity / housekeeping         items. The most serious findings are: (1) a field-of-study mismatch between the beneficiary’s degree (Electronics and Communication Engineering) and the position’s stated major-field requirement (Computer Science / Machine Learning / AI / closely related), which the support letter neither argues through nor resolves by clearly invoking the stated alternative qualification; (2) an internal inconsistency between the job-duties description — which repeatedly invokes         “    independent judgment,    ”         a         “    Senior    ”         title, and supervisory/mentoring duties — and the Level 2 prevailing wage classification, which DOL defines as         “    moderately complex tasks requiring limited judgment    ”    ; (3) a three-year block of claimed progressive experience (independent consulting, August 2020 – August 2023) that is wholly unsupported by any third-party documentation; and (4) a series of employment-date and title inconsistencies between the resume, the experience letters, and the support letter.  
      A consolidated issue table appears in Part III. Each issue is then analyzed in full in Part IV, with the specific documents implicated, the consequence for the petition, and a concrete recommended corrective action. Part V sets out cross-cutting observations; Part VI provides a prioritized remediation plan; Part VII states the limitations of this review.

        IV. Detailed Issue Analysis  
       Issue 1 — Field-of-Study Mismatch and Unestablished Qualification Path             (CRITICAL)  
          The problem.         The position’s minimum educational requirement, as stated in the support letter (§3), the LCA (G.7), and the PWD (§3), is         “    a Bachelor’s degree … in Computer Science, Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence, or a closely related field.    ”         The beneficiary’s single degree is a Bachelor of Technology in             Electronics and Communication Engineering (ECE)         from JNTU Hyderabad. The credential evaluation (Westbridge, §5) opines only that the B.Tech is         “    equivalent to a four-year Bachelor of Science (B.S.)             in Electronics and Communication Engineering    ”         — i.e., it equates the degree             in its own field    , not to a degree in computer science or a related field. Critically, the evaluation expressly disclaims the very question the petition needs answered: §7 states,         “    This evaluation             does not opine on whether the applicant’s field of study satisfies the requirements of any particular position or petition.    ”  
          What the record shows about the curriculum.         The evaluation’s own program analysis (§4) quantifies the computer-science / IT-related coursework at         “    approximately 12 credit hours out of the total approximately 160 credit hours … or approximately 7.5% of the overall curriculum.    ”         The transcript confirms the computer-related content is limited to: Computer Programming in C (Sem. I, 3 cr.), Advanced C Programming and Problem Solving (Sem. II, 3 cr.), Data Structures (Sem. IV, 3 cr.), and Introduction to Artificial Intelligence (Sem. VII, 3 cr., an elective). The remaining ~148 theory credits are core ECE (signals and systems, analog/digital communications, VLSI, DSP, antennas, microwave, radar, embedded systems, etc.). The support letter’s assertion that the curriculum         “    included coursework in programming, data structures, and artificial intelligence, providing a strong technical foundation for machine learning engineering    ”         relies on three courses totaling nine credits of directly relevant content — a thin reed on which to rest a         “    closely related field    ”         argument.  
          The available alternative path is not invoked.         The support letter (§3) thoughtfully provides an alternative qualification:         “    a Bachelor’s degree in             any engineering discipline         combined with             5 years of progressive experience in machine learning    .    ”         The beneficiary holds an engineering degree (ECE) and, on the face of the record, appears to satisfy the 5-year progressive-ML-experience prong (NovaStar April 2017 – July 2020; independent consulting August 2020 – August 2023; Brightfield September 2023 – present). Yet §4 of the support letter (Beneficiary’s Qualifications) never maps the beneficiary onto this alternative. Instead, it argues the primary path — degree + 3 years’ experience — and asserts the degree supplies         “    a strong technical foundation,    ”         without confronting the field-of-study question head-on. The letter also references only the         “    3-year minimum,    ”         not the 5-year alternative.  
          Consequence.         A field-of-study mismatch is one of the most common RFE and denial grounds in H-1B adjudication. Because the evaluation declines to opine on field-of-study sufficiency and the support letter does not independently bridge the ECE-to-CS/ML/AI gap, USCIS is likely to issue an RFE asking the petitioner to establish that ECE is a         “    closely related field    ”         to the proffered specialty, or to qualify the beneficiary under the alternative. If the response is inadequate, the petition is vulnerable to denial on the         “    qualifies to perform services in the specialty occupation    ”         element (INA § 214(i)(2); 8 CFR 214.2(h)(4)(iii)(C)).  
          Recommendation.         Do one of the following before filing (or in any RFE response), and make the chosen path explicit in the support letter:  
              (Preferred) Expressly qualify the beneficiary under the alternative         (engineering degree + 5 years’ progressive ML experience). Rewrite §4 to state that the beneficiary qualifies via this alternative, and document the 5 years of progressive ML experience with the supporting letters (see Issue 3, which must be resolved for this path to work). This sidesteps the         “    closely related field    ”         debate entirely.  
              (Alternative) Build a real                 “        closely related field        ”                 argument.         Obtain a supplemental expert opinion or an expanded evaluation that performs a course-by-course mapping from the ECE curriculum to the knowledge areas required for ML engineering (linear algebra/probability via Engineering Mathematics I–IV and Probability Theory; algorithmic thinking via C Programming, Data Structures, Computer Architecture; AI foundations via the AI elective; signal processing as a mathematical precursor to ML). The current evaluation’s 7.5% characterization actively undercuts this argument and should be addressed, not ignored.  
          In either case, correct the support letter’s mischaracterization of the evaluation (Issue 9) and stop relying on the bare         “    coursework in programming, data structures, and AI    ”         sentence.  
      
        Issue 2 — Prevailing Wage Level Inconsistency: Duties Imply Level 3/4; LCA Uses Level 2             (HIGH)  
          The problem.         The LCA (F.4) and PWD (§4) classify the position at             Wage Level 2         ($113,734), which DOL defines as workers who         “    have attained a good understanding of the occupation and perform moderately complex tasks requiring             limited judgment    .    ”         The offered wage is $128,000, which exceeds Level 2 but falls             below Level 3 ($137,946)         and Level 4 ($162,157).  
      The job description in the support letter, however, is written to a markedly higher level of responsibility:  
          The title is             “        Senior        ”         Machine Learning Engineer.  
          The position         “    exercises             independent judgment         in the design and implementation of machine learning solutions    ”         (support letter §2) — the phrase         “    independent judgment    ”         is the DOL Level 3 touchstone (    “    may exercise independent judgment    ”    ).  
          Duty 4 is         “    Leading and mentoring a team of 3 junior data scientists    ”         — supervisory/mentoring, which tracks Level 3 (    “    may have supervisory responsibilities    ”    ) and Level 4 (    “    supervise and train other workers    ”    ).  
          The position         “    reports directly to the Chief Technology Officer    ”         (support letter §2; org chart Level 3), placing it one rung below the CTO in the data-science chain.  
          The PWD’s own job-duty summary includes         “    lead a team of data scientists,    ”         i.e., the supervisory duty was before the NPWC when it issued Level 2.  
      The internal contradiction is direct: Level 2 is         “    limited judgment    ”    ; the support letter says         “    independent judgment.    ”         These are not reconcilable on the same record.  
          Consequence.         DOL certified Level 2 on the employer’s request, and USCIS generally accepts the certified LCA’s prevailing wage. The exposure is therefore not an automatic denial, but a             reclassification / audit risk    : (a) a DOL Wage and Hour investigation could conclude the wage level was understated and assess back-wage liability; and (b) USCIS, in adjudicating specialty occupation and the wage obligation, may question why a         “    Senior,    ”         supervisory, independent-judgment role is paid at a         “    qualified / limited judgment    ”         level — an inconsistency that can also bleed into the specialty-occupation analysis (Issue 11).             If the position were reclassified to Level 3, the offered wage of $128,000 would fall below the $137,946 prevailing wage, which is a fatal deficiency         requiring a new PWD, a new LCA, and an increased wage.  
          Recommendation.         Reconcile the description and the wage level before filing. Either:  
              (If Level 2 is intended and defensible)         soften the description to match Level 2 — remove or temper         “    independent judgment,    ”         the         “    Senior    ”         framing, and the supervisory/mentoring duty, and ensure the PWD job-duty summary does not assert team leadership; or  
              (If the duties are accurate)         request a new PWD at Level 3 (or 4), file a new LCA, and raise the offered wage to at least $137,946 (Level 3) or $162,157 (Level 4). Given the         “    Senior    ”         title, supervisory duties, and CTO reporting line, Level 3 is the more defensible classification.  
      Do not file with the description and the wage level pointing in opposite directions.  
      
        Issue 3 — Three Years of Claimed Experience (Independent Consulting) Is Undocumented             (HIGH)  
          The problem.         Both the support letter (§4) and the resume list         “    Independent Machine Learning Consultant (August 2020 – August 2023)    ”         — a three-year block. The package contains             no supporting documentation         for this period: no client letters, no 1099s or tax records, no contracts or statements of work, no LinkedIn/client corroboration. By contrast, the Kiran and NovaStar periods are each backed by a signed supervisory experience letter.  
          Why it matters.         This three-year block is load-bearing for the petition in two ways:  
              For the alternative qualification         (Issue 1’s preferred path), the beneficiary needs 5 years of progressive ML experience. Counting only             documented         ML experience — NovaStar (April 2017 – July 2020 ≈ 3.25 years) plus Brightfield (September 2023 – present ≈ 1.5 years) — yields roughly             4.75 years    , just             under         the 5-year threshold. The independent-consulting period is what pushes the beneficiary over 5 years. Without documentation, USCIS may decline to credit it, causing the alternative path to fail.  
              For the                 “        progressive experience        ”                 narrative generally    , a three-year gap in verifiable employment invites an RFE and undermines the credibility of the experience chronology.  
          Consequence.         Likely RFE for experience documentation; potential failure of the alternative qualification if the consulting period is not credited; cumulative credibility damage when combined with the date discrepancies in Issue 4.  
          Recommendation.         Obtain at least one (preferably two or more) client letters or engagement confirmations covering August 2020 – August 2023, each on client letterhead, signed by a supervisor or engagement lead, stating the dates, the nature of the ML/NLP work, the tools used, and that the work was performed by the beneficiary. Supplement with 1099/1040 Schedule C evidence and, if available, contracts or SOWs. If the consulting cannot be adequately documented, restructure the qualification argument to rely on documented experience only and confirm the math still satisfies the applicable threshold.

        Issue 5 — Current Job Title (    “    Data Scientist    ”    ) vs. Petitioned Title (    “    Senior Machine Learning Engineer    ”    ) Is Unexplained             (HIGH)  
          The problem.         The resume lists the beneficiary’s current Brightfield position, held since September 2023, as             “        Data Scientist.        ”         The petition, LCA, PWD, posting notice, and org chart all describe the petitioned position as             “        Senior Machine Learning Engineer.        ”         The support letter (§4) states the beneficiary         “    Has been contributing to the company’s machine learning and data science initiatives    ”         since September 5, 2023, but never reconciles the two titles. It is unclear whether: (a) the beneficiary is being promoted from Data Scientist to Senior ML Engineer effective October 1, 2025; (b)         “    Data Scientist    ”         and         “    Senior ML Engineer    ”         describe the same role inconsistently; or (c) the petition is for a new, distinct position.  
          Consequence.         The discrepancy raises two distinct concerns. First, an adjudicator may question whether the petitioned         “    Senior    ”         role is a bona fide new position or merely a relabeling of the beneficiary’s existing Data Scientist role — relevant to both the specialty-occupation analysis and the bona-fide-job-offer inquiry. Second, if the beneficiary is already performing Data Scientist duties for the petitioner on F-1 OPT, the relationship between the OPT employment and the H-1B position (different title, different — higher — wage level per Issue 2) should be explained to avoid the appearance that the H-1B position was tailored to the beneficiary.

Add a paragraph to the support letter that explicitly states the relationship between the current and petitioned roles — e.g., that the beneficiary is currently employed in a Data Scientist capacity on F-1 OPT and is being promoted/hired into the distinct Senior Machine Learning Engineer position effective October 1, 2025, with a description of how the Senior role’s duties and requirements differ from and exceed the Data Scientist role. Align the resume’s current title with whatever characterization is adopted.

          The problem.         Support letter duty 4 states the Senior ML Engineer will be         “    Leading and mentoring a team of             3         junior data scientists.    ”         The organizational chart shows the Senior ML Engineer with             2 direct reports         (two         “    Data Scientist (Filled)    ”         positions at Level 4), and the chart’s narrative expressly states         “    This position has             2 direct reports         as shown below.    ”

First, it is a factual inconsistency between two petition documents that an adjudicator will notice. Second, the supervisory duty is the principal indicator pushing the wage level above Level 2 (Issue 2) and supporting the         “    Senior    ”         designation; if the actual supervisory scope is 2 (not 3), the Level 3/4 argument weakens, while if it is 3, the org chart is inaccurate.           Recommendation.         Reconcile the number. If the team is 2, correct the support letter’s duty 4 to         “    2    ”         and update the PWD/LCA job-duty summary accordingly. If the team is (or will be) 3, update the org chart to show three direct reports. The two documents must state the same number.  
      
        Issue 7 — LCA Posting Period Is Deficient (8 Business Days vs. 10 Required)             (HIGH)  
          The problem.         20 CFR § 655.734(a)(1) requires that the notice of filing be posted         “    for a total of at least             10 consecutive business days    ”         in two conspicuous locations. The LCA (J.2, J.4/K.4) and the posting notice both record posting from             March 10, 2025 through March 19, 2025    . Counting business days inclusive (March 10 = Monday; March 19 = Wednesday, with the March 15–16 weekend excluded), the posting period spans only             8 business days         (Mar 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 17, 18, 19). To satisfy the 10-business-day rule, posting would have had to continue through at least             Friday, March 21, 2025    .  
      The LCA’s attestation (K.4) certifies that         “    notice of the filing of this labor condition application has been provided to workers … through physical posting at the worksite … posted on March 10, 2025 and removed on March 19, 2025    ”         — i.e., the employer has attested to compliance with a posting period that, on its face, is two business days short of the regulatory minimum.  
          Consequence.         A deficient posting period is a DOL compliance violation that can result in back-wage liability, civil money penalties, and a finding that the LCA attestation was misstated. It is also a potential USCIS concern because a defective notice undermines the LCA on which the petition depends. Because the deficiency is documented             in the LCA itself    , it is not easily cured after the fact.  
          Recommendation.         Before relying on this LCA, re-post the notice for a full 10 consecutive business days in two conspicuous locations (see also Issue 10), document the new posting dates, and — because the existing LCA’s attestation records the deficient period — consider whether a new LCA (with a corrected, compliant posting period) should be filed. At minimum, ensure the public access file reflects a compliant posting. Confirm the corrected dates in any re-filed LCA attestation.

          The problem.         The posting notice states it         “    is being posted in             two conspicuous locations         at the place of employment,    ”         tracking 20 CFR § 655.734(a)(1)(ii). The LCA (J.3), however, records only a single posting location:         “    4200 Congress Avenue, Suite 1100, Austin, TX 78701 — Employee common area / break room, first floor.    ”         No second location is identified anywhere in the package.

If only one location was actually used, the posting is non-compliant independent of the business-day deficiency (Issue 7). Identify and document two distinct conspicuous posting locations (e.g., the break room and a second common area or the primary worksite bulletin board), record both in the public access file, and ensure any re-filed LCA or corrected notice reflects both locations. Combine with the re-posting recommended under Issue 7.  
      
        Issue 11 — Specialty-Occupation Evidence Is Conclusory; No Employer Hiring History or O        NET Support             (MEDIUM)*  
          The problem.         The support letter (§3) argues specialty occupation under INA § 214(i)(1) and 8 CFR 214.2(h)(4)(ii) by asserting (a) a bachelor’s-degree minimum, (b) an         “    industry standard    ”         that comparable positions require a degree, and (c) the complexity of the work. These track several of the regulatory         “    prongs,    ”         but the support is largely conclusory: the         “    industry standard    ”         paragraph names no peer firms, cites no O*NET data, and provides no sample job postings; and there is             no evidence of the petitioner’s own past hiring practice         for similar roles (prong 3 —         “    the employer normally requires a degree    ”    ). The org chart shows two incumbent Data Scientists reporting to the petitioned role, but states nothing about their qualifications, which would have been useful prong-3 evidence.  
          Consequence.         USCIS adjudicators frequently issue RFEs on specialty occupation where the employer’s letter is self-serving and unsupported. The current letter is vulnerable to an RFE asking for O*NET evidence, peer-company job postings, and evidence of the petitioner’s own degree requirements for comparable positions.  
          Recommendation.         Issue 12 — Change-of-Status / OPT Mechanics Are Not Addressed             (MEDIUM)  
          The problem.         The support letter (§4) states the beneficiary         “    is currently maintaining valid F-1 Optional Practical Training (OPT) status and will transition to H-1B nonimmigrant status effective October 1, 2025.    ”         The letter does not address: (a) the             OPT expiration date         and whether the beneficiary’s employment authorization remains valid through October 1, 2025; (b) whether             cap-gap relief         is needed/available to bridge any gap between OPT expiration and the October 1 H-1B start; (c) whether the beneficiary is on a             STEM OPT extension         (the ECE degree is generally a STEM-designated field, which may be relevant); and (d) the mechanics of the requested change of status (Form I-539 for any dependents, maintenance-of-status evidence such as I-20s/EAD). No status documentation (I-20, EAD, I-94) appears in the reviewed package.  
          Consequence.         If the beneficiary’s OPT expires before October 1, 2025 and no cap-gap extension applies, the beneficiary may have a period of unauthorized presence/unemployment that could jeopardize the change of status and require consular notification instead. An RFE on maintenance of status is common in cap-subject change-of-status cases.  
          Recommendation.         Add to the support letter (or a separate status exhibit) the beneficiary’s current F-1/OPT details: program end date, OPT start/end dates, STEM-extension status if any, and the cap-gap basis (if any) bridging to October 1. Include the current I-20, EAD, and I-94 in the filing.          V. Cross-Cutting Observations  
          A. The qualification, wage-level, and supervisory issues are interlocking, not independent.         Issues 1, 2, 6, and 11 form a cluster. The         “    Senior    ”         title, the supervisory/mentoring duty, and the         “    independent judgment    ”         language are deployed in the support letter to bolster the specialty-occupation argument (Issue 11) — but the same language pushes the wage level above Level 2 (Issue 2), where the offered wage no longer prevails. Meanwhile, the supervisory scope is internally inconsistent (Issue 6: 3 vs. 2). The petitioner cannot have it both ways: if the role is genuinely senior/supervisory/independent-judgment, the wage level and wage must rise to Level 3+; if Level 2 is the intended (and wage-supportable) classification, the description must be softened and the supervisory claim reduced. This is the single most important strategic decision in the package and should be made deliberately before filing.  
          B. The qualification path should be chosen explicitly and documented to match.         Issue 1 (field of study), Issue 3 (undocumented consulting), Issue 9 (mischaracterized evaluation), and Issue 13 (experience conflation) all bear on whether the beneficiary is shown to         “    qualify to perform services in the specialty occupation.    ”         The cleanest resolution is to elect the alternative qualification (engineering degree + 5 years’ progressive ML experience), document the 5 years fully (resolving Issue 3), quote the evaluation accurately (Issue 9), and stop conflating software-development experience with ML experience (Issue 13). Pursuing the primary path instead would require a real         “    closely related field    ”         argument that the current 7.5%-CS-content evaluation does not support.  
          C. The resume is the least reliable document in the package and should be reconciled to the signed letters.         Issues 4, 5, 13, and 17 all involve the resume diverging from the support letter and/or the signed experience letters (overstated end dates, a different current title, a different experience figure, and a less precise start date). Because the signed experience letters and the support letter agree with each other and the resume is the outlier, the resume should be corrected to conform to the signed, verifiable record. An adjudicator comparing the resume to the letters will otherwise infer carelessness or worse.  
          D. LCA posting compliance has two distinct defects that should be fixed together.         Issue 7 (8 vs. 10 business days) and Issue 10 (one vs. two locations) both implicate 20 CFR § 655.734 and are recorded in the LCA’s own attestation. Because the LCA attests to the deficient posting, the cleanest course is to re-post correctly (10 business days, two locations, correct employer phone per Issue 8) and file a new LCA reflecting the compliant posting, rather than attempting to paper over the existing attestation.  
          E. Data-consistency hygiene.         Issues 8, 14, and 17 reflect a pattern of small but avoidable inconsistencies (four different employer phone numbers; a beneficiary’s personal phone on the posting notice; mismatched numbers within a single letter; date-precision mismatches). None is individually fatal, but collectively they signal a package assembled without a final cross-reference pass and invite broader scrutiny.

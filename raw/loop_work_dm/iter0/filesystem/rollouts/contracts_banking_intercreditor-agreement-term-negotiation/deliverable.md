             The Total LTV of 97.66% and the 25.78% equity cushion are the controlling facts.         The First Lien Agent’s insistence on structural protection and the Second Lien Agent’s insistence on preserving enforcement and residual rights both flow from the same arithmetic: aggregate secured debt nearly equals enterprise value, leaving the second lien with a thin cushion and the first lien with limited margin for value erosion. A credible compromise must give the first lien the hard protections it needs where the cushion is genuinely at risk, while giving the second lien the defined rights it needs to protect its position and to demonstrate to its own investors that the intercreditor structure preserves a meaningful recovery path.  
             Each side has identified, in without-prejudice communications, where it has genuine flexibility and where it does not.         The First Lien Agent has stated that par for funded debt is non-negotiable, that the hard sweep for large/extraordinary asset sales is essential, and that the standstill floor is 150 days — but has signaled room on the standstill duration (toward 180 days), on the interest rate basis (non-default rather than default), on the unfunded Revolver assumption (optional rather than mandatory), and on the restrictiveness test (narrowed plus MFN). The package below is built precisely on these seams of flexibility.  
             The compromise must be closeable by July 31, 2025.         Both agents have warned that material departures from their respective positions would trigger re-circulation to the full syndicate (seven first lien lenders) or re-approval within the Ridgeline fund structure, with attendant delay and re-pricing risk. The proposed terms are designed to sit within each side’s pre-authorized flexibility so that, if accepted as a package, they can be documented and approved without a fresh round of credit-committee escalation.  
      
        IV. The Single Indivisible Package — Framing and Linkages  
      You instructed that the four compromises be presented as a single, integrated package and not offered piecemeal. This is not merely a presentational choice; it reflects the substantive economics of the negotiation. The four provisions are linked, and the balance of the package depends on the linkages:  
             The 180-day standstill (longer than Ridgeline’s 90-day opening) is acceptable to Ridgeline only because it is paired with early-termination triggers and a Blockage Notice cap.         Those triggers, in turn, are acceptable to Cartwright only because the standstill is 180 days (within its signaled 150–180 day range) rather than 90. Decoupling the duration from the triggers collapses the compromise on both sides.  
             Par for funded debt (Cartwright’s non-negotiable) is acceptable to Ridgeline only because the unfunded Revolver assumption is optional (Ridgeline’s deal-breaker condition) and because accrued interest is calculated at the non-default rate.         The non-default-rate concession from Cartwright is, in turn, supportable only because Ridgeline has accepted par (giving up its FMV/dealer-poll alternative) and because the package preserves Cartwright’s hard sweep and MFN protections elsewhere.  
             The 10% principal cap (less than Ridgeline’s 20% opening) is acceptable to Ridgeline only because the restrictiveness test is narrowed to financial maintenance covenants (not a blanket test) and because the maturity floor adopts Ridgeline’s original-2L-maturity position.         The narrowed test is acceptable to Cartwright only because it is paired with an MFN right that gives the first lien the benefit of any tighter financial covenant in a refinancing.  
             The hard 100% sweep for large/extraordinary asset sales (Cartwright’s firm requirement) is acceptable to Ridgeline only because ordinary-course reinvestment is preserved below the $20M threshold and because the 2L receives residual proceeds after full 1L satisfaction.         The residual-access concession from Cartwright is supportable only because the hard sweep for significant sales is preserved and because robust anti-evasion language prevents threshold evasion.  
      In each case, a concession by one side is the price of a concession by the other. Unbundling the package would allow either agent to pocket the concessions it favors while re-litigating the concessions it dislikes, destroying the balance and, in all likelihood, the deal. For this reason, KTM recommends that the package be transmitted to both agents with an express statement that the four provisions are offered as an interdependent set and that Meridian is prepared to endorse and advocate for the package as a whole, but not for individual components in isolation.  
      
        V. Compromise Provision 1 — Standstill Period (Section 3.01(b))  
       A. Contested Positions  
          First Lien Agent (opening):         A 270-day standstill, commencing upon a Second Lien Default, with             no early termination events         under any circumstance — an         “    absolute and unconditional    ”         standstill. The First Lien Agent’s rationale rests on the thin equity cushion (25.78%), the risk that premature 2L enforcement would destroy value borne disproportionately by the first lien, the floating-rate structure (which compensates the 2L for the passage of time through continued interest accrual), and the proposition that a standstill subject to early termination is         “    no standstill at all.    ”         The First Lien Agent cites three precedent transactions (Hartsfield/Consolidated Metals at 270 days; Briarcliff/Weston Energy at 240 days with a single narrow carve-out; Kingsbridge/Atlas Materials at 300 days).  
          Second Lien Agent (opening):         A 90-day standstill, measured from delivery of the Second Lien Enforcement Notice, with early-termination triggers upon (A) commencement of a 1L enforcement action against material Collateral or (B) filing of an Insolvency Proceeding. The Second Lien Agent’s rationale rests on collateral-value erosion during a prolonged standstill (manufacturing equipment, specialized inventory, and real property all subject to distressed-condition depreciation), the risk that a 270-day standstill would convert lien subordination into de facto payment subordination, and Borrower alignment on prompt default resolution. The Second Lien Agent cites its own internal survey (median 120 days; nine of fourteen deals above 6.0x leverage used 90 days).  
          Signaled flexibility:         The First Lien Agent has stated that its credit committee’s hard floor is             150 days    , that it         “    could live with something in the 150- to 180-day range,    ”         and that         “    if a longer standstill — closer to 180 days — were paired with carefully defined early termination triggers, that is something the committee would be willing to consider,    ”         provided the triggers are         “    narrow and objectively verifiable.    ”         The Second Lien Agent has stated it         “    can accept up to a 180-day standstill, but only if the ICA includes meaningful early termination triggers,    ”         calling this         “    a firm condition and not something we can decouple from the duration.    ”         The two flexibility signals converge on the same landing zone:             180 days with triggers    .  
        B. Proposed Compromise Language  
      The following language is proposed to replace the bracketed Section 3.01(b) of the working draft ICA in its entirety:  
          Section 3.01(b) — Standstill Period.         Following the occurrence and during the continuance of a Second Lien Default, the Second Lien Agent shall not exercise any rights or remedies with respect to the Collateral for a period of one hundred eighty (180) days following delivery by the First Lien Agent to the Second Lien Agent of a Blockage Notice specifying the Second Lien Default giving rise thereto (the         “    Standstill Period    ”    ). The Standstill Period shall be measured from the date of actual receipt of the Blockage Notice by the Second Lien Agent. The Standstill Period shall terminate prior to the expiration of such 180-day period upon the occurrence of any of the following Early Termination Events (each, an         “    Early Termination Event    ”    ), in each case upon written notice from the Second Lien Agent to the First Lien Agent (which notice may be delivered concurrently with the occurrence of the Early Termination Event and shall specify the Early Termination Event relied upon):  
          (A) Cessation of Active Enforcement.         The First Lien Agent shall have failed to commence or, having commenced, shall have ceased to actively pursue any Enforcement Action with respect to any material portion of the Collateral for a period of one hundred twenty (120) consecutive days following delivery of the Blockage Notice; provided that the First Lien Agent shall be deemed to be actively pursuing an Enforcement Action if it is engaged in good-faith negotiations regarding a restructuring, consensual disposition, or refinancing of the First Lien Obligations, or is pursuing any judicial or non-judicial enforcement, collection, or realization process with respect to the Collateral;  
          (B) Disposition of Substantially All Collateral.         The First Lien Agent shall have consummated, or shall have consented to (including under Section 6.06 of this Agreement), a sale, transfer, or other disposition of substantially all of the Collateral (whether under Section 363 of the Bankruptcy Code or otherwise); or  
          (C) Impairing Plan Without 2L Consent.         A plan of reorganization or liquidation shall have been filed in any Insolvency Proceeding that (i) impairs the Second Lien Obligations without the consent of the Second Lien Agent and (ii) provides for the treatment of the First Lien Obligations or the application of proceeds of the Collateral in a manner inconsistent with the priorities and waterfall set forth in this Agreement.  
          Blockage Notice Cap.         Notwithstanding anything to the contrary contained herein, (x) the First Lien Agent may not deliver more than two (2) Blockage Notices during any rolling 365-day period, and (y) the aggregate number of days during which one or more Standstill Periods are in effect during any rolling 365-day period shall not exceed three hundred sixty (360) days. For the avoidance of doubt, if a Second Lien Default is cured or waived during a Standstill Period, such Standstill Period shall terminate in accordance with Section 3.05, and a new Standstill Period may be commenced only by delivery of a new Blockage Notice in connection with a subsequently occurring Second Lien Default, subject to the foregoing caps.  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Why 180 days is within each side’s flexibility.         The Market Survey of 42 comparable ICAs shows a             median standstill of 180 days         (mean 172.9; range 90–270; 25th percentile 150; 75th percentile 202.5). A 180-day standstill is therefore the single most defensible market-standard term available. It sits at the top of the First Lien Agent’s signaled 150–180 day range and above its 150-day hard floor, and it is the maximum the Second Lien Agent has signaled it can accept. Both agents have, in effect, independently identified 180 days as the term around which a deal can be built. The First Lien Agent’s opening of 270 days is an outlier: only one of the 42 surveyed deals (Deal-015) employed a 270-day standstill, and the survey’s note describes it as         “    negotiated by a strong sponsor.    ”         The Second Lien Agent’s opening of 90 days is likewise at the low end (only Deal-022 used 90 days, described as an outlier involving a single CLO-held second lien). The median is the natural landing zone.  
          Why early-termination triggers are both necessary and acceptable.         The Market Survey shows that             76% of the surveyed deals (32 of 42) include early-termination triggers    , and that             25 of 42 deals pair a standstill of 180 days or longer with early-termination triggers         — making the         “    180 days plus triggers    ”         combination the clear market-standard pairing. The First Lien Agent’s         “    no triggers under any circumstance    ”         position is therefore a minority position (only 24% of deals have no triggers). At the same time, the triggers proposed here are deliberately             narrow and objectively verifiable    , which is the precise formulation the First Lien Agent said its committee         “    would be willing to consider.    ”         The three triggers track the most common trigger types in the Market Survey: cessation/inactivity-type triggers, disposition-of-collateral triggers, and plan-impairment triggers (the survey’s most common triggers were         “    liquidation commenced    ”         in 29 deals,         “    credit bid by 1L    ”         in 22,         “    filing of involuntary petition    ”         in 13,         “    DIP financing approved    ”         in 13, and         “    sale motion filed without 2L consent    ”         in 9). None of the three proposed triggers permits the Second Lien to terminate the standstill merely because collateral value has declined or because a third-party creditor has filed a foreclosure — both of which the First Lien Agent specifically objected to, and neither of which is included here.  
          The 120-day cessation trigger protects both sides.         The trigger does not fire for a full 120 days — giving the First Lien Agent a meaningful, defined window (one-third of the standstill) to pursue remedies, negotiate a consensual resolution, or evaluate a restructuring before the Second Lien can act. The         “    deemed active    ”         proviso ensures that good-faith restructuring negotiations or an ongoing enforcement process keeps the standstill alive, addressing the First Lien Agent’s concern that the trigger could be used to         “    run out the clock.    ”         From the Second Lien Agent’s perspective, the trigger addresses its core concern — that a prolonged standstill could be         “    deployed tactically to indefinitely block Second Lien enforcement while collateral value declines    ”         — by ensuring that if the First Lien Agent itself goes dormant, the Second Lien is not indefinitely locked out.  
          The Blockage Notice cap is the balancing element for the longer standstill.         Neither party’s bracketed proposal included a cap on the number of Blockage Notices, and the drafting note in the working ICA flags this as an open issue. A 180-day standstill with no cap on serial Blockage Notices would permit the First Lien Agent, in theory, to issue successive standstills and keep the Second Lien perpetually locked out — the very         “    de facto preclusion of Second Lien rights    ”         the Second Lien Agent warned against. A cap of two Blockage Notices (and 360 aggregate days) per rolling 365-day period is a measured protection: it preserves the First Lien Agent’s ability to invoke a second standstill if a default is cured and recurs, while preventing indefinite serial blockage. This is a 2L-protective element that makes the 180-day duration (longer than Ridgeline’s 90-day opening) politically saleable to Ridgeline’s investors, and it is a modest concession from the First Lien Agent that does not impair the standstill’s core protective function in any realistic single-default scenario.  
          Economic impact.         The standstill operates only in distress, so its economic impact is best understood through the lens of collateral-value erosion, which the Second Lien Agent has emphasized. With a 25.78% equity cushion (~$165M of TEV above first lien debt), a TEV decline of 25.78% over the standstill period would reduce the Second Lien’s recovery to zero; a 15% decline (to ~$544M) would reduce the Second Lien’s theoretical recovery to ~$69M, or roughly 46 cents on the dollar, before liquidation costs. A 180-day standstill is materially shorter than the 270-day opening (a 33% reduction in the lockout period), reducing the window of collateral-value erosion risk to the Second Lien, while the early-termination triggers provide an additional safety valve in the scenarios (1L inactivity, wholesale disposition, impairing plan) where the standstill’s protective rationale is weakest. From the First Lien Agent’s perspective, the floating-rate structure continues to compensate the Second Lien for the passage of time (the 2L accrues interest at SOFR + 625 bps, ~11.58% all-in, during the standstill), and the 180-day period remains well within the         “    six to nine months    ”         the First Lien Agent identified as the minimum time reasonably necessary to pursue a comprehensive restructuring or orderly asset sale.  
      
         VI. Compromise Provision 2 — Second Lien Purchase Right (Section 5.01)  
       A. Contested Positions  
      This provision has four sub-issues: (i) the purchase-price mechanic (par vs. lesser-of-par-or-FMV); (ii) the interest-rate basis for accrued interest (default rate vs. non-default contract rate); (iii) mandatory vs. optional assumption of unfunded Revolver commitments; and (iv) the scope of exercise triggers.  
          First Lien Agent (opening):         Par — 100% of outstanding principal of all funded First Lien Obligations ($475M), plus accrued interest at the             default rate         (SOFR + 450 bps, ~9.83%), plus all fees/costs/expenses/indemnities/breakage costs, plus             mandatory         assumption of all unfunded Revolver commitments ($125M). The First Lien Agent calls par         “    non-negotiable,    ”         argues that default-rate interest prevents a 200 bps arbitrage windfall to the purchaser (~$9.5M/annum on $475M), and argues that mandatory Revolver assumption preserves the integrity of the First Lien Facility and the Borrower’s committed liquidity.  
          Second Lien Agent (opening):         The lesser of par (at the             non-default         contract rate, excluding default interest and punitive amounts) or             fair market value         determined by an independent dealer poll (minimum three quotations), with             no         obligation to assume unfunded Revolver commitments (which would terminate upon consummation of the purchase). The Second Lien Agent argues that a par-only construct renders the purchase right economically impracticable in distress (total capital at risk would approach TEV), that default-rate interest is a punitive charge not appropriate for a credit-acquisition mechanism, and that mandatory Revolver assumption is impossible given Ridgeline’s fund LPA constraints on open-ended commitment obligations.  
          Signaled flexibility:         The First Lien Agent has stated that             par for funded debt is non-negotiable    , but has signaled room on two subsidiary points: (a) accrued interest         “    may be    ”         acceptable at the             non-default contract rate         (SOFR + 250 bps, ~7.83%), and (b) unfunded Revolver assumption         “    may be    ”         made             optional rather than mandatory         (acknowledging Ridgeline’s LPA constraint as         “    a practical consideration, not a positional one    ”    ). The Second Lien Agent has stated that it             “        acknowledges that par for funded debt may be acceptable        ”         — a significant concession — but conditions that acceptance on the unfunded Revolver assumption being             optional         (calling mandatory assumption         “    a deal-breaker given the LPA constraints    ”    ) and is         “    willing to discuss    ”         the non-default rate. The convergence is clear:             par for funded debt, at the non-default rate, with optional Revolver assumption    .  
        B. Proposed Compromise Language  
      The following language is proposed to replace the bracketed Section 5.01 of the working draft ICA in its entirety:  
          Section 5.01 — Second Lien Purchase Right.         At any time following the occurrence and during the continuance of an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement, the Second Lien Agent (on behalf of the Second Lien Lenders, or one or more Second Lien Lenders, or any designee of the foregoing) shall have the right (but not the obligation) to purchase all (but not less than all) of the First Lien Obligations (the         “    Second Lien Purchase Right    ”    ) by paying to the First Lien Agent in immediately available funds a purchase price (the         “    Purchase Price    ”    ) equal to the sum of the following components, each calculated as of the close of business on the Business Day immediately preceding the purchase date (the         “    Purchase Date    ”    ):  
          (i) Outstanding Principal.         One hundred percent (100%) of the aggregate outstanding principal amount of all funded First Lien Obligations, including all amounts outstanding under the First Lien Term Loan A and all drawn amounts under the First Lien Revolver;  
          (ii) Accrued Interest at the Non-Default Contract Rate.         All accrued and unpaid interest on the First Lien Obligations, calculated at the non-default contract rate applicable under the First Lien Credit Agreement (i.e., Adjusted Term SOFR plus the Applicable Rate of 250 basis points, without giving effect to any default-rate increment or any late charges or other punitive amounts), through and including the Purchase Date, calculated on the basis of a 360-day year for the actual number of days elapsed;  
          (iii) Fees, Costs, Expenses, and Other Amounts.         All fees (including commitment fees, letter of credit fees, and agency fees), costs, expenses (including reasonable and documented fees and disbursements of counsel), indemnification obligations (to the extent then due and payable or asserted in writing), breakage costs (including any swap breakage or early termination costs under any hedging arrangements), yield protection amounts, and all other amounts then due and payable under the First Lien Loan Documents, whether or not invoiced;  
          (iv) LC Exposure Component.         An amount equal to the aggregate outstanding LC Exposure, which the purchaser shall satisfy, simultaneously with the consummation of the purchase, by either (x) depositing cash collateral in an amount equal to 103% of the aggregate face amount of all outstanding Letters of Credit in an account with the First Lien Agent (or its successor) for the benefit of the Issuing Bank, or (y) delivering to the Issuing Bank one or more back-to-back replacement letters of credit, in form and substance satisfactory to the Issuing Bank, in replacement of each outstanding Letter of Credit.  
          Assumption of Unfunded Revolver Commitments.         As a condition to the purchase, the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) shall have the             option, but not the obligation    , to assume all unfunded commitments under the First Lien Revolver by executing and delivering to the First Lien Agent an assumption agreement in form and substance reasonably satisfactory to the First Lien Agent, pursuant to which the assuming party shall be irrevocably obligated to fund any subsequent draws by the Borrower on terms no less favorable to the Borrower than those set forth in the First Lien Credit Agreement. If the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) does not elect to assume the unfunded Revolver commitments, the unfunded Revolver commitments shall terminate upon consummation of the purchase and the First Lien Revolver shall be permanently reduced to zero concurrent with the closing of the purchase; provided that the Borrower shall have the right, for a period of ninety (90) days following the consummation of the purchase, to replace the unfunded Revolver commitments with a new revolving credit facility from one or more lenders reasonably acceptable to the Borrower, on terms no less favorable to the Borrower than those set forth in the First Lien Credit Agreement (including with respect to interest rate, commitment fee, borrowing base provisions, maturity, and conditions to borrowing).  
      The purchase shall be on a par, non-recourse, as-is, where-is basis, without representation or warranty of any kind by the First Lien Agent or any First Lien Lender (other than customary representations as to the authority of the assigning lender to assign and the enforceability of the transfer). The mechanics of the purchase shall be governed by Section 5.02.  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Par for funded debt — the non-negotiable that both sides can live with.         The First Lien Agent has called par         “    non-negotiable,    ”         and the Second Lien Agent has         “    acknowledg[ed] that par for funded debt may be acceptable.    ”         The Market Survey strongly supports a par formulation:             71% of the surveyed deals (30 of 42) use a par purchase price    , 19% use a hybrid (lesser of par or FMV), and only 10% use pure FMV. The Second Lien Agent’s opening         “    lesser of par or FMV    ”         position is therefore a minority mechanic, and its concession to par for funded debt is well within market norms. The compromise drops the FMV/dealer-poll alternative entirely — a meaningful concession by Ridgeline — in exchange for the non-default rate and optional Revolver assumption. Notably,             Deal-007 in the Market Survey — which is structurally comparable to Meridian ($475M 1L facility, $150M 2L, $125M Revolver) — uses par for funded debt at the non-default contract rate with optional Revolver assumption    , producing an illustrative par purchase price of $484.3M. The Meridian compromise tracks this directly comparable precedent almost exactly.  
          Non-default contract rate — the majority market practice and within 1L flexibility.         The Market Survey shows that             69% of deals (29 of 42) exclude default-rate interest         from the par calculation and use the non-default contract rate, while only 31% (13 deals) include default-rate interest.             90% of deals (38 of 42) include the non-default contract rate         as a component. The use of the non-default rate is therefore the majority practice. The First Lien Agent has expressly signaled that the non-default rate         “    may be    ”         acceptable, and the Second Lien Agent is         “    willing to discuss    ”         it. The compromise adopts the non-default rate (SOFR + 250 bps, ~7.83% all-in).  
          Quantified economic impact of the interest-rate basis.         On the illustrative 90-day, full-draw basis used throughout the source documents:  
          Outstanding principal:             $475,000,000  
          Accrued interest at the             non-default         rate (7.83%, 90/360):             ~$9,300,000         ($475M × 7.83% × 90/360)  
          Accrued interest at the             default         rate (9.83%, 90/360):             ~$11,670,000         ($475M × 9.83% × 90/360)  
              Difference (the 200 bps default-rate increment, 90 days): ~$2,375,000  
          Illustrative par purchase price (funded, non-default, 90-day):             ~$484,300,000         ($475M + $9.30M)  
          With estimated fees/expenses (~$1.5M per the capital structure summary):             ~$485,800,000  
      The default-rate increment, annualized, is ~$9,500,000 per annum (200 bps on $475M). The compromise thus preserves par for the First Lien Lenders (they recover 100% of principal plus full non-default interest plus fees) while saving the purchaser approximately $2.375M per 90-day accrual period relative to the First Lien Agent’s opening default-rate position. This is a real but bounded concession: the First Lien Lenders are made whole on principal and earn their full contractual (non-default) interest, and the purchaser does not capture the punitive 200 bps spread that the First Lien Agent argued would constitute a windfall. The compromise also resolves the First Lien Agent’s         “    arbitrage    ”         concern in substance — the purchaser pays par plus non-default interest, not a discount — while accommodating the Second Lien Agent’s view that default-rate interest is a punitive charge inappropriate for a credit-acquisition mechanism.  
          Optional (not mandatory) Revolver assumption — the deal-breaker resolved.         The Market Survey shows that             62% of deals (26 of 42) make unfunded Revolver assumption optional         at the purchaser’s election, 24% make it mandatory, and 14% are silent. The trend is decisively toward optional:             77% of 2024 deals         make it optional, versus 50% of 2022 deals. The First Lien Agent has signaled that optional assumption         “    may be    ”         acceptable, and the Second Lien Agent has made optional assumption a firm condition, explaining that Ridgeline’s fund LPA         “    contains structural constraints that prevent the fund from taking on open-ended revolving credit commitment obligations    ”         — a limitation of the fund vehicle,         “    not a negotiating posture.    ”         Mandatory assumption is, on the Second Lien Agent’s representation, a genuine deal-breaker. The compromise makes assumption optional, which is both the majority market practice and within the First Lien Agent’s signaled flexibility.  
          Protecting the Borrower’s liquidity — the 90-day replacement right.         The First Lien Agent’s principal concern with optional (rather than mandatory) Revolver assumption is that the Borrower could lose access to the $125M of committed liquidity that the Revolver provides. The compromise addresses this directly: if the purchaser does not assume the unfunded Revolver, the Borrower is given a             90-day replacement right         to put in place a new revolving facility on terms no less favorable than the existing First Lien Revolver. This protects Meridian’s working-capital and letter-of-credit needs — a concern you raised in the engagement email — without forcing the Second Lien purchaser into an open-ended commitment its fund vehicle cannot accept. This is a Borrower-protective element that makes the optional-assumption compromise more palatable to all three parties.  
          LC Exposure — conforming to the First Lien Credit Agreement.         The compromise requires the purchaser to satisfy the LC Exposure component by cash-collateralizing outstanding Letters of Credit at             103%         of face amount or delivering replacement Letters of Credit, tracking Section 2.04(e) and 2.04(f) of the First Lien Credit Agreement exactly. This is consistent with the First Lien Credit Agreement’s own terms and ensures that the Issuing Bank is protected regardless of whether the purchaser assumes the Revolver. (Note: the working draft ICA’s definition of         “    Discharge of First Lien Obligations    ”         currently references 105% cash collateralization for Letters of Credit; this should be conformed to 103% to match the operative First Lien Credit Agreement. See Part IX.)  
          Exercise triggers.         The compromise adopts the First Lien Agent’s trigger formulation (exercise upon an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement), which is the universal market practice —             100% of the surveyed deals permit exercise upon acceleration of the 1L    , and 74% also permit exercise upon commencement of a 1L enforcement action. The Second Lien Agent’s broader proposed triggers (including a 2L Event of Default and 1L acceleration notice) are not adopted, as the narrower 1L-Event-of-Default trigger is the market standard and is sufficient to make the right exercisable in the distress scenarios for which it is designed.  
      
         VII. Compromise Provision 3 — Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness (Section 2.03(c))  
       A. Contested Positions  
      This provision has three sub-issues: (A) the principal increase cap; (B) the maturity floor; and (C) the restrictiveness test.  
          First Lien Agent (opening):         (A)             0% increase         — refinancing must be for equal or lesser principal; (B) maturity no earlier than             91 days after the 1L Maturity Date         (September 14, 2030); (C) a             blanket         “    no more restrictive in any material respect    ”         test applied to all terms of the refinancing indebtedness. The First Lien Agent argues that any increase would compress the already-thin equity cushion (a 10% increase would push Total LTV to ~100%), that the 91-day buffer prevents a competing maturity wall, and that only a blanket test prevents incremental restrictions from being introduced piecemeal.  
          Second Lien Agent (opening):         (A)             20% increase         ($30M on $150M) to accommodate refinancing costs (call premiums, arranger fees, OID, legal costs); (B) maturity no earlier than the             original 2L Maturity Date         (June 15, 2031); (C)             no restrictiveness test         at all, on the ground that the ICA governs intercreditor matters, not the bilateral commercial terms of the 2L facility. The Second Lien Agent argues that a 0% cap would prohibit virtually any refinancing with non-trivial transaction costs, that the original 2L maturity already exceeds the 91-day floor by ~9 months, and that a restrictiveness test would give the First Lien Agent an effective veto over 2L commercial terms.  
          Signaled flexibility:         The First Lien Agent has signaled openness to a             narrowed restrictiveness test paired with an MFN right         (rather than a blanket test), and has stated it is         “    willing to discuss    ”         the principal increase         “    in the context of the overall package.    ”         The Second Lien Agent has signaled it         “    can accept a 10% principal increase cap    ”         ($15M)         “    provided the increase is limited to refinancing-related fees and costs,    ”         and is         “    open to discussing whether a narrowed restrictiveness test paired with an MFN right could work.    ”         The convergence is on             10% limited to fees/costs, narrowed test plus MFN    .  
        B. Proposed Compromise Language  
      The following language is proposed to replace the bracketed definition of         “    Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness    ”         in Section 2.03(c) of the working draft ICA in its entirety:  
          “        Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness        ”         means indebtedness incurred to refinance, replace, or substitute the Second Lien Obligations, provided that:  
          (i) Principal Amount.         The aggregate principal amount of such refinancing indebtedness shall not exceed one hundred ten percent (110%) of the aggregate principal amount of the Second Lien Obligations being refinanced (i.e., based on the current $150,000,000 Second Lien Term Loan B, a maximum of $165,000,000, reflecting a $15,000,000 permitted increase); provided that the excess of such refinancing indebtedness over the principal amount of the Second Lien Obligations being refinanced shall be limited to the payment of (A) accrued and unpaid interest, (B) premiums (including call premiums or make-whole amounts payable under the Second Lien Credit Agreement), (C) original issue discount, (D) arrangement, underwriting, and placement fees, and (E) reasonable and documented legal, advisory, rating agency, and other transaction costs and expenses incurred in connection with such refinancing;  
          (ii) Maturity.         The stated final maturity date of such refinancing indebtedness shall be no earlier than the later of (A) the date that is ninety-one (91) days after the First Lien Maturity Date (i.e., no earlier than September 14, 2030) and (B) the Second Lien Maturity Date (i.e., no earlier than June 15, 2031);  
          (iii) Narrowed Restrictiveness Test — Financial Maintenance Covenants.         The financial maintenance covenants contained in the documentation governing such refinancing indebtedness shall not be more restrictive to the Borrower than the financial maintenance covenants contained in the Second Lien Credit Agreement as in effect on the Closing Date (it being understood and agreed that the existing financial maintenance covenant is a Total Leverage ratio not to exceed 6.50 to 1.00). For the avoidance of doubt, no restrictiveness test shall apply to any other terms of such refinancing indebtedness, including pricing (interest rate, fees, and original issue discount), non-financial covenants (affirmative covenants, negative covenants other than financial maintenance covenants), representations and warranties, events of default, remedies, mandatory prepayment provisions, or conditions to borrowing;  
          (iv) Most-Favored-Nation Right.         If the documentation governing such refinancing indebtedness contains any financial maintenance covenant that is more favorable to the lenders thereunder than the corresponding financial maintenance covenant in the First Lien Credit Agreement as in effect on the date of such refinancing (including, without limitation, a Total Leverage or First Lien Leverage maintenance covenant set at a level more restrictive than the then-applicable level under the First Lien Credit Agreement), then the First Lien Lenders shall automatically receive the benefit of such more favorable financial maintenance covenant, and the First Lien Credit Agreement shall be deemed amended (without further action or consent by any party) to incorporate such more favorable financial maintenance covenant as of the date such refinancing indebtedness is incurred; and  
          (v) Joinder.         The holders of such refinancing indebtedness shall have become party to this Agreement (or a replacement intercreditor agreement on substantially similar terms) by executing and delivering a joinder agreement in form and substance reasonably satisfactory to the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent.  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Principal increase cap — 10% limited to fees/costs is the market median and within 2L flexibility.         The Market Survey shows a             median principal increase cap of 10%         (mean 8.9%): 21% of deals permit 0%,             52% permit 1–10%    , 14% permit 11–15%, and 12% permit 16–20%. Critically,             79% of deals that permit any increase (26 of 33) limit the increase to fees, costs, and premiums         — exactly the formulation the Second Lien Agent has signaled it can accept. Deal-007 (the structurally comparable transaction) uses a             10% cap ($15M) limited to fees/costs    . The compromise thus tracks both the median and the most directly comparable precedent. The Second Lien Agent’s 20% opening ($30M) is at the high end of the market (only 12% of deals permit 16–20%), and its concession to 10% limited to fees/costs is a meaningful move. The First Lien Agent’s 0% opening is also a minority position (21% of deals).  
          Quantified economic impact of the principal cap.         The First Lien Agent’s concern is that any increase compresses the equity cushion. The arithmetic:  
              0% increase         (1L opening): total secured debt remains $625M; Total LTV remains 97.66%.  
              10% increase         (compromise): total secured debt becomes $640M ($625M + $15M); Total LTV becomes             100.0%         ($640M ÷ $640M).  
              20% increase         (2L opening): total secured debt becomes $655M ($625M + $30M); Total LTV becomes             102.3%         ($655M ÷ $640M) — secured debt would exceed TEV.  
      The 10% cap is the maximum increase that does not push Total LTV beyond 100% at the current TEV, and because the increase is             limited to refinancing fees/costs/premiums         (not new-money borrowing), it does not increase the Borrower’s economic exposure to its asset base — it merely accommodates the friction costs of a refinancing (call premium of 102%/101% in years 1–2 per the Second Lien Credit Agreement § 2.04(b); arranger fees of 200–350 bps; OID; legal costs). A 20% increase, by contrast, would push secured debt above TEV even before any value erosion, which is the precise outcome the First Lien Agent’s 0% position is designed to prevent. The 10%-limited-to-fees formulation is the defensible middle: it permits a commercially viable refinancing while capping the incremental secured debt at a level that does not, at current valuations, eliminate the equity cushion.  
          Maturity floor — both tests, because the 2L position inherently satisfies the 1L minimum.         The Market Survey shows that             93% of deals (39 of 42) use the 91-days-after-1L-maturity test    , and             81% (34 of 42) also include the original-2L-maturity floor    . The compromise adopts             both         tests (the later of the two controls), which is the majority practice. This resolves the dispute cleanly because, as both the engagement email and the capital structure summary note,             the original 2L Maturity Date of June 15, 2031 already exceeds the 91-day-post-1L date of September 14, 2030 by approximately nine months    . The Second Lien Agent’s position (maturity ≥ June 15, 2031) is therefore inherently             more restrictive         than the First Lien Agent’s minimum (≥ September 14, 2030) — there is no scenario in which the 2L position permits an earlier maturity than the 1L position would allow. Adopting both tests gives the First Lien Agent its stated 91-day buffer (satisfied) and gives the Second Lien Agent its stated original-maturity floor (satisfied), with no economic conflict between them. This is a rare instance in which the two positions are not in tension, and the compromise simply codifies both.  
          Narrowed restrictiveness test plus MFN — the market-standard pairing and within both sides’ flexibility.         The Market Survey shows that             52% of deals (22 of 42) use a narrowed restrictiveness test    , 33% use a blanket test, and 14% use no test. Most importantly,             100% of the deals that use a narrowed test pair it with an MFN provision         (22 of 22), and             82% of those MFN provisions are limited to financial maintenance covenants only         (18 of 22). The         “    narrowed test plus MFN on financial covenants    ”         combination is therefore the dominant market practice, and the trend is accelerating: the combination appeared in 38% of 2022 deals and 65% of 2024 deals. Both agents have signaled openness to this pairing: the First Lien Agent has proposed         “    a narrowed restrictiveness test … provided it is combined with a most-favored-nation right,    ”         and the Second Lien Agent is         “    open to discussing whether a narrowed restrictiveness test paired with an MFN right could work.    ”  
          Why the narrowed test (financial covenants only) is the right scope.         The First Lien Agent’s blanket test would give it an effective veto over all terms of any 2L refinancing — the Second Lien Agent’s core objection, and a position the market does not support at this leverage level (the Second Lien Agent notes that the four surveyed deals with blanket tests all had leverage below 5.0x). The compromise narrows the test to             financial maintenance covenants only    , which is where the First Lien Agent’s legitimate concern actually lies: a tightening of the Total Leverage covenant (currently 6.50x) in a refinancing could trigger a 2L default while the Borrower remains in compliance with its 1L covenants, destabilizing the capital structure. Limiting the test to financial maintenance covenants addresses that risk while leaving the 2L lenders free to negotiate pricing, non-financial covenants, events of default, and remedies with the Borrower — the bilateral commercial terms that, as the Second Lien Agent correctly notes, are governed by the Second Lien Credit Agreement, not the ICA.  
          Why the MFN right protects the First Lien Agent.         The MFN right ensures that if a 2L refinancing introduces a financial maintenance covenant more favorable to the refinancing lenders than the corresponding covenant in the First Lien Credit Agreement, the First Lien Lenders automatically receive the benefit of that tighter covenant. This is the protection the First Lien Agent sought in exchange for narrowing the restrictiveness test: rather than policing every term of the refinancing through a blanket test, the First Lien Lenders are guaranteed to capture any incremental financial-covenant protection the 2L refinancing lenders obtain. The MFN is benchmarked against the existing 2L financial maintenance covenant (Total Leverage ≤ 6.50x), consistent with the Second Lien Credit Agreement § 7.08(d), which already deems any total-leverage covenant set below 6.50x to be         “    more restrictive.    ”         This internal consistency between the 2L Credit Agreement and the proposed ICA language reduces the risk of documentation conflict.  
          Conforming note.         The Second Lien Credit Agreement’s own definition of         “    Permitted Refinancing Indebtedness    ”         currently permits up to 120% ($30M) of the original principal. To conform the 2L Credit Agreement to the ICA compromise, that definition should be amended to permit up to 110% ($15M) limited to fees/costs/premiums. The First Lien Credit Agreement § 6.09(b) already permits a principal increase         “    except for capitalized interest, premiums, fees, and customary refinancing costs permitted under the Intercreditor Agreement,    ”         so the 10%-limited-to-fees formulation is consistent with the 1L Credit Agreement as drafted. See Part IX.  
      
         VIII. Compromise Provision 4 — Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds (Section 4.02)  
       A. Contested Positions  
          First Lien Agent (opening):         A             100% hard sweep         of all Net Cash Proceeds from any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event to the First Lien Obligations, with an             express override         of the reinvestment rights in the First Lien Credit Agreement (including the 365-day reinvestment period and the $5M individual / $15M annual aggregate thresholds), and             no portion         of such proceeds available to the Second Lien Obligations or the Borrower until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations. The First Lien Agent argues that the 97.66% Total LTV means any leakage of proceeds directly impairs first lien recovery, and that the ICA must establish a single, clear waterfall that controls over the underlying credit agreements.  
          Second Lien Agent (opening):         Application of Net Cash Proceeds             in accordance with the reinvestment and mandatory prepayment mechanics in the First Lien Credit Agreement         (including the 365-day reinvestment period and the $5M/$15M thresholds), with             residual proceeds flowing to the Second Lien Obligations         after full 1L application. The Second Lien Agent argues that a blanket ICA sweep would deprive the Borrower of operational flexibility the First Lien Credit Agreement itself grants, would trap residual value above the 1L obligations without benefit to the 1L lenders, and that some form of 2L access to residual proceeds must be preserved.  
          Signaled flexibility:         The First Lien Agent has stated that a             hard sweep for large or extraordinary dispositions is essential and not subject to significant compromise    , but that             reinvestment rights for smaller, ordinary-course asset sales                 “        may be acceptable        ”         — specifically, that         “    a workable approach might be for the ICA to defer to the 1L Credit Agreement’s existing reinvestment mechanics for ordinary-course sales below a defined threshold, while imposing a hard 100% sweep to 1L obligations for larger or extraordinary dispositions,    ”         with             “        anti-evasion language … essential        ”         to prevent threshold evasion. The First Lien Agent has also stated that residual proceeds flowing to the 2L after full 1L satisfaction is         “    less of a concern if the hard sweep for significant sales is preserved.    ”         The Second Lien Agent has stated that its             primary concern is that some form of 2L access to residual proceeds be preserved         (    “    symbolically important even if economically limited    ”    ), that it         “    could live with the 1L reinvestment mechanics applying for ordinary-course sales    ”         provided residual flows to the 2L rather than the Borrower, and that             anti-evasion language is needed         to prevent asset-stripping through sub-threshold sales. The convergence is on a             bifurcated approach with a threshold, anti-evasion, and 2L residual access    .  
        B. Proposed Compromise Language  
      The following language is proposed to replace the bracketed Section 4.02 of the working draft ICA in its entirety:  
          Section 4.02 — Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds.  
          (a) Ordinary-Course Asset Dispositions.         Net Cash Proceeds from Asset Dispositions that do not constitute Extraordinary Asset Dispositions (as defined below) shall be applied in accordance with the reinvestment and mandatory prepayment mechanics set forth in the First Lien Credit Agreement, including the Borrower’s right to reinvest such Net Cash Proceeds in Permitted Reinvestment Assets during the 365-day Reinvestment Period and the mandatory prepayment sweep of non-reinvested proceeds upon expiration of such period, as set forth in Section 2.05(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement. For purposes of this Section 4.02(a), the individual Asset Disposition prepayment threshold of $5,000,000 and the annual aggregate threshold of $15,000,000 set forth in Section 2.05(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement shall apply.  
          (b) Extraordinary Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events — Hard Sweep.         Notwithstanding Section 4.02(a) or any provision of the First Lien Credit Agreement to the contrary, one hundred percent (100%) of all Net Cash Proceeds from (i) any Extraordinary Asset Disposition and (ii) any Casualty Event shall be applied to prepay the First Lien Obligations in accordance with the mandatory prepayment provisions of the First Lien Credit Agreement. The reinvestment rights granted to the Borrower under the First Lien Credit Agreement (including the 365-day Reinvestment Period) are hereby superseded by this Section 4.02(b) with respect to such Net Cash Proceeds, and no reinvestment in replacement assets shall be permitted with respect to any such Net Cash Proceeds. No portion of such Net Cash Proceeds shall be available for application to the Second Lien Obligations, for reinvestment by the Borrower, or for distribution to the Borrower, its equity holders, or any other person, in each case until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations.         “    Extraordinary Asset Disposition    ”         means (x) any Asset Disposition generating Net Cash Proceeds of $20,000,000 or more individually, or (y) any series of related Asset Dispositions generating aggregate Net Cash Proceeds of $20,000,000 or more, in each case as determined after giving effect to the anti-evasion provisions of Section 4.02(c).  
          (c) Anti-Evasion.         No Asset Disposition (and no series of related Asset Dispositions) shall be structured, timed, or otherwise arranged in a manner designed to avoid the $20,000,000 Extraordinary Asset Disposition threshold set forth in Section 4.02(b). The First Lien Agent may, in its reasonable discretion, aggregate any Asset Dispositions occurring within a 90-day period if the First Lien Agent reasonably determines that such Asset Dispositions constitute a single plan or related series of transactions, and, in such event, the Net Cash Proceeds from all such aggregated Asset Dispositions shall be treated as Net Cash Proceeds from a single Asset Disposition for purposes of determining whether the $20,000,000 threshold has been exceeded. In making such determination, the First Lien Agent shall consider, among other factors, the identity of the acquirer or acquirers, the nature and location of the assets disposed of, and the timing and sequencing of the dispositions. The foregoing anti-evasion provisions shall apply in addition to, and not in lieu of, the anti-evasion provisions set forth in Section 2.05(b)(iii) of the First Lien Credit Agreement.  
          (d) Residual Proceeds to Second Lien.         After the Discharge of First Lien Obligations (or after the First Lien Agent delivers written confirmation to the Second Lien Agent that no further application of Net Cash Proceeds to the First Lien Obligations is required or that the First Lien Obligations have been paid in full), any residual Net Cash Proceeds remaining from Asset Dispositions or Casualty Events (including, for the avoidance of doubt, Net Cash Proceeds not reinvested upon expiration of the Reinvestment Period under Section 4.02(a)) shall be released to the Second Lien Agent for application to the Second Lien Obligations in accordance with the terms of the Second Lien Credit Agreement, and shall not be distributed to the Borrower or any other person.  
          (e) Reporting.         The Borrower shall deliver to the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent (i) a quarterly certificate of compliance identifying all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events during the applicable fiscal quarter and the application of Net Cash Proceeds therefrom, (ii) prompt written notice (within five (5) Business Days) of any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event yielding Net Cash Proceeds in excess of $5,000,000, and (iii) an annual aggregate disposition report setting forth an itemized list of all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events during the applicable fiscal year and the aggregate Net Cash Proceeds therefrom.  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          100% sweep to the First Lien is universal and preserved.         The Market Survey shows that             100% of the 42 surveyed deals sweep Net Cash Proceeds to the First Lien         as the first priority. The First Lien Agent’s insistence on a 1L-first sweep is therefore not a contested point in the market — it is universal. The compromise preserves a 100% sweep to the First Lien for all proceeds; the dispute is not             whether         the 1L is paid first, but             how         (with or without reinvestment) and             what happens to the residual    .  
          Bifurcated approach — ordinary-course reinvestment below $20M, hard sweep above.         The Market Survey shows that             81% of deals (34 of 42) preserve a reinvestment right         for the Borrower, and that             91% of those deals (31 of 34) use a 365-day reinvestment period         tracking the First Lien Credit Agreement. The             median ordinary-course threshold is $20,000,000         (mean $19.7M; range $10M–$30M), and             97% of deals with an ordinary-course exclusion (33 of 34) include an anti-evasion clause at the same threshold    . Deal-007 (the structurally comparable transaction) uses an             ordinary-course threshold of $20M with an anti-evasion clause at the same level, a 365-day reinvestment period, a hard sweep for extraordinary sales, and 2L residual access after full 1L paydown         — the precise architecture proposed here. The compromise thus tracks both the market median and the most directly comparable precedent. The $20M threshold represents approximately 22.4% of LTM EBITDA ($89.4M), a level that accommodates the Borrower’s routine asset turnover (replacement of aging manufacturing equipment, upgrades to production lines) without permitting material collateral stripping — the operational flexibility you emphasized in the engagement email that the First Lien Credit Agreement’s own reinvestment mechanics were designed to protect.  
          Hard sweep for extraordinary sales and all Casualty Events — the First Lien Agent’s firm requirement, preserved.         The Market Survey shows that             90% of deals (38 of 42) include a hard sweep for large or extraordinary sales    . The compromise imposes a hard 100% sweep (with override of reinvestment rights) for any Asset Disposition generating $20M or more and for all Casualty Events, which is the First Lien Agent’s stated essential requirement. This protects the First Lien Agent’s core concern: that the 97.66% Total LTV leaves no room for leakage of proceeds from significant dispositions. Casualty Events are swept in full regardless of size, because insurance/condemnation proceeds are not part of the Borrower’s ordinary-course asset management and their diversion would directly erode collateral coverage.  
          Anti-evasion — essential to both sides and the market norm.         Both agents have independently flagged the need for anti-evasion language, and the Market Survey confirms it is near-universal (97% of deals with an ordinary-course exclusion include it). The compromise adopts the anti-evasion architecture already present in the First Lien Credit Agreement § 2.05(b)(iii): a 90-day aggregation period, with the First Lien Agent authorized to aggregate related dispositions based on the identity of the acquirer, the nature and location of the assets, and the timing and sequencing. This mirrors the Borrower’s existing obligations under the First Lien Credit Agreement (which already contains this anti-evasion language at the $5M/$15M thresholds), so it imposes no new operational burden — it extends the same logic to the $20M intercreditor threshold. The anti-evasion provisions apply             in addition to         (not in lieu of) the First Lien Credit Agreement’s own anti-evasion provisions, providing layered protection.  
          2L residual access — the Second Lien Agent’s firm minimum, accommodated.         The Market Survey shows that             79% of deals (33 of 42) provide the Second Lien with access to residual proceeds         after full 1L application. The First Lien Agent has stated that residual to the 2L after full 1L satisfaction is         “    less of a concern if the hard sweep for significant sales is preserved.    ”         The Second Lien Agent has stated that some form of 2L residual access         “    must be preserved    ”         —         “    symbolically important even if economically limited    ”         — and that residual should flow to the 2L rather than back to the Borrower. The compromise delivers exactly this: after the Discharge of First Lien Obligations (or the First Lien Agent’s confirmation that no further 1L application is required), residual proceeds flow to the Second Lien, not to the Borrower. This is economically limited (it only matters once the 1L is paid in full, which at 97.66% Total LTV requires near-complete satisfaction of the 1L from all sources), but it gives the Second Lien the waterfall right it needs to demonstrate to its investors and LPs, and it is more efficient than the alternative (trapping residual cash at the Borrower level) because it deleverages the capital structure for the benefit of the next-priority secured creditors.  
          Reporting — the administrability layer.         The Market Survey shows that             90% of deals require a quarterly certificate of compliance    ,             79% require event-driven notice         of sales above a trigger, and             38% require an annual aggregate disposition report    . The compromise adopts all three, tracking Deal-007’s reporting package (quarterly certificate + prompt notice of sales above $5M + annual aggregate report). The $5M event-driven notice threshold is set at the First Lien Credit Agreement’s individual prepayment trigger, giving both agents early visibility into potential mandatory-prepayment events and enabling monitoring of anti-evasion compliance — a concern both sides raised. This reporting layer did not appear in either party’s bracketed proposal and is drawn from the drafting note in the working ICA, which flagged the absence of a reporting mechanism as an open issue.  
      
         IX. Cross-Cutting Observations and Required Conforming Amendments  
      The four compromise provisions above are drafted to fit within the existing architecture of the working ICA and the two credit agreements. To ensure internal consistency across the transaction documents, the following conforming amendments and cross-document observations should be noted and addressed during documentation:  
             First Lien Credit Agreement § 2.08(b) — interest-rate basis for the Purchase Price.         Section 2.08(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement currently provides that         “    any calculation of First Lien Obligations for purposes of such Purchase Price shall include accrued and unpaid Default Rate interest.    ”         The compromise adopts the             non-default contract rate         for the Purchase Price. Because the ICA is the controlling intercreditor instrument (see ICA § 9.10, which provides that the ICA governs in the event of conflict), the ICA’s Purchase Price definition will control as between the lien levels. However, to avoid any ambiguity,             Section 2.08(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement should be conformed         to provide that, for purposes of the Second Lien Purchase Right under the ICA, accrued interest is calculated at the non-default contract rate. This is a conforming amendment within the First Lien Agent’s signaled flexibility (the First Lien Agent has stated the non-default rate         “    may be    ”         acceptable) and should not require syndicate re-approval if presented as part of the integrated package.  
             ICA definition of                 “        Discharge of First Lien Obligations        ”                 — LC cash-collateralization percentage.         The working draft ICA (Section 1.01) defines         “    Discharge of First Lien Obligations    ”         to require Letters of Credit to be         “    cash collateralized in an amount equal to one hundred five percent (105%).    ”         The operative First Lien Credit Agreement (Sections 2.04(e) and 2.04(f)) requires cash collateralization at             103%         of face amount. The compromise Purchase Price language adopts 103% to conform to the First Lien Credit Agreement.             The ICA’s                 “        Discharge of First Lien Obligations        ”                 definition should be conformed from 105% to 103%         to eliminate this inconsistency. This is a technical conforming change, not a negotiated term.  
             Second Lien Credit Agreement —                 “        Permitted Refinancing Indebtedness        ”                 definition.         The Second Lien Credit Agreement’s definition of         “    Permitted Refinancing Indebtedness    ”         (Section 1.01) currently permits up to             120%         ($30M) of the original principal, attributable to accrued interest, premiums, fees, and transaction costs. The ICA compromise caps the increase at             110% ($15M) limited to fees/costs/premiums    . Because the ICA governs the intercreditor relationship and the First Lien Credit Agreement § 6.09(b) already restricts 2L principal increases to amounts         “    permitted under the Intercreditor Agreement,    ”         the ICA’s 10% cap will control.             The Second Lien Credit Agreement’s                 “        Permitted Refinancing Indebtedness        ”                 definition should be conformed         from 120% to 110% (limited to fees/costs/premiums) to avoid any internal inconsistency within the 2L documents. The Second Lien Agent has signaled acceptance of the 10% cap, so this conforming amendment is within its signaled flexibility.  
             Second Lien Credit Agreement § 7.08(d) — MFN benchmark consistency.         Section 7.08(d) of the Second Lien Credit Agreement already provides that any Permitted Refinancing Indebtedness with a total-leverage maintenance covenant set below 6.50x         “    shall be deemed to impose a more restrictive covenant.    ”         The ICA compromise’s MFN right is benchmarked against the same 6.50x Total Leverage covenant, so the two provisions are internally consistent. No conforming amendment is required, but the documentation team should confirm that the MFN’s         “    more favorable to the lenders    ”         formulation aligns with the 2L Credit Agreement’s         “    more restrictive to the Borrower    ”         formulation so that the two tests operate in the same direction.  
             Blockage Notice mechanics.         The compromise introduces a Blockage Notice cap (two per rolling 365-day period; 360 aggregate days). The working ICA’s Section 3.01(b) and the standard-form ICA’s Section 3.5 both currently provide for no limit on the number of Standstill Periods. The cap is a new element that should be reflected in the standstill mechanics and cross-referenced in the definitions of         “    Blockage Notice    ”         and         “    Standstill Period.    ”         The drafting note in the working ICA already flags the absence of a Blockage Notice cap as an open issue, so this is anticipated.  
             Interaction with the First Lien Credit Agreement’s reinvestment provisions.         The compromise’s bifurcated Section 4.02 expressly defers to the First Lien Credit Agreement’s reinvestment mechanics for ordinary-course sales below $20M and expressly overrides them for extraordinary sales and Casualty Events. The First Lien Credit Agreement § 2.05(b)(iv) already provides that the reinvestment rights are         “    subject to the provisions of the Intercreditor Agreement, including Section 4.02 thereof,    ”         so the ICA’s override is contemplated by and consistent with the First Lien Credit Agreement as drafted. No conforming amendment to the First Lien Credit Agreement’s reinvestment provisions is required.  
      
        X. Package Balance — Why the Four Provisions Must Be Accepted or Rejected as a Whole  
      The four compromises are calibrated so that each side gives ground where it has flexibility and holds firm where it does not, and so that the concessions are reciprocal across provisions. The balance can be summarized as follows:  
          What the First Lien Agent gains (relative to its opening positions):  
          A 180-day standstill (down from 270, but at the market median and above its 150-day floor), with triggers that are narrow and objectively verifiable — the formulation its committee said it         “    would be willing to consider.    ”  
              Par for funded debt         — its non-negotiable — preserved in full.  
          A             hard 100% sweep         for extraordinary asset sales and all Casualty Events — its essential requirement — preserved in full.  
          A             narrowed restrictiveness test paired with an MFN right         — the specific formulation it proposed as a         “    reasonable middle ground    ”         — adopted in full, with the MFN benchmarked to the existing 6.50x covenant.  
          A             10% principal cap         (vs. its 0% opening) that, because it is limited to fees/costs, does not increase the Borrower’s economic exposure and does not push Total LTV beyond 100% at current valuations.  
          Robust             anti-evasion         language and a             reporting regime         that did not appear in either party’s bracketed proposal but that both sides flagged as necessary.  
          What the First Lien Agent concedes (within its signaled flexibility):  
          Accrued interest at the             non-default contract rate         (vs. default rate) — a bounded concession of ~$2.375M per 90-day period, and the majority market practice (69% of deals).  
              Optional         (not mandatory) Revolver assumption — within its signaled flexibility, and paired with a 90-day Borrower replacement right that protects Meridian’s liquidity.  
              Early-termination triggers         on the standstill — within its signaled flexibility, narrow and objectively verifiable, and the majority market practice (76% of deals).  
          A             Blockage Notice cap         — a modest protection against serial standstills.  
              2L residual access         to asset-sale proceeds after full 1L satisfaction — which the First Lien Agent stated is         “    less of a concern if the hard sweep for significant sales is preserved.    ”  
              Ordinary-course reinvestment         below $20M — which the First Lien Agent stated         “    may be acceptable.    ”  
          What the Second Lien Agent gains (relative to its opening positions):  
              Early-termination triggers         on the standstill — its firm, linked condition — adopted, with three triggers tracking the most common market trigger types.  
          A             Blockage Notice cap         — protection against indefinite serial standstills, which it warned against.  
              Optional         (not mandatory) Revolver assumption — its deal-breaker condition — adopted, resolving the LPA constraint.  
          Accrued interest at the             non-default contract rate         — which it advocated.  
          A             10% principal cap         ($15M) limited to fees/costs — its signaled compromise position — adopted, sufficient to cover refinancing friction costs.  
          The             original-2L-maturity floor         (June 15, 2031) — its stated position — adopted (alongside the 91-day floor, which it inherently satisfies).  
          A             narrowed restrictiveness test         (financial covenants only, not blanket) — its core objection to the blanket test addressed.  
              Ordinary-course reinvestment         below $20M and             2L residual access         after full 1L paydown — its firm minimum on the proceeds waterfall — adopted, with residual flowing to the 2L rather than the Borrower.  
              Anti-evasion         language — which it requested.  
          What the Second Lien Agent concedes (within its signaled flexibility):  
              Par for funded debt         (giving up its FMV/dealer-poll alternative) — a significant concession, but one it         “    acknowledges … may be acceptable,    ”         and the majority market practice (71% of deals).  
          A             180-day standstill         (up from 90) — at the market median and the maximum it signaled it can accept, acceptable only because of the triggers and cap.  
          A             10% cap         (down from 20%) — its signaled compromise position, limited to fees/costs.  
          A             narrowed restrictiveness test plus MFN         (rather than no test) — which it signaled it is         “    open to discussing.    ”  
          A             hard sweep         for extraordinary sales and Casualty Events (rather than full reinvestment for all sales) — acceptable because ordinary-course reinvestment and 2L residual access are preserved.  
          The reciprocal structure.         Each side’s concessions are the price of the other side’s concessions. The First Lien Agent’s non-default-rate and optional-Revolver concessions make par acceptable to Ridgeline; Ridgeline’s acceptance of par (giving up FMV) makes the non-default rate and optional Revolver saleable to Cartwright’s syndicate. The First Lien Agent’s early-termination-trigger and Blockage-Notice-cap concessions make the 180-day standstill acceptable to Ridgeline; Ridgeline’s acceptance of 180 days (up from 90) makes the triggers saleable to Cartwright’s credit committee. The First Lien Agent’s narrowed-test-plus-MFN concession makes the 10% cap and original-maturity floor acceptable to Ridgeline; Ridgeline’s acceptance of 10% (down from 20%) and the narrowed test makes the MFN saleable to Cartwright. The First Lien Agent’s ordinary-course-reinvestment and 2L-residual concessions make the hard sweep for extraordinary sales acceptable to Ridgeline; Ridgeline’s acceptance of the hard sweep makes the ordinary-course reinvestment and residual access saleable to Cartwright.  
      This reciprocal structure is why the package must be presented and accepted as a whole. If any single provision is extracted and re-litigated in isolation, the reciprocal concessions that support the other provisions lose their justification, and the package unravels. Meridian’s endorsement of the package as a whole — which you indicated you are willing to provide — is therefore not merely helpful advocacy; it is structurally necessary to preserve the balance.

        III. Governing Framework  
      The PERM application is filed pursuant to Section 212(a)(5)(A) of the Immigration and Nationality Act, 8 U.S.C. § 1182(a)(5)(A), and the implementing regulations at 20 CFR Part 656, as stated on the face of the form. The elements that govern this gap analysis are drawn from those authorities and from the structure of the ETA 9089 itself:  
             The employer’s minimum requirements must be bona fide and normal to the occupation.         Under 20 CFR 656.17, the job requirements stated on the application must be those normally required for the occupation; the Employer must actually require them of all applicants for the job opportunity, and the requirements may not be tailored to the alien’s specific qualifications. A requirement that is not applied to all applicants, or that is not normal to the occupation, is a basis for denial or revocation.  
             The employer must attest that the alien meets the minimum requirements.         The ETA 9089 requires the Employer to certify that the foreign worker meets the education, experience, and special-skill minimums stated in Section J.B (reflected in the Section K declarations and the employer certifications in Sections F and N). A false attestation that the alien possesses a required qualification is a material misrepresentation.  
             The alien must possess the minimum qualifications as of the priority date.         For a PERM-based I-140, U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) reviews whether the beneficiary held the minimum education, experience, and special requirements as of the priority date (here, March 15, 2025, the PERM filing date). Qualifications acquired after the priority date generally cannot be used to satisfy the certified minimum requirements.  
             A certified labor certification may be revoked for fraud or willful misrepresentation.         Under 20 CFR 656.31, the Department of Labor may revoke a certification — including after it has been granted — upon a finding of fraud or willful misrepresentation of a material fact. The form’s own alien declaration (Section L) warns that falsification may result in denial, revocation, and criminal prosecution under 18 U.S.C. § 1546.  
             The H-1B (I-129) is a separate proceeding.         The H-1B nonimmigrant petition requires (i) a specialty occupation (one requiring, at minimum, a U.S. bachelor’s degree or its equivalent in a specific specialty) and (ii) a beneficiary who holds the required degree. It does not require the PERM, does not require 5 years of post-master’s experience, and does not require the AWS ML Specialty certification. The PERM and the H-1B therefore stand or fall on different showings.  
      We have audited the supporting credentials against each element the Employer certified in Sections J.B and K, and we report the findings element by element below.  
      
        IV. Gap Analysis  
       Issue 1 — AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification required but not held (CRITICAL)  
          What the PERM requires.         Section J.B.5 states:         “    AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification or equivalent required.    ”         The word         “    required    ”         makes this a minimum special requirement, not a preference. The addendum to Section J.B.5 reiterates that         “    [t]he position requires the AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification or equivalent.    ”  
          What the credentials show.         The certifications compilation prepared by this firm (June 2025) lists exactly two credentials:  
              AWS Certified Cloud Practitioner         — Validation No. AWS-CCP-2022-0418-MEHROTRA, issued Jan. 18, 2022,             expired Jan. 18, 2025    , status         “    EXPIRED.    ”         The compilation expressly notes this is a             foundational-level         certification that         “    does not demonstrate specialization in any specific AWS service domain, including machine learning.    ”  
              TensorFlow Developer Certificate         — Validation No. TF-DEV-2023-0310-AM, issued March 10, 2023, no expiration, active. This is a             Google         framework-specific developer certificate; the compilation notes it         “    is not affiliated with Amazon Web Services (AWS).    ”  
      The compilation’s verification notes state unambiguously:         “    Counsel specifically inquired whether the beneficiary holds the AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification or any other AWS specialty-level certification; the beneficiary confirmed she does not hold any such certification.    ”  
          The gap.         The beneficiary does not hold the required AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification, and neither credential she does hold is a reasonable         “    equivalent    ”    :  
          The AWS Certified Cloud Practitioner is             foundational         (not specialty), covers general cloud concepts rather than machine learning, and is             expired    . It is not equivalent to a specialty-level ML certification.  
          The TensorFlow Developer Certificate is issued by a             different vendor (Google)    , validates proficiency in the TensorFlow framework, and does             not         cover AWS services, Amazon SageMaker, or cloud-based ML deployment — the very domains the AWS ML Specialty certification tests. It is not an         “    equivalent    ”         to an AWS specialty certification.  
          Severity: CRITICAL.  
          Consequence and risk.  
             False attestation on the certified PERM.         Sections K.3 and K.4 attest that the beneficiary possesses the special requirements in J.B.5 (which include the certification) and is qualified for the position. The supporting credentials — including our own compilation — contradict that attestation. This is a material misrepresentation that exposes the certified PERM to             revocation         under 20 CFR 656.31 and to the penalties referenced in the form’s Section L declaration (18 U.S.C. § 1546). Because the PERM is already certified, the Department of Labor’s post-certification revocation authority is the operative risk.  
             I-140 denial risk.         USCIS reviews whether the beneficiary held the minimum requirements as of the March 15, 2025 priority date. The beneficiary did not hold the AWS ML Specialty certification (or an equivalent) on that date. An I-140 filed on the current PERM is therefore at substantial risk of denial on the ground that the beneficiary does not meet the certified minimum special requirement.  
             The defect cannot be cured by later obtaining the certification.         Because the qualification must be held as of the priority date, Dr. Mehrotra’s obtaining the AWS ML Specialty certification after March 15, 2025 does             not         retroactively satisfy the requirement for the current PERM. A post-priority-date certification cannot be used to meet a certified minimum requirement.  
             Bona fides problem either way.         The certification requirement creates a dilemma that must be resolved on the facts:  
          If the certification             is         a bona fide minimum requirement (the Employer genuinely requires it of all applicants and it is normal to the occupation), then the beneficiary did not meet it at the priority date, and the current PERM is defective.  
          If the certification             is not         a bona fide minimum requirement (it was included but not actually applied to applicants, or it is not normal to the occupation), then the requirement should not have been listed, and the PERM is vulnerable under 20 CFR 656.17 for a requirement that is not normal to the occupation or not applied to all applicants.  
         The recruitment report (Section G.3) compounds this concern: it states that the three applicants were rejected for lacking educational credentials (two) or minimum years of experience (one), but it does             not         state that any applicant was screened against, or rejected for lacking, the AWS ML Specialty certification. If the certification were a true minimum requirement, the recruitment record should reflect that it was applied. Its absence from the rejection rationale is inconsistent with treating the certification as a bona fide minimum requirement. (See Issue 6.)  
          Remedial recommendation.  
              Do not file the I-140 on the current PERM until this is resolved.         Filing as-is risks denial, PERM revocation, and a potential fraud/misrepresentation referral.  
              Make a documented bona fides determination now.         In consultation with the Employer’s hiring managers, determine on the record whether the AWS ML Specialty certification is genuinely required of all applicants for the Senior ML Engineer role and is normal to the occupation. Capture the basis in a contemporaneous memorandum (job analysis, comparable postings, hiring-manager confirmation). This determination drives the remedy.  
              If the certification is bona fide:         The current PERM is defective because the beneficiary did not hold it at the priority date and cannot cure that defect after the fact. The clean path is to             withdraw the certified PERM and refile         a new PERM with minimum requirements the beneficiary actually meets as of the new filing date (and ensure she meets every requirement, including the certification, before refiling). This resets the priority date and recruitment cycle. The H-1B can bridge the beneficiary’s status during the new PERM cycle (see Section V).  
              If the certification is not bona fide:         The requirement should not have been included. The clean path is again to             withdraw and refile         a new PERM without the certification requirement (and with all remaining requirements confirmed as bona fide and met). Proceeding on a PERM that contains a non-bona-fide requirement carries audit and revocation risk.  
              Do not advance an                 “        equivalent        ”                 argument on the current credentials.         Neither the expired AWS Cloud Practitioner nor the TensorFlow Developer Certificate is a tenable         “    equivalent    ”         to the AWS ML Specialty certification. Arguing otherwise would itself be a misrepresentation and should not be made to USCIS or the DOL.  
              Have the beneficiary earn the AWS ML Specialty certification in any event.         It strengthens any refiled PERM, supports the H-1B specialty-occupation showing, and is a genuine professional credential for the role — but understand that it does not cure the current PERM’s priority-date defect.  
              Define                 “        or equivalent        ”                 if the requirement is retained.         If a refiled PERM keeps a certification requirement, specify precisely which alternative credentials (if any) will be accepted, so that the requirement is applied consistently in recruitment and to the beneficiary.

          Determine which document is erroneous.         Issue 4 — Supervisory scope: 2 reports versus 3–5 (LOW–MEDIUM)

        IV. Issue Analysis  
       A. Enforceability and Validity Defects (must fix before execution)  
          Issue 1 — OWBPA compliance: insufficient consideration period and missing revocation period. [High]  
      The draft invokes the OWBPA in §5 and purports to release ADEA claims, but it gives Mr. Delano only             14 calendar days         to consider the agreement (§5(c), §16) and contains             no seven-day revocation period    . For an individual (non-group) release of ADEA claims, the OWBPA requires that the employee be given             at least 21 days         to consider the release and a             seven-day revocation period         after signing, during which the release is not effective. A release that fails these requirements is not         “    knowing and voluntary    ”         and is             voidable by the employee         — meaning Mr. Delano could sign, take the money, and later challenge the ADEA release as invalid.  
          Consequence:         Without correction, the ADEA release — the single most valuable part of this settlement for Greenleaf, given Mr. Delano’s age (58) and the replacement-hire evidence — is at serious risk of being unenforceable. The company would have paid $525,000+ and still face an ADEA suit.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         §5(c) and §16 changed to             21 days    ; new revocation paragraph added to §16; §1(b) Effective Date redefined as the 8th day after signing (i.e., the day after the 7-day revocation period expires), with no payment or share delivery until the Effective Date. These are non-negotiable from a defensibility standpoint.  
          Issue 2 — RSU valuation uses an outdated 409A valuation. [High]  
      §3(d) values the 5,000 accelerated RSUs at             $10.00/share         based on the July 1, 2024 valuation, for a stated component value of $50,000. Greenleaf’s records show that the             current         409A valuation by the same firm (Ridgepoint Valuation Services), dated             January 15, 2025, is $12.50/share    , making the correct value of 5,000 RSUs             $62,500         and the correct total settlement value             $537,500    , not $525,000. The draft understates the equity component by $12,500.  
          Consequence:         Using a stale valuation misstates the consideration and the agreement’s total value, creates an internal inconsistency with the company’s own valuation records, and could complicate tax reporting (the income Mr. Delano recognizes on vesting will be measured by the fair market value at delivery, not the $10.00 figure in the agreement). It is also the kind of error that, if surfaced in litigation, undermines the company’s credibility.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         §3(d) corrected to $12.50/share (January 15, 2025 valuation) and $62,500; §3(e) total corrected to $537,500.             This correction does not change the actual delivery (5,000 shares) or increase cash outlay         — it simply states the value accurately. Note that the draft accelerates only 5,000 of the 7,500 shares Mr. Delano forfeited; the remaining 2,500 shares (≈$31,250 at current FMV) stay forfeited, which is favorable to Greenleaf and should be preserved.  
          Issue 3 — Non-compete is void in California and doubtful under Oregon law. [High]  
      §9 imposes an 18-month non-compete across             Oregon, Washington, Idaho, and California    . Two problems:  
              California.         Non-competes are void as a matter of California law (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600), with narrow exceptions that do not apply here. Including California renders the provision unenforceable there and invites a challenge that contaminates the restriction generally.  
              Oregon.         Oregon’s non-compete statute (ORS 653.295) imposes specific requirements — including that the restriction be entered into at the time of hire or upon a bona fide advancement, that the employee have access to trade secrets/genuinely confidential information, and that the term not exceed 18 months. A non-compete first imposed in a             post-termination settlement         does not fit neatly within the         “    time of hire/advancement    ”         requirement, creating real doubt about enforceability. There is also a deeper inconsistency: Greenleaf’s stated reason for termination was         “    elimination of position due to restructuring,    ”         yet the company simultaneously seeks to bar Mr. Delano from working for competitors — a tension a court could read against the company.  
          Consequence:         As drafted, the non-compete is partly void (California) and partly vulnerable (Oregon). An unenforceable restriction provides no protection and, worse, gives Mr. Delano an argument that the company overreached.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         Removed California and Idaho; limited scope to Oregon and Washington; added an ORS 653.295 compliance recital and a blue-pencil clause.             Your decision:         I recommend you consider             dropping the non-compete entirely         and relying on the non-solicitation covenant (§10), the new confidential-information/trade-secret covenant (§20), and the return-of-property covenant (§19), which are more defensible. If you want to keep a non-compete, the redlined version is the most defensible form, but you should confirm with Oregon employment counsel that a settlement-based non-compete will satisfy ORS 653.295 in this posture.  
          Issue 4 — Missing government-agency carve-outs in the release, confidentiality, and non-disparagement provisions. [High]  
      The draft’s release (§4), confidentiality clause (§6), and non-disparagement clause (§7) contain no carve-out for communications with government agencies. Provisions that purport to bar an employee from filing charges with, or cooperating with, agencies such as the EEOC, OSHA, the NLRB, the SEC, or the DOL are generally unenforceable as against public policy, and         “    no-file    ”    /    “    no-cooperate    ”         language can itself create liability. This is especially sensitive here because Mr. Delano filed the OSHA complaint that triggered the pending investigation.  
          Consequence:         Without carve-outs, these provisions are vulnerable to challenge and could be construed as an attempt to silence a whistleblower — which would be damaging both in the OSHA matter and in any retaliation litigation.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         Added a government-agency carve-out to §4 (preserving the right to file/cooperate; waiving only monetary recovery; confirming the OSHA investigation continues), to §6, and to §7. Also added a carve-out to §15(a)(ii) and §10. These preserve enforceability while protecting the company’s interest in barring Mr. Delano from profiting from the released claims.  
          Issue 5 — Oregon Workplace Fairness Act (OWFA) restrictions on confidentiality and non-disparagement. [High]  
      Oregon’s Workplace Fairness Act restricts confidentiality and non-disparagement provisions in settlements of claims involving discrimination (among other covered claims). Depending on how the OWFA is applied to an age-discrimination/retaliation settlement, the confidentiality and non-disparagement provisions in §§6–7 may be             voidable         unless the employee is given specific written disclosures (including the right to consult an attorney) and a revocation period.  
          Consequence:         If the OWFA applies and the requirements are not met, §§6–7 could be voided at Mr. Delano’s election, defeating the confidentiality and non-disparagement protections Greenleaf is buying.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         Added an OWFA disclosure paragraph to §6 (right to counsel; 21-day consideration; 7-day revocation) and tied it to the revocation mechanism in §16.             You should confirm with Oregon counsel whether the OWFA’s confidentiality/non-disparagement restrictions apply to this settlement         (the analysis turns on whether         “    discrimination    ”         under the OWFA is read to include age discrimination and retaliation). If it does, the drafted disclosures and revocation right should satisfy the statutory requirements.  
        B. Significant Gaps — Missing Provisions  
          Issue 6 — No governing law or venue clause. [Medium-High]  
      The draft contains no choice-of-law, jurisdiction, or venue provision. Given that the non-compete touched four states and the parties are Oregon-based, the absence creates forum uncertainty for any enforcement action.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         New §22 — Oregon law governs; exclusive venue in state and federal courts in Multnomah County, Oregon; prevailing-party attorney fees.  
          Issue 7 — No return-of-company-property covenant. [Medium]  
      Mr. Delano was a VP with access to sensitive supply-chain, supplier, pricing, and cold-chain information. The draft contains no representation that he returned company property and confidential information and retained no copies.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         New §19 — representation of return; no retention/transfer; duty to return or destroy later-found items and certify.  
          Issue 8 — No confidential-information / trade-secret covenant; missing DTSA notice. [Medium-High]  
      The draft protects the             confidentiality of the agreement         (§6) but contains             no continuing obligation protecting Greenleaf’s own confidential information and trade secrets         — a striking omission for a senior supply-chain executive. Any agreement that         “    governs    ”         trade secrets should also include the Defend Trade Secrets Act immunity notice (18 U.S.C. § 1833(b)); absent it, the company may forfeit the ability to recover exemplary damages and attorney fees in a later trade-secret action.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         New §20 — continuing confidentiality/trade-secret obligations (supplier lists, pricing, cold-chain protocols, logistics strategies, etc.) plus the DTSA immunity notice.  
          Issue 9 — No cooperation clause. [Medium]  
      There is no cooperation clause. Given the pending OSHA investigation and potential other matters, a limited cooperation right is valuable — but it must be carefully scoped because Mr. Delano is the OSHA complainant.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         New §21 — reasonable cooperation, with carve-outs for the OSHA investigation and any matter in which Mr. Delano is a complainant/adverse party, plus expense reimbursement.  
          Issue 10 — Entire Agreement supersedes the Equity Incentive Plan it relies on. [Medium]  
      §18 states the agreement         “    supersedes all prior … agreements    ”         including         “    employment agreements,    ”         yet §3(d) and §12 simultaneously rely on the             Greenleaf 2021 Equity Incentive Plan         and on         “    surviving obligations    ”         under prior agreements. As written, §18 could be read to extinguish the very plan and surviving obligations the rest of the agreement depends on.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         Added a carve-out to §18 preserving the 2021 Equity Incentive Plan and any surviving confidentiality, IP, proprietary-information, and non-solicitation obligations.  
          Issue 11 — Reference provision mandates a                 “        positive        ”                 reference. [Medium]  
      §11 requires Greenleaf to provide a         “    positive, neutral, or at minimum non-negative    ”         reference and to state that Mr. Delano         “    was a valued member of the Greenleaf team who made significant contributions.    ”         Mandating laudatory, non-factual language exposes the company to risk if a prospective employer relies on it, and it is inconsistent with a neutral-reference posture.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         Changed to a             neutral         reference confirming dates of employment, final title, and             eligibility for rehire    ; removed the mandated laudatory language; retained the instruction to route inquiries to HR and not to disclose cause/investigation information.  
        C. Accuracy and Consistency Issues  
          Issue 12 —                 “        Back pay        ”                 amount is inconsistent with the stated period. [Medium]  
      §3(b) pays $125,000 as         “    back pay representing lost wages from the Separation Date through the Effective Date.    ”         But the Separation Date (March 14, 2025) through the Effective Date (May 5, 2025) is roughly 7.4 weeks; at $218,000/year, actual lost wages for that window are only about             $31,000    . The $125,000 figure approximates             ~7 months         of salary, not 7 weeks. The label and the amount do not match.  
          Consequence:         The mislabeling creates an internal inconsistency and a tax-characterization question. As drafted, the full $125,000 is treated as W-2 wages, on which             Greenleaf pays employer payroll taxes         (FICA, FUTA/SUTA) — roughly             $10,000–$11,000         — even though only ~$31,000 represents actual back pay through the Effective Date.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         Revised §3(b) to describe the $125,000 as         “    back pay and front pay … through approximately seven (7) months following the Separation Date,    ”         making the description match the amount and keeping W-2 treatment.             Your decision:         Alternatively, the company could recharacterize the portion above actual back pay (~$94,000) as additional compensatory damages on Form 1099-MISC, which would             save Greenleaf roughly $7,000–$8,000 in employer payroll taxes    . Mr. Delano’s counsel may resist (W-2 treatment is generally more favorable to the employee), so this is a negotiation point for you to raise.  
          Issue 13 — RSU acceleration lacks the Compensation Committee approval condition. [Medium]  
      The records state that the             Compensation Committee has discretion to approve accelerated vesting         in connection with separation agreements. §3(d) says Greenleaf         “    shall cause    ”         acceleration unconditionally, with no condition that the Compensation Committee approve it.  
          Consequence:         The company could commit to acceleration that the Compensation Committee has not authorized, creating a corporate-authority problem.  
          Recommendation (drafted):         Added         “    subject to the approval of the Compensation Committee of the Board of Directors    ”         and acknowledged the committee’s authority under the plan.             Action item:         obtain Compensation Committee approval before execution.  
          Issue 14 — Recital misstates the division. [Low]  
      The first recital says Mr. Delano served in         “    the Company’s operations division.    ”         The records show he served in             Supply Chain         (Director of Procurement, then VP of Supply Chain Operations). Corrected to         “    supply chain division.    ”  
          Issue 15 — Unpaid 2024 bonus (~$43,600). [Medium]  
      The 2024 bonus (target $43,600; Mr. Delano received 20.6% of target in 2023) was         “    not determined    ”         and not paid before termination. The general release would cover any claim for the unpaid bonus, but you should be aware that this is a live wage claim of roughly $43,600 that the settlement is absorbing. Confirm that the back-pay/compensatory components are intended to cover it, and that no separate bonus obligation survives.  
          Issue 16 — Oregon final-paycheck timing. [Medium]  
      Oregon law (ORS 652.178) requires final wages to be paid within a specific timeframe after termination, and untimely payment can trigger penalty wages. The draft’s §2 acknowledgment that all wages through the Separation Date (including PTO) have been paid should be             verified against payroll records         before execution. If final wages or PTO were not timely paid, a penalty-wage exposure exists that the release would also absorb — but you should know the amount.  
          Issue 17 — Release of future workers’ compensation claims. [Low-Medium]  
      §4(xii) releases         “    workers’ compensation claims under ORS Chapter 656.    ”         Pre-releasing             future         workers’ compensation claims for injuries not yet sustained is generally disfavored and may be unenforceable. This is a minor point (Mr. Delano has no known pending workers’ comp claim), but consider limiting the release to workers’ comp claims accrued through the Effective Date.

         V. Cross-Cutting Strategic Risks  
      These are not drafting defects — they are the risks that make getting this settlement right so important.  
          Risk A — The replacement hire destroys the                 “        elimination of position        ”                 defense. [High]  
      This is the single most important fact in the file. Greenleaf terminated Mr. Delano on March 14, 2025 citing         “    elimination of position due to restructuring,    ”         then hired             Raj Mehta         for a substantially similar role (Senior Director of Supply Chain & Logistics) on             March 20, 2025 — six days later — at $185,000    , i.e., $33,000 less than Mr. Delano’s salary and, on the face of the record, a younger replacement. This sequence is strong evidence that the         “    restructuring    ”         rationale is pretextual and supports both the             retaliation         claim (ORS 654.062(5)) and the             age discrimination         claim (ADEA / ORS 659A). A jury is likely to find the timing and the salary differential compelling.  
          Risk B — The protected-activity timeline is a strong prima facie retaliation case. [High]  
      Internal complaint (Nov. 8, 2024) → OSHA complaint (Dec. 3, 2024) → OSHA investigation opened (Jan. 22, 2025) → administrative leave (Feb. 10, 2025) → termination (Mar. 14, 2025). The adverse action followed closely on protected activity, and the replacement hire supplies the pretext. This is a strong causation case for the plaintiff.  
          Risk C — The pending Oregon OSHA investigation is NOT resolved by this settlement. [High]  
      Settling with Mr. Delano releases             his         claims; it does             not         stop the Oregon OSHA investigation (opened Jan. 22, 2025), which the government will continue regardless. Mr. Delano remains the complainant and a likely witness. The confidentiality and non-disparagement provisions must be carefully scoped (per Issue 4) so they cannot be portrayed as obstructing his cooperation with OSHA. Greenleaf needs a             separate OSHA strategy         running in parallel — this settlement is not a substitute for it.  
          Risk D — EPLI carrier consent. [High]  
      Greenleaf carries EPLI with Timberline Insurance Group (Policy No. TIG-EPL-2024-00391). Most EPLI policies require the insured to tender claims and obtain the carrier’s             consent before settling    ; settling without consent can void or reduce coverage.             Tender this matter to Timberline immediately, provide the draft and the redline, and obtain written consent before execution.         Confirm whether the settlement falls within policy limits and whether the carrier will fund any portion. Do not let the 21-day consideration clock pressure you into signing before the carrier has signed off.  
          Risk E — Damages exposure is substantial. [High]  
      If this case does not settle, Greenleaf’s exposure is significant: back pay and front pay (Mr. Delano earned $218,000 base + bonus; mitigation may be limited by the non-compete itself); emotional-distress damages, which under the Oregon Workplace Fairness Act are             not subject to the federal Title VII caps    ; potential             punitive damages         available under Oregon law; and             attorney fees and costs         recoverable by a prevailing plaintiff under the Oregon whistleblower and discrimination statutes. The replacement-hire evidence raises the risk of a punitive-damages award. Against that backdrop, a total settlement value of $537,500 (corrected) is reasonable and arguably favorable to the company.  
          Risk F — Internal inconsistencies that help the plaintiff. [Medium]  
      Two provisions, if left as drafted, create narratives that help Mr. Delano: (1) §14 (re-employment eligibility) says Mr. Delano is eligible for rehire and the company will keep no         “    do not rehire    ”         flag — yet the company hired a replacement six days after firing him for         “    elimination of position    ”    ; and (2) §9 (non-compete) restricts him from competing even though the position was supposedly eliminated. These are not reasons to delete §14 (which supports the release and is favorable to the employee), but you should be aware of the inconsistency and ensure internal communications do not contradict the         “    restructuring    ”         rationale.  
      
        VI. Settlement Value Assessment  
      Correcting the RSU valuation to the current 409A ($12.50/share) increases the stated total from             $525,000 to $537,500    . This is an             accuracy correction, not added cash         — Greenleaf still delivers 5,000 shares. Given the exposure described in Part V (strong retaliation and age-discrimination claims, uncapped Oregon damages, punitive-damages risk, fee-shifting, and an active OSHA investigation), $537,500 is a defensible — indeed favorable — resolution. I do             not         recommend reducing the economic terms; the value of a clean, enforceable release of these claims justifies the payment. The leverage lies in fixing the             terms    , not the             price    .

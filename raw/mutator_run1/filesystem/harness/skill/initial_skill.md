## Analytical method (apply to every task)

Quality bar: a thorough specialist's work product, not a first-pass review. Do the analysis a careful
associate does and a first-pass reviewer skips — **in addition to**, never in place of, complete and
accurate extraction of what the documents say.

1. **Plan.** Enumerate every document and what each must contribute; list the issues/sections to cover.
2. **Read everything in full** — including appendices, schedules, and exhibits. Required facts and
   figures often hide in late schedules and worked tables, not the operative body.
3. **Extract completely first.** Capture every required fact, figure, date, party, and term the
   documents provide, accurately. Do not omit or alter a correct value, and do not restructure it away,
   when you add analysis on top.
4. **Then analyze (the part a first-pass reviewer skips):**
   - **Reconcile across documents.** For every figure / date / term that appears in more than one place,
     confirm it agrees or **flag the discrepancy** (double-counts, gaps, mismatches). Re-derive computed
     values and check them against stated totals — but do not overwrite a stated value; flag your check alongside it.
   - **Quantify every issue** — state the dollar amount **and** its % of the relevant base.
   - **Cite the governing authority** for each issue (controlling statute / regulation / rule / HTS GRI / contract section).
   - **Enumerate exhaustively.** Surface every issue in every document, including the routine ones. Add issues; do not drop facts to do so.
   - **Be skeptical.** Treat a party's own self-classifications, benchmarks, and claims as assertions to
     verify against the rest of the file — but verify before you contradict; do not replace a correct stated value with a guess.
5. **The deliverable IS the work product, not a report about it.** Never write a summary of what your
   memo/statement/markup "does" or "will cover" ("What the memo does…", "Key findings highlighted
   below…") in place of the memo/statement/markup itself. The rubric grades the visible artifact: every
   required issue, sub-point, computed figure, citation, and recommendation must appear **as the
   deliverable's own prose**, not be summarized away. A "work summary" that names a finding without
   printing its figure, authority, and consequence scores zero for that sub-point even when you did the
   work — because the artifact the grader reads does not contain it.

6. **Omissions-completeness ledger (separate pass, before self-review) — your #1 failure mode.** For this
   deliverable type, enumerate the clauses / sections / issues / cross-checks / figures that *should* be
   present, and audit your draft against that list, one row each, marking **present / MISSING → drafted
   now / n-a**. Critique the document itself (it fails to define / cite / classify / compute X), not only
   the underlying conduct. Then:
   - **Compute every figure** — if an item has inputs/base/offset, do the arithmetic and print the labeled
     number (equity = value − lien; interest = principal × rate × time; excess = actual − cap; % = part ÷
     base; roll up totals). Never park a computable as "to be determined" or leave it as a category
     ("summarizes figures…") without the actual number on the page.
   - **Cite the controlling authority** for each issue — the specific statute / regulation / rule / section
     / clause, not "a" standard or "the" rule.
   - **State the consequence and severity** per item — the specific legal/financial result and a
     critical/high/medium/low label — then close every `[___]` placeholder before delivering.
   Draft every MISSING row into the visible text now; an item left on the ledger is a point left on the table.

7. **Self-review before finishing — check BOTH retention and coverage.** Re-read each source document and
   confirm (a) every required fact, figure, and extraction it provides is still present and unaltered in
   your deliverable, AND (b) every issue, discrepancy, sub-point, computed figure, citation, and
   recommendation it warrants is reflected in visible prose (not described, not summarized). Close any
   gaps. (Validating a file's format is NOT a completeness check.)

Put your analysis where a reader sees it — in the deliverable's **visible text** (state the value or issue,
then the recommendation), not only as margin notes or silent edits. A fact not printed as a labeled number
or named authority in the deliverable's own body is, for grading purposes, not there.

## I. Executive Summary

This memorandum reports the results of a cross-reference review of the
H-1B petition package assembled on behalf of Brightfield Analytics
Inc. ("Petitioner") for the beneficiary Rajesh Venkataraman, to be
sponsored for the position of **Senior Machine Learning Engineer** (SOC
15-2051, Data Scientists) at an annual wage of **\$128,000**. The
package consists of ten documents: the beneficiary's résumé, the
certified Labor Condition Application ("LCA"), the foreign credential
evaluation, the employer support letter, the JNTU academic transcripts,
two prior-employment experience letters, the LCA posting notice, the
internal organizational chart, and the Prevailing Wage Determination
("PWD").

The package is internally consistent on most "core data points"
(identity, employer identity, SOC code, LCA case number, PWD case
number, wage figures, and employment period). However, the
cross-reference review identifies **fourteen issues** of varying
severity that should be resolved before filing. The most serious are:

1.  **The wage level appears understated.** The position is described as
    a *senior*, *supervisory* role requiring *independent judgment* and
    three years of experience --- duties that map to DOL **Wage Level
    3** (or higher), not the certified **Level 2**. If the position is
    properly classified at Level 3 (\$137,946), the offered wage of
    \$128,000 would fall **below** the prevailing wage, creating a
    wage-compliance defect that could invalidate the LCA and support a
    denial.

2.  **The beneficiary's degree field may not satisfy the "closely
    related field" requirement.** The position requires a degree in
    Computer Science, Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence, or a
    closely related field; the beneficiary holds a B.Tech in
    **Electronics and Communication Engineering**, in which only \~7.5%
    of coursework is computer-science related. The credential evaluation
    expressly disclaims any opinion on field-of-study relevance, and the
    "alternative qualification" path appears tailored to the beneficiary
    and is weakly documented.

3.  **Material date discrepancies between the résumé and the experience
    letters.** The résumé extends the Kiran Software tenure to March
    2017 (the experience letter confirms it ended December 9, 2016) and
    the NovaStar tenure to August 2020 (the experience letter confirms
    July 2020), overstating tenure and masking an approximately
    3.7-month employment gap.

4.  **A three-year independent-consulting period is undocumented** and
    is the only evidence of the specific deep-learning skills
    (TensorFlow, PyTorch, AWS SageMaker, NLP/LLM fine-tuning) the
    position requires; the documented NovaStar experience references
    only scikit-learn.

Each issue is detailed below, organized by severity, followed by
recommended corrective actions.

## IV. Issues Identified

### A. Critical Issues

#### Issue 1 --- Wage Level appears understated (Level 2 vs. Level 3/4); offered wage would be deficient at the correct level

**Documents:** PWD; certified LCA (§§ F.4, G.5); employer support letter
(§§ 2, 3); org chart.

The PWD and LCA classify the position at **Wage Level 2** (\$113,734),
which DOL defines as work involving "a good understanding of the
occupation" and "moderately complex tasks requiring **limited
judgment**." The offered wage of \$128,000 satisfies Level 2 but is
**below Level 3 (\$137,946)**.

The position as described in the support letter and org chart is
materially inconsistent with a Level 2 characterization:

-   The title is **"Senior"** Machine Learning Engineer.
-   The position requires a **minimum of 3 years** of progressive
    experience.
-   Duty 4 (15% of time) is **"Leading and mentoring a team of 3 junior
    data scientists"** --- a supervisory function.
-   The support letter states the role **"exercises independent
    judgment"** in designing and implementing ML solutions.
-   The role reports **directly to the Chief Technology Officer** and
    presents to C-suite executives.
-   The org chart shows the position with **direct reports** (Data
    Scientists).

These indicators map to **Level 3** ("sound understanding ... may
exercise **independent judgment** ... may have **supervisory
responsibilities**") and arguably to **Level 4** ("supervise and train
other workers," "broad latitude"). Notably, the PWD's own job-duty
summary recites that the role will "**lead a team of data scientists**"
--- a Level 3 indicator --- yet Level 2 was applied.

**Risk:** If USCIS or DOL determines the correct level is Level 3, the
offered wage of \$128,000 falls **\$9,946 below** the prevailing wage,
which (i) undermines the LCA wage attestation under INA § 212(n)(1)(A),
(ii) may render the certified LCA defective, and (iii) is a common basis
for RFE or denial. The internal inconsistency between the job
description (senior/supervisory/independent judgment) and the Level 2
certification (limited judgment, moderately complex) is the most
significant vulnerability in the package.

**Recommendation:** Re-evaluate the wage level. Either (a) revise the
position description to support a bona fide Level 2 classification
(remove or de-emphasize supervisory/"senior"/"independent judgment"
language --- though this creates its own specialty-occupation and
credibility concerns), or (b) obtain a new PWD and LCA at Level 3 and
**raise the offered wage to at least \$137,946**. Option (b) is strongly
preferred because it aligns the wage with the actual duties.

#### Issue 2 --- Beneficiary's degree field may not satisfy the "closely related field" requirement

**Documents:** Employer support letter (§ 3); credential evaluation (§§
4, 5, 7); JNTU transcripts.

The support letter requires a bachelor's degree in **"Computer Science,
Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence, or a closely related
field."** The beneficiary holds a B.Tech in **Electronics and
Communication Engineering (ECE)**. The credential evaluation equates the
degree to a U.S. B.S. in **Electronics and Communication Engineering**
--- not in CS, ML, or AI.

Two problems follow:

1.  **No opinion bridges the "closely related" gap.** The credential
    evaluation expressly states (§ 7) that it "does not opine on whether
    the applicant's field of study satisfies the requirements of any
    particular position or petition." The transcripts and evaluation
    show that **computer-science / IT coursework is only \~12 of \~160
    credit hours (\~7.5%)** of the program; the remaining \~92.5% is
    electronics, communications, signal processing, and related ECE
    subjects. The support letter's assertion that the curriculum
    "included coursework in programming, data structures, and artificial
    intelligence, providing a strong technical foundation" overstates
    the relevance of a 7.5% CS component. A reasonable adjudicator could
    conclude ECE is not "closely related" to CS/ML/AI for this
    specialty.

2.  **The "alternative qualification" appears tailored to the
    beneficiary.** The support letter offers, in the alternative, "a
    Bachelor's degree in **any engineering discipline** combined with
    **5 years of progressive experience in machine learning**." This
    formulation tracks the beneficiary's exact profile (an ECE degree
    plus \~6 years of ML experience) and is the type of alternative
    requirement USCIS scrutinizes as potentially tailored to the
    beneficiary. Moreover, the alternative is only weakly supported on
    the experience side (see Issues 4 and 14).

**Risk:** USCIS may find that neither the primary requirement (CS/ML/AI
or closely related field) nor the alternative (any engineering degree +
5 years ML) is adequately established, leading to a qualifications-based
RFE or denial.

**Recommendation:** Obtain a **supplemental expert opinion / credential
evaluation** that specifically addresses whether ECE is "closely
related" to the ML-engineering specialty, citing the AI/data-structures
coursework and the substantial post-graduate ML experience.
Alternatively, lean on the alternative-qualification path but **document
the 5 years of progressive ML experience robustly** (see Issue 4) and be
prepared to defend the alternative as a bona fide, pre-existing
requirement (e.g., with evidence of similar prior hires and the industry
standard).

### B. Significant Issues

#### Issue 4 --- Three-year independent-consulting period is undocumented; it is the only evidence of the specific required skills

**Documents:** Beneficiary résumé; NovaStar experience letter; employer
support letter (§ 3).

The position requires "3 years of progressive experience in machine
learning engineering, **including demonstrated proficiency with Python,
TensorFlow, PyTorch, and cloud-based ML deployment platforms such as AWS
SageMaker**," plus NLP and LLM fine-tuning. The beneficiary's claimed ML
experience is:

-   **NovaStar (April 2017 -- July 2020, \~3.3 years):** documented by
    an experience letter, but the letter references only **Python,
    scikit-learn, Tableau, A/B testing, and SQL** --- it does **not**
    mention TensorFlow, PyTorch, AWS SageMaker, NLP, or LLM fine-tuning.
-   **Independent Machine Learning Consultant (August 2020 -- August
    2023, \~3 years):** self-reported on the résumé only; **no
    experience letter, 1099s, client letters, contracts, or tax
    records** are included. The résumé attributes the
    TensorFlow/PyTorch/AWS SageMaker/NLP/LLM work to this period.
-   **Brightfield (Sept. 2023 -- present):** current; described on the
    résumé but not corroborated by a separate letter.

**Risk:** The **documented** ML experience (NovaStar) does not
corroborate the specific required skills; those skills appear only in
the **undocumented** consulting period. USCIS may find the beneficiary
has not established, by independent evidence, the 3 years of progressive
ML experience with the required tools. The independent-consulting period
is also inherently harder to verify.

**Recommendation:** Corroborate the consulting period with at least two
of: client engagement letters, 1099/ Schedule C tax records, signed
statements describing projects and tools, or a self-employment affidavit
with project artifacts. At minimum, obtain letters from 1--2 consulting
clients confirming the TensorFlow/PyTorch/AWS SageMaker/NLP work and
dates.

#### Issue 5 --- Supervisory team-size discrepancy (3 vs. 2 direct reports)

**Documents:** Employer support letter (§ 2, duty 4); org chart.

The support letter states the beneficiary will "lead\[\] and mentor\[\]
a team of **3 junior data scientists**." The org chart shows only **2
Data Scientists** as direct reports to the Senior Machine Learning
Engineer. This is an internal inconsistency in the package's own
description of the supervisory scope.

**Risk:** Beyond the bare inconsistency, the discrepancy bears directly
on the wage-level analysis (Issue 1): supervisory responsibility is a
Level 3 indicator, and the package is not even internally consistent on
how many people are supervised. USCIS may question the accuracy of the
position description.

**Recommendation:** Reconcile the number (confirm whether 2 or 3) and
make the support letter and org chart consistent. If the team is being
expanded, document the planned hires; if it is 2, correct the support
letter.

#### Issue 6 --- Current job title ("Data Scientist") differs from petitioned title ("Senior Machine Learning Engineer")

**Documents:** Beneficiary résumé; employer support letter; org chart.

The résumé lists the beneficiary's current Brightfield role as **"Data
Scientist"** (September 2023 -- present). The petitioned position is
**"Senior Machine Learning Engineer,"** which the org chart labels as a
**"Proposed"** position with the beneficiary as the **"Proposed"**
incumbent. The support letter does not explain the relationship between
the two titles.

**Risk:** USCIS may be confused about whether the beneficiary is already
performing the petitioned duties (and at what title) or is being
promoted into a new role. An unexplained title change can raise
questions about the bona fide nature of the petitioned position and
about whether the beneficiary is being "benched" or under-employed in
the interim.

**Recommendation:** Clarify in the support letter whether this is a
**promotion/new role** (and document the change in duties, reporting
line, and effective date) or whether "Data Scientist" on the résumé was
simply an imprecise label for the same role. Ensure the résumé, support
letter, and org chart describe one consistent position.

### C. Moderate Issues

#### Issue 7 --- Credential evaluator's qualifications may undermine the evaluation's weight

**Documents:** Credential evaluation (signature block).

The evaluation is signed by "Dr. Linda Farnsworth, Ph.D., Senior
Credential Analyst," whose credential line reads "**Ph.D., Education ---
Halford University**." Two concerns: (i) "Halford University" is not a
recognized, regionally accredited U.S. institution, which may diminish
the evaluator's credibility with USCIS; and (ii) the evaluator's
doctorate is in **Education**, a field unrelated to the
engineering/computer-science discipline being evaluated. While
Westbridge is represented as a NACES-member agency (which lends
institutional credibility), the individual evaluator's credentials are a
soft point, particularly where the evaluation is being asked to support
a technical specialty-occupation case.

**Recommendation:** Confirm Westbridge's NACES membership is current and
consider requesting an evaluation prepared or co-signed by an evaluator
with a credential in a technical/engineering discipline, or supplement
with an expert opinion from a qualified technical expert (see Issue 2).

#### Issue 8 --- LCA may have been filed outside the 6-month window

**Documents:** Certified LCA (§§ M.1, J.2); LCA posting notice; employer
support letter (§ 2).

The LCA was filed/certified on **March 10, 2025**, for a period of
employment beginning **October 1, 2025** --- approximately **205 days
(\~6.7 months)** before the start of employment. Under 20 C.F.R. §
655.733, an LCA may not be filed more than **6 months** before the
beginning of the period of intended employment (i.e., no earlier than
April 1, 2025). The filing date appears to be roughly three weeks early.

**Risk:** DOL's FLAG system ordinarily rejects filings outside the
window, so this may reflect a documentation inconsistency (e.g., the
"Date of LCA Filing" on the posting notice may not match the actual
submission date) rather than a true violation --- but the documents as
written show an apparent early filing, which should be reconciled.

**Recommendation:** Verify the actual LCA submission date in FLAG
against the dates printed on the LCA and posting notice. If the
documents misstate the filing date, correct them; if the LCA was
genuinely filed early, consult DOL guidance and consider whether a
corrected/refiled LCA is needed.

#### Issue 9 --- LCA posting period may be short of the 10-business-day requirement

**Documents:** Certified LCA (§ J.2); LCA posting notice.

The notice was posted **March 10, 2025** and removed **March 19, 2025**.
Counting consecutive business days (excluding the weekends of March
15--16), this period spans only **8 business days** --- short of the "at
least 10 consecutive business days" required by 20 C.F.R. §
655.734(a)(1).

**Risk:** An under-length posting is a technical LCA-compliance
deficiency that DOL can cite in an audit and that USCIS may question. To
reach 10 business days from a March 10 start, posting should have run
through at least **March 21, 2025** (Friday).

**Recommendation:** Confirm the actual posting dates. If the posting was
indeed only 8 business days, re-post for the full 10-business-day period
and update the posting notice / LCA documentation accordingly before
relying on the LCA.

#### Issue 10 --- F-1 OPT/STEM OPT validity and cap-gap are not documented

**Documents:** Employer support letter (§ 4).

The support letter states the beneficiary "is currently maintaining
valid F-1 Optional Practical Training (OPT) status and will transition
to H-1B nonimmigrant status effective October 1, 2025," but the package
contains **no evidence of current OPT/STEM OPT validity, no OPT end
date, and no cap-gap I-20**. Because Brightfield employment began
September 2023, standard 12-month post-completion OPT would have lapsed
around September 2024; continued work authorization through April 2025
implies a **STEM OPT extension**, which depends on the degree being a
STEM-designated program and on a timely-filed I-765.

**Risk:** If the beneficiary's work authorization lapses before October
1, 2025 (the H-1B start date), there is a
**status/employment-authorization gap** that could require the
beneficiary to cease work and/or depart the United States, and could
complicate the change-of-status request. The October 1 start date
(typical of cap-subject filings) makes cap-gap relief relevant.

**Recommendation:** Include current Form I-20s (including the STEM OPT
I-20 with the cap-gap recommendation if applicable), the EAD, and
evidence of timely STEM OPT filing. Confirm the ECE degree is on the
STEM-designated-degree-program list. If a gap is possible, plan for
consular notification rather than change of status.

#### Issue 11 --- Remote-work / additional-worksite coverage under the LCA

**Documents:** Certified LCA (§ H); employer support letter (§ 1); org
chart.

The LCA lists a **single worksite** (4200 Congress Avenue, Austin). The
support letter and org chart, however, describe a company with a
**Denver satellite office and a "distributed remote team."** If the
beneficiary will work from home (his résumé lists an Austin residence)
or from any non-Austin location --- even occasionally --- the LCA must
cover that worksite.

**Risk:** Post-pandemic, USCIS and DOL scrutinize worksite coverage
closely. Work at an unlisted worksite is an LCA-compliance violation and
can jeopardize the petition.

**Recommendation:** Confirm in writing that the beneficiary will work
exclusively at the Austin HQ. If any remote or Denver work is
contemplated, file an amended/new LCA covering the additional
worksite(s) and post notice there.

#### Issue 13 --- Posting-location documentation (two locations claimed, one documented)

**Documents:** Certified LCA (§ J.3); LCA posting notice.

The posting notice states the notice "is being posted in **two
conspicuous locations**," but the LCA (§ J.3) documents only **one**
location ("Employee common area / break room, first floor"). The second
posting location is not identified anywhere in the package.

**Recommendation:** Document both posting locations (with photographs or
a signed posting log) and ensure the LCA and posting notice are
consistent.

#### Issue 14 --- Experience-claim inconsistency ("7+" vs. "over 8" years) and characterization of the Kiran period

**Documents:** Beneficiary résumé (Professional Summary); employer
support letter (§ 4); Kiran experience letter.

Two related minor points:

1.  The résumé summary states "**7+ years** of experience," while the
    support letter states "**over 8 years** of progressive experience."
    Both understate the actual \~10.8 years of work history (June 2014
    -- April 2025), but the two documents are inconsistent with each
    other.
2.  The support letter aggregates "software development and machine
    learning engineering" experience. The **Kiran period (2014--2016) is
    general software development** (Java, Python, SQL, databases) ---
    **not machine learning** --- and therefore does **not** count toward
    the position's "3 years of progressive experience in machine
    learning engineering." The ML-specific requirement is met primarily
    by NovaStar (\~3.3 years, documented, scikit-learn) plus the
    undocumented consulting period (see Issue 4).

**Recommendation:** Align the years-of-experience figure across the
résumé and support letter, and be careful not to imply that the Kiran
software-development period satisfies the ML-specific experience
requirement.

## VI. Summary of Recommended Actions (Priority Order)

1.  **Resolve the wage-level issue (Issue 1).** Re-classify at Level 3
    and raise the offered wage to ≥ \$137,946, or restructure the
    position description to support Level 2 --- recognizing the latter
    creates specialty-occupation and credibility risks. *This is the
    single most important corrective action.*
2.  **Bridge the degree-field gap (Issue 2).** Obtain a supplemental
    expert opinion / evaluation addressing "closely related field," or
    fully document the alternative-qualification path.
3.  **Correct the résumé dates (Issue 3)** to match the experience
    letters and account for the Jan.--Mar. 2017 gap.
4.  **Document the consulting period (Issue 4)** with client letters /
    1099s / tax records evidencing the TensorFlow/PyTorch/AWS/NLP work.
5.  **Reconcile the team-size (Issue 5)** and the current-vs-petitioned
    title (Issue 6) across the support letter, résumé, and org chart.
6.  **Verify LCA filing date (Issue 8) and posting period (Issue 9)**;
    re-post for 10 business days if needed.
7.  **Document OPT/STEM OPT and cap-gap (Issue 10)** and confirm
    worksite coverage (Issue 11).
8.  **Clean up data-integrity items (Issues 12--14):** standardize the
    employer telephone (remove the beneficiary's personal number from
    the posting notice), document both posting locations, and align the
    years-of-experience figure.
9.  **Confirm PA-file completeness and assemble the remaining I-129
    exhibits (Section V).**

## VII. Conclusion

The petition package is largely coherent on its core data points, but it
contains a small number of serious substantive risks --- most
importantly the **wage-level / offered-wage mismatch** and the
**degree-field qualification gap** --- together with several
documentation and consistency problems (date discrepancies, an
undocumented consulting period, and scattered data-integrity errors).
The issues identified above are correctable. We recommend addressing the
Critical and Significant issues before filing and cleaning up the
Moderate and Minor items so that the package presents a single,
internally consistent, and fully documented record to USCIS.

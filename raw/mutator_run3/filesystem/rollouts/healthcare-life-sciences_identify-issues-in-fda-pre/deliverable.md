## I. Executive Summary

At Kate's request, I have reviewed the complete draft Pre-Submission
package for the VascuClear 3000 Thrombectomy System (the "Device"),
comprising: (1) the Pre-Submission cover letter; (2) the Device
Description and Predicate Comparison; (3) the Proposed Testing Plan; (4)
the Clinical Study Synopsis; (5) the draft Instructions for Use ("IFU");
(6) the ThrombEx 200 (K192847) 510(k) Summary; and (7) the internal
strategy email chain among Ms. Fessenden, Dr. Marsh, and Ms. Pressman.

The package is well-organized and reflects genuine effort. However, my
review identifies **twenty-three (23) discrete issues**, several of
which are serious enough to jeopardize the regulatory strategy, the
Pre-Submission meeting outcome, or the corporate milestone (510(k)
clearance by December 31, 2025) if not resolved before filing. The
issues fall into four priority tiers:

-   **Priority 1 --- Critical (3 issues):** The combination-product
    classification risk, the Investigational Device Exemption ("IDE")
    question, and the substantial-equivalence/predicate-selection
    argument. These three are interrelated and go to the heart of
    whether the proposed traditional 510(k) pathway is viable at all.
    Notably, **none of the three is currently presented as a question to
    FDA in the cover letter**, notwithstanding Ms. Pressman's
    recommendation in her May 12 email that the combination-product and
    IDE questions be added.

-   **Priority 2 --- High (9 issues):** Deficiencies in the
    indications-for-use language, biocompatibility contact-duration
    classification, software level of concern, electromagnetic
    compatibility ("EMC") testing, drug-delivery characterization,
    simulated-use bench testing, clinical study design, impeller fatigue
    testing, and the submission timeline/milestone. FDA is likely to
    raise each of these at the Pre-Submission meeting or in a subsequent
    Additional Information letter.

-   **Priority 3 --- Medium (7 issues):** MRI safety data gaps,
    particulate/embolization testing, dynamic hemolysis testing,
    shelf-life/sterile-barrier validation, console reprocessing
    validation, material-identification inconsistencies, and console
    dimensional specifications.

-   **Priority 4 --- Low (4 issues):** Contact-information
    inconsistencies, citation of a withdrawn FDA software guidance, an
    erroneous exclusion-criterion material reference, and
    corporate/financial coordination items.

A consolidated issue table appears at Section IV. My recommended next
steps appear at Section V.

The single most important takeaway: **the package should not be filed on
June 15 in its current form without (a) adding explicit FDA questions on
combination-product status and the IDE requirement, (b) re-evaluating
predicate selection in light of the pharmacomechanical feature, and (c)
reconciling the internal contradictions identified below.** Several of
these items can be resolved quickly; others (notably the
combination-product and IDE questions) require a strategic decision by
Clearfield management before filing.

## II. Background

Clearfield Medical Devices, Inc. ("Clearfield") intends to market the
VascuClear 3000, a catheter-based thrombectomy system with three
components: (i) a 10 Fr, 65 cm single-use catheter with a rotating
helical impeller (up to 12,000 RPM) and an integrated thrombolytic
infusion lumen; (ii) a reusable electromechanical console with an
aspiration pump (up to −650 mmHg); and (iii) the infusion lumen itself,
for local delivery of physician-specified thrombolytic agents (e.g.,
tPA/alteplase). The proposed indication is for "percutaneous removal of
thrombus and simultaneous local delivery of physician-specified
thrombolytic agents in adult patients with acute deep vein thrombosis
(symptom onset ≤14 days) of the iliofemoral venous segment."

Clearfield proposes a traditional 510(k) under product code GXD (21 CFR
870.1250), citing the ThrombEx 200 (K192847, Vasogen Therapeutics, Inc.)
as the sole predicate. The ThrombEx 200 is an 8 Fr, 55 cm, single-lumen,
purely mechanical Bernoulli-effect aspiration catheter with **no
rotating components and no drug-delivery capability**, cleared for
"percutaneous removal of thrombus from the peripheral vasculature"
(arterial and venous).

The internal email chain reflects an unresolved debate between Dr. Marsh
(who maintains the Device is not a combination product and that no IDE
is required) and Ms. Pressman (who has reservations on both points and
recommends asking FDA directly). Ms. Fessenden has not yet confirmed
whether the combination-product and IDE questions will be added to the
Pre-Submission. This memorandum is intended to inform that decision and
to surface additional issues.

## III. Prioritized Issues

### PRIORITY 1 --- CRITICAL

#### Issue 1. Combination-Product Classification Risk Is Not Addressed as an FDA Question

**Where:** Cover letter, Section VI (questions to FDA) --- no
combination-product question present; Clinical Study Synopsis, Section
6; Device Description, Sections 2.4 and 5.1; draft IFU, Sections 2 and
9; email chain (Pressman, May 5 and May 12; Marsh, April 28 and May 9).

**The issue.** The Device incorporates a lumen "purpose-built for drug
delivery" (Ms. Pressman's words), and the proposed indication expressly
claims "simultaneous local delivery of physician-specified thrombolytic
agents" as a therapeutic function. Under 21 CFR 3.2(e), a "combination
product" includes a device "intended for use with" a drug where both are
required to achieve the intended use, purpose, or effect. The "intended
for use with" inquiry does not turn solely on whether the manufacturer
co-packages the drug; it turns on whether the device is intended to be
used with a specified drug to achieve a therapeutic effect. Dr. Marsh's
infusion-pump analogy is distinguishable: a general-purpose infusion
pump is indicated for fluid delivery without reference to any particular
drug or therapeutic outcome, whereas the VascuClear 3000's indication
names a class of drugs (thrombolytics) and a therapeutic purpose
(thrombus removal enhanced by lysis). The Device Description and IFU
reinforce this by describing pharmacomechanical thrombectomy as the
"core differentiating feature" and the "primary design purpose."

**Why it matters.** If FDA determines the Device is a combination
product, the consequences are significant: (a) a Request for Designation
("RFD") to the Office of Combination Products ("OCP") may be required to
formalize the lead review center and assignment; (b) CDER consultation
on drug-device compatibility would likely be required even if CDRH
remains the lead center; (c) additional data (e.g., drug compatibility,
adsorption/leachables with the specific agent, delivery-dose accuracy)
would be expected; and (d) the review timeline and division may shift.
None of these contingencies is reflected in the current testing plan or
timeline. The package's stated position (not a combination product) may
ultimately prove correct, but the downside of being wrong --- discovered
at the Pre-Submission meeting or, worse, after 510(k) submission --- is
substantial.

**Recommendation.** Add an explicit question to FDA in the cover letter
asking whether the Agency considers the VascuClear 3000 a combination
product under 21 CFR Part 3, and, if so, what additional data or pathway
modifications would be expected. This is "cheap insurance," as
Ms. Pressman put it. I concur with her recommendation. If Clearfield
elects not to ask, the decision and its rationale should be documented
in the file.

#### Issue 2. IDE Requirement --- The "Post-Clearance" Framing Is Internally Contradictory and Likely Incorrect

**Where:** Clinical Study Synopsis, Sections 5 and 6 (study
characterized as "post-clearance"; "an IDE is not anticipated"; "no IDE
application will be submitted"); Synopsis Section 15 (First Patient
Enrollment Q4 2025); Proposed Testing Plan, Section 9.2 (testing
completion Nov--Dec 2025; 510(k) submission "as early as practical"
thereafter); email chain (Pressman, May 5, citing 21 CFR 812.3(m);
Marsh, April 28 and May 9).

**The issue.** The synopsis repeatedly characterizes the study as a
"post-clearance data collection effort" and states the Device "is
expected to have received 510(k) clearance prior to study initiation."
But the package's own timeline contradicts this:
bench/biocompatibility/sterilization testing is not complete until
November--December 2025, the 510(k) cannot be submitted until after
testing is complete, and FDA's 510(k) review period (ordinarily 90 days,
plus any Additional Information hold) means clearance cannot occur
before approximately Q1 2026 at the earliest. Yet the synopsis schedules
First Patient Enrollment for Q4 2025 --- **before the 510(k) is even
submitted, let alone cleared.** The study therefore cannot be
"post-clearance" as described.

Under 21 CFR 812.3(m), an "investigational device" is a device that is
the object of a clinical investigation. Because the VascuClear 3000 will
not be cleared when enrollment begins, clinical use of the Device in the
study is investigational use, and the study must be conducted under an
IDE --- either a full IDE (if the study is "significant risk" ("SR")) or
the abbreviated IDE controls of 21 CFR 812.2(b) (if "non-significant
risk" ("NSR")). The synopsis's statement that "no IDE application will
be submitted" is incorrect even for an NSR study: NSR studies still
require compliance with the abbreviated IDE requirements (IRB approval,
informed consent, monitoring, records, and reporting) and sponsor NSR
determination, and the IRB must concur in the NSR determination (FDA may
override).

The NSR/NSR determination itself is not self-evident. The Device
presents a novel rotating-impeller mechanism operating at 12,000 RPM in
a vein, combined with tPA infusion (bleeding risk). Whether this
presents "a potential for serious risk to health" (the SR threshold in
21 CFR 812.3(k)) is a judgment FDA and the IRB may not resolve in
Clearfield's favor by assumption.

**Why it matters.** If an IDE is required (full or abbreviated), the
timeline and budget expand materially: IDE preparation and FDA IDE
review (30-day default for full IDE), SR/NSR adjudication, and IRB
submissions all precede enrollment. Beginning enrollment without the
required IDE would be a serious regulatory violation. This directly
threatens the December 31, 2025 corporate milestone and the Series D
financing.

**Recommendation.** (a) Add an explicit question to FDA in the cover
letter asking whether an IDE is required for the proposed study design
and, if so, whether FDA considers the study SR or NSR. (b) Revise the
synopsis to remove the "post-clearance" characterization, which is
contradicted by the package's own timeline. (c) Plan provisionally for
an IDE (at minimum, abbreviated IDE controls) and reflect that in the
timeline and budget. (d) Reconcile the study start date with the 510(k)
submission/clearance timeline. I concur with Ms. Pressman that this must
be resolved before Stonebridge begins IRB submissions.

#### Issue 3. Substantial Equivalence / Predicate Selection --- The SE Argument Is Vulnerable on Both Prongs

**Where:** Cover letter, Section III; Device Description, Sections 3, 4,
and 5; ThrombEx 200 510(k) Summary (Enclosure 5), Sections 2 and 4.

**The issue.** The substantial-equivalence ("SE") determination requires
(1) the same intended use as the predicate, and (2) the same
technological characteristics, or different technological
characteristics that do not raise new questions of safety and
effectiveness, supported by performance data. The proposed predicate
fails on both prongs:

**(a) Intended use.** The predicate is cleared for "percutaneous removal
of thrombus from the peripheral vasculature" --- a **purely mechanical**
function, covering **both arterial and venous** peripheral vasculature,
with no DVT, anatomical-segment, symptom-onset, or age limitation
(510(k) Summary, Section 2). The subject Device is indicated for
mechanical thrombus removal **plus** "simultaneous local delivery of
physician-specified thrombolytic agents" in a defined venous DVT
population. The package argues the iliofemoral venous segment is a
"subset" of the predicate's peripheral scope (a narrowing). That is
correct as to anatomy. But the **addition of a drug-delivery therapeutic
function** is an **expansion** of intended use, not a narrowing. A
device generally cannot be found substantially equivalent to a predicate
that lacks a capability the subject device claims. The "same intended
use" prong is therefore strained.

**(b) Technological characteristics / new questions.** The
thrombus-removal mechanism is fundamentally different (rotating helical
impeller at 12,000 RPM vs. Bernoulli hydrodynamic aspiration with no
moving parts). The cover letter asserts that "the rotating impeller
mechanism has been employed in previously cleared thrombectomy devices,"
but **no such cleared device is identified or cited anywhere in the
package** as a predicate or reference device. If cleared
rotating-impeller thrombectomy devices exist, they should be named; if
the assertion is unsupported, it should be removed. Separately, the
integrated drug-delivery lumen is an entirely new technological
characteristic with **no predicate precedent whatsoever** --- the
predicate has no drug-delivery capability. The combination of (i) a
different high-speed mechanical mechanism and (ii) a new drug-delivery
feature is reasonably likely to be viewed by FDA as raising new
questions of safety and effectiveness (vessel injury,
particulate/embolization, hemolysis, impeller integrity, drug
compatibility/dose accuracy).

**(c) Mischaracterization of the predicate.** The Device Description's
comparison table (Appendix A, Table A-1) lists the predicate's "Intended
Use Population" as "Adults with acute DVT, iliofemoral venous segment."
This is **factually incorrect.** The predicate's 510(k) Summary (Section
2) states the indication "encompasses the peripheral vasculature,
including both the arterial and venous peripheral vascular systems,"
with no DVT, iliofemoral, symptom-onset, or age restriction. The cover
letter (Section III) likewise characterizes the predicate as intended
for "removal of venous thrombus," which understates the predicate's
actual (broader, arterial-and-venous) cleared scope. Misstating the
predicate's cleared indication undermines the SE comparison's
credibility and is the kind of error FDA reviewers flag immediately.

**Why it matters.** If FDA disagrees that the ThrombEx 200 is an
appropriate predicate, or finds new questions of safety and
effectiveness, the 510(k) could be placed on hold, returned for a
different predicate, or found not substantially equivalent. This is the
central risk to the entire submission.

**Recommendation.** (a) Correct the predicate's intended-use population
in Table A-1 and the cover letter to reflect the actual cleared
indication (broad peripheral, arterial and venous, mechanical only). (b)
Investigate whether a **pharmacomechanical** thrombectomy device (i.e.,
a device combining mechanical thrombus removal with thrombolytic
infusion) has been cleared and would be a more appropriate predicate or
reference device; the ThrombEx 200 is purely mechanical and provides no
precedent for the drug-delivery claim. (c) Identify and cite specific
cleared rotating-impeller/rotational thrombectomy devices to support the
"no new questions" assertion, or remove the unsupported assertion. (d)
Consider a multi-predicate or predicate-plus-reference-device strategy
(e.g., a mechanical thrombectomy predicate for the impeller function and
a separate reference device for the infusion lumen). (e) Be prepared for
FDA to ask, at the Pre-Submission meeting, whether the drug-delivery
function should be dropped from the indication to preserve SE --- which
circles back to Issue 1 (combination product) and Issue 4 (indications).

### PRIORITY 2 --- HIGH

#### Issue 4. Indications for Use --- Drug-Delivery Co-Primary Claim and Internal Inconsistencies

**Where:** Cover letter, Section IV; Device Description, Section 2.4;
draft IFU, Section 2; Clinical Study Synopsis, Section 7.2 (inclusion
criterion 1).

**The issue.** The indication claims "simultaneous local delivery of
physician-specified thrombolytic agents" as a co-primary therapeutic
function. This language is the principal driver of the
combination-product risk (Issue 1) and the SE problem (Issue 3): the
predicate has no such function. Dr. Marsh suggested de-emphasizing drug
delivery in the indication and describing it as a "feature" in the
device description. Ms. Pressman correctly noted that rewording does not
resolve the underlying combination-product question, and there is
tension between de-emphasizing drug delivery in the indication while the
Device Description, IFU, and clinical protocol all position
pharmacomechanical thrombectomy as the Device's "core differentiating
feature" and "primary design purpose." FDA will read the package as a
whole.

Separately, the patient-population definition is inconsistent across
documents: the IFU (Section 2) states "adult patients eighteen (18)
years of age and older" (no upper bound), while the clinical study
inclusion criterion 1 caps enrollment at "Age 18 to 75 years,
inclusive." The indication itself says "adult patients" without an upper
limit. FDA expects the indication, IFU, and study population to align.

**Recommendation.** (a) Make a deliberate, documented decision on
whether drug delivery remains a co-primary indication or is repositioned
as an ancillary feature --- and carry that decision consistently through
the cover letter, Device Description, IFU, and clinical protocol. (b)
Reconcile the age definition (18+ vs. 18--75) across the indication,
IFU, and study criteria. (c) Define "physician-specified thrombolytic
agents" more precisely or narrow it; the current breadth (any
thrombolytic, any dose) is inconsistent with the IFU's listing of
specific agents and with the drug-compatibility testing gap (Issue 8).

#### Issue 5. Biocompatibility --- Contact-Duration Classification Contradicts the 72-Hour Indwell Claim

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Sections 2.1 and 2.2 (catheter
classified "limited" contact, \<24 hours; subchronic systemic toxicity,
implantation, and genotoxicity excluded); Device Description, Section
2.2 and comparison table (catheter "may remain in situ for up to 72
hours"); draft IFU, Sections 1 and 8.4 (up to 72 hours extended
infusion).

**The issue.** The testing plan classifies the catheter as a
blood-contacting device with **"limited"** contact duration (\<24 hours)
per ISO 10993-1:2018 Table A.1, and on that basis **excludes**
subchronic systemic toxicity (ISO 10993-11), implantation testing (ISO
10993-6), and genotoxicity (ISO 10993-3). But the IFU and Device
Description both state the catheter "may remain in situ for up to 72
hours" for extended thrombolytic infusion. Seventy-two hours falls
within the **"prolonged"** contact category (\>24 hours to 30 days)
under ISO 10993-1, not "limited." The Device Description's own
comparison table captures the contradiction in adjacent rows: "Device
Contact Duration: Up to 72 hours (per draft IFU)" versus
"Biocompatibility: ... contact duration classified as 'limited' (\<24
hours)."

If the correct classification is "prolonged," the excluded endpoints
(subchronic systemic toxicity, implantation, and possibly genotoxicity)
become applicable, and the proposed biocompatibility battery is
under-scoped. FDA will almost certainly flag this.

**Recommendation.** (a) Reconcile the maximum contact duration. If
72-hour indwell is a claimed/IFU-permitted use, reclassify the catheter
as "prolonged" contact and add the endpoints required by ISO 10993-1
Table A.1 for prolonged blood-contacting devices (at minimum, subchronic
systemic toxicity and implantation; evaluate genotoxicity). (b)
Alternatively, if Clearfield wishes to preserve the "limited"
classification and the leaner test battery, remove or cap the 72-hour
indwell claim from the IFU and Device Description (limiting use to the
acute procedural session). (c) Note that the impeller fatigue test
(Issue 11) and the IFU's permission for a "repeat thrombectomy session"
after extended infusion (IFU Section 8.4, step 7) further complicate the
worst-case contact/operating profile.

#### Issue 6. Software Level of Concern --- "Minor" Is Under-Classified Relative to the Predicate and the Hazard Profile

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Sections 5.1--5.3 (software "Minor"
level of concern; reliance on hardware over-speed governor and
pressure-relief valve); Device Description, Section 2.3; ThrombEx 200
510(k) Summary, Section 5.6 (predicate software classified "Moderate").

**The issue.** Three concerns. **(a) Comparison to predicate.** The
predicate's software was classified "Moderate" (510(k) Summary, Section
5.6), and the predicate's software does less (saline-pump/aspiration
control only; no impeller speed control). The VascuClear console
software controls a rotating impeller up to 12,000 RPM --- a more
hazardous actuator --- yet is classified lower ("Minor"). FDA may
question the downgrade. **(b) Hazard profile.** A software failure that
causes impeller over-speed or uncommanded rotation could cause serious
injury (vessel perforation, dissection, intimal injury) --- the very
harms the IFU warns against (Warnings 2 and 4). The "Minor" rationale
rests on independent hardware safety mechanisms (mechanical over-speed
governor, pressure-relief valve), but the testing plan does **not**
include verification/validation of those hardware safety features. If
software is "Minor" only because hardware provides backup, the hardware
mitigations must be validated and described. **(c) Outdated guidance.**
The plan cites FDA's May 11, 2005 software guidance and its "level of
concern" framework. That guidance has been withdrawn and superseded by
FDA's current software guidance (which uses a "documentation level" ---
Basic/Enhanced --- framework) and by IEC 62304. The predicate's
submission used IEC 62304; the VascuClear plan does not reference it.

**Recommendation.** (a) Reassess the software documentation level.
"Moderate"/Enhanced is more defensible given the impeller hazard and the
predicate precedent; if "Minor"/Basic is retained, the rationale must be
robust and the hardware safety features must be validated and
documented. (b) Add verification/validation of the hardware over-speed
governor and pressure-relief valve to the testing plan. (c) Update the
software documentation framework to reference FDA's current software
guidance and IEC 62304, consistent with the predicate's approach.

#### Issue 7. EMC Testing (IEC 60601-1-2) Appears to Be Omitted

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Section 4 (electrical safety) and
Table 9-1 (summary lists "Electrical Safety (IEC 60601-1)" only);
ThrombEx 200 510(k) Summary, Section 5.3 (predicate underwent full IEC
60601-1-2 EMC battery).

**The issue.** The testing plan's electrical-safety section lists seven
IEC 60601-1 tests but **no electromagnetic compatibility (EMC) testing**
under IEC 60601-1-2. The summary table (Table 9-1) likewise lists only
"Electrical Safety (IEC 60601-1)." The VascuClear console is an
AC-powered electromechanical device with a variable-frequency motor
drive for the impeller --- a significant source of electromagnetic
emissions and a device that must be immune to EMI in the cath-lab
environment. The predicate underwent a full IEC 60601-1-2 EMC battery
(radiated/conducted emissions, ESD, RF immunity, EFT, surge, conducted
RF, magnetic-field, and voltage-dip immunity). FDA expects EMC testing
for a device of this type; its omission is a conspicuous gap relative to
the predicate.

**Recommendation.** Add IEC 60601-1-2:2014 (Ed. 4) EMC testing to the
electrical-safety section and to Table 9-1, covering the full immunity
and emissions battery, to be performed by Pinnacle Standards Testing,
Inc. (NRTL). Confirm the intended use environment (cath lab / IR suite)
and applicable emissions class (CISPR 11 Group 1 Class A, consistent
with the predicate).

#### Issue 8. Drug-Delivery Characterization Uses Saline Only; No Thrombolytic Compatibility Testing

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Section 8 (saline-only flow testing);
draft IFU, Section 9 ("Drug compatibility with catheter lumen materials
has not been independently verified... specific compatibility testing
with individual thrombolytic agents, diluents, or drug formulations has
not been performed"); IFU Section 9 (lists alteplase, reteplase,
tenecteplase as "compatible").

**The issue.** The drug-delivery characterization tests flow performance
with normal saline only. The IFU simultaneously (a) lists specific
thrombolytic agents as "compatible" and (b) states that compatibility
with those agents "has not been independently verified." This is
internally inconsistent and potentially misleading: a manufacturer
should not represent agents as "compatible" while conceding
compatibility is untested. If the Device is indicated to deliver
thrombolytics, material compatibility with the actual agents matters ---
tPA is a protein susceptible to surface adsorption (which could reduce
delivered dose), and the infusion-lumen materials (polyurethane, PTFE)
and adhesives may interact with the drug or its diluents. The testing
plan's own Question 6 to FDA concedes the gap by asking whether saline
is "sufficient." This issue is compounded if FDA views the Device as a
combination product (Issue 1), in which case drug-compatibility and
dose-accuracy data are expected.

**Recommendation.** (a) Add compatibility/adsorption testing with
representative thrombolytic agent(s) --- at minimum alteplase, the agent
named in the clinical protocol --- including assessment of drug recovery
(delivered vs. input dose) and leachables/extractables from the infusion
pathway in contact with the drug. (b) Reconcile the IFU: either remove
the "compatible agents" list pending testing, or qualify it to reflect
the testing actually performed. (c) Do not leave the adequacy of
saline-only testing as an open question to FDA; propose the tPA testing
proactively.

#### Issue 9. Simulated-Use Bench Testing Does Not Test the Claimed Pharmacomechanical Function

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Section 7 (bench test evaluates
"mechanical thrombectomy only"; "the thrombolytic infusion lumen is not
activated during simulated-use bench testing"; "combined
pharmacomechanical testing... is not included").

**The issue.** The Device's indication and "core differentiating
feature" is **pharmacomechanical** thrombectomy --- simultaneous
mechanical removal and drug delivery. But the bench test that is
supposed to demonstrate effectiveness tests the **mechanical component
only**, with the infusion lumen inactive. There is no bench evaluation
of the combined function that is actually claimed. FDA may require bench
data on the combined pharmacomechanical performance. Additional
concerns: (a) the single-use catheter is reused for up to four test runs
per unit (n=20 runs across 5 catheters), which is inconsistent with
single-use labeling and may mask fatigue/contamination effects; (b) the
primary acceptance criterion (≥80% thrombus removal in a single pass) is
set **above** the predicate's reported mean performance (78.4% mean;
range 65.2--91.7% per the 510(k) Summary, Section 5.5), which
complicates any comparative framing; (c) the bench test uses a single
pass, while the clinical protocol permits multiple passes, so the bench
model may not reflect clinical use.

**Recommendation.** (a) Add a bench arm evaluating combined
pharmacomechanical thrombectomy (impeller + aspiration + concurrent
infusion) to support the claimed indication. (b) Test each single-use
catheter once (or justify reuse with supporting data), consistent with
the single-use label. (c) Re-examine the 80% acceptance criterion
against the predicate's 78.4% mean and against any objective performance
criteria; be prepared to justify the threshold. (d) Consider a
multi-pass bench protocol to align with the clinical procedure.

#### Issue 10. Clinical Study Design --- Single-Arm, Short Follow-Up, and Weak Performance-Goal Justification

**Where:** Clinical Study Synopsis, Sections 5, 9, 11, and 12.

**The issue.** Multiple design concerns. **(a) No comparator / weak
OPC.** The study is single-arm with a 70% performance goal for ≥50%
thrombus removal at 24 hours. The basis for the 70% objective
performance criterion ("OPC") is not substantiated with literature or
predicate data; the synopsis asserts an expected 80% true rate but does
not anchor the 70% threshold to external evidence. **(b) Short
follow-up.** Follow-up ends at 30 days. The principal long-term clinical
benefit of iliofemoral thrombectomy is reduction of post-thrombotic
syndrome ("PTS"), which develops over months. Thirty days cannot assess
PTS (Villalta score / clinical outcomes at 6--24 months), as the ATTRACT
and CaVenT trials (cited in the synopsis's own references) demonstrate.
FDA may request longer follow-up. **(c) Venographic scoring.** The
primary endpoint relies on a "modified Marder scoring methodology (or an
equivalent validated scoring system)," but the scoring methodology is
listed as "in development" (Appendix C). A pre-specified, validated
scoring method with an independent core laboratory is expected. **(d) No
DSMB.** Safety monitoring is sponsor/CRO-only; for a novel device plus
tPA, an independent data safety monitoring board is advisable and may be
expected. **(e) Bleeding definition.** Major bleeding is defined as ≥2
units PRBC or re-intervention; FDA may expect a standardized, broader
bleeding scale (e.g., ISTH/GUSTO/BARC-type criteria) given the tPA
exposure. **(f) Dose/flow reconciliation.** The protocol specifies tPA
at 0.5--1.0 mg/hr, while the IFU specifies infusion flow rates of 2--10
mL/min; the relationship between dose (mg/hr) and flow rate (mL/min)
depends on reconstitution concentration, which is not fixed. This should
be reconciled and specified.

**Recommendation.** (a) Justify the 70% OPC with literature/predicate
data or revise. (b) Extend follow-up to at least 6 months (and consider
12 months) to capture PTS/Villalta outcomes, or justify a shorter
horizon to FDA. (c) Finalize and validate the venographic scoring
methodology pre-enrollment, with an independent core lab. (d) Establish
an independent DSMB. (e) Adopt a standardized bleeding definition. (f)
Specify the tPA concentration/dose/flow-rate relationship. (g) Revisit
the entire clinical strategy once the IDE question (Issue 2) is resolved
--- if an IDE is required, the design expectations rise.

#### Issue 11. Impeller Fatigue Testing --- 500 "Rotational Cycles" Equals \~2.5 Seconds of Operation

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Section 3.3 (impeller fatigue:
"minimum of 500 rotational cycles at maximum rated speed (12,000 RPM)";
"One rotational cycle is defined as one complete revolution (360°)");
Device Description, Section 2.2 ("500 complete activation cycles").

**The issue.** The fatigue test defines one "rotational cycle" as a
single 360° revolution and requires 500 such cycles. At 12,000 RPM, 500
revolutions = 500 ÷ 12,000 minutes ≈ **0.042 minutes ≈ 2.5 seconds** of
operation. This is not a meaningful fatigue test for a high-speed
rotating component. A single clinical pass of even one minute would
involve \~12,000 revolutions; a multi-pass procedure could involve tens
of thousands. For comparison, the predicate's (non-rotating) flexural
fatigue test ran 50,000 cycles. The 500-revolution specification appears
to reflect a unit-definition error (conflating "activation cycles" with
"revolutions") and is almost certain to be rejected by FDA as inadequate
to establish impeller durability. This is safety-critical: impeller
fracture or component detachment in vivo could embolize.

**Recommendation.** (a) Redefine the fatigue test in terms of total
revolutions (or total operating time) derived from the worst-case
cumulative clinical use (maximum number of passes × maximum duration per
pass × maximum RPM), with a safety margin (e.g., 2×--10×), and test to
that count --- likely in the hundreds of thousands to millions of
revolutions. (b) Clarify whether "activation cycle" in the Device
Description means an on/off event or a revolution, and make the testing
plan and Device Description consistent. (c) Ensure the fatigue test
envelope also covers the IFU-permitted "repeat thrombectomy session"
after extended infusion (IFU Section 8.4, step 7).

#### Issue 12. Submission Timeline / Corporate Milestone --- December 31, 2025 Clearance Appears Unachievable

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Section 9.2 (testing completion
Nov--Dec 2025; 510(k) submission "as early as practical" thereafter;
"milestone target of achieving 510(k) clearance by December 31, 2025");
Clinical Study Synopsis, Section 15 (enrollment Q4 2025; data Q2--Q3
2026); email chain (Fessenden, April 22; Marsh, May 9 --- Braddock
Ventures Series D milestone; \$18.3M cash; \$1.52M/month burn).

**The issue.** The testing timeline places completion in
November--December 2025. The 510(k) cannot be submitted until testing is
complete, and FDA's 510(k) review is ordinarily 90 days (plus any
Additional Information hold). Submitting in December 2025 and clearing
by December 31, 2025 is therefore not feasible. Realistically, clearance
would not occur before approximately Q1--Q2 2026. Meanwhile, the
clinical study (enrollment Q4 2025; data Q2--Q3 2026) is scheduled to
begin **before** the 510(k) is submitted and to generate data **after**
the clearance milestone --- which is inconsistent with the
"post-clearance" framing (Issue 2) and means clinical data will not be
available to support the initial 510(k) submission. If the regulatory
pathway changes (combination product, IDE required), the timeline slips
further. The Braddock Ventures term sheet milestone (clearance by
December 31, 2025) and Clearfield's cash position (\$18.3M;
\$1.52M/month burn; \~12 months of runway) create material corporate
exposure.

**Recommendation.** (a) Prepare a realistic timeline showing 510(k)
submission in Q1 2026 and clearance in Q2 2026 (or later), and socialize
it internally now; do not present the December 31, 2025 clearance date
to FDA. (b) As Ms. Pressman noted, if the pathway may change, loop in
Kendrick & Hale (corporate counsel) regarding the Braddock Ventures
milestone and financing implications, and consider whether the milestone
requires renegotiation. (c) Reconcile the clinical-study timing with the
submission/clearance timeline and the IDE determination (Issue 2).

### PRIORITY 3 --- MEDIUM

#### Issue 13. MRI Safety Data Is Incomplete (TBD Placeholders); No Predicate Basis

**Where:** draft IFU, Section 6 (MR Conditional claim with "\[TBD\] °C"
and "\[TBD\] T" placeholders); Device Description, comparison table
("MRI Status: MRI Conditional (per draft IFU)"); ThrombEx 200 510(k)
Summary (predicate MRI status "Not specified").

**The issue.** The IFU asserts the catheter is "MR Conditional" but
leaves the temperature rise and field strength as "\[TBD\]." An MR
Conditional claim requires completed testing per ASTM F2503 and a fully
specified conditions statement before it can be made. There is no
predicate basis for the claim (the predicate's MRI status is
unspecified). Given the catheter's rotating impeller and metallic
(stainless steel/nitinol) components, MRI testing should also address
whether the static and gradient fields could induce torque,
displacement, or --- critically --- **inadvertent impeller rotation**,
which would be a unique hazard for this device.

**Recommendation.** Complete MRI safety testing (ASTM F2503:
magnetically induced displacement force and torque, RF-induced heating,
and image artifact) and fill the placeholders before finalizing the IFU.
Specifically evaluate impeller behavior in the MRI field. If testing is
not yet complete, mark the claim as "to be determined" rather than
asserting MR Conditional.

#### Issue 14. Particulate/Embolization Testing --- Standard Selection and Distal-Embolization Gap

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Section 3.3 (particulate generation
per USP \<788\>; fatigue filtrate via 25 µm filter).

**The issue.** The impeller macerates thrombus at 12,000 RPM, creating a
risk of particulate/embolic debris. The plan applies USP \<788\>
(Particulate Matter in Injections) limits, but USP \<788\> governs
particulate in parenteral injectable solutions and may not be the
appropriate yardstick for particulate generated by a thrombectomy device
in the vasculature; its relevance and acceptance limits should be
justified to FDA. More importantly, the plan does not appear to evaluate
**distal embolization** --- i.e., the fate of macerated thrombus
fragments that are not aspirated. For venous thrombectomy, non-aspirated
fragments can embolize to the pulmonary circulation (PE), a key safety
concern. The bench test measures percent thrombus removed but does not
characterize the size distribution or destination of
residual/non-aspirated material.

**Recommendation.** (a) Justify the applicability of USP \<788\> limits
or adopt a more device-relevant particulate characterization. (b) Add
bench evaluation of distal embolization (size and quantity of
non-aspirated fragments, captured downstream of the treatment zone),
consistent with the venous PE risk. (c) Consider whether embolic
protection is warranted.

#### Issue 15. Dynamic Hemolysis Testing Under Impeller Operation Is Not Evident

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Section 2.2 (hemocompatibility per ISO
10993-4: hemolysis, complement, thrombogenicity); draft IFU, Warning 3
(excessive aspiration may cause hemolysis).

**The issue.** A rotating impeller at 12,000 RPM in contact with blood,
combined with aspiration at up to −650 mmHg, creates a hemolysis risk
(the IFU itself warns of it). The ISO 10993-4 hemolysis testing in the
biocompatibility plan is typically an extract-based or static-contact
test, not a dynamic test under actual impeller operating conditions and
aspiration flow. Static ISO 10993-4 hemolysis may not capture
shear-induced hemolysis from the rotating impeller or cavitation from
high vacuum.

**Recommendation.** Add dynamic hemolysis testing under worst-case
operating conditions (impeller at 12,000 RPM, aspiration at −650 mmHg,
blood or blood analog circulated through the device), with a defined
plasma-free hemoglobin acceptance criterion. This is standard for
high-shear blood-contacting rotary devices.

#### Issue 16. Shelf-Life / Sterile-Barrier System Validation Is Not in the Testing Plan

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Section 6 (sterilization validation
only; no shelf-life/sterile-barrier testing); draft IFU, Section 12
(expiration date on label); ThrombEx 200 510(k) Summary, Section 5.4
(predicate performed ASTM F1980 accelerated aging, ASTM D4169
distribution simulation, ASTM F2095 seal strength for a 2-year shelf
life).

**The issue.** The testing plan covers EtO sterilization validation (ISO
11135) and EtO residuals (ISO 10993-7) but does **not** include
shelf-life / sterile-barrier system validation. The IFU references an
expiration date, but the testing to support that date is absent. The
predicate performed a full shelf-life/distribution-simulation package
(ASTM F1980, D4169, F2095). Shelf-life validation is a standard
expectation for a sterile single-use device and its omission relative to
the predicate is conspicuous.

**Recommendation.** Add shelf-life and sterile-barrier system validation
to the testing plan: accelerated aging (ASTM F1980) and real-time aging
to support the claimed expiration date, package integrity/distribution
simulation (ASTM D4169), and seal strength (ASTM F2095), with a defined
shelf-life claim (e.g., 2 years, consistent with the predicate).

#### Issue 17. Console Reprocessing Validation Is Referenced but Not Specified

**Where:** Proposed Testing Plan, Section 6.1 (console
cleaning/disinfection "validated separately under Clearfield's reusable
device reprocessing validation protocol" --- not in the plan); draft
IFU, Section 11 (cleaning instructions: hospital-grade disinfectant
wipe).

**The issue.** The console is reusable. The testing plan disclaims
reprocessing validation ("validated separately") but does not include it
or cite a standard. FDA's reprocessing guidance expects validated
cleaning and disinfection instructions for reusable devices, with a
documented validation (soil test, inoculation, recovery). The IFU's
simple "disinfectant wipe" instruction should be validated.

**Recommendation.** Add reusable-device reprocessing validation
(cleaning and disinfection) to the testing plan, with a cited method
(e.g., AAMI TIR30 / FDA reprocessing guidance), and ensure the IFU
instructions match the validated procedure.

#### Issue 18. Material Identification Is Inconsistent Across Documents

**Where:** Device Description, Section 2.2 and comparison table
(impeller = 316L stainless steel; nitinol = "distal tip structural
elements"); Proposed Testing Plan, Section 2.3 (Helical Impeller =
"Nitinol (nickel-titanium alloy)"; Impeller Housing = "Stainless steel
316L"); draft IFU, Section 3, Contraindication 2 (lists "polyurethane,
stainless steel, and barium sulfate"); Clinical Study Synopsis, Section
7.3, Exclusion 6 (lists "polyurethane and silicone").

**The issue.** The impeller material is stated as **316L stainless
steel** in the Device Description but as **nitinol** in the Testing Plan
--- a direct contradiction about a critical, dynamically loaded,
blood-contacting component. The biocompatibility test articles and
chemical characterization (ISO 10993-18) depend on the correct material;
nitinol raises nickel-release concerns that 316L does not. Separately,
the IFU contraindication references **barium sulfate** and the study
exclusion references **silicone**, neither of which appears in the
Device Description or Testing Plan materials lists (the predicate uses a
barium-sulfate radiopaque marker, but the VascuClear materials list does
not include one). These inconsistencies must be reconciled before
testing is initiated and before the package is filed.

**Recommendation.** (a) Reconcile the impeller material across all
documents (Device Description, Testing Plan, IFU) and ensure the
biocompatibility/chemical-characterization test articles match. (b)
Confirm whether a barium-sulfate radiopaque marker is present; if so,
add it to the materials list and biocompatibility evaluation; if not,
remove it from the IFU contraindication. (c) Remove "silicone" from the
study exclusion criteria (it is not a listed device material) or add it
to the materials list if present.

#### Issue 19. Console Dimensions and Weight Are Inconsistent

# Tax Issue Identification Report

## Review of Transaction Structure Memorandum --- Acquisition of Prism Health Analytics, Inc.

**Prepared for:** Ridgeline Capital Partners Fund III, LP --- Investment
Committee **Re:** Tax Structure Memorandum dated February 10, 2025,
prepared by Calloway, Stern & Whitaker LLP **Subject:** Issues,
Inconsistencies, and Omissions Identified on Cross-Review Against
Supporting Deal Documents **Classification:** Privileged and
Confidential --- Attorney Work Product

## I. Executive Summary

This report presents the results of a cross-review of the Tax Structure
Memorandum dated February 10, 2025 (the "Memo"), prepared by Calloway,
Stern & Whitaker LLP ("CSW") for Ridgeline Capital Partners Fund III, LP
("Fund III" or "Buyer"), against the supporting deal documents made
available for the acquisition of Prism Health Analytics, Inc. ("Prism"
or the "Company"). The purpose of the review is to identify issues,
inconsistencies, and omissions in the Memo when measured against the
Stock Purchase Agreement, the Thornfield Accounting Group tax due
diligence report, the preliminary asset valuation / purchase price
allocation, the Prism capitalization table, the Beacon Insights
operating agreement, and the shareholder QSBS correspondence.

The review identified **28 distinct findings**, ranked across four
severity tiers: **3 Critical**, **7 High**, **8 Moderate**, and **10
Low**. The most significant issues are summarized below.

### Critical Findings

1.  **Aggregate Deemed Sale Price (ADSP) is overstated by \$48 million
    due to double-counting of funded debt.** The Memo computes ADSP as
    enterprise value (\$425M) plus funded debt (\$48M) = \$473M. Because
    enterprise value already embeds the funded debt (EV = equity value
    \$370M + funded debt \$48M + seller transaction expenses \$7M),
    adding the debt a second time double-counts it. Under Treas. Reg.
    1.338-4, ADSP should be computed as the amount realized on the stock
    (\$370M) plus target liabilities (\$48M, and arguably \$7M of seller
    expenses) = approximately \$418M--\$425M. This \$48M overstatement
    cascades into an overstated Class VII goodwill residual (\$272.8M
    vs. approximately \$196.3M), an overstated total deemed sale gain,
    and overstated buyer tax benefits. The Thornfield asset valuation
    summary independently flags this as a "significant discrepancy."

2.  **The Memo's S corporation eligibility conclusion directly
    contradicts the due diligence record.** The Memo states that "no
    events causing a termination of the S election have been identified"
    and that "all shareholders of Prism are U.S. individuals, and we are
    not aware of any ineligible shareholders." The Thornfield due
    diligence report (which the Memo represents it reviewed) flags as a
    **critical** open item the inability to confirm S corporation
    eligibility for restricted stock holders --- including an H-1B visa
    holder whose substantial-presence-test compliance is unverified and
    a possible transfer of shares to a family LLC (an ineligible
    shareholder). The capitalization table independently flags three
    holders with unverified eligibility. Valid S corporation status is a
    threshold requirement for the Section 338(h)(10) election; the
    Memo's affirmative representation is contradicted by its own cited
    source and by the Stock Purchase Agreement's eligibility
    representations, which may be inaccurate.

3.  **QSBS / Section 1202 analysis is entirely omitted despite an
    express commitment to include it.** The Memo contains no Section
    1202 analysis whatsoever. The Linden Rock Advisory correspondence
    shows that the selling shareholders' financial advisor formally
    requested a QSBS analysis in November 2024, that CSW's senior
    partner expressly committed in writing (November 20, 2024) to
    "include a QSBS analysis as part of the transaction structure
    memorandum," and that Linden Rock followed up on January 6, 2025
    noting that no analysis had been received. The Memo, dated February
    10, 2025, was prepared after that follow-up yet still omits the
    analysis. The QSBS exclusion is potentially worth seven-figure
    after-tax amounts to multiple shareholders, and CSW's own
    preliminary observation was that the 338(h)(10) deemed asset sale
    framework may be "in tension" with Section 1202 --- a structural
    decision point the Memo leaves entirely unaddressed.

### High-Severity Findings (summary)

-   The aggregate adjusted tax basis used in the Memo (\$42.8M) cannot
    be reconciled to the sum of individual asset tax bases in the
    Thornfield asset valuation summary (\$64.3M) --- a \$21.5M
    discrepancy that, combined with the ADSP error, overstates the
    deemed sale gain by approximately \$69.5M.
-   The Class V allocation is understated by \$28.5M relative to
    Thornfield's asset valuation (developed technology valued at \$19M
    vs. \$41.8M; government software licenses omitted entirely).
-   The non-transferable North Carolina data processing license --- on
    which approximately \$19M of annual government-sector revenue
    depends --- is treated as a minor commercial matter; the Memo does
    not analyze the consequences of a deemed sale of a non-transferable
    license.
-   The rollover equity treatment is internally inconsistent with the
    Memo's own ADSP computation, and no Section 351/721 analysis is
    provided.
-   The Memo's escrow tax-treatment recommendation (Section 453
    installment deferral) directly contradicts the Stock Purchase
    Agreement (which specifies reporting in the year of closing) and may
    be unavailable in the 338(h)(10) context.
-   The Beacon Insights gain is characterized as long-term capital gain
    without addressing Section 751 "hot assets," and no Section 754
    election analysis is provided.

### Overall Assessment

The Memo contains a sound high-level description of the reverse
triangular merger and Section 338(h)(10) mechanics, and several of its
conclusions (e.g., expiration of the Section 1374 built-in gains
recognition period) are correct and consistent with the due diligence
record. However, the Memo contains a material computational error in its
central ADSP calculation, an affirmative eligibility conclusion that is
contradicted by the due diligence it relies upon, and a complete
omission of a QSBS analysis that counsel expressly committed to deliver.
Several additional inconsistencies between the Memo and the definitive
agreement, the asset valuation, and the capitalization table require
reconciliation before the Memo can be relied upon by the Investment
Committee. The findings below are presented in severity-ranked order
with specific document cross-references, impact assessments, and
recommended actions.

### C. Methodology

Each provision of the Memo was compared against the corresponding
disclosure, representation, computation, or factual record in the
supporting documents. Findings are classified by severity based on (i)
the magnitude of the tax or economic impact, (ii) whether the issue
affects a threshold requirement for the elected structure, (iii) whether
the issue reflects an internal inconsistency or a contradiction with an
authoritative source document, and (iv) whether the issue is an omission
of a matter that the Memo represents or was requested to address.
Severity tiers are defined as follows:

-   **Critical** --- Affects a threshold requirement for the transaction
    structure, reflects a material computational error, or constitutes
    an omission of a matter expressly committed to be addressed; could
    invalidate the structure or cause a material misstatement of tax
    consequences.
-   **High** --- Material error or omission that significantly affects
    the quantification of tax consequences, contradicts a definitive
    agreement, or omits analysis required for an informed decision; must
    be resolved before reliance.
-   **Moderate** --- Notable error, inconsistency, or omission that
    warrants correction and may affect secondary tax consequences or
    deal terms.
-   **Low** --- Minor inconsistency, unsupported assumption, or
    completeness gap that should be corrected but is unlikely to alter
    the transaction structure or primary tax conclusions.

## III. Critical Findings

### C-1. ADSP Overstated by \$48 Million --- Double-Counting of Funded Debt

**Severity:** Critical

**Description.** The Memo computes the Aggregate Deemed Sale Price
(ADSP) as follows (Section IV.B):

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Component                           Memo Amount
  ----------------------------------- -----------------------------------
  Enterprise Value                    \$425,000,000

  Plus: Assumed Liabilities (Funded   \$48,000,000
  Debt at Closing)                    

  **ADSP**                            **\$473,000,000**
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

This computation is incorrect. Enterprise value already includes the
funded debt. The Memo itself establishes the relationship elsewhere
(Section II.C): Equity Value = Enterprise Value (\$425M) − Funded Debt
(\$48M) − Seller Transaction Expenses (\$7M) = \$370M. Stated
differently, EV = Equity Value + Funded Debt + Seller Transaction
Expenses. Adding the \$48M of funded debt to enterprise value therefore
counts the debt twice.

Under Treas. Reg. 1.338-4, the ADSP equals the grossed-up amount
realized on the sale of the target's stock plus the liabilities of old
target. The amount realized on the stock is the equity value --- i.e.,
the consideration paid for the shareholders' stock (\$333M cash + \$37M
rollover equity = \$370M) --- not the enterprise value. The correct
computation is:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Component                           Correct Amount
  ----------------------------------- -----------------------------------
  Amount realized on stock (equity    \$370,000,000
  value)                              

  Plus: Funded debt (target           \$48,000,000
  liability)                          

  Plus: Seller transaction expenses   \$7,000,000
  (target liability, if so treated)   

  **ADSP (including seller            **\$425,000,000**
  expenses)**                         

  **ADSP (excluding seller            **\$418,000,000**
  expenses)**                         
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The Thornfield asset valuation summary (Allocation Summary tab)
independently identifies this error, stating: "The memorandum computes
ADSP as \$425M enterprise value + \$48M liabilities = \$473M. This
double-counts the \$48M funded debt, which was already subtracted to
derive equity value (\$425M − \$48M − \$7M = \$370M) and is then added
back again."

**Impact.** The \$48M overstatement propagates throughout the Memo's
analysis:

-   **Class VII goodwill** is overstated by \$48M (the entire
    overstatement falls to the residual class): \$272.8M (Memo)
    vs. approximately \$224.8M (corrected, excluding seller expenses) or
    \$196.3M (per Thornfield, which also corrects the Class V allocation
    --- see H-2).
-   **Total deemed sale gain** is overstated by \$48M before considering
    the basis discrepancy in H-1.
-   **Buyer's annual amortization deductions** attributable to goodwill
    are overstated by approximately \$3.2M/year (\$48M ÷ 15 years), with
    a corresponding overstatement of projected cash tax savings.
-   The Memo's headline buyer benefit figures (Section VII.A: \~\$24.5M
    annual D&A; \~\$6.125M annual cash tax savings) are derived from the
    overstated ADSP and require recomputation.

**Recommendation.** Recompute the ADSP starting from the amount realized
on the stock (\$370M) plus target liabilities. Reconcile with
Thornfield's computation. Revise the purchase price allocation, the
deemed sale gain, the per-shareholder gain allocations, and the buyer
benefit estimates accordingly. Confirm whether seller transaction
expenses are properly treated as target liabilities for ADSP purposes
(the Memo excludes them; Thornfield includes them "for conservatism").

### C-2. S Corporation Shareholder Eligibility --- Memo Contradicts the Due Diligence Record

**Severity:** Critical

**Description.** The Memo states (Section III.A): "We have reviewed the
S election file and the annual shareholder records provided to us. No
events causing a termination of the S election have been identified. All
shareholders of Prism are U.S. individuals, and we are not aware of any
ineligible shareholders." The Memo reiterates in Section II.A that the
restricted stock holders are "individuals" and that the "other
shareholders" group consists of holders who are "all individuals."

These statements are directly contradicted by the Thornfield Tax Due
Diligence Report, which the Memo represents (in its opening paragraph
and Section I) to have reviewed. Thornfield identifies shareholder
eligibility as a **critical** open item (Section III.B; Summary Finding
No. 1):

-   **One restricted stock holder** is identified in personnel records
    as a Canadian national on an H-1B nonimmigrant visa. Thornfield
    could not confirm substantial-presence-test compliance under Section
    7701(b)(3) for each year since the grant. If the holder failed the
    substantial-presence test in any year, the holder would be a
    nonresident alien --- an ineligible S corporation shareholder under
    Section 1361(b)(1) --- causing retroactive termination of the S
    election under Section 1362(d)(2).
-   **One restricted stock holder** is the subject of an annotation in
    Prism's 2021 internal stock ledger referencing a "transfer to
    \[name\] Family Holdings, LLC." If the transfer occurred and the LLC
    is a multi-member LLC (or a single-member LLC owned by an ineligible
    person), the LLC is an ineligible shareholder, terminating the S
    election as of the transfer date.

The Prism capitalization table independently flags **three** restricted
stock holders with unverified S corporation eligibility:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Holder                              Issue (per cap table)
  ----------------------------------- -----------------------------------
  Lin Wei Zhang                       H-1B visa holder; PRC citizen;
                                      permanent residency application
                                      pending; residency/citizenship not
                                      verified

  Yusuf Al-Rashidi                    Dual citizen (U.S./Jordan); U.S.
                                      citizenship documentation not on
                                      file

  Nina Petrova                        Bulgarian-born; green card (lawful
                                      permanent resident) status
                                      unconfirmed on file
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The capitalization table's aggregate note confirms: "Unverified S corp
eligibility: 3 shareholders." The cap table also notes that these three
holders are in line to receive an aggregate of approximately \$2,775,000
in transaction consideration.

The Stock Purchase Agreement compounds the issue. Section 4.9(a)
represents that "each shareholder is an eligible S corporation
shareholder within the meaning of Section 1361(b)(1)," and Section 2.5
represents that "each holder of restricted stock is an individual who is
a citizen or resident of the United States." Given the Thornfield and
cap-table findings, these representations may be inaccurate. Section
8.2(c) makes maintenance of valid S corporation status through closing a
condition to Buyer's obligations.

**Impact.** Valid S corporation status is a threshold requirement for
the Section 338(h)(10) election. If the S election was inadvertently
terminated in any prior year, Prism would have been a C corporation for
that and all subsequent years, with cascading consequences: entity-level
federal and state C corporation income tax for all open years, penalties
and interest, invalidation of the state-level S elections (per
Thornfield Section V.B), and unavailability of the Section 338(h)(10)
election --- the central feature of the structure the Memo analyzes. The
Memo's affirmative statement that it is "not aware of any ineligible
shareholders" is inconsistent with the due diligence record it cites and
creates a risk that the Investment Committee relies on a conclusion the
supporting documents do not support.

**Recommendation.** The Memo should be revised to (i) disclose the
eligibility open items identified by Thornfield and the capitalization
table, (ii) withdraw or qualify the statement that no ineligible
shareholders have been identified, and (iii) make resolution of
shareholder eligibility an express condition precedent to closing and to
the 338(h)(10) election, consistent with Stock Purchase Agreement
Section 8.2(c) and (d). Immigration/residency documentation for the H-1B
holder and documentation of the alleged family-LLC transfer should be
obtained and reviewed before closing. The accuracy of the Section 4.9(a)
and 2.5 representations should be confirmed.

### C-3. QSBS / Section 1202 Analysis Entirely Omitted Despite Express Commitment

**Severity:** Critical

**Description.** The Memo contains no Section 1202 / qualified small
business stock (QSBS) analysis. There is no reference to Section 1202
anywhere in the Memo, no dedicated section, and no discussion of the
interaction between QSBS and the Section 338(h)(10) election. This
omission is significant in light of the following record:

-   **Thornfield Due Diligence Report** (Section VIII.B; Summary Finding
    No. 4) identifies QSBS as a moderate finding, noting that several
    angel investors raised Section 1202 questions and that "the
    interaction between QSBS treatment and the proposed Section
    338(h)(10) deemed asset sale framework has not been analyzed."
    Thornfield recommends that "tax counsel should provide a definitive
    analysis of QSBS eligibility."
-   **Linden Rock Advisory correspondence (November 2024):** The selling
    shareholders' financial advisor formally requested that CSW provide
    a written QSBS analysis, identifying four specific questions: (1)
    the effect of the C-to-S conversion on QSBS eligibility; (2) the
    interaction between Section 1202 and the Section 338(h)(10)
    election; (3) whether Prism met the qualified small business
    requirements (including the \$50M gross assets test); and (4) the
    position of the two California-resident shareholders. Linden Rock
    stated the issue was "time-sensitive" and requested analysis "before
    the shareholders are asked to commit to the definitive documents."
-   **CSW response (November 20, 2024):** CSW's senior partner
    acknowledged the issue, flagged that the 338(h)(10) deemed asset
    sale treatment may be "in tension with the statutory language of
    Section 1202," and expressly committed: "We intend to include a QSBS
    analysis as part of the transaction structure memorandum we are
    preparing for Ridgeline... My goal is to have the QSBS section
    drafted and incorporated into the memorandum well before the
    purchase agreement is executed."
-   **Linden Rock follow-up (January 6, 2025):** Linden Rock noted that
    no written analysis had been received since the November exchange,
    reiterated the urgency, raised an additional question regarding the
    \$22M escrow and QSBS timing, and asked whether an alternative
    structure (e.g., a straight stock sale without the 338(h)(10)
    election) would preserve the exclusion.
-   **The Memo is dated February 10, 2025** --- after the January 6
    follow-up and after the purchase agreement was signed (January
    28, 2025) --- yet contains no QSBS analysis.

The capitalization table confirms that multiple shareholders acquired
stock during the C corporation period (June 14 -- December 31, 2016) and
are potentially QSBS-eligible, including the two founders and at least
nine non-founder investors (Thomas Greenfield, Patricia Muñoz, David
Yoon, Robert Fielding, Karen Whitford, Samuel Okoye, Marcus Donnelly,
Priya Srinivasan, and Derek Hawkins). The cap table annotates each with
"Potential QSBS eligibility --- not analyzed." (Note: Thornfield's
report states that only four seed-round angel investors acquired during
the C corporation period; the capitalization table shows a larger number
--- see M-6.)

**Impact.** The QSBS exclusion (up to 100% of gain for stock acquired
after September 27, 2010, subject to per-issuer and per-taxpayer caps)
is potentially worth seven-figure after-tax amounts to multiple selling
shareholders. The Linden Rock correspondence characterizes the
difference between a 100% federal exclusion and full capital-gains
taxation as, "in some cases, a seven-figure swing in after-tax
proceeds." Critically, CSW's own preliminary observation was that the
338(h)(10) deemed asset sale framework may foreclose QSBS treatment ---
meaning the election that benefits the Buyer may eliminate a material
benefit for the selling shareholders. This is a structural decision
point (338(h)(10) vs. straight stock sale) that the Memo entirely fails
to address, despite an express written commitment to do so. The omission
also leaves the escrow/QSBS timing question (raised January 6, 2025)
unanswered.

**Recommendation.** The Memo should be supplemented with a dedicated
QSBS section addressing: (i) which shareholders' shares were issued
during the C corporation period and may qualify; (ii) whether the C-to-S
conversion affects the retained QSBS character of those shares; (iii)
whether the Section 338(h)(10) deemed asset sale / deemed liquidation
framework permits or forecloses the Section 1202 exclusion; (iv) the
\$50M aggregate gross assets test; (v) the per-taxpayer/per-issuer
limitations; (vi) the treatment of the escrow portion; and (vii) whether
an alternative structure would preserve the exclusion and the trade-offs
involved. Given that the purchase agreement has been signed, this
analysis is overdue and should be provided to the selling shareholders'
advisors promptly.

## IV. High-Severity Findings

### H-1. Aggregate Adjusted Tax Basis Cannot Be Reconciled to the Asset Valuation

**Severity:** High

**Description.** The Memo uses an aggregate adjusted tax basis of
\$42,800,000 for Prism's assets (Sections III.B and V.A), describing it
as "the most recent available date" figure "reflecting additions,
dispositions, and depreciation deductions claimed since the S election
became effective." This figure is used to compute the total deemed sale
gain: ADSP (\$473M) − basis (\$42.8M) = \$430.2M.

The Thornfield asset valuation summary (Asset Detail tab) provides an
adjusted tax basis for each individual asset. The sum of those
individual bases is **\$64,300,000**, computed as follows:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Asset                               Adjusted Tax Basis
  ----------------------------------- -----------------------------------
  Cash and cash equivalents           \$12,400,000

  Accounts receivable --- commercial  \$18,700,000

  Accounts receivable --- government  \$6,400,000

  Real property --- Raleigh office    \$9,100,000

  Furniture, fixtures & equipment     \$3,200,000

  Developed technology / software     \$4,800,000
  platform                            

  Government-sector software licenses \$1,200,000

  NC data processing license          \$0

  80.5% Beacon Insights interest      \$6,400,000

  Customer relationships (hospital)   \$0

  Customer relationships (insurer)    \$0

  Proprietary data sets / databases   \$2,100,000

  Non-compete agreements              \$0

  Assembled workforce                 \$0

  Goodwill                            \$0

  **Total**                           **\$64,300,000**
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The Memo's \$42.8M figure is \$21.5M lower than the \$64.3M sum of the
individual asset bases. The Memo does not provide a reconciliation or
explain which assets are included or excluded.

**Impact.** The aggregate basis directly determines the deemed sale
gain. Combined with the ADSP overstatement in C-1, the Memo's deemed
sale gain of \$430.2M may be overstated by approximately \$69.5M (the
\$48M ADSP overstatement plus the \$21.5M basis understatement), which
would reduce the correct gain to approximately \$360.7M (using
Thornfield's ADSP of \$425M and the asset-valuation basis of \$64.3M).
This affects every per-shareholder gain allocation in Section V.B and
the associated tax liability estimates. Even if a portion of the \$21.5M
difference is attributable to a legitimate methodology choice (e.g.,
treatment of cash or receivables), the Memo does not disclose the
composition of its \$42.8M figure, making it impossible to audit.

**Recommendation.** Provide a line-item reconciliation of the \$42.8M
aggregate basis to the individual asset bases in the Thornfield asset
valuation summary. If the figures differ due to timing, methodology, or
the treatment of specific assets (e.g., cash, receivables, the Beacon
interest), disclose and justify the treatment. Recompute the deemed sale
gain on a reconciled basis.

### H-2. Class V Allocation Understated by \$28.5 Million --- Developed Technology and Government Software Licenses

**Severity:** High

**Description.** The Memo's Class V allocation is \$68,500,000,
comprised of:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Class V Component (Memo)            Amount
  ----------------------------------- -----------------------------------
  Real property --- Raleigh office    \$14,200,000

  Furniture, fixtures & equipment     \$6,800,000

  Software / developed technology     \$19,000,000

  Prism's 80.5% Beacon interest       \$28,500,000

  **Total Class V (Memo)**            **\$68,500,000**
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The Thornfield asset valuation summary allocates \$97,000,000 to Class
V, comprised of:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Class V Component (Thornfield)      Amount
  ----------------------------------- -----------------------------------
  Real property --- Raleigh office    \$14,200,000

  Furniture, fixtures & equipment     \$6,800,000

  Developed technology / software     \$41,800,000
  platform                            

  Government-sector software licenses \$5,700,000

  NC data processing license          \$0

  Prism's 80.5% Beacon interest       \$28,500,000

  **Total Class V (Thornfield)**      **\$97,000,000**
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The \$28.5M variance is attributable to two items:

1.  **Developed technology:** The Memo values developed technology at
    \$19.0M; Thornfield values it at \$41.8M (relief-from-royalty
    method) --- a \$22.8M understatement in the Memo.
2.  **Government-sector software licenses:** The Memo omits these
    entirely; Thornfield values them at \$5.7M (income/DCF approach) ---
    a \$5.7M omission in the Memo.

(Note: Thornfield's reconciliation note (Note 2) states that the Memo's
Class V "does not appear to include the \$28,500,000 Beacon interest."
This attribution is incorrect --- the Memo's Class V table expressly
lists the Beacon interest at \$28.5M, and the Memo's Class V components
sum correctly to \$68.5M. The actual cause of the variance is the
developed technology valuation and the omitted government software
licenses, not the Beacon interest. This error in Thornfield's own
reconciliation underscores the need for a careful line-by-line
reconciliation between the two work products.)

**Impact.** The \$28.5M Class V understatement, combined with the \$48M
ADSP overstatement (C-1), drives the \$76.5M overstatement of Class VII
goodwill (\$272.8M Memo vs. \$196.3M Thornfield). This affects (i) the
character and timing of the buyer's depreciation and amortization
deductions (developed technology is generally amortizable over a shorter
period than goodwill, and is amortizable under Section 167 rather than
Section 197), (ii) the seller's gain character (Class V tangible gains
may include Section 1245/1250 recapture as ordinary income, whereas
goodwill is capital), and (iii) the overall allocation between ordinary
and capital gain. The Memo's deferral of the character analysis "upon
completion of the Thornfield asset valuation" does not excuse the use of
a developed-technology value (\$19M) that is less than half of
Thornfield's (\$41.8M) without explanation.

**Recommendation.** Reconcile the Class V allocation to the Thornfield
asset valuation. Adopt or justify the deviation from Thornfield's
\$41.8M developed technology value and include the \$5.7M government
software licenses. Correct Thornfield's misattribution of the variance.
Re-examine the Section 167 vs. Section 197 amortization treatment for
developed technology and software (the asset valuation notes that the
software platform is "not a §197 intangible" and is amortizable under
Section 167, which the Memo's Section VII.A does not reflect --- it
treats all Class V intangibles under MACRS without distinguishing
Section 167 software).

### H-3. Non-Transferable North Carolina Data Processing License --- Deemed Sale Consequences Not Analyzed

**Severity:** High

**Description.** The North Carolina data processing license
(No. DIT-2019-04821) is held by Prism Data Services, LLC and is, by its
terms, **non-transferable and non-assignable**. This is confirmed by two
independent sources:

-   **Thornfield asset valuation summary (Asset A-008):** "NC DIT data
    processing license is non-transferable and non-assignable per
    license terms. In a deemed asset sale under §338(h)(10), this
    license cannot be transferred and may need to be re-obtained by the
    surviving entity... If terminated, value associated with the
    government business line should be reassigned to goodwill or
    reduced."
-   **Stock Purchase Agreement Section 4.9(i):** The license "pursuant
    to its terms, is non-transferable and non-assignable."

The license supports approximately \$19,000,000 in annual
government-sector revenue (per the Memo, Thornfield, and the asset
valuation). Thornfield assigned the license \$0 fair market value for
purchase price allocation purposes precisely because it cannot be
transferred.

The Memo addresses the license in four places (Sections II.A, III.C,
VI.A, IX.A) but in each instance treats it as a minor commercial matter
to be handled by "deal counsel in the pre-closing period." Section VI.A
states: "we expect that the existing license should remain valid
post-closing, subject to confirmation with the North Carolina Department
of Information Technology."

**Impact.** The Memo does not analyze the central tax-structural
problem: a Section 338(h)(10) election treats the transaction as a

### H-5. Escrow Tax Treatment Contradicts the Stock Purchase Agreement; Section 453 Availability Questionable

**Severity:** High

**Description.** The Memo (Section V.C) recommends that the selling

The Stock Purchase Agreement specifies a different treatment. Section

These positions are in direct conflict: the Memo recommends deferral;

There is also a substantive question whether Section 453 installment
treatment is available at all in a Section 338(h)(10) transaction. Under
the 338(h)(10) election, the actual stock sale is disregarded; the
shareholders do not sell stock. Instead, old target is deemed to sell
its assets (recognizing gain at the entity level at the close of the
acquisition date) and is then deemed to liquidate, distributing the
deemed sale proceeds to the shareholders. The deemed sale gain is
recognized at the entity level in the year of the acquisition date and
passes through to shareholders on the final S corporation K-1. The
escrow (\$22M) is part of the cash consideration, which is part of the
amount realized on the stock that drives the ADSP; it is therefore
embedded in the deemed sale proceeds recognized at closing. Whether the
shareholders can separately defer the escrow portion under Section 453
--- when the gain has already been recognized at the entity level via
the deemed asset sale --- is a question the Memo does not address. The
Memo's analysis treats the escrow as if the shareholders sold stock
directly, which is not how a 338(h)(10) transaction works.

The Linden Rock correspondence (January 6, 2025) also raised the
escrow/QSBS timing question --- whether any exclusion applies to gain
attributable to escrow amounts released 18--24 months after closing ---
which the Memo does not address.

**Impact.** The inconsistency between the Memo and the definitive
agreement creates uncertainty for the selling shareholders, who may rely
on the Memo's deferral recommendation and find it inconsistent with the
Agreement's stated treatment. If Section 453 is unavailable in the
338(h)(10) context (because the gain is recognized at the entity level
at closing), the Memo's recommendation is legally incorrect and could
mislead shareholders into underpaying estimated taxes for 2025. The
escrow represents \$22M of consideration; the timing of gain recognition
on this amount is material to multiple shareholders.

**Recommendation.** Reconcile the Memo's escrow tax-treatment
recommendation with Section 7.4 of the Stock Purchase Agreement. Analyze
whether Section 453 installment treatment is available to shareholders
in a 338(h)(10) transaction given that the gain is recognized at the
entity level via the deemed asset sale, or whether the escrow is
necessarily included in the amount realized at closing. Address the
escrow/QSBS timing question raised by Linden Rock. If the Agreement's
"solely responsible" language is intended to leave the treatment to each
shareholder, the Memo should not recommend a specific treatment that
conflicts with the Agreement's stated intent without flagging the
conflict.

four-year employment agreement. The anti-churning rules restrict
amortization of intangibles acquired from related parties or in
transactions that do not result in a meaningful change of ownership. The
determination of whether Chandra's rollover and continued role create a
"related party" relationship under Section 197(f)(9) and the related
regulations (including the "related person" and "related transaction"
definitions) requires a more detailed analysis than the Memo provides,
particularly as to the intangibles that Chandra may be treated as having
an interest in (e.g., customer relationships, non-compete).

**Impact.** If the anti-churning rules apply to any intangibles, the
buyer would lose the Section 197 amortization deduction for those
intangibles --- a material reduction in the buyer's tax benefits. The
customer relationships (\$62M) and non-compete (\$15M) are the most
exposed.

**Recommendation.** Provide a detailed Section 197(f)(9) analysis
addressing (i) whether Chandra's 10% rollover equity and continued
employment/role create a "related person" or "related transaction"
relationship, (ii) which specific intangibles (if any) are subject to
the anti-churning rules, and (iii) whether any structuring modifications
(e.g., to the rollover percentage or employment terms) would mitigate
the risk.

## VI. Low-Severity Findings

### L-1. New York Sales Tax Exposure (\$150K--\$300K) Omitted

**Severity:** Low

The Thornfield due diligence report (Section X.A) identifies that Prism
has not been collecting New York sales tax on its SaaS subscription
revenue sourced to New York customers, with estimated exposure of
\$150,000--\$300,000 (inclusive of penalties and interest), and
recommends a voluntary disclosure agreement. The Memo does not mention
this exposure. As a pre-closing tax liability, it is relevant to the
indemnification provisions and the closing balance-sheet/working-capital
adjustments. **Recommendation:** Note the exposure and confirm it is
addressed in the indemnification or pre-closing remediation.

### L-2. North Carolina Individual Income Tax Rate for 2025

**Severity:** Low

The Memo (Section VI.A) states the 2025 North Carolina individual income
tax rate is 4.5%. Under the 2023 North Carolina tax reform (the
rate-reduction schedule enacted in House Bill 259), the individual rate
was 4.5% for 2024 and was scheduled to decrease to 4.25% for 2025
(subject to revenue triggers). The Memo should verify the applicable
2025 rate, as it affects the state tax estimates for the North
Carolina-resident shareholders. **Recommendation:** Confirm the 2025 NC
individual rate and update the state tax estimates if the 4.25% rate
applies.

### L-3. Dr. Chandra's Stock Basis --- Estimate Inconsistent with Capitalization Table

**Severity:** Low

The Memo (Section V.B) estimates Dr. Chandra's tax basis in his Prism
stock at "approximately \$1,500,000 based on his original investment at
the time of founding," noting it is "to be confirmed by Thornfield
Accounting Group." The capitalization table shows Chandra's total cost
basis as \$1,014.00 (1,014,000 shares at \$0.001 par value). The \$1.5M
estimate is not reconciled to the cap table and may include additional
capital contributions not reflected in the share-acquisition records.
The stock basis affects the deemed-liquidation gain characterization
(distribution less basis). **Recommendation:** Confirm Chandra's (and
all shareholders') stock basis against the capitalization table and
capital-account records, and reconcile any difference.

### L-4. Thornfield Report Date Cited Incorrectly

**Severity:** Low

The Memo's opening paragraph cites "the quality of earnings and tax due
diligence report prepared by Thornfield Accounting Group, LLP dated
January 10, 2025." The Thornfield report is dated January 15, 2025 (the
January 10 date appears in the report's scope section as the cutoff for
supplemental information received, not the report date).
**Recommendation:** Correct the cited date to January 15, 2025. (Note:
the asset valuation summary references a "Calloway, Stern & Whitaker LLP
Memo dated 1/28/2025," suggesting Thornfield reviewed an earlier draft;
the final Memo is dated 2/10/2025 and retains the same ADSP and
allocation figures, confirming the errors identified in C-1 and H-2
persist in the final version.)

### L-5. Section 163(j) EBITDA Figure Unsubstantiated

**Severity:** Low

The Memo (Section VII.B) references "Prism's historical EBITDA of
approximately \$58,000,000 (2024 estimate)" for the Section 163(j)
interest-deductibility analysis. This figure is not sourced to any
document in the record and implies a \~67% EBITDA margin on \$87M of
revenue, which is unusually high for a healthcare analytics/SaaS company
(Beacon's EBITDA margin, by comparison, is \~29%). The Thornfield
reports do not state a consolidated Prism EBITDA. **Recommendation:**
Reconcile the \$58M EBITDA to the quality-of-earnings analysis and
confirm the Section 163(j) capacity assessment on a supported figure.

### L-6. Post-Closing Entity Conversion Recommendation Is Cursory

**Severity:** Low

The Memo (Section VII.B) recommends that the buyer "consider converting
Prism from a C corporation to a disregarded entity or partnership"
post-closing via a check-the-box election, to flow the stepped-up
deductions through to Fund III. The Memo does not analyze the tax cost
of this conversion (a C corporation conversion to a partnership is
generally a taxable deemed liquidation under Section 331/336,
potentially triggering entity-level tax) or the interaction with the
Section 1371 post-termination transition period. **Recommendation:**
Analyze the tax cost of the post-closing conversion and the timing
before recommending it.

### L-7. Recapture and Ordinary-Income Character Analysis Deferred

**Severity:** Low

The Memo (Section V.A) correctly identifies that the deemed sale gain
includes ordinary-income components (Section 1245/1250 recapture,
accounts receivable) but defers the detailed character analysis "upon
completion of the Thornfield asset valuation." The asset valuation is
now available and shows, e.g., \$3.6M of FF&E gain (Section 1245
recapture), \~\$5.1M of real-property gain (Section 1250, including
unrecaptured Section 1250 at 25%), and a \~\$0.8M loss on receivables.
**Recommendation:** Complete the character analysis using the available
asset valuation and quantify the ordinary vs. capital split for each
shareholder.

### L-8. Virginia and New York State Tax Treatment Is Cursory

**Severity:** Low

The Memo (Section VI.C) addresses Virginia and New York in a single
paragraph, deferring to individual advisors. The Thornfield report notes
Virginia imposes no entity-level S corporation tax and New York imposes
a fixed dollar minimum tax (Prism paid \~\$4,500 for 2023). The New
York-resident shareholder (Samuel Okoye) will be subject to New York tax
on the gain; the Memo does not quantify any state-level exposure for
non-NC/CA residents or address New York's treatment of the 338(h)(10)
election. **Recommendation:** Expand the state analysis to address
Virginia and New York conformity to the 338(h)(10) election and the
resident/non-resident filing obligations for the affected shareholders.

### L-9. Form 8883 Not Referenced

**Severity:** Low

The Stock Purchase Agreement (Section 7.3(b)) requires each party to
file IRS Form 8883 (Asset Allocation Statement) consistent with the
final allocation. The Memo discusses Form 8023 (the 338(h)(10) election
form) but does not mention Form 8883 in its post-closing filings list
(Section IX.B). **Recommendation:** Add Form 8883 to the post-closing
filings list and note the 90-day allocation-preparation and 30-day
review timeline in Section 7.3(b).

### L-10. 2024 S Corporation Return Timing Not Addressed

**Severity:** Low

The Thornfield report (Section IV.A) notes that the 2024 Form 1120-S has
not been filed and is due March 15, 2025 --- the same date as the
expected closing. An extension will likely be required. The Memo does
not address the 2024 return timing or its interaction with the final
short-period return (January 1 -- March 15, 2025). **Recommendation:**
Address the 2024 return filing/extension and coordinate with the final
short-period return preparation.

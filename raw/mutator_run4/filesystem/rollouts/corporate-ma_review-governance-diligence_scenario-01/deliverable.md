## I. Executive Summary

This memorandum summarizes our review of the governance documents of
Terravox Systems, Inc. ("Terravox" or the "Company") in connection with
the proposed acquisition of 100% of the Company's outstanding equity by
Hargrove Capital Partners III, LP ("Hargrove" or "Buyer"), structured as
a reverse triangular merger at an enterprise value of \$485 million (the
"Transaction"). We reviewed the Company's Third Amended and Restated
Certificate of Incorporation (the "Restated Certificate"), Amended and
Restated Bylaws (the "Bylaws"), Stockholders' Agreement, Voting
Agreement, Registration Rights Agreement (the "RRA"), and Board minutes
covering January 2023 through September 2024 (the "Board Minutes").

**The central finding of this diligence is that Stonemill Capital
Partners Fund VI, LP ("Stonemill") holds a hard, charter-level blocking
right over the Transaction.** As the holder of 100% of the outstanding
Series C Preferred Stock, Stonemill alone controls the Series C
separate-class protective provision under Section 4.3.6(b)(i) of the
Restated Certificate, which requires the affirmative vote or written
consent of holders of at least 66⅔% of the Series C Preferred Stock,
voting as a separate class, to effect any "Liquidation Event." The
reverse triangular merger is unambiguously a "Liquidation Event."
Because Stonemill holds all 7,800,000 outstanding Series C shares, the
66⅔% threshold (≥5,200,000 shares) cannot be satisfied without
Stonemill's affirmative consent. This blocking right cannot be cured by
the drag-along mechanism in the Stockholders' Agreement with any
certainty under Delaware law, as discussed in Section IV.

A second, time-sensitive finding concerns the Company's letter of intent
with SensorLink Technologies, Inc. ("SensorLink"). The SensorLink LOI
contains an automatic-renewal exclusivity provision, and the Board
Minutes reflect uncertainty as to whether a formal termination notice
was delivered before the September 8, 2024 expiration date. If no notice
was sent, a live exclusivity obligation may be running in favor of
SensorLink and could conflict directly with the Company's ability to
sign definitive documentation with Hargrove. This requires immediate
confirmation and remediation.

## II. Transaction Overview and Capitalization

**Transaction structure.** Hargrove will form a wholly-owned acquisition
subsidiary ("Merger Sub") that will merge with and into Terravox, with
Terravox surviving as a wholly-owned subsidiary of Hargrove. All
outstanding equity interests of Terravox (Common Stock and all series of
Preferred Stock) will be cancelled and converted into the right to
receive cash merger consideration. The structure is a reverse triangular
merger under Section 251 of the Delaware General Corporation Law (the
"DGCL").

**Deal economics.** Enterprise value: \$485,000,000. Outstanding
indebtedness under the Clearfield National Bank term loan: approximately
\$12,000,000. Implied equity value: approximately \$473,000,000. Based
on FY2023 revenue of \$127.3 million and Adjusted EBITDA of \$31.8
million, the implied multiples are approximately 3.81x revenue and
15.25x EBITDA. On a fully diluted basis of approximately 33,900,000
shares, the blended implied per-share equity value is approximately
\$13.95, subject to the Preferred Stock liquidation waterfall.

**Liquidation waterfall.** The total aggregate liquidation preference of
the outstanding Preferred Stock is \$206,400,000 (Series C: 7,800,000 ×
\$18.50 = \$144,300,000; Series B: 5,200,000 × \$9.25 = \$48,100,000;
Series A: 3,500,000 × \$4.00 = \$14,000,000). Because the implied equity
value (\$473 million) substantially exceeds the aggregate liquidation

on an as-converted basis. The Board Minutes (Sept. 25, 2024) confirm
Meridian's preliminary view that participation on an as-converted basis

waterfall is expected to collapse to an as-converted distribution. This
is economically significant for the consent analysis: Stonemill's
economic upside on an as-converted basis (approximately 23% of the

## III. Area 1 --- Change-of-Control Consent Requirements

To consummate the reverse triangular merger, the following consents and
approvals must be obtained. We have mapped each requirement to its
source provision, identified the threshold and the parties whose consent
is required, and assessed the current status.

### A. Board approval (DGCL § 251(b); Bylaws Art. III)

The Board must adopt a resolution approving the merger agreement and the
merger (DGCL § 251(b)). Under Bylaws Section 3.7, a majority of the
total number of directors then in office (4 of 7) constitutes a quorum,
and the act of a majority of directors present at a quorum is the act of
the Board. The Board has authorized management to negotiate definitive
documentation (Board Minutes, Sept. 25, 2024, approved 6-1, Trask
dissenting), but final Board approval of the signed merger agreement has
not yet been obtained. **Status: Not yet obtained (pending definitive
documentation).**

### B. Stockholder approval --- DGCL § 251(c)

DGCL § 251(c) requires the approval of holders of a majority of the
outstanding shares of stock entitled to vote (the Company's Restated
Certificate and the protective provisions cause the Preferred to vote on
an as-converted basis with the Common as a single class on this matter).
This is a majority of the approximately 33,900,000 fully diluted shares,
i.e., more than 16,950,000 shares. **Status: Not yet obtained.** This
vote can be taken by written consent in lieu of a meeting under DGCL §
228 and Restated Certificate Article VI, Section 6.2 / Bylaws Section
2.6.

### C. All-Preferred protective provision --- Restated Certificate § 4.3.6(a)(vi)

So long as any Preferred Stock is outstanding, the Company may not
"effect any Liquidation Event" without the prior written consent or
affirmative vote of holders of at least a **majority (greater than
50%)** of the then-outstanding Preferred Stock, voting together as a
single class on an as-converted basis (Section 4.3.6(a)(vi)). The
Restated Certificate specifies that this requires the affirmative vote
or consent of holders of **more than 8,250,000 shares** of Preferred
(based on 16,500,000 Preferred shares outstanding: 3,500,000 Series A;
5,200,000 Series B; 7,800,000 Series C).

**Can this be satisfied without Stonemill?** Yes. Ridgeline
(3,500,000) + Northbridge (5,200,000) = 8,700,000 shares, which exceeds
the 8,250,000 threshold. Stonemill's 7,800,000 shares represent 47.3% of
the Preferred --- a large block, but not a blocking position on this
particular vote. **Status: Not yet obtained; achievable without
Stonemill.**

### D. Series C separate-class protective provision --- Restated Certificate § 4.3.6(b)(i) --- CRITICAL

So long as any Series C Preferred Stock is outstanding, the Company may
not "effect any Liquidation Event" without the prior written consent or
affirmative vote of holders of at least **66⅔%** of the then-outstanding
Series C Preferred Stock, voting **as a separate class** (Section
4.3.6(b)(i)). This consent is "separate from and in addition to" the
all-Preferred consent under Section 4.3.6(a). The Restated Certificate
specifies that this requires the affirmative vote or consent of holders
of **at least 5,200,000 shares** of Series C (based on 7,800,000 Series
C shares outstanding).

**Stonemill holds 7,800,000 shares of Series C --- i.e., 100% of the
outstanding Series C Preferred Stock.** The 66⅔% threshold (≥5,200,000
shares) therefore cannot be satisfied without Stonemill's affirmative
consent. This is a **hard, charter-level blocking right.** The Board
Minutes (Sept. 25, 2024) confirm that outside counsel Rourke advised the
Board of this requirement and that Julian Trask stated on the record
that "the Series C separate class vote... cannot be satisfied without
Stonemill's affirmative consent." **Status: Not yet obtained; cannot be
obtained without Stonemill.**

### E. Drag-along (Stockholders' Agreement Art. 3) --- conditional

If the drag-along is invoked and its trigger conditions are met, all
Restricted Stockholders (including Stonemill) would be obligated to vote
in favor of the Sale of the Company. Whether the drag-along can be used
to satisfy the Series C separate-class protective provision is the
central legal question addressed in Section IV. **Status: Not yet
invoked.**

### F. DGCL § 251(f) "short-form" alternative --- not available

We considered whether the merger could be structured to avoid a
stockholder vote under DGCL § 251(f) (no stockholder vote required
where, among other things, no amendment to the certificate of
incorporation is made in the merger and either the acquirer owns 90% of
the target or certain other conditions are met). This is not available
here: Hargrove does not currently own any Terravox stock, and the merger
will cancel all outstanding equity for consideration. A stockholder vote
(or written consent) is required.

### G. Other protective provisions implicated

We note for completeness that several other Section 4.3.6(a) protective
provisions are not directly triggered by the merger itself but are
relevant to related actions: Section 4.3.6(a)(i) (charter/bylaw
amendments adversely affecting Preferred --- relevant if the merger
agreement requires a charter amendment); Section 4.3.6(a)(ii)
(increasing/decreasing authorized shares); and Section 4.3.6(a)(vii)
(increasing/decreasing Board size). The merger as structured
(cancellation of all equity for cash) does not require a charter
amendment, so these are not independently triggered, but we should
confirm that the merger agreement does not contemplate any pre-closing
charter amendment that would independently require Preferred consent.

## IV. Area 2 --- Drag-Along Analysis

The Stockholders' Agreement (Article 3) contains drag-along rights that,
if properly triggered, obligate all Restricted Stockholders to vote in
favor of, and participate in, a Sale of the Company. This section (i)
runs the numbers on whether the drag-along can be triggered without
Stonemill, and (ii) addresses the critical legal question of whether the
drag-along can override the charter-level Series C protective provision.

### A. The three-pronged trigger (Section 3.1(a))

A "Drag-Along Approval" requires the satisfaction of **all three** of
the following conditions (Section 3.1(b) confirms that fewer than all
three is insufficient):

1.  **Common majority (as-converted):** holders of at least a majority
    of the outstanding shares of Common Stock, calculated on an
    as-converted basis (treating Preferred as converted) --- i.e., more
    than 16,950,000 of the 33,900,000 fully diluted shares;
2.  **Preferred majority (as-converted, single class):** holders of at
    least a majority of the outstanding Preferred Stock on an
    as-converted basis --- i.e., more than 8,250,000 of the 16,500,000
    Preferred shares; and
3.  **Investor Stockholder approval:** at least two (2) of the three (3)
    Investor Stockholders.

**Prongs (2) and (3) are satisfied without Stonemill.** Prong (1) is the
obstacle: the Founders, Ridgeline, and Northbridge together hold
16,000,000 fully diluted shares (47.2%), which falls just short of the
16,950,000 majority threshold. To satisfy prong (1) without Stonemill,
the Company would need to secure the support of at least approximately
950,000 additional shares from the "various common holders / option
pool" block (10,100,000 shares, \~29.8% of the cap table).

**Assessment.** Prong (1) is *marginally* achievable without Stonemill
but is not guaranteed. The 10,100,000-share block consists of employee
option/RSU holders and other common holders, many of whom are not
parties to the Stockholders' Agreement (the drag-along binds only
"Restricted Stockholders," i.e., parties to the agreement, but the
*trigger threshold* in prong (1) counts all outstanding Common on an
as-converted basis, including non-party holders). In a cash-out merger
at a premium, employee holders would generally be supportive, but (a) a
meaningful portion of outstanding options appear to be out-of-the-money
(see Section IX), reducing the economic incentive for those holders to
vote, and (b) we have not yet confirmed which option/common holders are
parties to the Stockholders' Agreement via joinder. We recommend
confirming the joinder status of the option/common holders and modeling
the realistic vote count for prong (1).

**If prong (1) can be satisfied** (with the support of sufficient
option/common holders), then a Drag-Along Approval can be obtained
without Stonemill's affirmative support, and the drag-along would compel
Stonemill --- as a Restricted Stockholder --- to vote its 7,800,000
Series C shares in favor of the Sale of the Company (Section 3.1(a),
obligations A--D) pursuant to the irrevocable proxy granted in Section
3.3.

### C. Can the drag-along override the Series C separate-class protective provision? --- THE KEY LEGAL QUESTION

This is the decisive question for the Transaction. Even if the
drag-along is triggered without Stonemill and compels Stonemill to vote
its Series C in favor, the question is whether that compelled vote
satisfies the charter's separate-class protective provision in Section
4.3.6(b)(i), which requires the "affirmative vote or written consent" of
66⅔% of the Series C voting as a separate class.

**Textual arguments that the drag-along CAN satisfy the protective
provision:**

1.  **The charter is satisfied by a vote, not by a discretionary veto.**
    Section 4.3.6(b) is satisfied by "the prior written consent or
    affirmative vote" of 66⅔% of the Series C. It does not require a
    *voluntary* or *uncoerced* consent; it requires a vote or consent of
    the requisite threshold. If the drag-along compels Stonemill (100%
    of the Series C) to vote in favor, that is an "affirmative vote" of
    100% of the Series C, which exceeds the 66⅔% threshold. On this
    reading, the charter requirement is *satisfied*, not *waived* or
    *amended* --- the protective provision is given effect through a
    compelled vote.

2.  **Stonemill contractually agreed to be dragged.** By executing the
    Stockholders' Agreement, Stonemill agreed to the drag-along with a
    trigger that expressly permits approval by only two of the three
    Investor Stockholders (Section 3.1(a)(iii)) --- i.e., Stonemill
    agreed to be dragged *without its own affirmative support*, so long
    as the other thresholds are met. The drag-along includes an
    irrevocable proxy "coupled with an interest" (Section 3.3) to vote
    Stonemill's shares "in favor of... any matter required to be
    approved in connection with such Sale of the Company" --- which
    would include the Series C separate-class vote. Article 3 is further
    protected from amendment without Stonemill's consent (Section 7.4),
    reflecting that Stonemill knowingly accepted these terms.

3.  **Delaware enforces stockholder voting agreements.** DGCL § 218(b)
    validates agreements among stockholders as to the manner in which
    they will vote. Delaware courts routinely enforce voting agreements,
    drag-along provisions, and irrevocable proxies coupled with an
    interest as binding contracts.

**Arguments that the drag-along CANNOT override the protective
provision:**

1.  **The protective provision is a charter (class) right of superior
    dignity.** Section 4.3.6(b) is embedded in the certificate of
    incorporation --- the supreme corporate document --- and grants the
    Series C, *as a class*, a separate-class consent right over any
    Liquidation Event. A stockholders' agreement is a contract among
    stockholders; it cannot amend or override the certificate of
    incorporation except through the charter's own amendment procedures
    (which themselves require Series C consent under Section
    4.3.6(b)(ii)). Allowing a drag-along to compel the sole Series C
    holder to vote away its class veto arguably defeats the protective
    provision's purpose and effectively amends the charter by contract
    --- something Delaware law does not permit.

2.  **The protective provision protects the class, not just the
    holder.** The Series C protective provision is framed as a *class*
    right (voting "as a separate class"). A drag-along that compels the
    only class member to vote in favor arguably eliminates the class
    protection that the charter was specifically negotiated to provide.
    A court could view this as an impermissible circumvention of the
    charter's class-voting structure.

3.  **Uncertainty and litigation risk.** There is no Delaware Supreme
    Court decision squarely holding that a stockholders'-agreement
    drag-along can compel a vote that satisfies a charter-level
    separate-class protective provision over the objecting holder's
    opposition. While the textual arguments above are colorable, the
    question is genuinely unsettled, and Stonemill would have a credible
    basis to challenge enforcement.

**Our assessment.** This is a close and unsettled question of Delaware
law. The textual argument that the drag-along can compel a vote that
*satisfies* (rather than *overrides*) the protective provision is
reasonably strong, particularly given that Stonemill expressly agreed to
a 2-of-3-investor trigger and granted an irrevocable proxy. However, we
cannot advise the client with confidence that a Delaware court would
enforce the drag-along to override Stonemill's separate-class veto over
its objection. Stonemill would almost certainly litigate, seeking
injunctive relief to prevent closing on the basis that the Series C
separate-class consent was not *validly* obtained. Even if Hargrove
ultimately prevailed, the litigation would introduce material closing
delay, cost, and deal uncertainty --- outcomes that are anathema to a
private-equity buyer with a fixed fund life and a Q1 2025 target close.

**Recommendation.** Treat Stonemill's affirmative consent as the primary
and preferred path to closing. Preserve the drag-along as a negotiating
lever and a fallback: the existence of a *credible* (if contested)
alternative path to closing is itself valuable in negotiations, because
it shifts Stonemill's calculus from "I can block" toward "I may be
dragged unless I negotiate." We recommend obtaining a focused
Delaware-law opinion (or at minimum a thorough memorandum) on the
drag-along/protective-provision interplay before relying on the
drag-along in negotiations or as a closing mechanism. We also recommend
that the merger agreement be drafted to permit closing upon either (a)
Stonemill's affirmative Series C consent, or (b) a court-enforced
drag-along, with appropriate closing-condition drafting.

### D. Drag-along terms affecting consideration (Section 3.2)

If the drag-along is invoked, Section 3.2(a) provides that each
Restricted Stockholder receives the same form and amount of
consideration per share (as-converted), *except* that liquidation
preferences apply and consideration is distributed "in accordance with
the liquidation waterfall provisions of the Restated Certificate as if
the Sale of the Company were a Liquidation Event." This means the
drag-along does *not* override the liquidation waterfall --- Preferred
holders still receive their preference-ordered distribution. Given that
all Preferred is in the money on an as-converted basis, this is
consistent with an as-converted distribution. Section 3.2(d) provides
that no dragged stockholder can be required to give non-competes or to
indemnify in excess of consideration received --- useful protections
that should be reflected in the merger agreement's stockholder
representative / indemnity provisions.

## V. Area 3 --- Impediments to Closing a Reverse Triangular Merger

### A. Does the "Liquidation Event" definition capture a reverse triangular merger?

Yes, unambiguously. Section 4.3.2(d)(i) of the Restated Certificate
defines a "Liquidation Event" to include "any merger, consolidation, or
other business combination of the Corporation with or into another
entity (whether or not the Corporation is the surviving entity), other
than a merger... in which the stockholders of the Corporation
immediately prior to such transaction continue to hold at least fifty
percent (50%) of the voting power of the surviving or resulting entity
immediately following such transaction."

In the reverse triangular merger, Merger Sub merges into Terravox and
Terravox survives --- but as a wholly-owned subsidiary of Hargrove.
Immediately following the merger, Terravox's former stockholders hold
**0%** of the voting power of the surviving entity (Hargrove, through
Merger Sub, owns 100%). The 50% continuation exception therefore does
**not** apply, and the merger is a Liquidation Event under Section
4.3.2(d)(i). It is independently captured by Section 4.3.2(d)(iii) (a
transaction resulting in the transfer of more than 50% of the Company's
voting power to any person), since Hargrove will hold 100% post-closing.

The "Sale of the Company" definitions in the Stockholders' Agreement
(Section 1.1) and the Voting Agreement (Section 1) likewise capture the
merger: both define a Sale of the Company to include any merger in which
the Company's stockholders do not retain a majority of the voting power
of the surviving or resulting entity, and any transaction transferring
more than 50% of the outstanding voting power. The reverse triangular
merger satisfies both. **No structural ambiguity exists as to whether
the merger triggers the Liquidation Event / Sale of the Company
definitions.**

### B. Structural features and advantages of the reverse triangular merger

The reverse triangular merger structure is generally favorable for this
Transaction. Because Terravox survives as the legal entity (rather than
disappearing into Merger Sub), the Company's contracts, licenses,
permits, and regulatory approvals remain in place at the surviving
entity without requiring consents or novations (subject, of course, to
any change-of-control provisions in those contracts --- a separate
diligence workstream). This is the principal advantage of the reverse
triangular structure over a forward merger. The structure does not, by
itself, create any governance impediment beyond the consent requirements
analyzed in Section III.

### C. The one structural ambiguity: the RRA's "Deemed Liquidation Event"

The RRA terminates upon "a Deemed Liquidation Event" (Section 9(c)), but
the RRA does **not define** "Deemed Liquidation Event" and does not
cross-reference the Restated Certificate's "Liquidation Event"
definition. This is the one structural/definitional ambiguity affecting
the Transaction and is addressed in detail in Sections VIII and XII. In
summary: the merger almost certainly *is* intended to be a "Deemed
Liquidation Event" (the term is conventionally used to mean a
change-of-control transaction treated as a liquidation for preferred
purposes), but the RRA's failure to define or cross-reference the term
creates a non-trivial risk that registration rights could be argued to
survive the merger. This should be cured by an express
termination/waiver of the RRA at closing.

### D. Indebtedness and change-of-control provisions

The \$12 million Clearfield National Bank term loan is secured by
substantially all of the Company's assets. The loan agreement almost
certainly contains change-of-control and/or default-on-acceleration
provisions that will be triggered by the merger. Repayment or
refinancing of this debt at or before closing is a condition to the
Transaction (the \$485 million enterprise value reflects \$12 million of

## VI. Area 4 --- Transfer Restrictions

### A. ROFR and co-sale mechanics (Stockholders' Agreement Art. 2)

The Stockholders' Agreement imposes a general restriction on transfers
(Section 2.1): no Restricted Stockholder may transfer Shares except (a)
in compliance with Article 2, (b) pursuant to a Permitted Transfer
(Section 2.7), or (c) in connection with a Sale of the Company
consummated in accordance with Article 3. The right of first refusal
(Section 2.2) gives the Company 30 days to purchase shares subject to a
bona fide third-party offer, followed by a 20-day stockholder ROFR
period. The co-sale right (Section 2.3) allows Investor Stockholders to
participate in a proposed transfer if the Company and other stockholders
do not purchase all the shares. Tag-along rights (Section 2.4) apply to
Founder transfers of more than 5% of the outstanding shares.

### B. Does the merger trigger ROFR or co-sale?

**In principle, no --- but the carve-out is conditional, and the
condition must be carefully managed.** Transfers "in connection with a
Sale of the Company" are exempted from the transfer restrictions in two
places: Section 2.1(c) (a general carve-out) and Section 2.7(e) (a
Permitted Transfer). **However, both carve-outs are expressly
conditioned on the Sale of the Company being "consummated in accordance
with Article 3"** --- i.e., the drag-along article.

This creates a potential trap. If the Transaction is approved and
consummated by stockholder consent *without* formally invoking the
drag-along (for example, if the Company simply solicits the DGCL § 251
vote and the protective-provision consents directly, without a
Drag-Along Approval), then a litigant could argue that the Sale was not
"consummated in accordance with Article 3" and that the ROFR/co-sale
carve-outs do not apply. While the better view is that a company-wide
merger for uniform consideration is not the kind of individual
stockholder sale that the ROFR/co-sale provisions were designed to
govern, the conditional language ("consummated in accordance with
Article 3") creates arguable ambiguity.

**Recommendation.** Structure the Transaction so that the Sale of the
Company is expressly effected "in accordance with Article 3" of the
Stockholders' Agreement --- i.e., obtain a Drag-Along Approval and
invoke the drag-along as the mechanism for the stockholder vote. This
cleanly brings the Transaction within the Section 2.1(c)/2.7(e)
carve-outs and eliminates ROFR/co-sale ambiguity. As a
belt-and-suspenders measure, obtain written ROFR/co-sale waivers from
all Restricted Stockholders at signing or closing. Note that invoking
the drag-along for this purpose is consistent with --- and does not
conflict with --- the analysis in Section IV; the drag-along can be
invoked to secure the stockholder vote and the ROFR/co-sale carve-out
even if the Series C protective provision is separately satisfied by
Stonemill's affirmative consent.

### C. Termination of the Stockholders' Agreement

Section 6.1(b) provides that the Stockholders' Agreement terminates upon
"a Sale of the Company (as defined in Section 1.1)." The merger is a
Sale of the Company. Accordingly, the Stockholders' Agreement terminates
at closing, and upon termination all transfer restrictions, ROFR,
co-sale, tag-along, and drag-along rights cease (Section 6.3), and the
Company must remove the related legends. **Status: Will terminate at
closing, provided the merger is a Sale of the Company (which it is).**
The survival of Article 7 (Miscellaneous) and pre-termination breach
liability (Section 6.2) should be noted but do not impede closing.

## VIII. Area 6 --- Cross-Document Definitional Inconsistencies

We compared the change-of-control defined terms across the five
governance documents. The principal findings are below.

### A. "Liquidation Event" (Restated Certificate § 4.3.2(d)) vs. "Sale of the Company" (Stockholders' Agreement § 1.1; Voting Agreement § 1)

These terms are **substantially consistent** in capturing the reverse
triangular merger, but differ in two respects:

1.  **Threshold for the merger continuation exception.** The Restated
    Certificate's "Liquidation Event" (Section 4.3.2(d)(i)) excludes a
    merger in which the Company's stockholders continue to hold "**at
    least fifty percent (50%)**" of the voting power --- i.e., a merger
    in which stockholders retain exactly 50% is *not* a Liquidation
    Event. The "Sale of the Company" definitions in the Stockholders'
    Agreement and Voting Agreement exclude a merger in which
    stockholders "do not retain a **majority**" of the voting power ---
    i.e., a merger in which stockholders retain exactly 50% *is* a Sale
    of the Company (because 50% is not a majority). This is a subtle
    inconsistency that could produce different outcomes in the rare case
    of a merger in which stockholders retain exactly 50%. **It does not
    affect the Hargrove Transaction** (stockholders retain 0%), but it
    should be noted and, if practicable, conformed in any post-closing
    cleanup.

2.  **Scope.** The "Liquidation Event" definition includes a fourth
    sub-part (Section 4.3.2(d)(iv): voluntary or involuntary
    liquidation, dissolution, or winding up) that the "Sale of the
    Company" definitions do not expressly include. This is a minor scope
    difference; a liquidation/dissolution would not typically be a "Sale
    of the Company" but would be a "Liquidation Event." Again, not
    relevant to the merger.

### B. "Deemed Liquidation Event" (RRA § 9(c)) --- UNDEFINED --- Significant

The RRA terminates upon "a Deemed Liquidation Event" (Section 9(c)) but
**does not define "Deemed Liquidation Event"** anywhere in the RRA and
does not cross-reference the Restated Certificate's "Liquidation Event."
This is the most significant definitional gap in the document set.

-   **Likely intent.** "Deemed Liquidation Event" is a conventional VC
    term that refers to a change-of-control transaction (merger, asset
    sale, etc.) treated as a liquidation for preferred-stock purposes
    --- i.e., functionally identical to the Restated Certificate's
    "Liquidation Event." The parties almost certainly intended the RRA
    to terminate upon the same change-of-control events that trigger the
    charter's Liquidation Event and the other agreements' Sale of the
    Company.
-   **The risk.** Because the term is undefined, a holder could argue
    that the RRA's termination trigger is ambiguous and that
    registration rights survive the merger. In an all-cash merger, the
    practical impact is limited because post-closing there are no
    "Registrable Securities" outstanding (the Preferred is cancelled for
    cash; no Common is issued upon conversion), so the registration
    rights would be largely moot. **However**, the definitional gap (i)
    creates avoidable ambiguity, (ii) would become material if the deal
    structure ever included stock consideration, and (iii) leaves the
    RRA's indemnification provisions (Section 7, which survive
    termination under Section 9) technically in play.
-   **Recommendation.** Do not rely on the undefined "Deemed Liquidation
    Event" alone. The merger agreement should include an express
    termination of the RRA at closing and/or the Company and Holders of
    a majority of the Registrable Securities should execute a written
    termination under Section 9 (second clause) as a belt-and-suspenders
    measure. See Section XII.

### C. Referenced but unproduced agreements --- potential additional definitional sources

The Board Minutes' introductory paragraph references four governance
agreements executed as of November 3, 2021: "the Investors' Rights
Agreement, the Voting Agreement, the Right of First Refusal and Co-Sale
Agreement, and the Stockholders' Agreement." We have received the
Stockholders' Agreement, the Voting Agreement, and the RRA --- but the
Minutes reference an "Investors' Rights Agreement" and a separate "Right
of First Refusal and Co-Sale Agreement" as *distinct* from the
Stockholders' Agreement.

This raises a document-integrity question: either (a) the Company
executed separate Investors' Rights and ROFR/Co-Sale agreements that we
have not received (in which case there may be additional consent rights,
transfer restrictions, or information rights not captured in the
documents we reviewed), or (b) the Stockholders' Agreement we received
is a combined instrument that subsumes the ROFR/co-sale and
information-rights provisions (and the RRA subsumes the
registration-rights portion of what would conventionally be an
Investors' Rights Agreement), and the Minutes use imprecise terminology.
**This must be confirmed with Avery, Pollard & Glenn.** If separate
agreements exist, they must be produced and reviewed, as they could

### C. Economic observation --- out-of-the-money options (Moderate)

The implied per-share equity value (\~\$13.95, blended) is *below* the

## XI. Area 9 --- Fiduciary Duty / Sale Process Concerns

### A. Special Committee composition --- independence concern (Significant)

The Special Committee was formed on April 12, 2023, and consists of

    Northbridge has a substantial economic interest in the Transaction

-   Under Delaware law, a special committee formed to evaluate a
    sale-of-control transaction should be composed of **disinterested
    and independent** directors in order to provide the benefits of a
    special committee (e.g., shifting the burden of proof under *entire
    fairness* review, or supporting business-judgment deference in

    director (Okafor) on the Special Committee **undermines the
    committee's independence** and could deprive the Company of the
    procedural protections a special committee is meant to provide.

**Vulnerability.** Stonemill (through Trask) is actively looking for
ammunition to challenge the Transaction. A Special Committee that
includes a Northbridge designee with a direct economic stake is a clear
target. Stonemill could argue that the sale process was tainted by the
participation of an interested director whose designator (Northbridge)
stands to receive significant merger proceeds, and that the committee's
recommendation therefore does not carry the weight of an independent
committee. This is particularly potent given that Northbridge's Series B
Director voted *in favor* of the Hargrove engagement and exclusivity at
every step.

**Recommendation.** **Reconstitute the Special Committee to exclude
interested directors.** Remove Okafor from the Special Committee,
leaving the two independent directors (Lau and Garrett). If a
three-member committee is desired for quorum/robustness, add a new
independent director (subject to the Voting Agreement's
board-composition mechanics --- note that adding a director may require
Investor consent under Voting Agreement Section 2.1 and the charter's
Section 4.3.6(a)(vii) Board-size protective provision; alternatively,
the committee can operate with two independent members). The
reconstituted committee should ratify its prior actions and issue a
fresh recommendation on the definitive merger agreement, supported by
the Thornbury & Associates fairness analysis. This materially
strengthens the sale process's defensibility.

### C. Overall sale-process assessment

financial advisor, formation of a special committee, engagement of a

## XII. Area 10 --- Registration Rights Post-Transaction

### A. RRA termination provisions (Section 9)

Registration rights terminate with respect to a particular Holder upon
the earliest of: (a) the Holder being able to sell all its Registrable
Securities in a single three-month period under Rule 144 without
volume/manner-of-sale restrictions and without the
current-public-information requirement; (b) the fifth anniversary of the
IPO closing; or (c) **"a Deemed Liquidation Event."** The Agreement
terminates in its entirety upon the earliest of (i) termination as to
all Holders under (a)/(b)/(c), or (ii) the written consent of the
Company and Holders of a majority of the then-outstanding Registrable
Securities.

### B. Does the merger terminate the RRA?

**Probably yes, but not cleanly.** The merger is intended to be a
"Deemed Liquidation Event," which would trigger termination under
Section 9(c). However, as detailed in Section VIII.B, **"Deemed
Liquidation Event" is not defined in the RRA** and is not
cross-referenced to the Restated Certificate's "Liquidation Event." The
term is conventionally understood to mean a change-of-control
transaction treated as a liquidation for preferred purposes, and the
parties almost certainly intended the RRA to terminate on the same
events as the charter's Liquidation Event. On that reading, the merger
terminates the RRA.

**Residual risk.** In an all-cash merger, the practical consequence of
the RRA surviving is limited because post-closing there are no
"Registrable Securities" outstanding (the Preferred is cancelled for
cash; no Common is issued upon conversion; the former Holders hold no
Company securities). The demand, piggyback, and S-3 registration rights
would therefore be largely moot. **However:**

1.  The RRA's **indemnification provisions (Section 7) expressly survive
    termination** (Section 9). While indemnification relates to
    registration statements (which will not exist post-closing in an
    all-cash deal), the survival creates theoretical post-closing
    exposure.
2.  If the deal structure ever included **stock consideration** (e.g.,
    Hargrove equity issued to former Terravox holders), the undefined
    "Deemed Liquidation Event" gap could leave registration rights alive
    with respect to the stock consideration --- a material post-closing
    liability.
3.  The ambiguity itself is a diligence finding that should be cured.

### C. Demand registration timing note

We note that demand registration rights under Section 2.1(a) became
exercisable on **November 3, 2024** (three years after the RRA date),
upon request of Holders of at least 30% of the then-outstanding
Registrable Securities (the "Initiating Holders," threshold 4,950,000
shares per Schedule A). Stonemill alone holds 7,800,000 Registrable
Securities (as-converted) and could satisfy the 30% Initiating Holder
threshold by itself. While a demand registration is unlikely to be
pursued in the midst of an acquisition (and would be moot post-closing
in an all-cash deal), Stonemill's ability to demand registration is an
additional (minor) lever and reinforces the importance of cleanly
terminating the RRA at closing.

### D. Recommendation

1.  **Express termination at closing.** The merger agreement should
    include a covenant requiring the Company to terminate the RRA at
    closing, and the RRA should be terminated by written consent of the
    Company and Holders of a majority of the Registrable Securities
    under Section 9 (second clause) --- which can be obtained from the
    same Holders whose consents are being solicited for the merger. This
    eliminates reliance on the undefined "Deemed Liquidation Event."
2.  **Holder waivers/terminations.** Obtain individual written
    confirmations from each Holder (Ridgeline, Northbridge, Stonemill)
    acknowledging termination of the RRA in connection with the merger.
3.  **Disclosure.** Disclose the RRA and its (cured) termination in the
    merger agreement disclosure schedules.

### C. The available paths to closing

1.  **Path 1 --- Stonemill's affirmative consent (preferred).** Obtain
    Stonemill's affirmative Series C separate-class consent (satisfying
    § 4.3.6(b)(i)) along with the all-Preferred consent, the DGCL § 251
    stockholder vote, and Board approval. This is the clean,
    litigation-free path. It likely requires negotiating with Stonemill
    --- potentially on consideration allocation, side arrangements, or
    post-closing management/consulting rights --- but it eliminates
    closing risk.

2.  **Path 2 --- Drag-along override (contested fallback).** Trigger the
    drag-along without Stonemill (satisfying prongs (2) and (3) via
    Ridgeline + Northbridge, and prong (1) with sufficient
    option/common-holder support), then compel Stonemill's Series C vote
    via the irrevocable proxy to satisfy § 4.3.6(b)(i). This path is
    *legally uncertain* (Section IV.C) and would likely trigger
    Stonemill litigation seeking to enjoin closing. It is a negotiating
    lever and a last-resort mechanism, not a primary plan.

3.  **Path 3 --- Charter amendment (not viable).** Amending the charter
    to eliminate the Series C veto would itself require Series C consent
    under § 4.3.6(b)(ii) --- the very consent Stonemill can withhold.
    This is circular and not a viable workaround.

**Strategic recommendation.** Pursue Path 1 as the primary strategy
while developing Path 2 as leverage. The credible existence of Path 2
(the drag-along) is itself a negotiating asset: it shifts Stonemill from
a pure blocking posture toward a negotiated settlement, because
Stonemill must weigh the risk of being dragged (and litigating, with
uncertain outcome and cost) against the certainty of a negotiated deal.
We recommend obtaining a Delaware-law opinion on the Path 2 viability
before deploying it in negotiations, and structuring the merger
agreement to permit closing on either path.

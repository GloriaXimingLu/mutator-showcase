## I. INTRODUCTION AND PURPOSE

You have asked Kesswick, Tanaka & Moreau LLP ("KTM") to prepare proposed
compromise language for the four contested provisions of the
Intercreditor Agreement, dated as of July 31, 2025 (the "ICA"), among
Cartwright National Bank, N.A. ("Cartwright") as First Lien Agent,
Ridgeline Capital Partners, LP ("Ridgeline") as Second Lien Agent, and
Meridian as Borrower. The four contested provisions are:

1.  **Section 3.01(b) --- Standstill Period;**
2.  **Section 5.01 --- Second Lien Purchase Right (Purchase Price);**
3.  **Section 2.03(c) --- Permitted Second Lien Refinancing
    Indebtedness;** and
4.  **Section 4.02 --- Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty
    Proceeds.**

As you directed, the four proposals are presented below as a **single,
integrated, and indivisible package**. The concessions on each provision
are deliberately linked to, and offset by, concessions on the others.
The package is calibrated to the median market practice reflected in
KTM's survey of 42 first lien / second lien intercreditor agreements
closed between January 2022 and December 2024 (the "KTM Market Survey"
or "Survey"), to the structural economics of the Meridian capital
structure, and to the documented flexibility of each side as expressed
in the position letters of Aldgate & Pryor LLP (on behalf of Cartwright)
and Sternfeld Yates & Carroll LLP (on behalf of Ridgeline) and the
informal, without-prejudice guidance subsequently provided by each
side's counsel. The package is designed to be credible and closeable
within the time remaining before the July 31, 2025 drop-dead date.

For each provision, this memorandum sets forth (A) the open issues and
the respective party positions, (B) the proposed compromise language in
full contractual form ready for insertion into the draft ICA, and (C) a
negotiation rationale explaining why the compromise falls within each
side's documented flexibility, citing the KTM Market Survey and
quantifying the economic impacts where possible.

Two features of this baseline drive the analysis. **First**, the Total
LTV of 97.66% means the aggregate secured debt nearly equals enterprise
value, leaving a thin 25.78% second lien equity cushion. This supports
Cartwright's insistence on structural protections (a meaningful
standstill, a par purchase right, a hard sweep on large asset sales, and
refinancing discipline) and simultaneously supports Ridgeline's
insistence that those protections not be so absolute as to convert lien
subordination into permanent payment subordination or to render the
second lien position economically unviable. **Second**, both facilities
are floating-rate, so interest continues to accrue during any standstill
at above-market rates (the 2L all-in rate of 11.58% generates
approximately \$17.37M of annual interest on \$150M, or roughly \$4.34M
per 90-day quarter). This time-value feature, which Cartwright itself
has invoked, materially mitigates the cost to Ridgeline of a longer
standstill and is relevant to the standstill compromise below.

## IV. GUIDING PRINCIPLES FOR THE COMPROMISE PACKAGE

KTM has applied five principles in calibrating the package:

1.  **Market median as the anchor.** Where the Survey shows a clear
    median or majority practice, the compromise tracks it. Deviating
    from market median in a thinly-cushioned, time-constrained
    transaction invites re-trading and re-approval cycles that the
    calendar will not tolerate.

2.  **Respect each side's documented hard constraints.** Cartwright's
    credit committee has set a 150-day standstill floor and has declared
    par for funded debt non-negotiable. Ridgeline's fund limited
    partnership agreement ("LPA") imposes structural constraints that
    prevent it from assuming open-ended revolving credit commitments.
    These are not positional bargaining points; they are institutional
    limits. The package respects each.

3.  **Pair each side's gain with a corresponding concession.** The
    package is balanced: every provision in which one side receives its
    opening or near-opening position is offset by a provision in which
    that same side moves meaningfully toward the other.

4.  **Quantify the economics.** Where a compromise has a measurable
    dollar impact, the memorandum quantifies it so that each side's
    credit committee and investment committee can evaluate the trade-off
    concretely rather than abstractly.

5.  **Closeability.** The package is designed to be approvable by
    Cartwright's seven-lender syndicate and Ridgeline's three-lender
    group without re-syndication or re-pricing, and to permit definitive
    documentation, internal approvals, and execution on or before July
    31, 2025.

## VI. PROVISION 1 --- STANDSTILL PERIOD (SECTION 3.01(b))

### A. Open Issues and Party Positions

The standstill is the widest gap in the negotiation. The draft ICA
brackets three open points:

-   **(A) Duration.** Cartwright proposes 270 days; Ridgeline proposes
    90 days.
-   **(B) Early Termination.** Cartwright proposes no early-termination
    triggers under any circumstance; Ridgeline proposes early
    termination upon (i) commencement of an enforcement action by the
    First Lien Agent against material Collateral, or (ii) the filing of
    an Insolvency Proceeding by or against the Borrower.
-   **(C) Blockage Notice Cap.** Neither party's bracketed proposal
    includes a cap on the number of Blockage Notices (and thus
    standstill periods) that may be imposed.

**Documented flexibility.** Cartwright's counsel has advised, on a
without-prejudice basis, that Cartwright's credit committee has approved
a **minimum standstill of 150 days** as a hard floor, that the syndicate
"could live with something in the 150- to 180-day range," and that "if a
longer standstill --- closer to 180 days --- were paired with carefully
defined early termination triggers, that is something the committee
would be willing to consider," provided the triggers are "narrow and
objectively verifiable." Ridgeline's counsel has advised that Ridgeline
"can accept up to a **180-day standstill**, but only if the ICA includes
**meaningful early termination triggers**," describing this as "a firm
condition and not something we can decouple from the duration," and
identifying candidate triggers including cessation of active enforcement
activity, disposition of substantially all of the Collateral, and filing
of a plan of reorganization that impairs 2L claims without 2L consent.

### B. Proposed Compromise Language

The following language replaces the bracketed text in Section 3.01(b) of
the draft ICA and adds a new Section 3.01(c):

> Agent shall not exercise any rights or remedies with respect to the
> Collateral for a period of one hundred eighty (180) days following
> delivery by the First Lien Agent to the Second Lien Agent of a written
> notice (a "Blockage Notice") specifying in reasonable detail the
> Second Lien Default giving rise thereto (the "Standstill Period"). The
> Standstill Period shall be measured from the date of actual receipt of
> the Blockage Notice by the Second Lien Agent. The Standstill Period
> shall remain in effect for the full one hundred eighty (180)-day

> Section 3.01(c). There shall be no more than two (2) Blockage Notices

### C. Negotiation Rationale

**Duration --- 180 days.** The 180-day duration is the **median** of the
42-deal KTM Market Survey (mean 172.9 days; 25th percentile 150 days;
75th percentile 202.5 days; range 90--270 days). It falls **above
Cartwright's hard floor of 150 days** and **at the top of Ridgeline's
documented flexibility of "up to 180 days."** It is therefore within the
zone of agreement that both sides' counsel have independently
identified. A 180-day period also falls within the six-to-nine-month
window that Cartwright itself has cited as the minimum reasonably
necessary to pursue a comprehensive restructuring, orderly asset
disposition, or refinancing of the First Lien Facility, and it is 90
days shorter than Cartwright's 270-day opening --- a meaningful
concession by Cartwright that should be creditable to Ridgeline.

**Economic impact of duration.** Because both facilities are
floating-rate, the cost to Ridgeline of a longer standstill is
substantially mitigated by the continued accrual of interest on the
Second Lien Obligations at the 2L all-in rate of 11.58%. On
\$150,000,000, interest accrues at approximately **\$4.34 million per
90-day quarter** (or approximately \$17.37 million per annum). Relative
to Ridgeline's 90-day opening, a 180-day standstill entails an
additional 90 days of accrued 2L interest of approximately **\$4.34
million**, which accrues to the benefit of the Second Lien Lenders and
is recoverable (subject to the priority waterfall) upon resolution.
Relative to Cartwright's 270-day opening, the 180-day compromise
**shortens** the standstill by 90 days, reducing the period during which
Ridgeline is foreclosed from acting. The floating-rate structure ---
which Cartwright has itself invoked as a reason a longer standstill is
tolerable to the Second Lien Lenders --- thus supports a duration at the
market median rather than at either extreme.

**Early termination triggers --- narrow and objectively verifiable.**
The four triggers are drawn directly from the most common triggers
observed in the Survey: liquidation commenced (29 of 42 deals), credit
bid by the 1L (22 of 42), DIP financing approved (13 of 42), and sale
motion filed without 2L consent (9 of 42). **Seventy-six percent (76%)
of surveyed deals include early-termination triggers**, and ---
critically --- **25 of 42 deals (60%) pair a standstill of 180 days or
longer with early-termination triggers**, which is the precise
combination proposed here. This is the market-standard pairing, not an
outlier formulation.

The triggers are deliberately **narrow and objectively verifiable**, as
Cartwright's counsel required. Each is triggered by a discrete,
externally confirmable event (a liquidation proceeding, a credit bid, a
Section 363 sale motion, or a DIP/cash-collateral order entered over 2L
objection) rather than by a subjective assessment of collateral value or
enforcement activity. The package **excludes** the trigger that
Cartwright most forcefully objected to in its position letter --- early
termination upon the standalone filing of an involuntary bankruptcy
petition --- while still protecting Ridgeline in the bankruptcy
scenarios that most directly threaten its position (a free-and-clear
sale without its consent, or a DIP financing that subordinates its lien
over its objection). The "cessation of active enforcement activity"
trigger that Ridgeline's counsel floated is also excluded as
insufficiently objective and susceptible to dispute. The four retained
triggers give Ridgeline the "meaningful early termination triggers" it
has made a firm condition of accepting 180 days, while preserving for
Cartwright a standstill that is absolute in the ordinary distress
scenario and breachable only upon objectively verifiable,
value-destructive events.

**Blockage Notice cap --- 2 periods / 360 days per rolling 12 months.**
Neither party's bracketed proposal addressed the absence of a cap on
Blockage Notices, which the draft ICA's drafting note flagged as an open
point. An uncapped blockage right would permit the First Lien Agent, by
delivering successive Blockage Notices as each standstill expires, to
extend the standstill indefinitely and convert lien subordination into
permanent payment subordination --- the precise outcome Ridgeline has
warned against. The proposed cap of two Standstill Periods (360 days
aggregate) per rolling 12-month period gives Cartwright up to a **full
year of aggregate standstill protection** in any 12-month window ---
substantially more than a single 180-day period and more than sufficient
to pursue a restructuring or orderly disposition --- while foreclosing
indefinite tactical use. This is a 2L-protective element that is the
natural complement to the 1L-protective 180-day duration, and it is
offered as part of the indivisible package.

**Why this falls within each side's flexibility.** For Cartwright: 180
days exceeds the 150-day hard floor and is paired with triggers that are
narrow, objectively verifiable, and drawn from market precedent --- the
precise formulation Cartwright's counsel said the committee "would be
willing to consider." For Ridgeline: 180 days is the maximum Ridgeline's
counsel said Ridgeline "can accept," and it is conditioned on the
meaningful early-termination triggers that Ridgeline's counsel made a
"firm condition." The Blockage Notice cap addresses Ridgeline's concern
about indefinite tactical blockage. Both sides move from their openings
toward the market median, and each receives a structural feature (longer
duration for Cartwright; triggers and a cap for Ridgeline) that its
counsel has identified as acceptable.

## VII. PROVISION 2 --- SECOND LIEN PURCHASE RIGHT (SECTION 5.01)

### A. Open Issues and Party Positions

The draft ICA brackets four open points on the purchase right:

-   **(A) Purchase Price.** Cartwright proposes par (100% of principal
    plus default-rate accrued interest plus fees); Ridgeline proposes
    the lesser of par (at the non-default contract rate) or fair market
    value determined by an independent dealer poll.
-   **(B) Interest Rate for Accrued Interest.** Cartwright proposes the
    default rate; Ridgeline proposes the non-default contract rate.
-   **(C) Unfunded Revolver Assumption.** Cartwright proposes mandatory
    assumption of all unfunded commitments under the \$125M First Lien
    Revolver; Ridgeline proposes no assumption, with the revolver
    commitments terminating upon consummation of the purchase.
-   **(D) Scope of Exercise Triggers.** Cartwright proposes exercise
    only upon an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement;
    Ridgeline proposes broader triggers (a 1L Event of Default, a 2L
    Event of Default, or delivery of a notice of acceleration of the
    First Lien Obligations).

**Documented flexibility.** Cartwright's counsel has stated that **"par
purchase price for funded debt is non-negotiable"** and that the
purchase price must include 100% of outstanding principal on all funded
1L obligations plus accrued interest plus fees and expenses. Cartwright
has, however, signaled room on two subsidiary points: (i) accrued
interest "calculated at the non-default contract rate" is something "the
committee could potentially accept"; and (ii) on unfunded revolver
assumption, "there may be room to soften this requirement, potentially
making assumption of unfunded Revolver commitments optional rather than
mandatory." Ridgeline's counsel has stated that Ridgeline
**"acknowledges that par for funded debt may be acceptable"** as the
Purchase Price, but that this acceptance is **"conditioned on the
unfunded revolver assumption being optional, not mandatory,"**
explaining that Ridgeline's LPA contains structural constraints that
"prevent the fund from taking on open-ended revolving credit commitment
obligations" and that mandatory assumption "is a deal-breaker given the
LPA constraints." Ridgeline has also expressed willingness to discuss
accrued interest at the non-default contract rate.

the draft ICA:

> the purchase, all unfunded commitments under the First Lien Revolver
> shall terminate and the First Lien Revolver shall be permanently
> closed; provided that the First Lien Agent shall use commercially
> reasonable efforts to facilitate the orderly wind-down of any
> outstanding letters of credit issued under the First Lien Revolver as
> of the date of purchase, including through cash collateralization at
> 103% of the undrawn face amount or the issuance of replacement letters
> of credit by the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) or another
> financial institution reasonably acceptable to the First Lien Agent.

### C. Negotiation Rationale

**Par for funded debt --- non-negotiable, accepted.** The compromise
adopts par (100% of outstanding funded principal) as the Purchase Price,
satisfying Cartwright's declared non-negotiable. Ridgeline has
acknowledged that par for funded debt "may be acceptable," and the
Survey confirms that **71% of deals use a par mechanic** (with 19%
hybrid and only 10% pure FMV). The pure-FMV dealer-poll formulation that
Ridgeline opened with appears in only 4 of 42 surveyed deals (10%), each
involving a single-CLO second lien holder or a non-sponsor media/energy
credit --- contexts unlike Meridian's three-lender second lien group.
Dropping the FMV alternative is therefore a concession by Ridgeline that
is well-supported by market practice and that removes a valuation
mechanism Cartwright has resisted as introducing uncertainty and
below-par transfer risk.

**Non-default contract rate for accrued interest.** The compromise
calculates accrued interest at the **non-default contract rate** (SOFR +
250 bps; all-in 7.83% at current SOFR) rather than the default rate
(SOFR + 450 bps; all-in 9.83%). This is the **market majority**: **69%
of surveyed deals exclude default-rate interest** from the par
calculation, and **90% include the non-default contract rate**. Deal-007
(the Meridian-comparable) uses par at the non-default contract rate.
Cartwright's counsel has expressly signaled that the non-default rate
"could potentially" be accepted by the committee.

**Economic impact of the rate choice.** On the full \$475,000,000 First
Lien Facility at full draw, the difference between the default rate and
the non-default rate is 200 basis points, or approximately **\$9.5
million per annum**. On a 90-day accrual (the illustrative period used
by both sides), the default-rate increment is approximately **\$2.375
million** (\$475M × 2.00% × 90/360); on a 180-day accrual, approximately
**\$4.75 million**; on a 270-day accrual (Cartwright's proposed
standstill), approximately **\$7.125 million**. Using the non-default
rate, the illustrative par Purchase Price for funded debt on a 90-day
accrual is approximately **\$484.30 million** (\$475M principal +
\$9.30M accrued interest), consistent with the figure both sides'
counsel have independently cited and with Deal-007's illustrative par
price of \$484.3M. The non-default rate thus preserves par recovery of
principal and contract interest for the First Lien Lenders --- the core
of what they underwrote --- while avoiding the transfer of the
default-rate spread (a punitive, post-default increment) to the
purchaser, which the market majority treats as outside the par purchase
price.

**Optional (not mandatory) unfunded revolver assumption.** The
compromise makes assumption of the \$125,000,000 unfunded revolver
commitment **optional at the Second Lien Agent's election**, with the
revolver commitments terminating if the election is not made. This is
the **clear market trend**: **62% of surveyed deals make revolver
assumption optional**, and the trend is accelerating --- **77% of 2024
deals** use optional assumption (versus 50% of 2022 deals). Deal-007
uses optional assumption. The compromise directly addresses Ridgeline's
LPA constraint, which Ridgeline's counsel has described as a structural
limitation of the fund vehicle rather than a negotiating posture, and
which Ridgeline has made a "deal-breaker" if assumption is mandatory.
Cartwright's counsel has acknowledged this is "a practical
consideration, not a positional one" and has signaled that optional
assumption is something "the committee is willing to discuss."

The compromise preserves Cartwright's underlying interest in the
integrity of the First Lien Facility in two ways. First, the purchaser
**may** assume the unfunded commitments if it is able and willing to do
so, preserving the option to keep the \$125M revolver available to the
Borrower. Second, if the purchaser does not assume, the unfunded
commitments **terminate** upon consummation of the purchase --- there is
no bifurcation of the syndicate into a funded-debt purchaser and
lingering unfunded-commitment holders, which was Cartwright's stated
concern. The orderly wind-down of outstanding letters of credit (cash
collateralization at 103% or replacement issuance) addresses the LC
component of the revolver and protects the Borrower's trade and
performance obligations.

**Economic impact of optional assumption.** If assumption were
mandatory, Ridgeline would be required to commit up to an additional
\$125,000,000 of contingent funding on top of the \~\$484.3M par
Purchase Price, bringing total capital at risk to approximately **\$609
million** (and, with the existing \$150M second lien position, total
exposure approaching \$759 million) --- a figure that exceeds the
appraised TEV of \$640 million and that Ridgeline's LPA forbids.
Optional assumption removes this contingent obligation, rendering the
purchase right economically exercisable in precisely the distressed
scenarios in which it is most needed, while preserving the option for a
purchaser that can assume.

**Exercise trigger --- 1L Event of Default.** The compromise adopts
Cartwright's trigger (an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit
Agreement, expressly including upon acceleration) and does not adopt
Ridgeline's broader triggers (a 2L Event of Default). This is the
market-standard formulation: **100% of surveyed deals include
"acceleration of the 1L" as a trigger**, and 74% include "commencement
of enforcement action" --- both 1L-side triggers. No common Survey
trigger is keyed to a 2L-only default, which makes economic sense:
purchasing the senior position at par is rational only when the senior
position is itself in distress. This is a concession to Cartwright on
this sub-point that is offset, within the package, by Ridgeline's gains
on the interest rate and the optional revolver assumption.

**Why this falls within each side's flexibility.** For Cartwright: par
for funded debt is preserved (non-negotiable satisfied); the trigger is
keyed to a 1L Event of Default (Cartwright's position); and the orderly
LC wind-down protects the facility's integrity. For Ridgeline: the
default-rate interest is replaced by the non-default contract rate
(saving up to \~\$2.375M on a 90-day accrual, and more over longer
periods); the unfunded revolver assumption is optional, resolving the
LPA deal-breaker; and the purchase right remains economically
exercisable. Each side's counsel has independently signaled acceptance
of every element of this compromise.

## VIII. PROVISION 3 --- PERMITTED SECOND LIEN REFINANCING INDEBTEDNESS (SECTION 2.03(c))

### A. Open Issues and Party Positions

The draft ICA brackets three open points on the refinancing definition:

-   **(A) Principal Increase Cap.** Cartwright proposes 0% (equal or
    lesser principal only); Ridgeline proposes 20% (\$30,000,000 on the
    \$150M 2L facility).
-   **(B) Maturity Floor.** Cartwright proposes a floor of 91 days after
    the First Lien Maturity Date (i.e., September 14, 2030); Ridgeline

-   **(C) Restrictiveness Test.** Cartwright proposes a blanket "no more

MFN ensuring that if any 2L refinancing contains financial maintenance
covenants more favorable than those in the 1L Credit Agreement, the 1L
lenders receive the benefit automatically. Cartwright has also
acknowledged "willingness to discuss" the principal increase "in the

Ridgeline "can accept a **10% principal increase cap** --- that is,
\$15M --- provided the increase is limited to refinancing-related fees
and costs," and is "open to discussing whether a narrowed
restrictiveness test paired with an MFN right could work." On maturity,

### C. Negotiation Rationale

**(A) Principal Increase Cap --- 10% (\$15M), limited to refinancing
costs.** The 10% cap is the **median** of the Survey (mean 8.9%; 52% of
deals fall in the 1--10% range; 21% permit 0%; 14% permit 11--15%; 12%
permit 16--20%). Critically, **79% of deals that permit any increase
limit the increase to fees, costs, and premiums only** --- the precise
limitation proposed here. Deal-007 (the Meridian-comparable) permits a
10% cap (\$15M) limited to fees and costs. The compromise therefore
tracks the market median and the most directly on-point precedent.

**Why a 0% cap is unworkable, and why a 20% cap is excessive.** A 0% cap
would render any non-trivial refinancing impracticable, because
real-world refinancings incur friction costs that must be funded.
Quantifying the realistic cost stack on a \$150M refinancing: (i) call
premium --- up to 2.00% in year one (i.e., up to **\$3.0M**) and 1.00%
in year two (up to \$1.5M), per the 2L Credit Agreement § 2.04(b); (ii)
arranger/underwriting fees --- 200 to 350 bps of committed amount (i.e.,
**\$3.0M to \$5.25M**); (iii) original issue discount --- 1 to 3 cents
on the dollar (i.e., **\$1.5M to \$4.5M**); and (iv) legal, advisory,
rating agency, and filing costs --- approximately **\$1.0M to \$2.0M**.
The realistic total is approximately **\$8.75M to \$14.75M**, which the
\$15M cap covers with modest headroom, while a \$0 cap would not.
Conversely, Ridgeline's 20% opening (\$30M) would permit incremental
secured debt beyond any plausible refinancing cost and would, on the
current capital structure, increase total secured debt from \$625M to
\$655M and push the Total LTV from 97.66% to approximately **102.3%**
--- eliminating the equity cushion and exposing the First Lien Lenders
to impairment on any enterprise-value decline. The 10% cap increases
total secured debt to \$640M, producing a Total LTV of approximately
**100%**; but because the \$15M is **limited to refinancing friction
costs** (not new risk capital deployed into the business or distributed
to equity), it does not introduce incremental credit risk of the kind
the First Lien Lenders underwrote against. This is the distinction the
1L position letter elides when it treats any upsize as an "end-run"
around the indebtedness covenants: an upsize confined to fees, premiums,
and transaction costs is not new money; it is the cost of replacing
like-for-like debt on improved terms.

**Internal consistency with the 2L Credit Agreement.** The 2L Credit
Agreement itself, in Section 7.01(d), already permits Permitted
Refinancing Indebtedness in a principal amount equal to the refinanced
amount "plus an amount not to exceed twenty percent (20%) of such
principal amount to the extent attributable to accrued and unpaid
interest, premiums, fees, and transaction costs." The ICA compromise at
10% is therefore **more restrictive than the 2L Credit Agreement's own
refinancing permission** --- giving the First Lien Lenders incremental
protection beyond what the Borrower and the Second Lien Lenders already
agreed between themselves. This is a meaningful concession by Ridgeline
(from its 20% opening and from its own credit agreement's 20% bucket)
and a creditable gain for Cartwright.

**(B) Maturity Floor --- the later of 91 days after the then-applicable
1L maturity and June 15, 2031.** This is the most straightforward of the
three sub-issues, because **Ridgeline's proposed floor (June 15, 2031)
is more protective of the First Lien Lenders than Cartwright's own
proposed floor (September 14, 2030)** --- June 15, 2031 is approximately
nine months later. Adopting Ridgeline's floor therefore gives Cartwright
more, not less, protection. The Survey confirms that **93% of deals use
the 91-day-post-1L test** and **81% also include the original 2L
maturity as a floor**; the "later of" formulation captures both and is
the market-standard dual test. The dynamic element --- keying the 91-day
test to the "then-applicable" 1L maturity --- ensures that if the First
Lien Obligations are ever extended, refinanced, or replaced to a later
maturity, the 91-day buffer recalibrates automatically and continues to
protect the First Lien Lenders. Because June 15, 2031 currently binds,
the practical effect today is the 2L's position; because the 91-day test
is retained as a backstop, the 1L's structural concern is preserved
against future 1L maturity extensions. Both sides get their floor;
neither side gives anything up.

**(C) Restrictiveness Test --- narrowed test on enumerated provisions,
paired with an MFN right limited to financial maintenance covenants.**
The compromise replaces Cartwright's blanket test (which appears in 33%
of Survey deals and which Ridgeline has rejected as an effective veto
over 2L commercial terms) and Ridgeline's "no test" position (which
appears in only 14% of Survey deals) with the **market-majority
formulation**: a **narrowed test** (52% of deals) **paired with an MFN
right** (100% of narrowed-test deals pair with an MFN; 82% of MFN
provisions are limited to financial covenants). Deal-007 uses a narrowed
test with an MFN on financial covenants.

**Scope of the narrowed test.** The test is confined to four enumerated
categories --- (A) financial maintenance covenants, (B) events of
default and cure periods, (C) mandatory prepayment provisions, and (D)
lien priority, collateral sharing, and intercreditor provisions --- each
of which maps to a specific risk Cartwright identified in its position
letter: a tightening of the Total Leverage covenant (currently 6.50x)
that could trigger a 2L default while the Borrower remains in compliance
with its 1L covenants; a reduction in cure periods that could accelerate
2L enforcement inconsistently with the standstill; and any term that
would undermine the ICA's priority or waterfall. Pricing, fees, OID,
redemption/prepayment premiums, affirmative covenants, and negative
covenants other than those enumerated are **excluded** from the test,
preserving Ridgeline's freedom to negotiate commercial terms with the
Borrower and avoiding the subjective, term-by-term veto that Ridgeline
has objected to. This exclusion is consistent with the 2L Credit
Agreement's own refinancing permission (Section 7.01(d)(iii)), which
excludes "pricing, fees, redemption or prepayment terms, and covenant
holidays" from its restrictiveness comparison.

**The MFN right.** The MFN gives the First Lien Lenders the automatic
benefit of any financial maintenance covenant in the refinancing that is
more favorable to lenders than the corresponding covenant in the First
Lien Credit Agreement --- for example, if a refinancing imposes a Total
Leverage covenant tighter than the 1L Credit Agreement's corresponding
level, the 1L lenders receive the benefit of that tighter level. This
converts the principal risk Cartwright identified (a tighter 2L covenant
destabilizing the structure) into an upside for the First Lien Lenders,
while leaving the commercial terms of the refinancing to the 2L market.
The 60-day notice window and automatic-amendment mechanism make the MFN
self-executing and administrable without requiring case-by-case consent.
The existing 2L financial maintenance covenant (Total Leverage ≤ 6.50x,
per Section 7.08 of the 2L Credit Agreement) provides the benchmark
against which the MFN operates, and Section 7.08(d) of the 2L Credit
Agreement already establishes 6.50x as the baseline and deems any lower
level "more restrictive" --- so the narrowed test and MFN are internally
consistent with the 2L Credit Agreement's own framework.

**Why this falls within each side's flexibility.** For Cartwright: the
blanket test is replaced by a narrowed test that still covers the
specific cross-default and waterfall risks Cartwright identified, and
Cartwright receives an MFN upside --- the precise pairing Cartwright's
counsel proposed. For Ridgeline: the test is narrowed to enumerated
provisions (not a blanket veto), commercial terms are excluded, and the
"no test" position is replaced by a market-majority formulation that
Ridgeline's counsel has said Ridgeline is "open to discussing." The 10%
cap (limited to costs) is the figure Ridgeline's counsel specifically
said Ridgeline "can accept," and it is more restrictive than the 2L
Credit Agreement's own 20% bucket. The maturity floor adopts Ridgeline's
position, which is more protective of Cartwright than Cartwright's own.

anti-evasion clause at the same level, a 365-day reinvestment period
(per the 1L Credit Agreement), a hard sweep for extraordinary sales, and
2L residual access after full 1L paydown. The compromise therefore
replicates the most directly on-point precedent and the market median.

**Hard sweep for large/extraordinary sales --- Cartwright's essential
requirement, preserved.** The 100% hard sweep to the First Lien
Obligations for any sale or casualty event yielding Net Cash Proceeds
above \$20M, overriding reinvestment rights, satisfies Cartwright's
declared essential requirement. This is the provision that protects the
First Lien Lenders against leakage of the large, value-impacting
dispositions that, given the 97.66% Total LTV and the \$165M equity
cushion above the First Lien Facility, would most directly impair first
lien recovery. With enterprise value of \$640M and first lien debt of
\$475M, the margin of collateral coverage for the first lien debt is
\$165M; a single large disposition (e.g., a plant sale or a major
casualty) that bypassed the waterfall could erode that margin and
increase the probability of first lien impairment in distress. The hard
sweep forecloses that risk for the dispositions that matter most.

**Ordinary-course reinvestment carve-out --- preserves the Borrower's
operational flexibility and the 1L Credit Agreement's own bargain.** For
sales and casualty events at or below \$20M, the ICA defers to the First
Lien Credit Agreement's existing reinvestment and mandatory prepayment
mechanics (the 365-day reinvestment period, the \$5M individual trigger,
and the \$15M annual aggregate trigger). This is not a concession that
undermines the First Lien Lenders; it is a decision to **incorporate, by
reference, the very mechanics the First Lien Lenders themselves
negotiated with the Borrower** in the First Lien Credit Agreement. The
First Lien Credit Agreement's Section 2.05(b)(iv) expressly provides
that the reinvestment rights are "subject to the provisions of the
Intercreditor Agreement, including Section 4.02 thereof" --- so the
First Lien Credit Agreement already contemplates that the ICA governs
the intercreditor application of these proceeds, and the compromise
simply directs that, for ordinary-course sales, the ICA apply the First
Lien Credit Agreement's own thresholds. The \$20M threshold represents
approximately **22.4% of LTM EBITDA** (\$89.4M), a level calibrated to
permit routine asset turnover (replacement of aging equipment, sale of
surplus inventory, ordinary-course casualty repairs) without triggering
the hard sweep, while capturing any disposition large enough to be
value-impacting. This addresses Meridian management's expressed need to
maintain and modernize its industrial asset base, and it is consistent
with the operational framework the First Lien Lenders agreed to when
they negotiated the First Lien Credit Agreement.

**Residual to 2L --- symbolically important, economically disciplined.**
For ordinary-course sales, any residual proceeds (after application of
the First Lien Credit Agreement's mandatory prepayment waterfall and the
expiration of the reinvestment period for non-reinvested proceeds) flow
to the Second Lien Obligations rather than being trapped at the Borrower
level. This preserves the 2L's waterfall right --- which Ridgeline's
counsel has described as "symbolically important even if economically
limited" --- and directs residual value to the reduction of the
next-priority secured indebtedness rather than to unrestricted Borrower
cash. Cartwright's counsel has acknowledged that 2L residual access "is
less of a concern if the hard sweep for significant sales is preserved,"
which it is. In practice, given the 97.66% Total LTV, residual proceeds
will arise only in the unusual case in which proceeds exceed the First
Lien Obligations; the provision preserves the principle without creating
meaningful economic leakage.

**Anti-evasion --- robust, and consistent with the 1L Credit
Agreement.** The 90-day aggregation mechanism mirrors the anti-evasion
provision already in Section 2.05(b)(iii) of the First Lien Credit
Agreement, extending the same logic to the ICA's \$20M threshold. It
permits the First Lien Agent to aggregate sales within a 90-day window
that appear to constitute a single plan or related series, defeating any
attempt to structure a large disposition as a sequence of sub-threshold
sales. This addresses the concern both sides' counsel have raised about
asset-stripping through sequenced or related transactions, and it does
so using a standard the First Lien Agent already administers under the
First Lien Credit Agreement. The 97% market prevalence of anti-evasion
clauses in deals with an ordinary-course exclusion confirms that this is
expected practice, not an outlier.

**Reporting --- transparency for both lien levels.** The quarterly
certificate, the event-driven notice for sales above \$5M (matching the
First Lien Credit Agreement's individual prepayment trigger and
providing early visibility), and the annual aggregate disposition report
give both the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent ongoing
visibility into asset disposition and casualty activity and the
application of proceeds. The Survey shows that 90% of deals require a
quarterly certificate, 79% require event-driven notice, and 38% require
an annual aggregate report; the compromise adopts all three, matching
Deal-007's reporting package. This is a low-cost, high-transparency
feature that should reassure both lender groups that the bifurcated
waterfall is being administered as drafted.

**Why this falls within each side's flexibility.** For Cartwright: the
hard sweep for large/extraordinary sales (the essential,
non-compromisable requirement) is preserved; the ordinary-course
carve-out merely incorporates the First Lien Credit Agreement's own
negotiated mechanics; and robust anti-evasion and reporting protect
against erosion of the threshold. For Ridgeline: ordinary-course
reinvestment is preserved for the Borrower; residual proceeds flow to
the 2L (not back to the Borrower); and the 2L retains a documented
waterfall right. Both sides' counsel have independently described this
bifurcated structure as acceptable.

## X. THE PACKAGE AS AN INDIVISIBLE WHOLE

The four provisions above are presented, and should be negotiated, as a
**single, integrated, and indivisible package**. This is not a
presentational preference; it reflects the internal economics of the
compromise. Each provision in which one side receives a favorable
outcome is offset by a provision in which that same side concedes.
Unbundling the package would destroy the balance and would, in several
cases, leave one side having made a disproportionate concession with no
offsetting gain --- the circumstance most likely to trigger re-approval,
re-pricing, or withdrawal.

The principal cross-linkages are as follows:

1.  **Standstill duration (1L gain) ↔ Standstill triggers and Blockage
    Notice cap (2L gain).** Cartwright receives a 180-day standstill ---
    double Ridgeline's 90-day opening and 30 days above Cartwright's own
    150-day floor --- which is a meaningful extension of the protected
    period. Ridgeline accepts that extension only because it is paired
    with (a) four objectively verifiable early-termination triggers
    and (b) a Blockage Notice cap that prevents indefinite tactical
    extension. If Cartwright were to take the 180-day duration but strip
    the triggers and the cap, Ridgeline would receive nothing in return
    for accepting 180 days and would, on its counsel's express
    statement, decline --- the triggers are a "firm condition" that is
    "not something we can decouple from the duration."

2.  **Par purchase price (1L gain) ↔ Non-default interest rate and
    optional revolver assumption (2L gain).** Cartwright receives par
    for funded debt (its non-negotiable) and a 1L-side exercise trigger
    (its position). Ridgeline accepts par only because it is paired
    with (a) the non-default contract rate (saving up to \~\$2.375M on a
    90-day accrual and more over longer periods) and (b) optional ---
    not mandatory --- unfunded revolver assumption, which resolves
    Ridgeline's LPA deal-breaker and keeps the purchase right
    economically exercisable. If Ridgeline were to accept par but be
    denied optional revolver assumption, the purchase right would be
    unexercisable for Ridgeline given its LPA constraints, and the
    concession of par would be uncompensated.

3.  **10% refinancing cap (2L gain over 1L's 0%) ↔ Narrowed test + MFN
    (1L gain over 2L's "no test").** Ridgeline receives a \$15M
    principal-increase bucket (versus Cartwright's 0%) sufficient to
    cover real-world refinancing costs. Cartwright accepts that bucket
    only because it is paired with (a) a narrowed restrictiveness test
    covering the specific cross-default and waterfall risks Cartwright
    identified and (b) an MFN right that converts any tighter
    refinancing covenant into an automatic upside for the First Lien
    Lenders. If Cartwright were to accept the 10% cap but be denied the
    MFN, the incremental secured debt would be permitted without any
    compensating structural protection. Conversely, if Ridgeline were to
    accept the narrowed test and MFN but be denied the 10% cap,
    refinancing would be impracticable and the restrictiveness test
    would operate as a one-way ratchet.

4.  **Hard sweep for large sales (1L gain) ↔ Ordinary-course
    reinvestment and 2L residual (2L gain).** Cartwright receives the
    100% hard sweep for sales above \$20M (its essential requirement).
    Ridgeline accepts that sweep only because it is paired with (a) an
    ordinary-course reinvestment carve-out that preserves the Borrower's
    operational flexibility and the First Lien Credit Agreement's own
    bargain and (b) 2L residual access after full 1L application. If
    Cartwright were to take the hard sweep but strip the ordinary-course
    carve-out, the Borrower would lose routine asset-management
    flexibility that the First Lien Credit Agreement itself grants --- a
    result Meridian would not endorse and that would undermine the
    operational framework the First Lien Lenders agreed to.

5.  **Maturity floor --- a true win-win.** The "later of" maturity floor
    is the one element that is not a trade-off: it adopts Ridgeline's
    position (June 15, 2031), which is more protective of Cartwright
    than Cartwright's own proposed floor (September 14, 2030), while
    retaining the 91-day test as a dynamic backstop. It is included in
    the package because it is part of the integrated refinancing
    definition and because its inclusion demonstrates that the package
    is calibrated to give each side the most protection consistent with
    market practice, rather than to split every difference down the
    middle.

**Consequences of unbundling.** Both Cartwright's counsel and
Ridgeline's counsel have, on a without-prejudice basis, signaled that
their clients are prepared to discuss these points as an integrated
package and have conditioned key flexibility signals on package
treatment. Cartwright's counsel has expressly noted that the
principal-increase cap "will inevitably be part of the broader trade"
and that material departures from the package would require
re-circulation to the full seven-lender syndicate, with a substantial
risk of re-pricing or modified terms. Ridgeline's counsel has stated
that Ridgeline's "strong preference is that these points be addressed as
a single, integrated package rather than piecemeal." Meridian's
endorsement of the package (Part XI) is itself conditioned on its
integrated presentation. For these reasons, the package should be
presented to both agents as a unit, with the express statement that it
is not severable.

## XI. MERIDIAN'S ROLE AND ENDORSEMENT

As you have indicated, Meridian is prepared to actively signal its
support for this compromise package to both Cartwright and Ridgeline to
help break the deadlock. KTM has drafted the package with that objective
in mind: each provision is balanced, anchored to market median practice,
and respectful of each side's documented hard constraints, so that
Meridian can genuinely endorse and advocate for the package with both
agents rather than appearing to favor either side.

From the company's perspective, the package is consistent with
Meridian's interests on each point:

-   **Standstill.** A 180-day standstill with triggers and a cap creates
    a defined, reasonable window for consensual resolution of defaults,
    after which the possibility of Second Lien enforcement introduces
    constructive urgency into the workout --- the outcome Meridian's
    management has indicated it prefers over prolonged enforcement
    limbo, which would undermine customer and supplier confidence in
    Meridian's precision industrial components business.
-   **Purchase right.** Par for funded debt at the non-default rate,
    with optional revolver assumption, preserves a viable Second Lien
    purchase right (protecting against a scenario in which the senior
    and junior creditors' incentives diverge) while not imposing an
    unexercisable mandatory obligation that would render the right a
    dead letter.
-   **Refinancing.** A 10% cap limited to costs, a maturity floor that
    adopts the original 2L maturity, and a narrowed test with an MFN
    preserve Meridian's ability to refinance the Second Lien facility on
    improved terms in the future --- essential to managing the capital
    structure over the five-to-six-year life of these lender
    relationships --- while giving the First Lien Lenders the structural
    protections they require.
-   **Proceeds waterfall.** The bifurcated structure preserves
    Meridian's ability to manage routine asset turnover and to repair or
    replace damaged assets (essential to a capital-intensive
    manufacturing business), while protecting both lien levels against
    leakage from large or extraordinary dispositions.

Meridian's financial position --- approximately \$89.4M LTM EBITDA,
5.31x first lien leverage, 6.99x total leverage, and a 97.66% Total LTV
--- leaves no room for either lender group to walk away. The package is
designed to keep both groups fully committed and to close on time. These
will be Meridian's lenders for the next five to six years, and the tone
of the compromise is deliberately balanced to preserve strong working
relationships with both Cartwright and Ridgeline.

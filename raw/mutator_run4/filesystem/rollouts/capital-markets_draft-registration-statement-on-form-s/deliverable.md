## I. Introduction and Scope

This memorandum summarizes the inconsistencies, disclosure gaps, and
risks identified during our review of the source documents provided in
connection with the preparation of the draft Registration Statement on
Form S-1 (the "Form S-1" or "Registration Statement") for the proposed
initial public offering (the "IPO") of Crestline Software, Inc. (the
"Company"). The source documents reviewed include: (i) the audited
consolidated financial statements for fiscal years 2023 and 2024; (ii)
the CFO's IPO readiness memorandum; (iii) the amended and restated
certificate of incorporation and bylaws; (iv) the equity plan summary
and grant records; (v) the management questionnaire responses; (vi) the
term loan summary; (vii) the underwriting term sheet and lock-up form;
and (viii) the draft roadshow presentation.

The issues are organized by severity and category. **Critical** items
must be resolved before the Form S-1 is filed because they affect the
accuracy of the financial statements or could result in material
misstatements. **High** items require resolution before filing but are
disclosure or consistency matters. **Medium** items are open items or
risks that should be addressed during the drafting and review process.
Each item includes a recommended action.

A companion draft Form S-1 has been prepared using the audited financial
statements as the authoritative source for all financial figures. Where
the source documents conflict, the audited figures have been used in the
draft Form S-1, and the conflicts are flagged below.

## II. Executive Summary

We identified **33 discrete issues**, including:

-   **Three internal reconciliation errors in the audited financial
    statements** (cash roll-forward, term loan roll-forward, and
    short-term investment activity) that must be resolved with Archer
    Pryce LLP before the Form S-1 is filed, because the Form S-1
    incorporates the audited financial statements and the SEC reviews
    for internal consistency.
-   **A material arithmetic error in the Adjusted EBITDA
    reconciliation** that appears in the CFO's IPO readiness memorandum
    and the draft roadshow presentation. The stated Adjusted EBITDA of
    \$22.8 million for fiscal year 2024 is incorrect; the correct
    figure, using the stated add-backs, is \$20.5 million. The fiscal
    year 2023 figure of \$7.6 million is also incorrect and uses
    add-backs that do not tie to the audited financial statements.
-   **A misleading characterization of professional services as
    "high-margin"** in the roadshow presentation, when the audited
    financial statements show a negative professional services gross
    margin of approximately (0.5)%.
-   **Numerous cross-document inconsistencies**, including the identity
    of the term loan lender, the transfer agent, the size and
    composition of the board of directors, the fiscal year 2023 revenue
    split between subscription and professional services, and several
    balance sheet line items.
-   **Data integrity issues in the equity plan grant records**,
    including director names that do not match the actual directors and
    an apparent conflict regarding the identity and title of the General
    Counsel / Chief Revenue Officer.
-   **Several open items** that must be resolved before filing,
    including the controlled company exemption election, the beneficial
    ownership status of Aldersgate Capital, missing director ages,
    lock-up coverage for fund entities, the final 409A valuation, and
    the Section 382 study.
-   **Material legal and structural risks** requiring prominent risk
    factor disclosure, including the pending Optivance patent
    litigation, the absence of a dual-class sunset provision, the
    federal forum selection clause, and potential limitations on net
    operating loss carryforwards.

## III. Critical Issues --- Financial Statement Reconciliation

The following issues relate to internal inconsistencies within the
audited consolidated financial statements. Because the Form S-1
incorporates the audited financial statements, these must be resolved
with Archer Pryce LLP before filing. The SEC's Division of Corporation
Finance routinely issues comments where the cash flow statement, balance
sheet, and notes do not reconcile.

### Issue 1 --- Cash and Cash Equivalents Roll-Forward Does Not Reconcile (CRITICAL)

**Description.** The consolidated statements of cash flows show the
following:

-   Fiscal year 2023: beginning cash \$8,800; net increase \$59,700;
    ending cash \$68,500.
-   Fiscal year 2024: beginning cash \$78,400; net increase \$16,300;
    ending cash \$94,700.

The fiscal year 2024 beginning cash balance of \$78,400 does **not**
equal the fiscal year 2023 ending cash balance of \$68,500, nor does it
equal the December 31, 2023 balance sheet cash balance of \$68,500. The
difference is \$9,900. Internally, the fiscal year 2024 column
reconciles (\$78,400 + \$16,300 = \$94,700, which matches the December
31, 2024 balance sheet), but the beginning balance is overstated by
\$9,900 and the net increase is understated by \$9,900 (the two errors
offset to produce the correct ending balance). The actual year-over-year
change in cash per the balance sheets is \$94,700 − \$68,500 = \$26,200,
not the \$16,300 shown as the net increase.

**Impact.** This is an internal inconsistency in the audited financial
statements. The cash flow statement is a required financial statement
and must reconcile to the balance sheet. An SEC reviewer is likely to
issue a comment.

**Recommended Action.** Refer to Archer Pryce LLP for correction.
Confirm whether the fiscal year 2024 beginning cash should be \$68,500
(matching the prior year ending balance and the December 31, 2023
balance sheet) and whether the net increase should be \$26,200, or
identify the \$9,900 reconciling item (e.g., a reclassification, a
prior-period adjustment, or an unrecorded item). Do not file the Form
S-1 until the cash flow statement reconciles.

### Issue 2 --- Term Loan Roll-Forward Does Not Reconcile (CRITICAL)

**Description.** The audited financial statements reflect the following
with respect to the term loan:

-   Balance sheet: Term loan, non-current --- \$40,000 at both December
    31, 2024 and December 31, 2023.
-   Cash flow statement, fiscal year 2023: Proceeds from term loan
    borrowings --- \$15,000; no repayments.
-   Cash flow statement, fiscal year 2024: Repayment of term loan ---
    \$(5,000); no borrowings.
-   Note 8 (Debt): Future principal repayments as of December 31, 2024
    --- 2025: \$5,000; 2026: \$10,000; 2027: \$25,000; Total: \$40,000.

These do not reconcile. If \$40,000 was outstanding at December 31, 2023
and \$5,000 was repaid in fiscal year 2024 with no new borrowings, the
December 31, 2024 balance should be \$35,000, not \$40,000. Conversely,
the future principal payments table begins at \$40,000 (implying no
fiscal year 2024 repayment), which conflicts with the \$5,000 repayment
shown in the cash flow statement. The fiscal year 2023 cash flow shows
\$15,000 of borrowings, which (combined with a beginning balance) must
reach \$40,000 by December 31, 2023, implying approximately \$25,000 was
outstanding at the beginning of fiscal year 2023.

**Related inconsistency --- date of the facility.** Note 8 states that
"In June 2023, the Company entered into a term loan facility with
Kestridge West Credit Partners." The term loan summary, however, states
that the Term Loan and Security Agreement is "Dated as of December 15,
2022" and that "The initial draw of \$40,000,000 was made on the closing
date." These two statements are inconsistent as to both the date of the
facility and the amount and timing of the initial draw. The term loan
summary's statement that \$40 million was drawn at closing (December
2022) is also inconsistent with the fiscal year 2023 cash flow
statement, which shows only \$15 million of term loan borrowings in
fiscal year 2023.

**Impact.** The term loan balance, the cash flow statement, and the debt
note do not reconcile. This affects the "Description of Indebtedness"
section of the Form S-1, the MD&A liquidity discussion, and the
contractual obligations table. The inconsistency between the audited
Note 8 (June 2023) and the term loan summary (December 2022) must be
resolved because the Form S-1 will describe the facility and file the
credit agreement as an exhibit.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Refer to Archer Pryce LLP to reconcile the
term loan balance, the cash flow activity, and the future payments
table. (ii) Confirm the actual date of the credit agreement and the
timing and amount of all draws and repayments. (iii) Reconcile the term
loan summary to the audited financial statements and correct whichever
document is in error. (iv) Ensure the "Description of Indebtedness" in
the Form S-1 is consistent with the audited financial statements and the
filed credit agreement.

### Issue 3 --- Short-Term Investment Activity Does Not Reconcile (CRITICAL)

**Description.** The consolidated statements of cash flows show
"Purchases of short-term investments" of \$(215,000) thousand (i.e.,
\$215.0 million) for fiscal year 2023, with "Maturities of short-term
investments" of \$10,000 thousand. The short-term investments balance
was \$38,500 at December 31, 2023 and \$42,300 at December 31, 2024. For
the fiscal year 2023 activity to reconcile (ending = beginning +
purchases − maturities), the beginning balance would need to be
approximately \$243.5 million, which is inconsistent with the Company's
cash position at the beginning of fiscal year 2023 (beginning cash of
only \$8.8 million). The fiscal year 2024 activity reconciles
(\$38,500 + \$28,800 − \$25,000 = \$42,300), confirming the methodology,
which makes the fiscal year 2023 \$215 million figure appear to be an
error (likely a transcription error, e.g., an extra digit).

**Impact.** The investing activities section of the cash flow statement
for fiscal year 2023 does not reconcile with the short-term investments
balances. This is an internal inconsistency in the audited financial
statements.

**Recommended Action.** Refer to Archer Pryce LLP to confirm the correct
fiscal year 2023 purchases of short-term investments (and any offsetting
maturities or sales not reflected). Correct the cash flow statement
before filing.

## IV. High-Severity Issues --- Non-GAAP Measures and Misleading Disclosures

### Issue 4 --- Adjusted EBITDA Arithmetic Error (HIGH)

**Description.** The CFO's IPO readiness memorandum and the draft
roadshow presentation (Slides 3, 23, and 34) state Adjusted EBITDA of
\$22.8 million for fiscal year 2024. The reconciliation uses the
following add-backs: net loss \$(24.8)M + depreciation and amortization
\$8.7M + stock-based compensation \$38.2M + interest expense \$3.1M −
interest income \$6.2M + income tax provision \$0.3M + restructuring
charges \$1.2M. The correct sum of these items is **\$20.5 million**,
not \$22.8 million. The \$2.3 million discrepancy is unexplained. The
stated Adjusted EBITDA margin of 12.2% is also incorrect; using the
correct \$20.5 million figure, the margin is 10.9% (\$20.5M / \$187.3M).

The fiscal year 2023 Adjusted EBITDA of \$7.6 million stated in the
memorandum is also incorrect. The memorandum's fiscal year 2023
add-backs (depreciation and amortization \$6.9M; stock-based
compensation \$29.1M; interest expense \$2.8M; interest income \$4.6M;
restructuring \$0.6M) do **not** tie to the audited financial
statements, which show depreciation and amortization of \$7.1M,
stock-based compensation of \$26.4M, interest expense of \$3.1M,
interest income of \$5.8M, and no restructuring charges in fiscal year
2023. Using the audited figures, fiscal year 2023 Adjusted EBITDA is
approximately **\$3.5 million**.

**Impact.** Presenting an incorrect non-GAAP reconciliation in a
registration statement would violate Regulation G and Item 10(e) of
Regulation S-K and would likely draw an SEC comment letter. The error
has propagated from the CFO's memorandum into the roadshow presentation.
The draft Form S-1 has been prepared using the correct figures (\$20.5
million for fiscal year 2024 and \$3.5 million for fiscal year 2023,
reconciled to the audited financial statements).

**Recommended Action.** (i) Correct the Adjusted EBITDA reconciliation
in all investor-facing materials and the Form S-1 to \$20.5 million
(fiscal year 2024) and \$3.5 million (fiscal year 2023), using add-backs
that tie to the audited financial statements. (ii) Investigate the
source of the \$2.3 million fiscal year 2024 discrepancy and the
incorrect fiscal year 2023 add-backs. (iii) Ensure the non-GAAP
reconciliation in the Form S-1 complies with Regulation G and Item
10(e), including appropriate cautionary language. (iv) Confirm whether
the Company intends to present Adjusted EBITDA as a non-GAAP measure in
the Form S-1 and finalize the definition.

### Issue 5 --- Misleading Characterization of Professional Services as "High-Margin" (HIGH)

**Description.** The draft roadshow presentation describes professional
services as "high-margin and accretive" on Slides 12, 17, 21, and 27,
and projects professional services gross margins of 15% to 20% at scale.
The audited financial statements show professional services revenue of
\$18.7 million against professional services cost of revenue of \$18.8
million for fiscal year 2024, yielding a gross margin of approximately
**(0.5)%** (negative). The appendix slide (Slide 35) correctly shows the
professional services gross margin as (0.5)%, creating an internal
inconsistency within the same presentation.

**Impact.** Characterizing a negative-margin business as "high-margin"
is misleading and could expose the Company to liability under the
federal securities laws if repeated in the registration statement or in
marketing materials used in connection with the offering. The SEC has
brought enforcement actions for misleading non-GAAP and operational
disclosures.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Remove the "high-margin" characterization
from all investor-facing materials. (ii) In the Form S-1, disclose the
actual professional services gross margin of (0.5)% and frame the 15% to
20% figure as a forward-looking target with appropriate cautionary
language and risk factor disclosure (a risk factor to this effect has
been included in the draft Form S-1). (iii) Ensure the roadshow
presentation is consistent with the prospectus.

### Issue 6 --- Restructuring Disclosure Inconsistencies (HIGH)

**Description.** The audited financial statements (Note 9) state that
the workforce reduction occurred in the **third quarter of 2024** and
affected approximately **46 employees**. The CFO's IPO readiness
memorandum (Section 5.3) states that the restructuring related to a
workforce reduction of approximately **30 positions** in the **second
quarter of 2024**. These are inconsistent as to both the timing (Q2
vs. Q3) and the number of employees affected (30 vs. 46).

**Impact.** The Form S-1 must accurately describe the restructuring.
Inconsistency between the audited financial statements and the MD&A
narrative would draw an SEC comment.

**Recommended Action.** Use the audited financial statements as the
authoritative source (third quarter of 2024; approximately 46 employees)
and correct the CFO's memorandum. Ensure the MD&A in the Form S-1 is
consistent with Note 9.

## V. High-Severity Issues --- Cross-Document Disclosure Inconsistencies

### Issue 7 --- Term Loan Lender Name Inconsistency (HIGH)

**Description.** The audited financial statements (Note 8), the CFO's
IPO readiness memorandum, and the term loan summary refer to the lender
as **"Kestridge West Credit Partners."** The draft roadshow presentation
(Slides 20, 24, and 27) refers to the lender as **"Silicon Valley Credit

"Kestridge West Credit Partners."

**Impact.** The Form S-1 "Description of Indebtedness" and the filed

name in the prospectus would be a material inaccuracy.

**Recommended Action.** Confirm the correct legal name of the lender and
use it consistently throughout the Form S-1, the roadshow presentation,
and all offering documents. Correct the roadshow presentation.
Investigate the "SVCP" abbreviation in the term loan summary.

### Issue 8 --- Transfer Agent Inconsistency (HIGH)

**Description.** The underwriting term sheet and the CFO's IPO readiness
memorandum (Section 10, action item 6) identify the transfer agent as
**"Oakvale Transfer Transfer Co."** (note the duplicated word
"Transfer"). The amended and restated bylaws (Section 5.1) identify the
transfer agent as **"Computershare Transfer Co."** The lock-up agreement
form (Section 6) refers to "Oakvale Transfer Transfer Co."

**Impact.** The Form S-1 must identify the correct transfer agent. The
discrepancy must be resolved, and the duplicated word "Transfer" in
"Oakvale Transfer Transfer Co." appears to be a typographical error.

**Recommended Action.** Confirm the identity of the appointed transfer
agent and correct all documents for consistency. The draft Form S-1 uses
a placeholder pending confirmation.

### Issue 9 --- Board Size and Composition Inconsistency (HIGH)

**Description.** The source documents are inconsistent as to the size of
the board of directors and whether Katerina "Kate" Litvanova serves as a
director:

-   The amended and restated certificate of incorporation (Article V,
    Section 5.2) provides that the initial number of directors shall be

**Impact.** The Form S-1 "Management" section must accurately state the
number of directors and identify each director. The classified board
structure and the controlled company analysis depend on the board

directors.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Confirm whether the board will consist of
six or seven directors following the IPO and whether Ms. Litvanova will
serve as a director. (ii) Reconcile the certificate of incorporation,
the questionnaire responses, and the roadshow presentation. (iii) If the
board will have seven directors including Ms. Litvanova, update the
questionnaire and roadshow; if six, update the certificate of
incorporation's classified board provisions. (iv) The draft Form S-1 has
been prepared with a placeholder pending confirmation and lists
Ms. Litvanova as a director consistent with the certificate of
incorporation.

### Issue 10 --- Fiscal Year 2023 Revenue Split Inconsistency (HIGH)

**Description.** The CFO's IPO readiness memorandum (Section 4.1)
presents the fiscal year 2023 revenue split as subscription revenue of
**\$113.4 million** and professional services revenue of **\$15.5
million**. The audited consolidated statements of operations show fiscal
year 2023 subscription revenue of **\$116.4 million** and professional
services revenue of **\$12.5 million**. The total (\$128.9 million) is
the same, but approximately \$3.0 million has been shifted from
subscription to professional services in the memorandum. As a result,
the memorandum's stated subscription growth rate of 48.7% and
professional services growth rate of 20.6% are incorrect; using the
audited figures, subscription growth was 44.8% and professional services
growth was 49.6%.

**Impact.** The Form S-1 MD&A must use the audited figures. The
memorandum's narrative regarding the subscription/professional services
mix and growth rates is incorrect.

**Recommended Action.** Use the audited financial statements as the
authoritative source (\$116.4 million subscription; \$12.5 million
professional services for fiscal year 2023). The draft Form S-1 uses the
audited figures. Correct the CFO's memorandum and any derived materials.

### Issue 11 --- Balance Sheet Line Item Inconsistencies (HIGH)

**Description.** The CFO's IPO readiness memorandum (Section 6.1)
presents a condensed balance sheet as of December 31, 2024 that differs
from the audited balance sheet in two line items:

-   Accounts receivable, net: memorandum **\$28.2 million**; audited
    **\$29.8 million**.
-   Prepaid expenses and other current assets: memorandum **\$13.2
    million**; audited **\$11.6 million**.

The memorandum's "Total current assets" of \$178.4 million matches the
audited total, but the components do not.

**Impact.** The Form S-1 must use the audited balance sheet figures. The
memorandum's condensed balance sheet is internally inconsistent with the
audited financial statements.

**Recommended Action.** Use the audited balance sheet figures throughout
the Form S-1. Correct the CFO's memorandum. The draft Form S-1 uses the
audited figures.

### Issue 12 --- Post-IPO Ownership Estimates Do Not Tie to Beneficial Ownership (MEDIUM-HIGH)

**Description.** The CFO's IPO readiness memorandum (Section 7.2)
presents estimated post-IPO ownership percentages (e.g., Graymont
Ventures \~19.3%; Ridgeline Growth Partners \~12.7%; Katerina Litvanova
\~8.4%) based on 102,416,667 total shares outstanding. These percentages
do not tie to the beneficial ownership figures elsewhere in the
memorandum and the questionnaire. For example, Graymont Ventures'
23,607,067 shares divided by 102,416,667 shares equals approximately
23.0%, not 19.3%; Ridgeline Growth Partners' 15,594,267 shares equals
approximately 15.2%, not 12.7%; and Ms. Litvanova's 9,693,733 shares
equals approximately 9.5%, not 8.4%.

**Impact.** The "Principal Stockholders" table in the Form S-1 must be
accurate. Inconsistent ownership percentages across documents create
confusion and could result in inaccurate disclosure.

**Recommended Action.** Recompute the post-IPO ownership percentages
using a consistent methodology and share base. Reconcile to the
beneficial ownership table and the capitalization table. The draft Form
S-1 presents the pre-IPO beneficial ownership table (on a fully diluted
basis) and notes the post-IPO voting power; the post-IPO ownership table
should be finalized once the share base and methodology are confirmed.

## VI. Equity Plan and Capitalization Data Integrity Issues

### Issue 13 --- Director Names in Equity Grant Records Do Not Match Actual Directors (HIGH)

**Description.** The equity plan summary and grant records (RSU Grants
tab) show fiscal year 2024 non-employee director RSU grants
(RSU-2024-009 through RSU-2024-013) made to the following individuals:
"Anand Venkatesh," "Claudia Moreno," "David Park," "Soo-Yeon Kwon," and
"Emmanuel Obi." The actual non-employee directors of the Company are
**Priya Venkatesh, Thomas J. Moreno, Dr. Helen Park, James Kwon, and
Adaora Obi.** The first names in the grant records do not match the
actual directors. In addition, "David Park" appears both as a "VP
Product" employee (OPT-2021-002) and as a "Non-Employee Director"
(RSU-2024-011), which is internally inconsistent.

**Impact.** The equity grant records are a source for the Form S-1's
stock-based compensation disclosures, the "Recent Sales of Unregistered
Securities" section, and the equity plan footnotes. Incorrect grantee
names could indicate data entry errors, misdirected grants, or a failure
to maintain accurate cap table records, any of which would undermine the
reliability of the equity disclosures and could raise questions about
the Company's internal controls over equity administration.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Reconcile the equity grant records to the
cap table and the transfer agent's records. (ii) Confirm the correct
names of all grant recipients and correct the grant records. (iii)
Determine whether any grants were made to individuals who are not the
intended directors or employees. (iv) Verify that the director RSU
grants were properly approved by the board or the compensation
committee. (v) Consider whether a broader review of equity
administration controls is warranted in light of these errors.

### Issue 14 --- General Counsel / Chief Revenue Officer Identity and Title Conflict (HIGH)

**Description.** The management questionnaire responses, the amended and
restated bylaws, and the draft roadshow presentation identify **Sandra
Y. Chen** as the General Counsel and Corporate Secretary (who joined the
Company in 2019 and was formerly an associate at Thorncroft & Wexler
LLP). However, the equity plan grant records identify "Sandra Chen" as
**"VP Sales (now CRO)"** (OPT-2018-002, granted August 2018) and as
**"CRO"** (OPT-2024-005 and RSU-2024-004, granted in 2024). The 2018
grant predates Ms. Chen's 2019 joining as General Counsel. Separately,
the equity grant records identify **"Thomas Brennan"** as "General
Counsel" (OPT-2022-002, granted April 2022; RSU-2024-005, granted March
2024), which conflicts with Ms. Chen's role as General Counsel.

**Impact.** The Form S-1 "Management" section must accurately identify
the executive officers and their titles. If there is a Chief Revenue
Officer who is an executive officer, that person must be disclosed under
Item 401 of Regulation S-K. The current draft Form S-1 lists only four
executive officers (Ramaswamy, Litvanova, Hartwell, Chen). If a CRO
exists, the executive officer disclosure is incomplete. The conflict
between "Sandra Chen (CRO)" and "Sandra Y. Chen (General Counsel)" and
the presence of "Thomas Brennan (General Counsel)" must be resolved.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Confirm whether "Sandra Chen" in the equity
grant records is the same person as Sandra Y. Chen (General Counsel) or
a different individual. (ii) Confirm whether Thomas Brennan is or was
the General Counsel and whether he is currently employed by the Company.
(iii) Determine whether the Company has a Chief Revenue Officer and, if
so, whether that person is an executive officer required to be disclosed
in the Form S-1. (iv) Reconcile the equity grant records, the management
questionnaire, and the organizational documents. (v) Update the Form S-1
"Management" section accordingly.

### Issue 15 --- Equity Grant Record Reconciliation (MEDIUM)

**Description.** The Option Grants tab of the equity plan summary shows

**Impact.** The Form S-1 stock-based compensation footnotes and the
"Recent Sales of Unregistered Securities" section rely on accurate grant
records. Discrepancies between the grant records and the audited
financial statements could draw an SEC comment.

**Recommended Action.** Reconcile the equity grant detail records to the
cap table, the transfer agent's records, and the audited financial
statements. Confirm the weighted-average exercise price of \$8.42 per
share and the outstanding share counts. Document the reconciliation.

## VII. Open Items Requiring Resolution Before Filing

### Issue 16 --- Controlled Company Exemption Election Not Formally Adopted (HIGH)

**Description.** Following the IPO, the co-founders will hold
approximately 71.8% of the total voting power, causing the Company to be
a "controlled company" under Nasdaq Listing Rule 5615(c). The management
questionnaire responses (Section 6 and Open Item 1) confirm that **no
board resolution is on record** regarding whether the Company will rely
on the controlled company exemptions. The questionnaire responses
reflect awareness of the controlled company status but no definitive
election. Current board composition (three of six, or three of seven,
directors independent) indicates that reliance on at least the
majority-independent-board exemption is likely necessary unless an
additional independent director is appointed before the IPO.

**Impact.** The Form S-1 must disclose whether the Company intends to
rely on the controlled company exemptions and the resulting governance
implications. The draft Form S-1 includes risk factor disclosure
regarding controlled company status and notes that the Company has not
yet made a final determination. This must be resolved before the
effective date.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Obtain a formal board resolution determining
whether the Company will rely on the controlled company exemptions. (ii)
If relying on the exemptions, finalize the Form S-1 disclosure,
including the specific exemptions relied upon and the resulting
governance differences. (iii) Consider whether to appoint an additional
independent director to achieve a majority-independent board. (iv)
Finalize committee compositions consistent with the determination.

### Issue 17 --- Aldersgate Capital Beneficial Ownership Gap (HIGH)

**Description.** Aldersgate Capital participated in the Company's Series
B financing (April 2020) and is referenced in the management
questionnaire responses (Section 3.1 and Open Item 3). However,
Aldersgate Capital does not appear in any current beneficial ownership
disclosure, and no questionnaire respondent reports Aldersgate Capital
as a current stockholder. The draft roadshow presentation (Slide 29
notes) references "Crestview Capital" as a Series B participant, which
is a third variation of the name (Aldersgate Capital / Crestview
Capital), further complicating the analysis.

**Impact.** If Aldersgate Capital (or "Crestview Capital") holds 5% or
more of any class of the Company's securities, it must be included in
the "Principal Stockholders" table in the Form S-1 under Item 403 of
Regulation S-K. The principal stockholders table cannot be finalized
until this is resolved. The inconsistent naming (Aldersgate
vs. Crestview) must also be resolved.

### Issue 22 --- Cheap Stock / 409A Timing Risk for Q1 2023 Grants (MEDIUM)

**Description.** The audited financial statements (Note 12(e)) and the
equity plan summary disclose that during the three months ended March
31, 2023, the Company granted 2,400,000 stock options with an exercise
price of \$9.50 per share, based on the September 30, 2022 409A
valuation. A subsequent 409A valuation as of March 31, 2023 determined
the fair value to be \$14.20 per share. The grants were made before the
updated valuation was obtained. Several other grants referenced
valuations that were completed after the grant date or that were within
the 12-month reliance window.

**Impact.** The SEC frequently comments on "cheap stock" issues where
option exercise prices are based on stale or lower valuations,
particularly in the period leading up to an IPO. The Company may face
questions regarding whether the grant-date fair values were appropriate
and whether additional stock-based compensation expense should be
recognized.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Review the 409A valuation history with the
valuation specialist and counsel. (ii) Be prepared to respond to SEC
comments regarding the Q1 2023 grants and the reliance on the September
30, 2022 valuation. (iii) Ensure the stock-based compensation footnote
fully discloses the valuation methodology and the timing of grants
relative to valuations. (iv) Consider whether any additional SBC expense
or correction is required.

## VIII. Legal, Regulatory, and Structural Risks

### Issue 23 --- Pending Optivance Patent Litigation (HIGH)

**Description.** The Company is a defendant in *Optivance, Inc. v.
Crestline Software, Inc.*, Case No. 6:24-cv-00831 (E.D. Tex.), filed
September 12, 2024, alleging infringement of U.S. Patent No. 11,284,107.
Outside patent counsel estimates potential exposure of \$5.0 million to
\$20.0 million in the event of an adverse judgment, characterized as low
probability. No loss accrual has been recorded.

**Related risk --- term loan default threshold.** The term loan
agreement (Section 8.1(h)) provides that one or more judgments for the
payment of money in excess of \$5.0 million in the aggregate that remain
undischarged for 60 days constitute an event of default. The upper end
of the estimated exposure (\$20.0 million) exceeds this \$5.0 million
threshold. An adverse judgment at or above the threshold that is not
bonded, appealed, or stayed could trigger an acceleration of the \$40.0
million term loan. The term loan agreement also requires the Company to
notify the lender of material litigation.

**Impact.** The litigation must be disclosed in the Form S-1 "Legal
Proceedings" and "Risk Factors" sections (the draft Form S-1 includes
this disclosure). The interaction with the term loan default threshold
should be specifically disclosed.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Ensure the Form S-1 disclosure of the
litigation is complete and consistent across the "Legal Proceedings,"
"Risk Factors," "Description of Indebtedness," and the financial
statement notes. (ii) Disclose the term loan judgment threshold and the
potential consequences of an adverse judgment. (iii) Monitor the
litigation and develop bonding, appeal, or stay strategies in the event
of an unfavorable result. (iv) Confirm that the lender has been notified
of the litigation as required by the credit agreement. (v) Consider
whether the range of loss estimate should be updated as the litigation
progresses.

### Issue 24 --- Absence of Dual-Class Sunset Provision (HIGH)

**Description.** The amended and restated certificate of incorporation
(Article IV, Section 4.3) provides that Class B common stock
automatically converts into Class A common stock only upon (i) transfer
to a non-permitted transferee or (ii) the death or incapacity of the
holder. There is **no time-based sunset** (e.g., 7, 10, or 12 years from
the IPO) and **no ownership-percentage-based sunset**. The dual-class
structure will persist indefinitely.

**Impact.** The absence of a sunset is a significant governance concern
for institutional investors and proxy advisors. Certain index providers
(including S&P Dow Jones Indices and FTSE Russell) exclude companies
with multi-class share structures from inclusion in certain indices,
which could reduce demand for the Class A common stock and adversely
affect the trading price. The draft Form S-1 includes a risk factor
regarding the absence of a sunset.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Consider whether to adopt a sunset provision
before the IPO, weighing the benefits of founder control against the
potential impact on index inclusion and investor demand. (ii) If no
sunset is adopted, ensure the risk factor disclosure is prominent and
complete, including the potential for index exclusion. (iii) Disclose
the conversion triggers and the absence of a sunset in the "Description
of Capital Stock."

### Issue 25 --- Federal Forum Selection Clause for Securities Act Claims (MEDIUM)

**Description.** The amended and restated certificate of incorporation
(Article X, Section 10.2) designates the federal district courts of the
United States as the exclusive forum for claims arising under the
Securities Act. This provision is permissible following *Salzberg v.
Sciabacucucchi* (Del. 2020), but it limits the ability of stockholders
to bring Securities Act claims (including Section 11 claims) in state
court, where such claims are concurrently available under *Cyan Inc. v.
Beaver County Employees Retirement Fund* (2018).

**Impact.** The exclusive federal forum provision may discourage the
filing of Securities Act claims and may be challenged. The draft Form
S-1 includes a risk factor regarding the exclusive forum provisions.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Ensure the risk factor disclosure regarding
the exclusive forum provisions is complete, including the potential
inability to bring claims in state court and the uncertainty regarding
enforceability. (ii) Monitor legal developments regarding federal forum
selection clauses. (iii) Confirm with counsel that the provision is
enforceable under Delaware law.

### Issue 26 --- Related Party Transaction Disclosure Inconsistency --- Vantara Logistics (HIGH)

**Description.** The Vantara Logistics, Inc. SaaS license agreement
(\$1.2 million annual contract value; three-year term; entered April
2023) is a related party transaction because Vantara is a portfolio
company of Graymont Ventures, whose Managing Partner, Priya Venkatesh,
serves on the Company's board of directors (and on Vantara's board). The
management questionnaire responses (Open Item 2) confirm that
Ms. Venkatesh disclosed the transaction as a related party transaction,
but the CFO (Mr. Hartwell) did not identify it as a related party
transaction in his questionnaire response. The CFO's IPO readiness
memorandum and the draft roadshow presentation (Slide 14) treat Vantara
as a standard customer case study without disclosing the related party
relationship.

**Related issue --- term loan affiliate transactions covenant.** The
term loan summary (Section 7.2) notes that the credit agreement requires
prior written notification to the lender for affiliate transactions
exceeding \$500,000. The Vantara agreement (\$1.2 million annually)
would require such notification. Confirm whether notification was
provided.

**Impact.** The transaction must be disclosed under Item 404 of
Regulation S-K in the Form S-1. The draft Form S-1 includes the
disclosure in the "Certain Relationships and Related Party Transactions"
section. The inconsistency across the source documents must be
reconciled, and all investor-facing materials must consistently identify
Vantara as a related party.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Confirm with the Audit Committee the
arm's-length pricing determination and obtain supporting documentation.
(ii) Ensure all investor-facing materials (including the roadshow
presentation) consistently disclose the Vantara related party
relationship. (iii) Confirm whether the lender was notified of the
Vantara transaction under the affiliate transactions covenant. (iv)
Consider whether any other customer relationships involve related
parties.

### Issue 31 --- Emerging Growth Company Accommodations and Financial Statement Requirements (MEDIUM)

**Description.** The Company qualifies as an emerging growth company
("EGC") under the JOBS Act (revenue of \$187.3 million in fiscal year
2024, well below the \$1.235 billion threshold; within five years of
first sale of common equity). As an EGC, the Company may present two
years of audited financial statements (rather than three) and may take
advantage of reduced executive compensation disclosure, exemption from
SOX 404(b) auditor attestation, and an extended transition period for
new accounting standards.

**Impact.** The Form S-1 presents two years of audited financial
statements (fiscal years 2023 and 2024), which is sufficient for an EGC.
If the Form S-1 is filed later in 2025, interim financial statements
(e.g., for the quarter ended March 31, 2025) may be required.

**Recommended Action.** (i) Confirm the EGC accommodations the Company
intends to adopt and disclose them in the Form S-1. (ii) Determine
whether interim financial statements will be required based on the
filing date. (iii) Consider whether to adopt the extended transition
period for new accounting standards, and if so, include the required
risk factor disclosure. (iv) The draft Form S-1 includes the EGC
disclosure.

## XI. Conclusion

The source documents provide a substantial foundation for the Form S-1,
but the issues identified above must be resolved before the Registration
Statement is filed. The most pressing matters are the three internal
reconciliation errors in the audited financial statements (Issues 1--3),
which must be corrected with Archer Pryce LLP, and the Adjusted EBITDA
arithmetic error (Issue 4), which must be corrected in all
investor-facing materials. The data integrity issues in the equity grant
records (Issues 13--15) and the conflict regarding the General Counsel /
Chief Revenue Officer identity (Issue 14) warrant prompt investigation,
as they may indicate broader issues with the Company's equity
administration and internal controls that could be raised by the SEC or
the underwriters' counsel during due diligence.

The accompanying draft Form S-1 has been prepared using the audited
financial statements as the authoritative source for all financial
figures and includes risk factor and disclosure language addressing the
principal risks identified (controlled company status, dual-class
structure without a sunset, the Optivance litigation, customer
concentration, the term loan covenants and notice requirement, NOL
limitations, and dilution). Bracketed placeholders \[●\] indicate items
that require completion or confirmation before filing.

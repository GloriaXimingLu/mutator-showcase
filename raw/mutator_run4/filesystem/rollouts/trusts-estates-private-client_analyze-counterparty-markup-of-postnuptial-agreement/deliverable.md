## I. Executive Summary

On January 6, 2025, we received from Thomas Baxter of Hargrove Baxter
LLP a marked-up draft of the postnuptial agreement (the "Redline"),
transmitted with a cover email characterizing the edits as "modest
clarifications and a few requests for balanced treatment" that do not
"move us far from where we started." **That characterization is not
accurate.** The Redline is a substantial, top-to-bottom rewrite that,
behind a layer of definitional and formatting refinements, dismantles
nearly every protection we built into the original draft (the "Original
Draft") and strikes directly at each of Diane's five stated objectives.

The most consequential changes, none of which Mr. Baxter flagged in his
cover email, are:

1.  **Deletion of the \$1.2 million premarital-savings reimbursement
    right** --- Diane's single most important objective and the reason
    she engaged us. This is approximately a **\$600,000 swing** against
    Diane on the joint investment account alone.

2.  **Gutting of the financial-disclosure and voidability protections**
    --- replacing our "material omission" standard (no intent, no dollar
    threshold) with a heightened "intentional concealment + clear and
    convincing evidence + \$100,000 aggregate + two-year limitations"
    regime, and deleting the ongoing disclosure duty entirely. This is
    the precise outcome our intake analysis warned against, and it is
    especially dangerous given Marcus's documented pattern of financial
    concealment.

3.  **Reversal of the commingling/transmutation framework** ---
    converting our client-protective presumption (separate property used
    for the household becomes marital) into a Husband-protective
    reimbursement-only rule that insulates his \$3.8 million inheritance
    from any marital claim.

4.  **A new "Earned Income" definition that excludes bonuses, equity,
    and RSUs**, then cross-references that definition in the Marital
    Property clause --- a hidden term that could read variable
    compensation out of the marital estate, directly contradicting
    Diane's instruction that the bonus be treated as income "for all
    purposes."

5.  **A unilateral modification mechanism** allowing either party to
    amend the agreement by 90 days' written notice unless the other
    objects --- the one-sided change process Diane specifically asked us
    to prohibit.

Set against these, the Redline offers Diane a small number of genuinely
favorable items --- most notably an active/passive appreciation
framework for her practice interest (supported by the Strand valuation
report) and a set of maintenance provisions that, because Diane is the
higher-earning spouse, operate in her favor as the likely payor. These
appear designed to make the Redline look "balanced" and to shore up the
overall enforceability of an agreement that is otherwise heavily tilted
toward Marcus.

**Bottom-line recommendation:** We should not treat the Redline as a
near-final draft. We should prepare a written response before January 27
that (a) rejects the Tier 1 items below as non-negotiable, (b) signals
our willingness to accept the genuinely balanced items (active/passive
appreciation, maintenance framework, severability, execution
formalities), and (c) requires reconciliation of numerous factual
errors. The goal for the four-way conference should be a focused,
prioritized negotiation --- but our "short list" will be materially
longer than Mr. Baxter's email implies, because the Redline moved us a
great distance from the starting point on the issues that matter most to
Diane.

## V. Detailed Change Analysis

### A. Wife's Premarital-Savings Reimbursement Right --- **REJECT (non-negotiable)**

**Original Draft §5.2.** Diane holds a first-priority reimbursement
claim of \$1,200,000 against the joint investment account ("JIA"),
satisfied *before* any equal division. After reimbursement, the
remaining \$670,000 of appreciation is split 50/50 (\$335,000 each).
Result: Diane receives **\$1,535,000** from the JIA; Marcus receives
**\$335,000**.

**Redline.** Section 5.2 is deleted in its entirety (TC-22). Margin
comment MC-05 calls it "duplicative of Section 5.1 general property
division provisions." Under the Redline's general 50/50 division, the
\$1,870,000 JIA is split equally: **\$935,000 each**.

**Impact.** This is **not** duplicative --- it is the opposite of what
general 50/50 division produces. The reimbursement right is the
mechanism that returns Diane's traceable premarital contribution
*first*, before the marital residue is shared. Deleting it transfers
**approximately \$600,000 from Diane to Marcus** (\$1,535,000 →
\$935,000 for Diane; \$335,000 → \$935,000 for Marcus). The
financial-disclosure summary (Line W-5 / Line J-2) documents the tracing
in detail: \$1,200,000 wired from Diane's premarital Northbridge account
(acct. *0184, closed 2014) into the joint account (acct.* 3291), with
\$670,000 of appreciation during the marriage. The tracing is
well-supported and was the basis for our advice that a contractual
first-dollar reimbursement right is enforceable under 750 ILCS 5/503(c).

**Recommendation.** Restore §5.2 in full. This is Diane's #1 objective
and she has characterized it as non-negotiable. We should rebut the
"duplicative" characterization squarely in our response letter: the
general division provision yields \$935,000 to Diane, not \$1,535,000;
the reimbursement right is therefore not duplicative but operative and
essential.

### B. Financial Disclosure and Voidability --- **REJECT**

**Original Draft.** §3.1 "full, fair, and complete disclosure"; §3.3
voidable for *any* material omission or misrepresentation, "without
limitation," with no heightened evidentiary standard and no dollar
threshold; §3.4 a continuing duty to disclose material changes (new
assets/debts, income/employment changes, gifts/inheritances over
\$10,000) within 30 days.

**Redline.**

-   §1.5 (TC-06) and §12.1 (TC-41): disclosure is "substantially full" /
    "substantially complete" (not "full and complete").

-   §3.1(d) (TC-15): each party is "generally satisfied" (not
    "satisfied").

-   §12.2 (TC-42): the agreement is voidable **only if** a party proves
    by **clear and convincing evidence** that the other **intentionally
    concealed** assets/income/liabilities with an **aggregate value
    exceeding \$100,000**. Inadvertent errors or omissions are expressly
    excluded. A **two-year** limitations period runs from discovery.

-   The ongoing disclosure duty (orig. §3.4) is **deleted entirely**,
    with no replacement.

**Impact.** This is the exact outcome our November 5 intake analysis
warned against. Diane told us: *"If Marcus is hiding a \$10,000 account
or a \$50,000 account, I want to be able to void this agreement. I don't
want a provision that only kicks in if he hides a million dollars."* The
Redline installs a \$100,000 floor, an intent requirement, a
clear-and-convincing burden, and a two-year clock --- the precise
"heightened intentional-concealment standard or dollar-amount threshold"
we were directed to resist. It is particularly troubling in light of
Marcus's documented conduct: the \$68,000 hidden credit-card debt
discovered in mid-2024, and Diane's report of "at least two prior
occasions" of undisclosed purchases or accounts. The deletion of the
ongoing disclosure duty removes the transparency mechanism Diane
specifically requested as a guard against future concealment.

**Recommendation.** Restore "full, fair, and complete" disclosure
language; restore the §3.3 material-omission standard (no intent, no
dollar threshold, no heightened burden); delete the two-year limitations
period; restore the §3.4 ongoing disclosure duty. These are Tier 1.

### C. Ongoing Disclosure and Future-Debt Reporting --- **REJECT**

The Original Draft's §3.4 (ongoing disclosure of material changes) and
§11.3 (future debts over \$5,000 disclosed within 15 days; failure =
material breach) are both deleted in the Redline without replacement.
The Redline's §11.1 states only a general principle that each party is
responsible for individual-name debts. There is no continuing
transparency obligation of any kind.

**Impact.** Given that the precipitating event for this entire agreement
was Marcus's concealed debt, the absence of any ongoing disclosure or
debt-reporting duty is a significant regression and contradicts
Objective 2.

**Recommendation.** Restore §3.4 and §11.3. At minimum, require annual
disclosure exchanges and prompt written notice of any new individual
debt above an agreed threshold.

### D. Commingling, Transmutation, and Separate-Property Protection --- **REJECT / RENEGOTIATE**

**Original Draft §4.3.** Separate property commingled with marital
property is marital to the extent of commingling (subject to tracing).
Critically, §4.3(b) provided that separate property used for the joint
benefit of the household (mortgage, improvements, family expenses,
tuition, vacations) is **deemed a gift to the marriage and treated as
marital**, with a **rebuttable presumption of transmutation** and **no
requirement to prove donative intent**. This protected Diane: if Marcus
deployed inheritance funds into the household, those sums could be
pulled into the marital estate.

**Redline §4.3 (TC-18, TC-19, MC-03, MC-04).** Reverses the framework.
§4.3(b) weakens commingling to "may, depending on the circumstances,
result in reclassification... provided that tracing... remains
possible." §4.3(c) provides that separate property used for joint
benefit **"shall not be deemed commingled solely by reason of such
use,"** and the contributing party **"shall retain a claim for
reimbursement... with such property retaining its classification as
Separate Property."** §4.2 (TC-17) further grants Marcus "the sole and
exclusive right to manage, control, sell, transfer, encumber, or
otherwise dispose of his Separate Property without the consent or
joinder of Wife."

**Impact.** This insulates Marcus's \$3.8 million inheritance from the
marital estate. Under the Original Draft, if Marcus paid down the
\$612,000 mortgage or funded improvements with inheritance funds, Diane
could invoke the transmutation presumption to claim a marital interest.
Under the Redline, Marcus gets a reimbursement claim (repaid from the
marital estate) **and** the funds retain separate character --- he is
made whole and the inheritance is preserved. Diane flagged this exact
concern at intake: she "is concerned about how the funds might be
deployed during the marriage and the implications for property
classification." The Redline defeats that concern. The broad disposal
right in §4.2 compounds the exposure by allowing Marcus to dissipate
inheritance assets without Diane's consent.

**Recommendation.** Reject the transmutation reversal. We should propose
a balanced provision that (i) retains a reimbursement right for the
contributing party (a reasonable concession), but (ii) preserves a
rebuttable presumption of transmutation (or, at minimum, a
marital-equity interest) for substantial expenditures of separate
property for joint benefit --- particularly mortgage pay-downs and
capital improvements that increase equity in jointly held property. We
should also narrow §4.2's disposal right to ordinary management and
require consent for transactions that materially diminish the
separate-property pool or affect jointly held assets.

### E. Definition of Marital Property and the "Earned Income" Definition --- **REJECT (hidden term)**

**Original Draft §2.1(g).** Marital Property expressly "includes...
income, earnings, bonuses... equity compensation (including restricted
stock units, stock options...)... appreciation on Separate Property
attributable to marital efforts, contributions, or the investment of
marital funds... and any other property that would be classified as
marital property under 750 ILCS 5/503."

**Redline.** New §2.1(d) (TC-10, MC-02) defines "Earned Income" as base
salary, regular wages, and standard employment compensation, and
**excludes** "discretionary bonuses, commissions, equity compensation,
performance incentives, restricted stock or restricted stock units,
stock options, or other forms of variable or contingent compensation."
§2.1(b) (TC-08) and §5.1 (TC-21) then define Marital Property as "all
property acquired by either Party during the marriage **from Earned
Income or joint efforts**." The Original Draft's express inclusion of
bonuses, equity, and marital-effort appreciation is **removed**.

**Impact.** This is the most carefully disguised change in the Redline.
Presented as a "standard" definitional clarification for "calculating
contribution obligations" (MC-02), the "Earned Income" definition is
cross-referenced into the Marital Property clause. Read together,
property acquired from bonuses, equity, and RSUs is arguably *outside*
"Earned Income," and therefore arguably outside the Marital Property
definition. The §5.1 catch-all ("all other property acquired during the
marriage and not otherwise classified as Separate Property") may
recapture some of this, but the express exclusion creates ambiguity
Marcus could exploit to argue that bonuses and assets acquired with
bonuses are not marital. This directly contradicts (a) Diane's
instruction that the bonus be "treated as income for all purposes," (b)
the financial-disclosure classification of the bonus as "Marital (earned
during marriage)" (Line H-2), and (c) the Original Draft's express
inclusion. Separately, removing "appreciation on Separate Property
attributable to marital efforts... or the investment of marital funds"
from the Marital Property definition further narrows the marital estate
in Marcus's favor (his inheritance appreciation would be entirely
separate).

**Recommendation.** Reject the use of "Earned Income" to define Marital
Property. Restore the Original Draft's express inclusion of bonuses,
equity compensation, and RSUs as Marital Property, and restore the
marital-effort/marital-funds appreciation language. If Mr. Baxter wants
a defined term for the maintenance calculation, we can confine "Earned
Income" (or a differently named term) to Article X only, so it cannot
bleed into property classification.

### F. Wife's Practice Interest --- Active/Passive Appreciation --- **ACCEPT WITH REFINEMENTS**

**Original Draft §6.1(b).** *All* appreciation above the \$1,650,000
baseline is marital, "regardless of the source or cause," split 50/50.

**Redline §6.1 (TC-25, MC-06).** Only appreciation "directly
attributable to the active personal efforts of Wife" is marital;
appreciation from market forces, reimbursement-rate changes, regulatory
changes, passive investment returns, or other non-effort factors remains
Wife's separate property. A qualified forensic valuation expert
(mutually agreed or court-appointed) makes the determination.

**Impact.** This change is **favorable to Diane** and is supported by
the Strand Valuation Report. Section 9 of the Strand report decomposes
the practice's revenue growth: approximately **60% is passive**
(insurance-reimbursement rate increases ≈35%; demographic expansion
≈25%) and **≈40% is active** (physician recruitment ≈25%; operational
improvements ≈15%). The report's Application Note concludes that "a
significant majority of any appreciation... approximately 60%... would
likely be attributable to passive, market-driven factors." Under the
Original Draft, Marcus would take 50% of *all* appreciation; under the
Redline, he takes 50% only of the active slice --- roughly 50% × 40% =
**\~20% of total appreciation**. This is a meaningful gain for Diane.

We should nevertheless recognize why Husband's counsel offered it: the
Original Draft's "all appreciation is marital regardless of source" was
legally aggressive and vulnerable to an overreaching/unconscionability
challenge; conceding the recognized Illinois active/passive doctrine
removes that vulnerability and helps the overall (Marcus-favorable)
agreement withstand scrutiny. It also packages the Redline as
"balanced." We can accept it on the merits while keeping our focus on
the items that harm Diane.

**Refinements to negotiate.** (i) The "directly attributable to the
active personal efforts of Wife" standard is *stricter* than the
report's "active factors involving the physician-owners (including
Wife)." While a stricter standard favors Diane, it also invites
expensive, contentious forensic analysis at dissolution --- the very
"burdensome valuation methodology" Diane asked us to avoid (Objective
4). We should propose a streamlined process: pre-agree the Strand 60/40
attribution as a rebuttable default, cap valuation frequency, or specify
a single agreed firm. (ii) Lock the \$1,650,000 baseline and the Strand
methodology. (iii) Preserve the Redline's helpful acknowledgment that
the Operating Agreement's transfer restrictions control and that Wife
cannot be compelled to transfer the interest.

### G. Husband's RSUs --- Coverture Fraction --- **NEGOTIABLE**

**Original Draft §7.1--7.2.** The 12,000 RSUs are "entirely marital"
(grant and full vesting period within the marriage) and split 50/50.

**Redline §7.1 (TC-27, MC-07).** Applies a coverture fraction: Wife
receives 50% × (months of marriage during the "Relevant Service Period"
through Dissolution or vesting, whichever is earlier) ÷ (total months in
the Relevant Service Period). The "Relevant Service Period" runs from
Marcus's **hire date (March 15, 2018)** through the final vesting date
of the applicable tranche.

**Impact.** Because the marriage (June 2013) predates Marcus's hire
(March 2018), the entire service period falls within the marriage. For
any tranche that has **already vested** at dissolution, the fraction is
1.0 and Wife gets 50% --- identical to the Original Draft. The reduction
arises only for **unvested** tranches at dissolution. Illustrative
example (dissolution January 2026, after Tranche 1, before Tranches 2
and 3):

-   Tranche 2 (vests 1/1/2026): fraction ≈ 87/94 ≈ 0.93 → Wife ≈ 46.3%
    (vs. 50%).

-   Tranche 3 (vests 1/1/2027): fraction ≈ 87/106 ≈ 0.82 → Wife ≈ 41.0%
    (vs. 50%).

At the current \$38.00/share, the aggregate reduction is modest --- on
the order of **\$20,000--\$50,000** depending on timing --- though it
grows if dissolution occurs earlier and if the share price appreciates.
Notably, Mr. Baxter's choice of the **hire date** (rather than the grant
date) as the service-period start is *relatively favorable* to Diane,
because the pre-grant service (2018--2024) is counted in both numerator
and denominator, pulling the fraction closer to 1.0 than a grant-based
fraction would.

**Recommendation.** This is a recognized Illinois methodology and the
dollar impact is modest. We can accept the coverture framework in
exchange for concessions on Tier 1 items, provided the mechanics are
clear (counting method, treatment of the already-vested Tranche 1, and
the notice/documentation obligations, which the Redline usefully
strengthens). If we prefer to hold the line on Diane's "equal division"
expectation, we can seek to restore 50/50 as a fallback trade.

### H. Spousal Maintenance --- **ACCEPT (favorable to Diane), with one caution**

Diane earns \$487,000; Marcus earns \$305,000 (base \$215,000 + bonus
\$90,000). Diane is the higher earner and therefore the **payor**;
Marcus is the prospective **recipient**. Every maintenance change in the
Redline reduces Marcus's recovery and therefore favors Diane:

-   **§10.2 (TC-36, MC-08):** duration cap reduced from **5 years to 3
    years**.

-   **§10.3 (TC-37, MC-09):** new automatic, irrevocable waiver if the
    eligible (lower-earning) spouse's gross annual income exceeds
    **\$200,000**. Marcus's current gross (\$305,000) exceeds \$200,000,
    so he would be **waived entirely**.

-   **§10.4 (TC-38):** maintenance calculated on **Earned Income**
    (excluding bonuses/equity), further constraining the base.

**Impact.** Under the Original Draft (5-year cap, Gross Income, no
waiver), Marcus could have sought roughly **\$84,000/year for up to 5
years (≈\$422,000)** under the 750 ILCS 5/504(b-1) formula. Under the
Redline, the \$200,000 waiver eliminates his claim in the expected case
--- a potential **\~\$400,000+ savings** for Diane, contingent on
dissolution occurring while Marcus's income exceeds \$200,000. Margin
comment MC-09 calls the waiver "fair and reasonable" and says it
"benefits both parties equally." It does not: it eliminates the
*lower-earning* spouse's maintenance, and the lower-earning spouse here
is Marcus. The framing is misleading.

**Caution.** The \$200,000 threshold sits just below Marcus's base
salary (\$215,000). A modest income decline (loss of bonus, salary
adjustment, job change) could drop him below \$200,000 and **restore**
his eligibility --- at which point the 3-year cap and Earned-Income
calculation apply. The waiver is therefore less robust than it appears.
Separately, the Earned-Income calculation (excluding Marcus's bonus)
would, counterintuitively, *increase* the income disparity and could
*raise* any maintenance award in the sub-\$200,000 scenario. We should
accept these provisions as net-favorable but not over-rely on the
waiver, and we must ensure the "Earned Income" term is confined to
Article X (see Part V.E).

**Recommendation.** Accept the maintenance framework. Treat it as a
Marcus concession that we will not disturb --- but do not allow it to
distract from the certain property losses, and do not let "Earned
Income" migrate into the property definitions.

### I. Identified Debt (\$68,000) Indemnification --- **REJECT the de minimis threshold**

**Original Draft §11.1.** Marcus assumes sole responsibility for the
\$68,000, indemnifies Diane "without limitation as to the amount or
nature of individual claims," and Diane has no obligation to contribute.

**Redline §11.2 (TC-40, MC-10).** Marcus indemnifies Diane, **"provided
that such claims are material and exceed Five Thousand Dollars (\$5,000)
individually,"** and the indemnity "shall not extend to claims... less
than Five Thousand Dollars (\$5,000)." The Redline usefully adds a
24-month payoff requirement and quarterly written statements.

**Impact.** Diane told us she wants the debt assigned to Marcus "with no
loopholes or materiality thresholds." The \$5,000 de minimis is
precisely the kind of loophole she forbade. Credit-card collections
often arrive as multiple smaller charges or sub-\$5,000 actions; the
threshold could leave Diane exposed to nuisance claims below the floor.
The 24-month payoff and quarterly reporting are good and we should keep
them.

**Recommendation.** Delete the \$5,000 de minimis; restore "without
limitation as to the amount or nature of individual claims." Retain the
24-month payoff and quarterly-statement provisions.

### J. Marital Residence Buyout --- **NEGOTIATE TERMS**

**Original Draft §9.2.** Diane has a right of first refusal; 60 days to
exercise; payment within **24 months**; AFR interest; three-appraiser
tiebreaker if parties cannot agree.

**Redline §9.2 (TC-33, TC-34).** Diane has a first right of purchase;
**30 days** to exercise after receiving the appraisal; payment over **18
months**; AFR interest **compounded monthly**, **secured by a lien**;
adds Husband's right to purchase on the same terms if Diane declines;
appraisal within 60 days of commencement of dissolution proceedings.

**Impact.** The core protection Diane wanted --- a first right to
purchase so the children can remain in their home and school district
--- is preserved, which is good. The terms are tighter: 30 days (vs. 60)
to exercise and 18 months (vs. 24) to pay make it harder for Diane to
arrange financing. The lien is reasonable security for Marcus and is
acceptable. Adding Husband's purchase right if Diane declines is fair.

**Recommendation.** Accept the structure; negotiate the exercise period
back to **60 days** and the payment period back to **24 months** to give
Diane realistic financing room. Accept the lien and the Husband's
fallback purchase right.

### K. Attorney's Fees --- **REJECT**

**Original Draft §13.2.** Prevailing party recovers reasonable fees and
costs.

**Redline §13.2 (TC-44, MC-11).** Each party bears its own fees; mutual
waiver of any fee award. MC-11 frames this as promoting "amicable
resolution."

**Impact.** Diane asked for a prevailing-party provision to deter
frivolous challenges (Objective 5(d)). The mutual waiver removes her
ability to recover fees if she must enforce the agreement --- a real
concern given Marcus's history of concealment, which may require
enforcement. The "amicable resolution" framing cuts against Diane: the
party more likely to need to enforce (Diane, policing disclosure and the
reimbursement right) loses the fee incentive.

**Recommendation.** Restore the prevailing-party provision.

### L. Modification and Amendment --- **REJECT the unilateral mechanism**

**Original Draft §14.1.** Modifications only by a written instrument
signed by both parties; oral and unilateral modifications void.

**Redline §14.1--14.2 (TC-45, TC-46).** §14.1 says modification is
permitted "except as provided in this Article XIV." New §14.2 then
permits **either party to propose a modification by written notice that
becomes effective 90 days after delivery unless the other party delivers
a written objection** within that period.

**Impact.** This is the one-sided change process Diane specifically
asked us to prohibit (Objective 5(f): "a requirement that any
modifications be made only by mutual written consent, to prevent
one-sided changes"). A 90-day silent-acceptance mechanism is dangerous:
a missed deadline or overlooked notice could alter the agreement over
Diane's objection. It is also unusual for a domestic-relations agreement
and itself vulnerable to an unconscionability challenge.

**Recommendation.** Delete §14.2 entirely. Retain
mutual-written-consent-only (the Original Draft's §14.1). This should be
non-negotiable.

### M. Venue --- **REJECT**

**Original Draft §15.2.** Exclusive venue in the Circuit Court of Cook
County.

**Redline §15.2 (TC-47, MC-12).** Exclusive venue in the Circuit Court
of **DuPage County**, citing Marcus's office in Naperville and
"scheduling efficiency."

**Impact.** The marital residence (Winnetka) is in Cook County; the
property-tax records confirm Cook County jurisdiction. Diane, the
children, and our firm are in Cook County. DuPage is convenient for
Marcus's commute, not for the family or for Diane. This contradicts
Objective 5(e).

**Recommendation.** Restore Cook County venue.

### N. Sunset Clause --- **REJECT the shortening**

**Original Draft §15.4.** 15-year term (to ≈June 2040), renewable in
5-year increments.

**Redline §15.5.** 7-year term (to ≈June 2032).

**Impact.** Diane wanted the agreement to run at least until Julian
turns 18 (≈2033--2034) and preferably through ≈2040 (Objective 3). A
7-year sunset (≈2032) expires before Julian reaches majority. Mr. Baxter
frames the shorter term as encouraging periodic refresh; in substance it
shortens the protective window Diane sought.

**Strategic note.** Because the Redline as a whole tilts toward Marcus,
a shorter sunset would, perversely, revert Diane to default Illinois
equitable distribution sooner (where her tracing claim and the default
transmutation rules might be more favorable). But Diane's stated
preference is a longer term for the children's stability, and the
maintenance caps (which favor Diane) would also expire. The cleanest
position is to negotiate favorable terms *and* a longer sunset.

**Recommendation.** Restore at least a term running until Julian turns
18, and push for the original 15-year term with renewal.

## IX. Enforceability Considerations (750 ILCS 5/502)

-   **Procedural fairness.** The Redline's additions (severability,
    notarization/witnesses, 60-day review representation, counsel
    acknowledgments) improve the procedural-fairness profile and we
    should keep them. Independent counsel for both parties is
    documented.

-   **Substantive fairness.** Several Redline provisions --- the
    reimbursement deletion, the transmutation reversal, the "Earned
    Income" narrowing, the weakened disclosure remedies --- tilt the
    economics toward Marcus and could, if accepted, support a later
    challenge by Diane on overreaching/unconscionability grounds. We
    want an agreement that is *both* enforceable *and* fair to Diane,
    not one we would have to attack later. Restoring the Tier 1 items is
    the best way to ensure the agreement withstands scrutiny while
    protecting Diane.

-   **The unilateral modification mechanism (§14.2)** is itself
    vulnerable to challenge as unconscionable and should be removed on
    enforceability grounds as well as policy grounds.

-   **The active/passive and maintenance changes** improve the fairness
    profile (from Diane's perspective) and thus the agreement's overall
    enforceability --- another reason to accept them.

-   **Disclosure.** Full and fair disclosure is a prerequisite to
    enforceability. The "substantially complete" language and the
    heightened voidability standard are not only contrary to Diane's
    objectives but also weaken the disclosure foundation on which
    enforceability rests. Restoring robust disclosure protections serves
    both client objectives and enforceability.

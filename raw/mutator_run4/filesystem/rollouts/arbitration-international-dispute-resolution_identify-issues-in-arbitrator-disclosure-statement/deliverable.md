## I. Introduction and Executive Summary

This memorandum assesses the adequacy of the June 17, 2024 Arbitrator
Disclosure Statement submitted by Hon. Margaret R. Fairhaven (Ret.) in
the above-captioned matter, and evaluates the grounds on which either
party may challenge her appointment as Sole Arbitrator under AAA
Commercial Arbitration Rule R-18. It builds on the factual compilation

cross-references the arbitrator's appointment history data.

The arbitrator's disclosure is facially thorough in form --- it
addresses professional background, prior and pending arbitrations
involving counsel, financial interests, family relationships,

parties' counsel, her advisory-board service alongside Ellen Wu of

    President of R&D and the claimant's anticipated key expert witness

2.  **An undisclosed shared organizational affiliation** between the

3.  **An undisclosed CLE co-panelist relationship** between the

    topic directly germane to this dispute, hosted by the arbitrator's

4.  **An incomplete financial-interest disclosure** that disclaims only
    "direct" equity positions while leaving unaddressed a potential
    *indirect* interest in Whitmore Fund II LP --- the fund that owns
    Volaris, the direct beneficiary of the challenged license ---

5.  **An undisclosed pending arbitration** (*Halcyon Biotech v.

    omits the financial and structural terms of the arbitrator's

**Bottom line.** Several of these omissions fall squarely within
categories that AAA Rule R-17 requires be disclosed, and the cumulative
pattern gives rise to justifiable doubt as to the arbitrator's
impartiality or independence within the meaning of Rules R-16, R-17, and
R-18, as well as potential "evident partiality" under Section 10(a)(2)
of the Federal Arbitration Act. The grounds do not all run in the same
direction: the spouse--witness co-authorship (item 1) supports a
challenge by the **respondent**, while items 2--7 predominantly support
a challenge by the **claimant**. The challenge deadline under Rule R-18
is **June 24, 2024**. We recommend prompt action as set forth in Part
VII.

## II. Procedural Framework and Governing Standards

### A. AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules R-16, R-17, and R-18

**Rule R-16** establishes the general disclosure obligation and directs
that it "shall be construed broadly to further the goal of ensuring that
arbitrators are and remain impartial and independent." The standard of
"justifiable doubt as to the arbitrator's impartiality or independence"
is intended to "encompass all circumstances that a reasonable person
would consider relevant in evaluating an arbitrator's ability to render
a fair and unbiased determination."

**Rule R-17(a)** requires disclosure of, among other things:

-   **(i)** "\[a\]ny known direct or indirect financial or personal
    interest in the outcome of the arbitration, including any interest,
    whether legal or beneficial, in the subject matter of the dispute,
    in any party, or in any entity that may be affected by the result of
    the proceeding" (emphasis added);
-   **(ii)** "\[a\]ny known existing or past financial, business,
    professional, family, or social relationships with any party to the
    arbitration, its counsel, representatives, or witnesses that are
    likely to affect impartiality or that might reasonably create an
    appearance of partiality or bias," expressly "includ\[ing\], without
    limitation, relationships arising from employment, professional
    association, joint ventures, board memberships, academic
    collaboration, or regular social contact" (emphasis added);
-   **(iii)** "\[a\]ny prior or pending cases in which the arbitrator
    has been or is involved as arbitrator, counsel, expert, or in any
    other capacity, with any party to the arbitration, its counsel, or
    representatives," extending to matters before any institution; and
-   **(iv)** a catch-all covering "\[a\]ny other circumstances that
    might cause a reasonable party to question the arbitrator's ability
    to render a fair and impartial determination," expressly
    "includ\[ing\] but not limited to the arbitrator's prior
    publications, public statements, or expressed views on legal or
    factual issues relevant to the dispute."

**Rule R-17(b)** makes disclosure a **continuing obligation**: the
arbitrator "shall not await inquiry by a party or by the AAA before
making any required supplemental disclosure."

**Rule R-17(d)** provides that "\[t\]he failure of an arbitrator to make
complete and timely disclosures as required by this Rule may result in
the arbitrator's removal in accordance with Rule R-18, and may
constitute grounds for vacatur of any resulting award under applicable
law, including but not limited to Section 10 of the Federal Arbitration
Act (9 U.S.C. § 10)."

**Rule R-18(a)** governs challenges. A challenge "must be made by
written notice to the AAA and to the other party, stating with
specificity the grounds for the challenge, within **seven (7) calendar
days** after (i) the appointment of the challenged arbitrator has been
communicated to the challenging party, or (ii) the circumstances giving
rise to the challenge became known or should reasonably have become
known to the challenging party, whichever is later."

**Rule R-18(f)** provides that a party that fails to make a timely
challenge "shall be deemed to have waived its right to challenge the
arbitrator on the grounds then known to the party," except for "grounds
that were not and could not reasonably have been known at the time of
the deadline," and preserves the right to raise "evident partiality" in
a later proceeding to confirm, modify, or vacate an award.

### B. Federal Arbitration Act § 10(a)(2)

Section 10(a)(2) of the FAA, 9 U.S.C. § 10(a)(2), permits vacatur of an
arbitral award "where there was evident partiality or corruption in the
arbitrators, or either of them." "Evident partiality" has been read to
require disclosure of facts that might reasonably lead a party to
question the arbitrator's impartiality, even absent actual bias.
Nondisclosure of relationships falling within Rule R-17's categories is
the paradigmatic evident-partiality fact pattern.

### C. IBA Guidelines on Conflicts of Interest in International Arbitration

Although not binding, the IBA Guidelines are widely consulted as a
disclosure benchmark and are expressly referenced in the AAA rules
excerpts. Their General Standards provide that (1) every arbitrator must
be impartial and independent; (2) conflicts extend to the arbitrator's
law firm and to the arbitrator's family members and close personal
relationships; and (3) "if in doubt, disclose." The Guidelines
categorize circumstances into a Non-Waivable Red List, a Waivable Red
List, an Orange List (circumstances that may raise doubts and require
disclosure, deemed waived if no objection within 30 days), and a Green
List (no disclosure needed). Several of the matters identified below
fall within the Orange List at a minimum.

### D. AAA/ABA Code of Ethics for Arbitrators in Commercial Disputes

Canon II of the Code imposes a duty to disclose "any interest or
relationship likely to affect impartiality or that might reasonably
create an appearance of partiality," and Canon I requires upholding the
integrity and fairness of the process. The Code reinforces the
broad-construction approach of Rule R-17.

### E. The Challenge Deadline

The arbitrator's disclosure is dated June 17, 2024. The
seven-calendar-day deadline under Rule R-18(a) therefore falls on **June
24, 2024** --- a date the arbitrator herself identified in her
disclosure. For circumstances that "became known or should reasonably
have become known" to a party after June 17 (for example, facts surfaced
through the research reflected in our June 19 memorandum), the
later-knowledge prong of Rule R-18(a)(ii) may extend the window for
those specific grounds. Prudence nonetheless counsels filing any
challenge on or before June 24, 2024.

## IV. Identified Disclosure Deficiencies

### A. Undisclosed Academic Collaboration Between the Arbitrator's Spouse and the Claimant's Key Expert Witness

**Facts.** The arbitrator identified her husband, Dr. Alan Fairhaven, as
a retired University of Minnesota mechanical engineering professor and
affirmatively represented that he "has no financial or professional
relationship with Cascade Thermal Systems Inc., Whitmore Capital
Partners LLC, or any of their officers, directors, or employees." Public
academic publication records show, however, that Dr. Alan Fairhaven
co-authored a peer-reviewed article with **Dr. Rita S. Peshkov** ---
Cascade's Vice President of Research & Development and the inventor or
co-inventor on the majority of Cascade's ThermalCore patents, whom the
arbitration demand identifies as the claimant's anticipated key expert
witness. The article, "Optimization of Phase-Change Heat Exchangers in
High-Load Industrial Applications," was published in the *Journal of
Advanced Thermal Engineering*, Vol. 42, Issue 3 (September 2022). Its
subject matter --- phase-change heat exchangers in high-load industrial
applications --- is directly on point with the ThermalCore technology
whose valuation is the central issue in this arbitration.

**Analysis.** This omission implicates Rule R-17(a)(ii), which expressly
reaches "professional ... relationships with any party ... its counsel,
representatives, or witnesses" and specifically enumerates "academic
collaboration" as a disclosable relationship. The IBA Guidelines extend
the conflict analysis to the arbitrator's family members (General
Standard 2), and a co-authorship within the past three years with a
witness is, at minimum, an Orange-List circumstance requiring
disclosure. The omission is compounded by the arbitrator's affirmative
representation that her husband has *no* professional relationship with
Cascade or its officers or employees --- a statement that is difficult
to reconcile with a peer-reviewed co-authorship with Cascade's VP of
R&D. Dr. Peshkov is not a peripheral witness; she is the claimant's lead
technical expert on the very technology whose fair-market value the
tribunal must determine, and her credibility and the weight given her
testimony may be decisive.

**Direction of bias / party favored.** This ground supports a challenge
by the **respondent** (Whitmore), because it suggests a potential
channel of favorability toward the claimant through the arbitrator's
spouse and the claimant's central witness.

**Assessment.** **Strong.** The relationship falls squarely within an
expressly enumerated disclosure category, involves a key witness on the
central issue, and is accompanied by an arguably inaccurate affirmative
representation. This is among the most serious deficiencies identified.

### B. Undisclosed Shared Organizational Affiliation with the Respondent's Principal (Garrett T. Whitmore)

**Facts.** The arbitrator disclosed her own advisory-board service on
the Minnesota ADR Ethics Institute and disclosed that Ellen Wu (a Barrow
& Thistlewood partner) also serves on that advisory board. She did
**not** disclose that **Garrett T. Whitmore** --- the founder, managing
partner, and principal of the respondent --- serves on the **Board of
Trustees** of the same Institute, as shown by the Institute's IRS Form
990 for fiscal year 2023. The Board of Trustees is the Institute's
governing body, responsible for strategic direction, budgetary
oversight, and appointments to leadership positions, including the
advisory board on which the arbitrator serves. The Institute is
described as a small nonprofit whose bylaws and posted minutes reflect
annual meetings and periodic joint sessions between the Board of
Trustees and the advisory board.

**Analysis.** Rule R-17(a)(ii) expressly reaches relationships with "any
party ... its counsel, \[or\] representatives" arising from "board
memberships." The arbitrator recognized the analogous relationship with
Ms. Wu as disclosable but omitted the relationship with the respondent's
own principal --- an inconsistency that itself undermines the
completeness of the disclosure. Although the advisory board and the
Board of Trustees are formally distinct bodies, they serve the same
small organization, the Trustees govern and appoint the advisory board,
and the overlapping meeting structure reasonably supports an inference
of direct personal interaction. Under the IBA Guidelines, shared
organizational service with a party's principal is an Orange-List
circumstance warranting disclosure.

**Direction of bias / party favored.** This ground supports a challenge
by the **claimant** (Cascade), because it suggests a potential channel
of favorability toward the respondent through the arbitrator's
organizational relationship with the respondent's principal.

**Assessment.** **Moderate to strong.** The relationship is professional
and organizational rather than financial, and the two bodies are
formally distinct, which somewhat limits its force. But the small size
of the organization, the governance relationship between the two bodies,
the joint meeting structure, and --- most importantly --- the
arbitrator's inconsistent treatment of the analogous Wu relationship
combine to support justifiable doubt.

### C. Undisclosed CLE Co-Panelist Relationship with the Respondent's Lead Counsel (Dominic Hargrave)

**Facts.** On October 14, 2023 --- approximately five months before the
arbitration demand and eight months before the arbitrator's appointment
--- the arbitrator and **Dominic Hargrave** (lead counsel for the
respondent in this matter) served as co-panelists at a Continuing Legal
Education event titled "Arbitrating Complex IP Licensing Disputes,"
hosted by the Lakeview Dispute Resolution Center. The panel addressed
valuation methodologies, arbitrator selection in technology-intensive
cases, and the management of expert testimony --- topics directly
germane to this arbitration, which turns on the fair-market valuation of
a technology license and the management of technical expert testimony.
The arbitrator's disclosure does not reference this event or her
co-panelist relationship with Mr. Hargrave.

**Analysis.** This omission implicates both Rule R-17(a)(ii) (a
professional relationship with counsel) and Rule R-17(a)(iv) (the
catch-all, which expressly reaches "the arbitrator's prior publications,
public statements, or expressed views on legal or factual issues
relevant to the dispute"). A CLE panel on IP-licensing-dispute
arbitration is precisely the kind of forum in which an arbitrator may
express views on valuation methodology, the handling of expert
witnesses, or arbitrator-selection practices that bear on this dispute.
The fact that the event was hosted by Lakeview --- the arbitrator's own
affiliated institution --- and that it occurred recently and with the
very lawyer now leading the opposing party's case, heightens the
concern. Under the IBA Guidelines, a co-panelist relationship with
counsel within the preceding three years is an Orange-List circumstance
requiring disclosure.

**Direction of bias / party favored.** This ground supports a challenge
by the **claimant**, because it suggests a professional relationship
with the respondent's lead counsel and possible pre-formed views
favorable to the respondent's positions.

**Assessment.** **Moderate to strong.** A single CLE co-panelist
appearance is, in isolation, a relatively modest professional contact.
What elevates it here is the convergence of four factors: (1) the
co-panelist is the respondent's lead counsel; (2) the topic was directly
germane to the central issues in this arbitration; (3) the event was
hosted by the arbitrator's own affiliated institution; and (4) the
catch-all provision specifically targets expressed views on relevant
legal or factual issues, which a CLE panel of this kind may well have
produced.

### D. Incomplete Disclosure of Indirect Financial Interest in Whitmore Fund II LP

**Facts.** The arbitrator disclosed that her portfolio is managed on a
discretionary basis by Northbridge Wealth Advisors and stated that she
holds "no direct equity positions in any party to this arbitration." She
acknowledged that she had not undertaken a position-by-position review
of pooled vehicles and stated she was "not aware of any" indirect
interest that would be material. Public SEC filings show that
Northbridge Wealth Advisors also manages the **Northbridge Select
Alternatives Fund**, a fund-of-funds product whose most recent public
filing (March 2024) reports a **2.3% limited partner interest in
Whitmore Fund II LP**. Whitmore Fund II LP owns Volaris Manufacturing
Group LLC --- the direct beneficiary of the challenged below-market
license. The economic outcome of this arbitration (rescission,
renegotiation, repricing, or damages against Whitmore) could affect the
value of Whitmore Fund II LP's portfolio and, derivatively, the value of
interests in that fund.

**Analysis.** Rule R-17(a)(i) expressly reaches "**indirect** financial
... interest\[s\] in the outcome of the arbitration, including any
interest ... in any party, or in any entity that may be affected by the
result of the proceeding" (emphasis added). The arbitrator's framing ---
"no direct equity positions" --- is technically accurate but sidesteps
the indirect-interest question that the rule squarely poses. The
potential chain is: arbitrator's portfolio (managed by Northbridge) →
possible allocation to the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund (managed
by the same firm) → 2.3% LP interest in Whitmore Fund II LP → ownership
of Volaris → beneficiary of the challenged license. The arbitrator's own
acknowledgment that she had not reviewed her pooled-vehicle holdings is,
in effect, a concession that she could not rule out such an interest.

**Important caveat.** The public record does **not** confirm that the
arbitrator's personal portfolio includes an allocation to the
Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund. The interest is therefore
*potential* rather than established. Moreover, a 2.3% LP interest held
indirectly through a fund-of-funds is, in dollar terms, likely modest.
These considerations temper, but do not eliminate, the concern: Rule
R-17 and the IBA Guidelines require disclosure where a reasonable basis
exists to believe an indirect interest may be present, so that the
parties can evaluate it. The arbitrator's disclosure did not perform
that function.

**Direction of bias / party favored.** This ground supports a challenge
by the **claimant**, because any indirect financial interest is aligned
with the respondent's fund family.

**Assessment.** **Moderate.** The interest is indirect, layered through
multiple intermediaries, and unconfirmed as to the arbitrator's actual
holdings. Standing alone it may not compel disqualification. But the
disclosure's "no direct equity positions" framing is a meaningful gap
given Rule R-17(a)(i)'s express coverage of indirect interests in
entities "affected by the result," and it warrants a targeted
supplemental inquiry.

### E. Undisclosed Pending Arbitration (*Halcyon Biotech v. Stonewall Partners*)

**Facts.** The arbitrator disclosed two pending matters involving Barrow
& Thistlewood LLP: the instant case and *Orion Fabrication v. Mountain
West Supply* (AAA Case No. 01-23-0002856). Public AAA records identify a
**third** pending matter, *Halcyon Biotech v. Stonewall Partners* (AAA
Case No. 01-24-0003105), in which Barrow & Thistlewood LLP represents a
party; the arbitrator was appointed on April 22, 2024, the matter is in
the pre-hearing phase, and estimated remaining fees are approximately
\$175,000. This matter does not appear in the arbitrator's disclosure,
notwithstanding her certification that her listed matters were "a
complete list."

**Analysis.** Rule R-17(a)(iii) requires disclosure of "\[a\]ny prior or
pending cases in which the arbitrator has been or is involved ... with
any party ... its counsel, or representatives." The omission of a
pending matter involving the respondent's counsel is a direct failure
under this provision, and the arbitrator's express certification of
completeness makes the omission more, not less, significant. Whether
inadvertent or otherwise, the result is that the disclosure understates
the number of concurrent pending engagements the arbitrator shares with
the respondent's counsel.

**Direction of bias / party favored.** This ground supports a challenge
by the **claimant**, because it reflects an additional ongoing
professional and economic relationship with the respondent's counsel.

**Assessment.** **Strong (as a disclosure failure).** The omission is
clear and falls squarely within an enumerated disclosure category. Its
substantive weight is amplified by the repeat-player economics discussed
in Part IV.F.

**Analysis.** Two distinct concerns arise.

*First, the undisclosed prior matters.* Rule R-17(a)(iii) requires
disclosure of prior cases involving the parties' counsel. The arbitrator
disclosed six of the completed matters involving the parties' counsel
but omitted at least seven others identifiable from the case-by-case
data. Each omission is a discrete Rule R-17(a)(iii) failure, and the
arbitrator's certification of completeness aggravates the pattern.

*Second, the repeat-player economics.* Repeat appointments from a single
firm are common in the arbitration market and are not, by themselves,
disqualifying; the IBA Guidelines generally treat repeat appointments as
a Green-List matter. Here, however, the concentration is pronounced: a
single firm accounts for roughly 45--48% of the arbitrator's recent
matters and income, that firm's clients prevail at a high rate (77.8%),
the arbitrator was that firm's client's first-choice candidate, and the
arbitrator concurrently holds three pending matters with that firm (one
of them undisclosed). The economic interest an arbitrator has in
*future* appointments from a major referrer is not a "direct" interest
in the outcome of any single case, but the cumulative pattern --- income
concentration, win-rate asymmetry, first-choice ranking, and concurrent
pending matters --- is the kind of circumstance that "might reasonably
create an appearance of partiality or bias" within Rule R-17(a)(ii) and
that a "reasonable party" might question under Rule R-17(a)(iv).

**Direction of bias / party favored.** This ground supports a challenge
by the **claimant**, because the pattern runs toward the respondent's
counsel.

**Assessment.** The **undisclosed prior matters are a strong, clear
disclosure failure.** The **repeat-player economics are moderate as a
standalone ground** (repeat appointments are generally tolerated) but
**materially amplify** the other claimant-favoring grounds ---
particularly the undisclosed *Halcyon* pending matter (Part IV.E), the
CLE co-panelist relationship (Part IV.C), and the Lakeview referral
dependency (Part IV.G). Considered cumulatively, the pattern is
significant.

### G. Incomplete Disclosure of the Lakeview Dispute Resolution Center Affiliation

**Facts.** The arbitrator disclosed her affiliation with Lakeview as a
neutral and acknowledged that Lakeview receives case referrals from both
parties' counsel. The disclosure did not address the financial or
structural terms of the affiliation --- for example, whether Lakeview
takes a percentage of the arbitrator's fees for referred matters,
whether the arbitrator is an employee, independent contractor, or
equity/member-owner of Lakeview, or the extent to which Lakeview's
revenue model depends on maintaining referral relationships with the
parties' counsel. Lakeview also hosted the October 14, 2023 CLE event at
which the arbitrator and Mr. Hargrave co-presented (Part IV.C).

**Analysis.** Rule R-17(a)(i) and (ii) reach financial and business
relationships that may affect impartiality or create an appearance of
partiality. If the arbitrator's compensation is tied to Lakeview's
receipt of referrals from Barrow & Thistlewood (the appointment data
confirm that firm is a substantial source of the arbitrator's
engagements), there is a chain of economic interdependency between the
arbitrator and the respondent's counsel that the disclosure did not
explore. The disclosure was therefore partial: it acknowledged the
affiliation and the existence of referrals but omitted the financial
mechanics that determine whether the affiliation creates an economic
interest.

**Direction of bias / party favored.** This ground supports a challenge
by the **claimant**, because the economic interdependency runs toward
the respondent's counsel.

**Assessment.** **Moderate.** Institutional affiliations with ADR
providers are ordinary and generally acceptable, and the present matter
was administered by the AAA rather than Lakeview. The concern arises
from the combination of the undisclosed financial terms, Lakeview's
referral dependence on the respondent's counsel, and Lakeview's role as
host of the undisclosed CLE. Standing alone this is a secondary point;
in combination with Parts IV.C and IV.F it contributes to the cumulative
picture.

## V. Analysis: Strength of Grounds for Challenge

### A. Grounds Favoring a Challenge by the Respondent (Whitmore)

The respondent's strongest ground is **Part IV.A --- the undisclosed
academic collaboration between the arbitrator's spouse and the
claimant's key expert witness, Dr. Peshkov.** This is a clear Rule
R-17(a)(ii) omission (the rule expressly names "academic collaboration"
and "witnesses"), it involves the claimant's central expert on the
dispositive technical issue, the subject matter of the collaboration
overlaps directly with the technology in dispute, and it is accompanied
by an arguably inaccurate affirmative representation that the
arbitrator's spouse has no professional relationship with Cascade or its
employees. A reasonable party in the respondent's position could
justifiably doubt the arbitrator's ability to evaluate Dr. Peshkov's
testimony with complete neutrality, and the omission is precisely the
kind of fact that supports "evident partiality" under FAA § 10(a)(2) if
left unremedied.

### B. Grounds Favoring a Challenge by the Claimant (Cascade)

The claimant has a substantial, multi-pronged case:

-   **Part IV.E (undisclosed *Halcyon* pending matter)** and **Part IV.F
    (undisclosed prior matters)** are clear Rule R-17(a)(iii) failures,
    aggravated by the arbitrator's certification of completeness.
-   **Part IV.C (CLE co-panelist with lead counsel on a germane topic)**
    is a Rule R-17(a)(ii)/(iv) omission with direct subject-matter
    overlap and a possible "expressed views" dimension.
-   **Part IV.B (shared organizational service with the respondent's
    principal)** is a Rule R-17(a)(ii) omission, aggravated by
    inconsistent treatment of the analogous Wu relationship.
-   **Part IV.D (indirect financial interest in Whitmore Fund II LP)**
    is a Rule R-17(a)(i) gap, tempered by the indirect and unconfirmed
    nature of the interest but significant because the rule expressly
    reaches indirect interests in entities "affected by the result."
-   **Part IV.G (incomplete Lakeview disclosure)** and the
    **repeat-player economics in Part IV.F** supply the connective
    tissue that links the CLE, the referral dependency, the income
    concentration, the win-rate asymmetry, and the first-choice ranking
    into a coherent appearance-of-partiality picture.

### C. Cumulative Assessment

No single claimant-favoring ground (other than the clear Rule
R-17(a)(iii) omissions) is, in isolation, necessarily disqualifying. But
Rule R-16 directs that the disclosure obligation "shall be construed
broadly," and Rule R-17(a)(iv) is an express catch-all. The grounds here
do not appear in isolation: they form a pattern in which the arbitrator
(1) shares ongoing and undisclosed professional engagements with the
respondent's counsel; (2) co-presented with that counsel on the very
subject of this dispute; (3) serves an organization governed by the
respondent's principal; (4) may hold an indirect financial stake in a
fund that benefits from the challenged transaction; and (5) derives a
substantial share of her income from the respondent's counsel's
referrals. The convergence of these facts is more than the sum of its
parts and, in our assessment, clears the "justifiable doubt" threshold
of Rules R-16 and R-17 for a reasonable party.

The respondent's ground (Part IV.A) is independently strong and runs in
the opposite direction. The net effect is that **both parties have
colorable grounds for challenge**, and the arbitrator's disclosure ---
as written --- does not adequately equip either party to make an
informed waiver decision, which is the core purpose of Rule R-17.

## VI. Procedural and Strategic Considerations

### A. The June 24, 2024 Deadline

The seven-calendar-day challenge deadline under Rule R-18(a) runs from
the June 17, 2024 disclosure and expires on **June 24, 2024**. Any
challenge must be in writing, served on the AAA and the opposing party,
and must "stat\[e\] with specificity the grounds for the challenge."
Rule R-18(a)(ii)'s later-knowledge prong may extend the window for
grounds that a party could not reasonably have known from the disclosure
alone (for example, the undisclosed matters surfaced through
public-record research), but relying on that extension is riskier than
meeting the June 24 date.

### B. Request for Supplemental Disclosure

Rule R-17(d) authorizes the AAA, in its discretion, to "bring any
disclosure deficiency to the attention of the arbitrator and request
supplemental disclosure before acting on any challenge." Before or
alongside any formal challenge, counsel may consider requesting that the
AAA direct the arbitrator to supplement her disclosure with respect to:
(1) the spouse--Peshkov co-authorship and any other collaboration or
contact between Dr. Alan Fairhaven and Dr. Peshkov; (2) Garrett T.
Whitmore's service on the Institute's Board of Trustees and the nature
of any interaction between the arbitrator and Mr. Whitmore; (3) the
October 14, 2023 CLE co-panelist relationship with Mr. Hargrave and any
views the arbitrator expressed on IP-licensing valuation or
expert-testimony management; (4) whether the arbitrator's portfolio
holds any interest in the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund or any
other vehicle with exposure to Whitmore Fund II LP, Whitmore Fund III
LP, Cascade, or Volaris; (5) the *Halcyon Biotech v. Stonewall Partners*
pending matter; (6) the additional completed matters involving the
parties' counsel listed in Part IV.F; and (7) the financial and
structural terms of the Lakeview affiliation. A candid supplemental
disclosure may resolve some concerns (for example, by confirming that
the arbitrator holds no Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund interest)
and sharpen others.

### C. Waiver and the Continuing Duty

Rule R-18(f) deems a party that fails to timely challenge to have waived
grounds "then known" to it, but preserves (i) grounds "that were not and
could not reasonably have been known at the time of the deadline" and
(ii) the right to raise "evident partiality" in a later
confirmation/vacatur proceeding. Because the deficiencies identified
here were largely *not disclosed*, a party learning of them through
public-record research has a strong argument that they were not "known"
within the meaning of Rule R-18(f) at the deadline. Nonetheless, now
that the research has surfaced these facts (June 19, 2024), prompt
action is advisable to avoid any waiver argument. Rule R-17(b)'s
continuing-duty obligation also means the arbitrator herself should
supplement regardless of any party request.

### D. Evident Partiality and Post-Award Vacatur Risk

Even if no pre-award challenge is pursued (or if a challenge is denied
by the AAA, whose decision is administrative and non-appealable within
the AAA process under Rule R-18(e)), the undisclosed facts create a
meaningful post-award risk. Rule R-17(d) expressly ties disclosure
failures to vacatur grounds, and FAA § 10(a)(2)'s "evident partiality"
standard has been applied to nondisclosure of relationships a reasonable
party would want to know. An award issued over these undisclosed facts
--- particularly the spouse--witness co-authorship and the indirect
financial interest --- would carry vacatur risk that could undo a
substantial investment of time and fees (estimated at \~\$340,000 in
arbitrator fees alone, plus party costs). This risk is relevant to both
parties: the prevailing party's interest in an enforceable award, and
the non-prevailing party's interest in preserving vacatur options.

## VII. Recommendations

1.  **Act before June 24, 2024.** Any party intending to challenge
    should file a written, specific challenge with the AAA and serve it

3.  **For the respondent:** The spouse--Peshkov co-authorship (Part

## VIII. Conclusion

The arbitrator's June 17, 2024 disclosure statement, while comprehensive
in form, contains multiple material omissions when measured against the
publicly available source documents. The most serious are: (1) the
undisclosed academic collaboration between the arbitrator's spouse and
the claimant's key expert witness on a paper directly concerning the
technology in dispute; (2) the undisclosed pending *Halcyon* matter and
additional undisclosed prior matters involving the parties' counsel; (3)
the undisclosed CLE co-panelist relationship with the respondent's lead
counsel on a directly germane topic; (4) the undisclosed shared
organizational service with the respondent's principal; and (5) the
incomplete treatment of a potential indirect financial interest in
Whitmore Fund II LP. Several of these fall squarely within expressly
enumerated Rule R-17 categories, and the cumulative pattern gives rise
to justifiable doubt as to the arbitrator's impartiality or
independence. Both parties have colorable grounds for challenge --- the
respondent principally on the spouse--witness relationship, the claimant
on the cluster of relationships and economics running toward the
respondent's counsel. Given the June 24, 2024 deadline and the
post-award vacatur risk under FAA § 10(a)(2), we recommend prompt,
specific action and a contemporaneous request for supplemental
disclosure.

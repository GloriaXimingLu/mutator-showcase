       1.0 Executive Summary  
      This memorandum summarizes the environmental issues identified during our review of the Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (    “    Phase I ESA    ”    ) and supporting environmental and transactional documents prepared in connection with Cornerstone Industrial Partners LLC’s (    “    Buyer    ”         or         “    Cornerstone    ”    ) proposed acquisition of the approximately 14.7-acre former manufacturing/warehouse complex located at 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo, Lucas County, Ohio 43611 (the         “    Property    ”    ) from Breckenridge Manufacturing Co. (    “    Seller    ”    ). The acquisition is being financed in part by a $6,125,000 loan from Great Lakes Capital Bank (the         “    Lender    ”    ).  
          Overall Environmental Risk Rating: HIGH.         The Property was used for metal stamping, trichloroethylene (TCE) vapor degreasing (1961–1992), and hexavalent chromium/nickel electroplating (1965–2004) before being converted to warehouse use in December 2019. The Phase I ESA (Greenfield Environmental Consultants Inc., dated October 25, 2024) identified two on-site recognized environmental conditions (    “    RECs    ”    ), and our review of the supporting record identifies several additional material concerns — including an off-site tetrachloroethylene (PCE) groundwater plume migrating toward the Property, an active leaking drum of waste TCE, a 39-year gap in hazardous waste disposal records, a prior Ohio EPA Notice of Violation, two unregistered underground storage tanks (    “    USTs    ”    ), and apparent inaccuracies in the Seller’s environmental representations and warranties.  
          Headline findings:  
              Two CRITICAL on-site RECs         — (i) former TCE vapor degreasing with elevated photoionization detector (    “    PID    ”    ) readings (42.3 ppm), no closure documentation, and a documented 1988 solvent spill; and (ii) 39 years of hexavalent chromium electroplating with visible chromium staining, an uncharacterized sump containing liquid with an iridescent sheen, and chemically degraded concrete.  
              A HIGH-severity off-site PCE groundwater plume         from the former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility (120 feet upgradient), with PCE at 87 µg/L (17.4× the Ohio standard of 5 µg/L) migrating south-southeast toward the Property, which lies within a designated Groundwater Management Zone. Notably, the Phase I ESA             did not classify this condition as a REC    , which appears inconsistent with the Lender’s express due-diligence requirements.  
              Apparent breaches of the Seller’s environmental representations         in the Asset Purchase Agreement (the         “    Purchase Agreement    ”    ), including the         “    no releases    ”         representation (§ 4.12(a)), the         “    no Environmental Notices    ”         representation (§ 4.12(c)), and the completeness of the UST schedule (§ 4.13 / Schedule 4.13).  
              Lender funding conditions are not satisfied.         Because RECs were identified, the Lender will not fund on the Phase I ESA alone; a Phase II ESA, Pollution Legal Liability insurance, and potentially a remediation escrow are required, on a timeline that runs concurrently with the Buyer’s October 31, 2024 termination right.  
          Headline recommendations:         (1) Commission a Phase II ESA immediately, scoped to both on-site RECs, the off-site PCE plume/vapor intrusion, the unregistered USTs, and the former outdoor drum storage area; (2) evaluate the § 6.3(b) termination right before October 31, 2024; (3) demand Seller corrections to the environmental representations and Schedule 4.13, and negotiate price/indemnity/escrow concessions; (4) satisfy the Lender’s Phase II/insurance/escrow deliverables by the November 15–30, 2024 deadlines; and (5) arrange immediate removal of the abandoned waste TCE drum.             Closing should not occur until the Phase II results, Lender conditions, and representation issues are resolved.

        5.0 Detailed Discussion of Issues  
       5.1 On-Site Recognized Environmental Conditions  
       Issue 1 — Former TCE Vapor Degreasing: Potential Chlorinated Solvent Release to Soil and Groundwater  
          Severity: CRITICAL         |             Phase I ESA REC No. 1  
      TCE was used as a vapor degreasing solvent in the northwest corner of the main manufacturing building from 1961 through approximately 1992 — a period of more than 30 years. The Phase I ESA documented the following indicators of a release to the subsurface:  
              Elevated PID reading of 42.3 ppm         recorded at a crack in the floor slab of the former degreasing area, against background readings of less than 0.5 ppm elsewhere in the building — a strong indicator of residual volatile organic compound vapors emanating from beneath the slab.  
              Visible dark staining         penetrating the concrete floor slab, with multiple cracks (up to ¼-inch), and staining on interior walls up to ~3 feet (consistent with vapor condensation/splash).  
              No closure documentation, decommissioning records, or confirmation sampling         was located for the degreasing area following the reported cessation of TCE use in 1992.  
          A             1988 chemical spill         of         “    unknown quantity of degreasing solvent    ”         from an overturned drum is documented in City of Toledo Fire Department records; cleanup was performed by Seller’s employees with no follow-up environmental assessment.  
          Why CRITICAL.         TCE is a known human carcinogen and a dense, persistent chlorinated solvent that forms dense non-aqueous phase liquids (DNAPL) capable of penetrating deep into aquifers and generating long-lived groundwater plumes and soil-vapor sources. The combination of 30+ years of use, an elevated PID reading two orders of magnitude above background, undocumented closure, and a documented spill presents a strong likelihood of a release to soil and potentially groundwater beneath the building. This condition drives both direct-contact/vapor-intrusion risk and potentially significant remediation cost. A Phase II ESA (soil borings through the slab, groundwater monitoring wells, and soil-vapor sampling) is required.  
        Issue 2 — Former Hexavalent Chromium Electroplating: Potential Metals Release; Uncharacterized Sump Liquid  
          Severity: CRITICAL         |             Phase I ESA REC No. 2  
      Electroplating using hexavalent chromium and nickel solutions was conducted in the south wing of the main building from 1965 through 2004 — approximately 39 years. Observed indicators include:  
              Prominent blue-green staining         on concrete floors and lower walls (to ~4 feet), consistent with chromium residues, concentrated where plating tanks were historically located.  
              Chemical degradation (pitting, etching, surface erosion)         of the concrete floor consistent with prolonged exposure to acidic/alkaline plating solutions — a pathway for migration through the slab.  
          An             in-ground concrete sump         (~3 ft × 3 ft × ~3 ft deep) in the center of the room containing approximately             four inches of liquid with a visible iridescent sheen         and a greenish tint; the source and composition are undetermined (not sampled).  
          Hazardous waste disposal documentation exists             only for 2004         (six manifests; 14,200 lbs; RCRA codes D007 chromium and F006 electroplating sludge).             No manifests or disposal records exist for 1965–2003         (see Issue 7).  
          A             2003 Ohio EPA Notice of Violation         for improper hazardous waste storage (see Issue 8).  
          Why CRITICAL.         Hexavalent chromium is highly toxic and carcinogenic. Thirty-nine years of plating operations with visible residual contamination, an uncharacterized sump containing liquid with a sheen, and a chemically degraded concrete floor present a strong likelihood of metals release to the subsurface. The sump liquid itself may constitute an uncharacterized hazardous waste accumulation. A Phase II ESA is required, including characterization of the sump liquid, soil borings through/beneath the slab, and groundwater monitoring wells analyzed for hexavalent and total chromium, nickel, and related metals.  
         5.2 Active / Hazardous Conditions  
       Issue 3 — Abandoned, Leaking 55-Gallon Drum of Waste TCE  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Phase I ESA REC No. 1 (observed)  
      A 55-gallon drum labeled         “    WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE    ”         was observed on the ground surface behind the maintenance/utility building. The drum is approximately half full, with a             rusted bottom and evidence of past leakage    . Stained soil (~3 ft × 3 ft, dark/oily, vegetation absent) is present beneath the drum. The Seller stated the drum has been there         “    for years,    ”         “    probably from the early ’90s.    ”  
          Why HIGH.         This is an             active, ongoing release         of a listed hazardous waste (TCE) to soil at the Property — a condition that is both an environmental liability and a RCRA compliance concern (improper storage/accumulation of hazardous waste). Although the areal extent appears limited and the drum is readily removable, an active leaking drum of a carcinogenic solvent warrants             immediate removal and disposal by a licensed transporter    , followed by characterization of the stained soil. This should not await the Phase II.  
         5.3 Off-Site Contamination  
       Issue 4 — Off-Site PCE Groundwater Plume (Lake Erie Dry Cleaning) Migrating Toward the Property  
          Severity: HIGH         |             EDR Database Summary; Ohio EPA response  
      The former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility at 4900 Lakeshore Industrial Drive is located approximately             120 feet north         of the Property and is             hydraulically upgradient    . Key facts, confirmed by both the EDR report and the Ohio EPA response:  
          A confirmed             PCE groundwater contamination plume         is documented (Ohio EPA Master Sites List; VAP ID NW-0923).  
          Monitoring well             MW-7         (located ~120 feet north of the Property boundary) detected             PCE at 87 µg/L         —             17.4× the Ohio generic groundwater standard of 5 µg/L    .  
          The plume migrates             south-southeast — toward the Property         — consistent with regional groundwater flow.  
          The Property is located within a             designated Groundwater Management Zone (        “        GMZ        ”        )         established by Ohio EPA for this plume.  
          A             Covenant Not to Sue         was issued under the Ohio VAP in 2018, but it             applies exclusively to the dry cleaner parcel and does not address off-site migration         onto the Property.  
          An environmental covenant recorded against the dry cleaner parcel restricts groundwater use and requires maintenance of a sub-slab vapor mitigation system there.  
              No investigation of PCE in groundwater beneath the Property has been conducted    , and the lateral/vertical extent of plume migration onto the Property is             undelineated    .  
          Why HIGH.         A documented carcinogenic-solvent plume at 17× the standard is migrating toward the Property from 120 feet away, the Property lies within the associated GMZ, and no on-site investigation has confirmed or ruled out impacts. This presents both groundwater contamination and vapor intrusion risk (see Issue 10). Depending on Phase II results, this could escalate to CRITICAL. The GMZ designation may also restrict future groundwater withdrawals/land use.  
        Issue 5 — Phase I ESA Did Not Classify the Off-Site PCE Plume as a REC  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Phase I ESA vs. Lender Requirements Letter  
      The Phase I ESA describes the Lake Erie Dry Cleaning PCE plume in detail in its records-review section (§ 5.1.1) and EDR appendix, but             does not list it as a REC, CREC, HREC, or de minimis condition in its Findings and Conclusions (§ 9.0)    . The report’s findings identify only REC No. 1 (TCE) and REC No. 2 (electroplating).  
      This appears inconsistent with the Lender’s express requirements. The Lender’s letter (§ 2) states that the Phase I ESA         “    must evaluate off-site sources of contamination that may be migrating onto or otherwise affecting the subject Property, including … contaminated groundwater plumes from adjacent or nearby properties,    ”         and that         “    any such conditions             must be identified as recognized environmental conditions    .    ”         The Lender specifically referenced the former dry cleaning facility at 4900 Lakeshore Industrial Drive.  
          Why HIGH.         The omission creates two risks: (i) a potential             AAI-compliance gap         — the Lender’s environmental counsel (Whitfield & Crane LLP) will independently review the Phase I ESA for AAI compliance and may reject or require supplementation of the report; and (ii) a             misalignment between the due-diligence deliverable and the Lender’s funding conditions    , which could delay or jeopardize loan funding. We recommend that the environmental consultant be directed to supplement the report to expressly classify the off-site PCE plume (and the GMZ/vapor-intrusion implications) as a REC, or to provide a written rationale if it declines to do so. Under ASTM E1527-21, the documented migration of a hazardous-substance plume toward a property for which on-site impacts have not been ruled out is, at minimum, a condition warranting REC classification and Phase II evaluation.  
         5.4 Historical Underground Storage Tanks  
       Issue 6 — Unregistered/Uncharacterized Historical USTs (2,000-Gallon Waste Solvent; 500-Gallon Heating Oil)  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Sanborn maps; Ohio EPA response; EDR  
      Two historical USTs appear on Sanborn fire insurance maps but have             no registration, closure, removal, or site-assessment records         in the Ohio BUSTR database (confirmed by Ohio EPA):  
              2,000-gallon waste solvent UST         — depicted on the 1987 Sanborn map on the east side of the Property, between the main building and the eastern boundary, in the vicinity of the former outdoor drum storage area (see Issue 9).         “    Waste solvent    ”         is consistent with the chlorinated solvents historically used at the facility. Its current status is             unknown — it may remain in place    .  
              500-gallon heating oil UST         — depicted on the 1948 and 1955 Sanborn maps serving the Halstead family farmhouse (northwest corner), which was demolished in 1961. The Phase I ESA classified this as an HREC on the assumption it was removed with the farmhouse, but             no removal documentation exists         and the assumption is unverified.  
          Why HIGH.         Two unregistered USTs with no closure documentation represent potential unidentified subsurface sources, particularly the 2,000-gallon waste solvent UST given its contents and proximity to the former drum storage area. Ohio EPA expressly recommended geophysical surveys, interviews, and review of fire department permit records to determine the status of these tanks. The Phase I ESA’s classification of the heating oil UST as an HREC rests on an unverified assumption and should be reconsidered pending a geophysical survey. This is also a Seller-representation issue (see Issue 11): Schedule 4.13 lists only the active 10,000-gallon diesel UST and states that         “    No other Underground Storage Tanks are or have been located on the Property.    ”  
         5.5 Hazardous Waste Management and Regulatory History  
       Issue 7 — 39-Year Gap in Hazardous Waste Disposal Records (1965–2003)  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Ohio EPA response; Phase I ESA  
      Ohio EPA confirmed that the only hazardous waste manifests/biennial reports on file for the facility are for reporting year 2004 (six manifests; 14,200 lbs; D007 and F006).             No manifests or generation reports exist for 1965 through 2003         — the entire 39-year period of electroplating operations. The Seller stated that pre-2004 records were         “    not available    ”         /         “    not retained.    ”  
          Why HIGH.         The near-total absence of disposal documentation for four decades of chromium- and solvent-bearing waste generation is a significant data gap that raises questions about historical waste management practices and creates potential RCRA/CERCLA exposure (including for off-site disposal sites at which Buyer, as the current owner, could face successor liability or contribution claims). This gap also undermines the reliability of the Seller’s compliance representations (§ 4.12(b)) and document-production representation (§ 4.12(d)).  
        Issue 8 — 2003 Ohio EPA Notice of Violation and Consent Order; Resolution Unverified  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Ohio EPA response  
      A July 17, 2003 Ohio EPA Compliance Evaluation Inspection documented: (i)             improper storage of hazardous waste in rusted, visibly leaking 55-gallon drums         in an outdoor area on the east side, containing electroplating sludge and spent chromium-bearing wastewater, with staining beneath the drums; (ii) failure to label containers with accumulation start dates; and (iii) failure to maintain hazardous waste determination records. Ohio EPA issued a             Notice of Violation (File No. NW-2003-0471)         in August 2003, resolved through a             Director’s Final Findings and Orders (consent order)         requiring a facility-wide waste audit, proper characterization/disposal of accumulated waste, a written waste management plan, and a compliance certification within 120 days.  
          Critical caveat:         The consent order file (including the signed order and any compliance certification) is             archived at Ohio EPA’s central repository and was not produced    ; Ohio EPA states only that the matter was         “    reportedly resolved.    ”         The Seller could not produce a copy of the consent order.  
          Why HIGH.         This is a documented regulatory violation involving leaking drums of chromium waste — directly relevant to the electroplating REC (Issue 2) and the former drum storage area (Issue 9). The inability to verify completion of the consent order’s corrective actions is a material data gap. This NOV is also an         “    Environmental Notice    ”         that does not appear on Schedule 4.12(c) (which states         “    None    ”    ), implicating the Seller’s representations (Issue 11). Buyer should obtain the consent order and compliance certification from Ohio EPA Central Office (30–45 business days) before closing.  
        Issue 9 — Former Outdoor Drum Storage Area with Stressed Vegetation  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Phase I ESA; aerial photographs; Ohio EPA  
      An approximately 40 ft × 60 ft area on the east side of the Property, delineated by deteriorating concrete curbing, exhibits             stressed/sparse, yellowed vegetation         relative to surrounding areas. Historical aerial photographs (1990, 2000) show an estimated 100+ drums stored on this unpaved surface. The 1987 Sanborn map places the 2,000-gallon waste solvent UST in this vicinity (Issue 6). The 2003 Ohio EPA inspection found rusted, leaking drums of electroplating sludge/chromium waste in an outdoor east-side storage area — likely this location. The Seller acknowledged,         “    We used to keep drums out there before the EPA came around,    ”         but could not detail the materials stored.  
          Why HIGH.         Historical outdoor storage of hazardous waste (chromium sludge and solvents) on unpaved ground without secondary containment, with confirmed leaking drums in 2003 and current stressed vegetation, indicates a potential soil (and possibly groundwater) release. The Phase I ESA noted this area in its reconnaissance (§ 6.2.4) but did not classify it as a separate REC in its findings. The Phase II should include soil borings in this area.  
         5.6 Vapor Intrusion  
       Issue 10 — Combined Vapor Intrusion Risk (On-Site TCE + Off-Site PCE)  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Phase I ESA; Ohio EPA; Lender letter  
      The Property is subject to vapor intrusion risk from two volatile chlorinated-solvent sources: (i) on-site TCE in the subsurface beneath the former degreasing area (elevated PID; Issue 1) and (ii) the off-site PCE plume migrating toward the Property (Issue 4). The Property is currently used for warehousing (occupied structures). The Lender’s requirements (§ 3.B.5) mandate a             vapor intrusion assessment         if VOCs are identified in soil or groundwater at or beneath the Property, and a             vapor mitigation plan         for any occupied/occupiable structure if risks warrant. The adjacent dry cleaner parcel is itself subject to a vapor mitigation requirement under its environmental covenant.  
          Why HIGH.         Vapor intrusion poses potential human-health risk to warehouse occupants and is a Lender condition precedent. The Phase II should include sub-slab soil-vapor and indoor-air sampling, and the acquisition plan should budget for potential vapor mitigation systems.  
         5.7 Transactional, Legal, and Lender Issues  
       Issue 11 — Seller’s Environmental Representations Appear Inaccurate/Incomplete (Breach Risk)  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Purchase Agreement §§ 4.12–4.13 vs. documented record  
      Comparison of the Seller’s representations against the documented record reveals apparent inaccuracies:  
              § 4.12(a) (        “        no Release        ”        ) — Schedule 4.12(a):                 “        None.        ”         Documented releases include the 1988 degreasing-solvent spill (Toledo Fire Department), the active leaking waste TCE drum (Issue 3), and the 2003 leaking chromium-waste drums (Issue 8). The         “    to Seller’s knowledge    ”         qualifier is strained as to the 2003 release, which Ohio EPA documented at the Seller’s facility during the tenure of Gerald Breckenridge III (involved since the mid-1980s).  
              § 4.12(b) (material compliance with Environmental Laws).         The 2003 NOV for improper hazardous waste storage is inconsistent with         “    at all times … material compliance.    ”  
              § 4.12(c) (        “        no Environmental Notice        ”        ) — Schedule 4.12(c):                 “        None.        ”         The 2003 NOV is an         “    Environmental Notice    ”         as defined in the Purchase Agreement. The Seller acknowledged the NOV to the consultant but did not disclose it on the schedule.  
              § 4.12(d) (delivery of all material environmental documents).         The consent order, 39 years of waste disposal records, corporate records (1961–1990), and any records associated with monitoring well MW-1 were not produced.  
              § 4.13 / Schedule 4.13 (complete UST list).         The schedule lists only the active 10,000-gallon diesel UST and states         “    No other Underground Storage Tanks are or have been located on the Property,    ”         omitting the 2,000-gallon waste solvent UST and 500-gallon heating oil UST (Issue 6).  
          Why HIGH.         These are potential breaches of the Seller’s representations and warranties, giving rise to indemnification claims under § 8.2(a)(i) and (a)(iii) (Pre-Closing Environmental Liabilities). Importantly, the Seller’s statements to the consultant — denying knowledge of the 1988 spill (    “    not aware of any spills    ”    ), claiming         “    all the chemicals were handled properly,    ”         and characterizing the NOV as         “    resolved years ago    ”         — are inconsistent with documented facts and may support an argument of             fraud or intentional misrepresentation    , which under § 8.4(d) is             not subject to the Basket, the Indemnification Cap, or the Survival Period    . Buyer should demand that the Seller correct the representations and Schedule 4.13 before closing and should preserve all evidence of the inconsistencies.  
        Issue 12 — Lender Funding Conditions Not Satisfied; Phase II/Insurance/Escrow Triggered  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Lender letter § 3.B; Phase I ESA  
      Because the Phase I ESA identified RECs, the Lender             will not fund the Loan on the Phase I ESA alone    . The following become conditions precedent to funding:  
              Phase II ESA         (ASTM E1903-19) with quantified remediation cost estimates (best-case, most-likely, worst-case), including methodology, standards, and time to completion.  
              Pollution Legal Liability (PLL) insurance         — limits of the greater of the worst-case estimate or $5,000,000, 10-year term, carrier rated A− or better, with the Bank as additional insured and loss payee. (Non-negotiable where RECs are identified.)  
              Environmental Escrow         — if the most-likely estimate exceeds $250,000, an escrow of 125% of the most-likely estimate, drawable only with the Bank’s approval.  
              Remediation Plan         — if estimated costs exceed $500,000 (most-likely or worst-case), a plan demonstrating remediation within 36 months, approved by a licensed professional and (where applicable) Ohio EPA.  
              Vapor Intrusion Assessment         — required because VOCs are indicated (Issue 10).  
          Delivery deadlines:         Phase I ESA by             November 15, 2024    ; Phase II ESA, PLL binder, and escrow evidence by             November 30, 2024         (closing December 15, 2024). The Lender may decline to fund if worst-case remediation exceeds 50% of appraised value, if the Property is listed/proposed on the NPL, if subject to an active RCRA corrective-action order, or if unresolved data gaps prevent adequate risk characterization (§ 3.C).  
          Why HIGH.         The RECs trigger a cascade of time-sensitive, cost-bearing conditions. The timeline is compressed (Phase II by November 30), and the cost estimates will drive the insurance/escrow/remediation-plan requirements and the Lender’s         “    unacceptable risk    ”         determination. Buyer should commission the Phase II immediately and engage an insurance broker for the PLL policy in parallel.  
        Issue 13 — Purchase Agreement Termination Right; $500,000 REC Remediation Threshold  
          Severity: HIGH         |             Purchase Agreement § 6.3(b)  
      Under § 6.3(b), Buyer may terminate the Purchase Agreement and recover its $437,500 earnest money if the Phase I ESA (or any environmental investigation during the due-diligence period) identifies RECs that, in Buyer’s reasonable judgment, could result in             Remediation Costs exceeding $500,000    , by written notice delivered             no later than 5:00 p.m. ET on October 31, 2024    .

       I. Introduction and Purpose  
      This memorandum assesses whether grounds exist to challenge the appointment of Hon. Margaret R. Fairhaven (Ret.) as Sole Arbitrator in the above-captioned matter. Our assessment is based on a side-by-side review of the arbitrator’s June 17, 2024 Arbitrator Disclosure Statement (the         “    Disclosure Statement    ”    ) against (a) the Arbitration Demand and Statement of Claims filed March 15, 2024; (b) the AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules excerpts (Rules R-16 through R-20); (c) the Entity and Relationship Profiles memorandum prepared by this firm dated June 19, 2024 (the         “    Profiles Memo    ”    ); and (d) the arbitrator appointment and fee dataset compiled from public AAA records.  
      The Disclosure Statement is, on its face, a thorough and professionally drafted document. It affirmatively discloses the arbitrator’s Lakeview Dispute Resolution Center affiliation, seven prior or pending arbitrations involving counsel for the parties, her advisory-board service at the Minnesota ADR Ethics Institute (and Ellen Wu’s concurrent service), her investment arrangements, and her family circumstances. The arbitrator twice certifies that the disclosure is         “    complete    ”         and that she has         “    not knowingly withheld any information.    ”  
      Our review nevertheless identifies             at least seven material disclosure deficiencies or inaccuracies         — five wholly undisclosed relationships, one affirmative misstatement, and one materially incomplete disclosure — together with a significant understatement of the arbitrator’s repeat-appointment history with the parties’ counsel. Several of these deficiencies implicate specific disclosure categories enumerated in AAA Rule R-17(a) and, if left unaddressed, create a meaningful risk of vacatur of any resulting award under Section 10(a)(2) of the Federal Arbitration Act (    “    FAA    ”    ), 9 U.S.C. § 10(a)(2).  
      This memorandum (1) sets out the procedural framework and deadline; (2) details each identified deficiency; (3) analyzes each under the applicable AAA rules, the IBA Guidelines on Conflicts of Interest, the AAA/ABA Code of Ethics, and the FAA; (4) assesses the relative strength of each ground; and (5) recommends a course of action.  
      
        II. Procedural Framework and Deadline  
       A. Governing Rules and Authorities  
      The arbitrator’s disclosure obligations and the parties’ challenge rights arise from several overlapping authorities:  
             AAA Commercial Arbitration Rule R-16         establishes the general disclosure standard: an arbitrator         “    shall disclose to the AAA any circumstance likely to give rise to justifiable doubt as to the arbitrator’s impartiality or independence, including any bias or any financial or personal interest in the result of the arbitration, or any past or present relationship with the parties, their representatives, or their counsel.    ”         The obligation is to be         “    construed broadly.    ”  
             AAA Rule R-17(a)         enumerates four non-exhaustive disclosure categories:  
              (i)         Any known             direct or indirect         financial or personal interest in the outcome,         “    including any interest, whether legal or beneficial, in the subject matter of the dispute, in any party, or in any entity that may be affected by the result of the proceeding    ”    ;  
              (ii)         Any known existing or past financial, business, professional, family, or social relationships with any party, its counsel, representatives, or witnesses         “    that are likely to affect impartiality or that might reasonably create an appearance of partiality or bias,    ”         expressly         “    including without limitation, relationships arising from employment, professional association, joint ventures, board memberships, academic collaboration, or regular social contact    ”    ;  
              (iii)         Any prior or pending cases in which the arbitrator has been involved         “    with any party to the arbitration, its counsel, or representatives,    ”         regardless of institution or neutral/non-neutral capacity; and  
              (iv)         A catch-all covering         “    any other circumstances that might cause a reasonable party to question the arbitrator’s ability to render a fair and impartial determination,    ”         expressly         “    including but not limited to the arbitrator’s prior publications, public statements, or expressed views on legal or factual issues relevant to the dispute.    ”  
             AAA Rule R-17(b)         makes disclosure a             continuing         obligation; the arbitrator         “    shall not await inquiry by a party or by the AAA before making any required supplemental disclosure.    ”  
             AAA Rule R-17(d)         provides that failure to make complete and timely disclosures         “    may result in the arbitrator’s removal in accordance with Rule R-18, and may constitute grounds for vacatur of any resulting award under applicable law, including but not limited to Section 10 of the Federal Arbitration Act.    ”  
             AAA Rule R-18(a)         governs the challenge procedure and deadline (see subsection B below).  
             The IBA Guidelines on Conflicts of Interest in International Arbitration (2014)    , while not binding, are widely consulted by the AAA and U.S. courts as persuasive authority on disclosure adequacy. They categorize conflicts into a Non-Waivable Red List, Waivable Red List, Orange List (disclosable; deemed accepted if no objection within 30 days), and Green List (no disclosure required).  
             The AAA/ABA Code of Ethics for Arbitrators in Commercial Disputes (2004)    , particularly Canon II (disclosure obligations) and Canon I (upholding the integrity and fairness of the process).  
             FAA § 10(a)(2)    , which permits vacatur of an award         “    where there was evident partiality or corruption in the arbitrators.    ”         The         “    evident partiality    ”         standard is satisfied not only by actual bias but by nondisclosure of material information that would lead a reasonable person to doubt the arbitrator’s impartiality.             See Commonwealth Coatings Corp. v. Continental Casualty Co.    , 393 U.S. 145 (1968).  
        B. The Challenge Deadline  
      Rule R-18(a) requires that a challenge be made by written notice to the AAA and the other party         “    within seven (7) calendar days after (i) the appointment of the challenged arbitrator has been communicated to the challenging party, or (ii) the circumstances giving rise to the challenge became known or should reasonably have become known to the challenging party,             whichever is later    .    ”  
      The arbitrator’s Disclosure Statement is dated June 17, 2024, and states a challenge deadline of             June 24, 2024    . That deadline governs any ground apparent on the face of the Disclosure Statement or known to the parties as of June 17.  
      Critically, however, several of the deficiencies identified in this memorandum were not disclosed and did not become known to us until the preparation of the Profiles Memo, completed June 19, 2024. Under the         “    whichever is later    ”         language of Rule R-18(a)(ii), the seven-day clock for those newly discovered grounds runs from June 19, 2024, yielding a deadline of             June 26, 2024    . We recommend acting conservatively and treating June 24, 2024 as the operative deadline for all grounds, while preserving the argument that the later-discovered grounds are not subject to waiver under Rule R-18(f) (which provides that a party         “    shall not be deemed to have waived its right to challenge based on grounds that were not and could not reasonably have been known at the time of the deadline    ”    ).

        IV. Detailed Analysis of Each Deficiency  
       A. Undisclosed Co-Authorship Between the Arbitrator’s Spouse and the Claimant’s Key Expert Witness  
          The facts.         The arbitrator’s husband, Dr. Alan Fairhaven, is a retired professor of mechanical engineering at the University of Minnesota. The claimant’s anticipated key expert witness is Dr. Rita Peshkov, Vice President of Research & Development at Cascade Thermal Systems Inc., the named inventor or co-inventor on the majority of Cascade’s fourteen ThermalCore patents, and the technical authority on the very technology at issue in this arbitration. Public academic databases reveal that Dr. Alan Fairhaven and Dr. Rita Peshkov             co-authored         a peer-reviewed paper:  
      “    Optimization of Phase-Change Heat Exchangers in High-Load Industrial Applications,    ”             Journal of Advanced Thermal Engineering    , Vol. 42, Issue 3, September 2022 (authors: Alan R. Fairhaven, Ph.D.; Rita S. Peshkov, Ph.D.).  
      The paper addresses optimization of phase-change heat exchangers in industrial settings — a subject directly on point with Cascade’s proprietary ThermalCore technology, which the Arbitration Demand describes as a         “    phase-change heat-exchange system designed for heavy industrial manufacturing environments.    ”         Co-authorship of a peer-reviewed article in a specialized field ordinarily reflects a sustained professional collaboration involving joint research design, data analysis, drafting, and revision over many months; the September 2022 publication indicates a working relationship that predated publication and may have continued thereafter.  
          What the Disclosure Statement says.         Section V of the Disclosure Statement identifies Dr. Alan Fairhaven as the arbitrator’s spouse and states:  
      “    [T]o the best of my knowledge, he has no financial or professional relationship with Cascade Thermal Systems Inc., Whitmore Capital Partners LLC, or any of their officers, directors, or employees.    ”  
      This is an             affirmative misstatement    , not merely an omission. Dr. Peshkov is an officer of Cascade (its Vice President of R&D) and the claimant’s designated key expert witness. The co-authored paper establishes a professional relationship between the arbitrator’s spouse and that officer/witness. The arbitrator also states that her husband         “    is not currently engaged in any active research collaborations with industrial partners    ”         — a statement directed at current industrial collaborations that does not address (and does not cure) the undisclosed prior academic collaboration with a witness in this very proceeding.  
          Analysis.         This deficiency squarely implicates             Rule R-17(a)(ii)    , which requires disclosure of         “    family … relationships with any party … its counsel, representatives, or witnesses    ”         and expressly includes relationships         “    arising from … academic collaboration.    ”         The relationship here runs from the arbitrator’s             spouse         to a             witness         — precisely the family/witness nexus the rule targets. The direct subject-matter overlap between the co-authored paper and the disputed ThermalCore technology elevates the concern beyond a generic academic acquaintance: the arbitrator’s husband collaborated on research concerning the identical type of technology whose valuation is the central issue in this arbitration.  
      Under the             IBA Guidelines    , a relationship between the arbitrator’s family member and a witness, particularly one involving academic collaboration on a topic relevant to the dispute, falls at least within the             Orange List         (disclosable; deemed accepted only if no timely objection). The subject-matter specificity and the affirmative misstatement may place it higher.  
      Under             FAA § 10(a)(2)    , nondisclosure of a family member’s professional relationship with a key witness — especially where the arbitrator affirmatively represented the contrary — is precisely the type of         “    evident partiality    ”         that supports vacatur. The affirmative misstatement is significant: courts and arbitral institutions treat an inaccurate disclosure more seriously than a mere omission, because it suggests either a lack of diligence or an intent to mislead, and because it deprives the parties of an informed opportunity to evaluate or waive the conflict.  
          Assessment: STRONG.         This is the single most serious deficiency. It combines (1) a family relationship, (2) with a key witness, (3) on a topic directly relevant to the disputed technology, and (4) an affirmative misstatement in the disclosure. It is also the ground most likely to be raised by the             claimant         (since it suggests potential sympathy toward the claimant’s expert), which has strategic implications discussed in Part VII.  
        B. Undisclosed Shared Organizational Service With the Respondent’s Principal (Garrett T. Whitmore)  
          The facts.         The arbitrator has served on the             advisory board         of the Minnesota ADR Ethics Institute since 2021. She disclosed this service, and she disclosed that Ellen Wu (a Senior Partner at Barrow & Thistlewood LLP) also serves on that advisory board. What she did             not         disclose is that             Garrett T. Whitmore         — the Founder and Managing Partner of Whitmore Capital Partners LLC (the respondent), who personally directed the challenged transaction and is identified in the Arbitration Demand as an anticipated adverse fact witness — serves on the             Board of Trustees         of the same organization, per the Institute’s IRS Form 990 filing for fiscal year 2023.  
      The Board of Trustees is the Institute’s             governing body    , responsible for strategic direction, budgetary oversight, and leadership appointments — including appointments to the advisory board on which the arbitrator sits. The Institute is described in its public materials as a small nonprofit. Its bylaws and posted minutes indicate that the Board of Trustees and the advisory board attend the Institute’s annual meetings and hold periodic joint sessions to coordinate programming and strategy. The small size of the organization and the overlapping meeting structures make direct personal interaction between the arbitrator and Mr. Whitmore reasonably likely.  
          What the Disclosure Statement says.         Section VI discloses the arbitrator’s advisory-board service and Ms. Wu’s concurrent service, and opines that the service         “    creates no conflict of interest.    ”         It does not mention Mr. Whitmore, does not identify any party or party principal as serving the Institute in any capacity, and does not disclose the governance relationship between the Board of Trustees (on which Mr. Whitmore sits) and the advisory board (on which the arbitrator sits).  
          Analysis.         This implicates             Rule R-17(a)(ii)    , which expressly covers         “    board memberships    ”         and relationships with         “    parties … [or] representatives.    ”         Mr. Whitmore is both the principal of the respondent and an anticipated witness. The partial disclosure — naming Ms. Wu (counsel’s partner) but omitting Mr. Whitmore (the party’s principal and a witness) — is notable, because the arbitrator evidently undertook to identify other individuals associated with the Institute but stopped short of identifying the respondent’s own principal.  
      Under the             IBA Guidelines    , the arbitrator and a party’s managing director serving on boards/committees of the same organization is a classic             Orange List         circumstance (Specific Situation 3.3.1: the arbitrator and         “    a person who is a manager, director, or member of the supervisory board of a party, or a person who has a similar leading position in a party    ”         are members of the same board or committee). The hierarchical relationship here (the Board of Trustees appoints and oversees the advisory board) is arguably more concerning than a simple co-membership, because the arbitrator serves at the pleasure of a body on which the respondent’s principal sits.  
          Assessment: STRONG.         The relationship is to the respondent’s own principal and a designated witness, the governance structure creates a reporting/oversight dynamic, and the disclosure was partial in a way that suggests the arbitrator did not fully canvass the Institute’s leadership. This ground is somewhat unusual strategically because it implicates our own client’s principal; we address that in Part VII.  
        C. Undisclosed CLE Co-Panelist Relationship With Respondent’s Lead Counsel (Dominic Hargrave)  
          The facts.         On October 14, 2023 — approximately five months before the Arbitration Demand was filed and eight months before the arbitrator’s appointment was confirmed — the Lakeview Dispute Resolution Center hosted a Continuing Legal Education event titled             “        Arbitrating Complex IP Licensing Disputes.        ”         The listed panelists were:  
          Hon. Margaret R. Fairhaven (Ret.) — the sole arbitrator in this matter;  
              Dominic Hargrave         — Lead Partner, Barrow & Thistlewood LLP, and lead counsel for the respondent in this arbitration;  
          Professor Naomi J. Strickland, University of Minnesota Law School; and  
          Marcus DelVecchio, Partner, Henning & Burwell P.A.  
      The event addressed         “    current trends, best practices, and emerging issues in the arbitration of intellectual property licensing disputes, with a focus on valuation methodologies, arbitrator selection in technology-intensive cases, and the management of expert testimony.    ”         That subject matter is             directly germane         to this arbitration, which concerns the valuation and enforceability of a technology license for Cascade’s proprietary ThermalCore heat-exchange technology. Co-panelist service ordinarily involves shared preparation, coordination of presentation materials, and informal professional interaction before and after the event.  
          What the Disclosure Statement says.         The Disclosure Statement does not reference this CLE event, does not identify the arbitrator and Mr. Hargrave as co-panelists, and does not otherwise disclose any professional relationship with Mr. Hargrave beyond the arbitration matters in which Barrow & Thistlewood LLP has appeared as counsel.  
          Analysis.         This implicates             Rule R-17(a)(ii)         (professional association with counsel) and, potentially,             Rule R-17(a)(iv)         (the catch-all covering         “    the arbitrator’s prior publications, public statements, or expressed views on legal or factual issues relevant to the dispute    ”    ). The arbitrator participated in a public panel on the arbitration of complex IP licensing disputes — the precise subject of this case — alongside the lawyer who would, months later, lead the respondent’s defense. Her expressed views on IP licensing valuation, arbitrator selection in technology cases, and expert testimony management may bear on issues she will decide here.  
      Under the             IBA Guidelines    , a one-time co-panelist relationship is generally treated as             Green List         or lower-end             Orange List    . The relationship is attenuated (a single event, professional rather than personal, and not involving a party directly). However, three features elevate it: (1) the topic was directly germane to this dispute; (2) the co-panelist is lead counsel for a party; and (3) the event was hosted by Lakeview, the arbitrator’s own affiliated institution, which itself receives referrals from both parties’ counsel (see Part IV.G). The non-disclosure is also notable because Rule R-17(a)(iv) specifically calls for disclosure of public statements on issues relevant to the dispute.  
          Assessment: MODERATE.         Standing alone, a single CLE co-panelist event may not create justifiable doubt. But combined with the directly germane topic, the lead-counsel identity, and the Lakeview hosting context, it is a disclosable relationship that should have been surfaced. It also contributes to the overall pattern of non-disclosure.  
        D. Potential Undisclosed Indirect Financial Interest in Whitmore Fund II LP  
          The facts.         The arbitrator’s personal investment portfolio is managed on a discretionary basis by             Northbridge Wealth Advisors    . Northbridge Wealth Advisors also manages a fund-of-funds product called the             Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund    , which allocates capital across private equity, venture capital, and alternative investment funds. Per the fund’s most recent public filing (March 2024), the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund holds a             2.3% limited partner interest in Whitmore Fund II LP    .  
      Whitmore Fund II LP is the Delaware limited partnership that             owns Volaris Manufacturing Group LLC         — the direct beneficiary of the disputed below-market ThermalCore technology license that is the subject of this arbitration. The economic outcome of this proceeding could affect the value of Whitmore Fund II LP’s portfolio (e.g., rescission of the license would deprive Volaris of below-market access to ThermalCore technology; a damages award against Whitmore Capital Partners LLC would be borne by the fund manager). The potential chain of indirect interest is:  
      Arbitrator’s portfolio (managed by Northbridge Wealth Advisors) → potential allocation to Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund (managed by the same firm) → 2.3% LP interest in Whitmore Fund II LP → Whitmore Fund II LP owns Volaris → Volaris is the beneficiary of the disputed license.  
          Important caveat:         The public record does             not confirm         that the arbitrator’s personal portfolio includes an allocation to the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund. The concern arises because the same firm that manages her portfolio also manages the fund-of-funds that holds the Whitmore Fund II LP interest.  
          What the Disclosure Statement says.         Section IV states that the arbitrator         “    hold[s] no direct equity positions in any party to this arbitration    ”         and has not transacted in the securities of Cascade, Whitmore,         “    or any entity that I understand to be a direct subsidiary or affiliate of either party.    ”         The statement is framed in terms of             direct         equity positions and             direct         subsidiaries/affiliates. It does not address             indirect         interests, fund-of-funds exposure, or the specific holdings of pooled vehicles. The arbitrator expressly acknowledges that she         “    ha[s] not undertaken a position-by-position review of each fund or sub-investment held within my managed accounts    ”         and that she         “    rel[ies] on Northbridge Wealth Advisors to manage my portfolio.    ”         She concludes that she is         “    not aware of any … investment that would constitute a material financial interest in the outcome of this proceeding.    ”  
          Analysis.         This implicates             Rule R-17(a)(i)    , which requires disclosure of         “    any known             direct or indirect         financial or personal interest in the outcome of the arbitration, including any interest, whether legal or beneficial, in the subject matter of the dispute, in any party, or in             any entity that may be affected by the result of the proceeding    .    ”         The rule’s express reference to             indirect         interests and to interests in             entities affected by the result         (not just parties) is directly on point: Whitmore Fund II LP is not a party, but it is an entity whose portfolio value may be affected by the result, and Volaris (its portfolio company) is the direct beneficiary of the challenged license.  
      The arbitrator’s         “    no direct equity positions    ”         formulation is, as the Profiles Memo observes,             technically accurate         even if she holds an indirect interest through a fund-of-funds — but Rule R-17(a)(i) reaches beyond direct equity to indirect and beneficial interests. The arbitrator’s own acknowledgment that she has not reviewed the underlying holdings of her pooled vehicles, combined with the fact that her manager offers a fund-of-funds that holds a Whitmore Fund II LP interest, means she cannot credibly certify the absence of an indirect interest without further inquiry. The disclosure thus falls short of the         “    diligent inquiry    ”         the arbitrator certifies she undertook.  
      Under the             IBA Guidelines    , an indirect financial interest in a party or an entity affected by the outcome is at minimum an             Orange List         circumstance requiring disclosure; if the interest were significant, it could approach the             Waivable Red List    . A 2.3% LP interest held through two layers of intermediation is unlikely to be         “    significant    ”         in dollar terms, but the             existence         of the indirect interest — and the arbitrator’s inability to rule it out — is itself disclosable, because the standard turns on what         “    might reasonably create an appearance    ”         of partiality, not on the magnitude of the economic stake.  
      Under             FAA § 10(a)(2)    , an undisclosed financial interest in an entity affected by the outcome is a classic basis for         “    evident partiality.    ”         The risk is not hypothetical: if the arbitrator rules on rescission or damages in a manner that affects Whitmore Fund II LP’s value, and the indirect interest is later discovered, the award is vulnerable.  
          Assessment: STRONG (if the interest is confirmed) / MODERATE (as a basis to compel supplemental disclosure).         Even without confirmation, the arbitrator’s disclosure is deficient because Rule R-17(a)(i) requires disclosure of             indirect         interests and interests in             entities affected by the result    , and the arbitrator has not conducted (or has not disclosed) the inquiry necessary to rule out such an interest. At minimum, supplemental disclosure is warranted — the arbitrator should be asked to confirm whether her portfolio includes an allocation to the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund (or any other vehicle with exposure to Whitmore Fund II LP, Whitmore Fund III LP, Cascade, or Volaris). If the interest is confirmed, this becomes a strong independent ground for challenge.  
        E. Omission of a Pending Arbitration (        Halcyon Biotech v. Stonewall Partners    )  
          The facts.         Public AAA records identify a pending arbitration,             Halcyon Biotech v. Stonewall Partners         (AAA Case No. 01-24-0003105), in which Barrow & Thistlewood LLP represents one of the parties and the arbitrator serves as sole arbitrator. The arbitrator was appointed on April 22, 2024; the matter is in the pre-hearing phase, with estimated remaining fees of approximately $175,000.  
          What the Disclosure Statement says.         Section III lists seven matters, of which two are pending and involve Barrow & Thistlewood LLP: the instant matter and             Orion Fabrication v. Mountain West Supply Co.         (Case No. 01-23-0002856).             Halcyon Biotech         is not listed. The arbitrator certifies that the seven matters represent         “    a complete list of arbitrations in which I have served alongside counsel from either Barrow & Thistlewood LLP or Fielding Marsh & Collier LLP.    ”  
          Analysis.         This is a clear violation of             Rule R-17(a)(iii)    , which requires disclosure of         “    prior or pending cases in which the arbitrator has been or is involved … with any party to the arbitration, its counsel, or representatives,    ”         including         “    all matters, whether administered by the AAA or by any other arbitral institution.    ”         A pending matter in which the arbitrator is simultaneously engaged with the respondent’s counsel is precisely what the rule targets, because it bears on the arbitrator’s ongoing professional and economic relationship with that firm and on the firm’s ongoing interest in maintaining a productive relationship with the arbitrator.  
      The omission also means the parties were not informed that the arbitrator currently has             three         concurrent pending matters with Barrow & Thistlewood LLP (this case,             Orion    , and             Halcyon    ), rather than two. As detailed in Part IV.F, the aggregate pending fees from Barrow & Thistlewood matters are substantial.  
          Assessment: MODERATE.         A single omitted pending matter, viewed in isolation, might be characterized as an inadvertent error. But it is a clear disclosure failure under R-17(a)(iii), it contradicts the arbitrator’s certification of completeness, and it contributes to a pattern in which the arbitrator’s concurrent engagement with the respondent’s counsel is understated. It is not, by itself, likely to be dispositive of a challenge, but it materially supports the cumulative case.  
        F. Material Understatement of the Repeat-Appointment History With the Parties’ Counsel  
          The facts.         The appointment dataset compiles the arbitrator’s last twenty (20) completed arbitrations from 2021–2024, together with pending matters. The data reveals the following:  
             Barrow & Thistlewood LLP         appeared as counsel in             9 of the 20         completed matters (45% of the arbitrator’s completed docket during this period). The Barrow & Thistlewood client prevailed in             7 of 9         matters (a 77.8% prevailing rate). Aggregate estimated fees from Barrow & Thistlewood matters total approximately             $1,020,000    , representing approximately             48%         of the arbitrator’s estimated total arbitration income over the trailing 36 months.  
             Fielding Marsh & Collier LLP         appeared as counsel in             5 of the 20         completed matters (25% of the docket). The Fielding Marsh client prevailed in             1 of 4         adjudicated matters (a 25% prevailing rate; the fifth matter settled). Aggregate estimated fees total approximately             $410,000         (approximately 19% of total income).  
         The arbitrator currently has             three         pending matters involving Barrow & Thistlewood LLP (this case,             Orion Fabrication    , and             Halcyon Biotech    ), with aggregate estimated pending fees of approximately             $560,000         — roughly             67%         of the arbitrator’s total pending fee exposure.  
      The Disclosure Statement lists only             seven         matters involving the parties’ counsel (six completed plus             Orion         pending). Cross-referencing the dataset, at least             six         completed matters involving Barrow & Thistlewood LLP or Fielding Marsh & Collier LLP were             not disclosed    , and are marked         “    Not disclosed by arbitrator    ”         in the dataset:  
              Crestfield Energy v. Loran Industrial         (2022) — Barrow & Thistlewood LLP (claimant) — award for claimant (B&T client prevailed) — est. $110,000;  
              Ashford Medical Devices v. Greenway Therapeutics         (2022) — Barrow & Thistlewood LLP (respondent) — award for respondent (B&T client prevailed) — est. $92,000;  
              Westbrook Partners v. Arcadian Ventures         (2022) —             both firms         (Fielding Marsh as claimant; Barrow & Thistlewood as respondent) — award for respondent (B&T client prevailed) — est. $130,000;  
              Trident Coatings v. Bayshore Chemical         (2023) — Barrow & Thistlewood LLP (claimant) — award for respondent (B&T client did not prevail) — est. $105,000;  
              Redstone Logistics v. Fairfield Transport         (2023) — Barrow & Thistlewood LLP (respondent) — award for respondent (B&T client prevailed) — est. $118,000; and  
              Warwick Technologies v. Cypress Digital         (2023) — Barrow & Thistlewood LLP (claimant) — award for claimant (B&T client prevailed) — est. $97,000.  
      A seventh matter,             Thornwall Systems v. Kessler Automation         (2023), in which Fielding Marsh & Collier LLP represented the respondent (and the Fielding Marsh client did not prevail), likewise does not appear in the Disclosure Statement.  
          What the Disclosure Statement says.         The arbitrator lists seven matters and certifies that they represent         “    a complete list of arbitrations in which I have served alongside counsel from either Barrow & Thistlewood LLP or Fielding Marsh & Collier LLP.    ”         She further certifies in Section X that         “    based on a diligent inquiry, the foregoing represents all circumstances known to me that might give rise to justifiable doubt as to my impartiality or independence.    ”  
          Analysis.         This implicates             Rule R-17(a)(iii)         (prior/pending cases with counsel). Two distinct concerns arise:  
             The certification of completeness is inaccurate.         The arbitrator has served in at least thirteen (and as many as fourteen) completed matters involving the parties’ counsel, plus three pending matters — not seven. The         “    complete list    ”         certification is contradicted by the public record. Whether the omissions are inadvertent or otherwise, the result is that the parties were not given an accurate picture of the arbitrator’s appointment history with their counsel.  
             The magnitude of the repeat-player relationship creates an appearance concern.         Repeat appointments, in isolation, are generally not disqualifying; the IBA Guidelines place repeat appointments in the             Green List    . But the concentration here is exceptional: a single firm (Barrow & Thistlewood LLP, respondent’s counsel) accounts for 45% of the arbitrator’s completed docket, 48% of her estimated income, and 67% of her pending fee exposure, with a 77.8% prevailing rate for that firm’s clients — against a 25% prevailing rate for the claimant’s counsel’s clients. While prevailing rates are not, by themselves, proof of bias (they depend on the merits of individual cases), the stark disparity, combined with the arbitrator’s substantial economic dependence on one side’s counsel, is the kind of circumstance that         “    might reasonably create an appearance of partiality    ”         within the meaning of Rule R-17(a)(ii) and that a         “    reasonable party    ”         might consider relevant under Rule R-17(a)(iv). The economic-dependence point is sharpened by the arbitrator’s own fee estimate in this matter (~$340,000), which would further increase Barrow & Thistlewood’s share of her income.  
          Assessment: MODERATE.         The inaccurate         “    complete list    ”         certification is a clear disclosure deficiency. The repeat-player statistics, standing alone, are unlikely to sustain a challenge (repeat appointments are common and generally acceptable), but they materially strengthen the cumulative case and are relevant to the         “    evident partiality    ”         analysis should an award later be challenged. The 77.8% versus 25% prevailing-rate disparity is the most attention-getting datum, but it should be advanced with care and appropriate caveats, as prevailing rates are heavily merits-dependent.  
        G. Incomplete Disclosure of the Lakeview Dispute Resolution Center Affiliation  
          The facts.         The arbitrator is affiliated with the Lakeview Dispute Resolution Center as a panel neutral. Lakeview is located at 750 Nicollet Mall, Suite 3200, Minneapolis — the same address at which the arbitrator signs her disclosure. Lakeview’s Managing Director is Harold Lund. Lakeview receives case referrals from both Barrow & Thistlewood LLP and Fielding Marsh & Collier LLP. Critically, Lakeview also             hosted         the October 14, 2023 CLE event at which the arbitrator and Mr. Hargrave co-panelisted (Part IV.C). The public record does not reveal the financial structure of the arbitrator’s Lakeview affiliation — i.e., whether Lakeview takes a percentage of the arbitrator’s fees for referred matters, whether the arbitrator is an employee, independent contractor, or equity/member-owner, or whether Lakeview’s revenue model depends on maintaining referral relationships with the law firms that retain its neutrals.  
          What the Disclosure Statement says.         Section VII discloses the affiliation, identifies Mr. Lund as Managing Director, states that Lakeview receives referrals from both firms, and asserts that the present matter was administered by the AAA (not through Lakeview). The arbitrator opines that the referral relationships         “    do[] not influence my decision-making.    ”         The Disclosure Statement does not address the financial terms or structural nature of the affiliation.  
          Analysis.         This implicates             Rule R-17(a)(ii)         (professional/financial association). The arbitrator disclosed the             existence         of the relationship but not its             financial terms    . Where an arbitrator’s affiliated institution derives revenue from referrals by the parties’ counsel, the financial interdependency between the arbitrator (through her institution) and counsel may be material to the appearance of impartiality — particularly where, as here, one firm accounts for a very large share of the arbitrator’s overall docket. The fact that Lakeview also hosted the undisclosed CLE co-panelist event (Part IV.C) ties this ground to Ground C.  
          Assessment: WEAK–MODERATE.         The relationship was disclosed in substance; the deficiency is the omission of financial detail. This is best addressed through a request for supplemental disclosure rather than as an independent basis for challenge. It does, however, reinforce the broader picture of an arbitrator whose professional and economic ecosystem is intertwined with the parties’ counsel in ways not fully captured by the Disclosure Statement.  
      
         V. Analysis Under the Applicable Standards  
       A. AAA Rule R-17  
      Each deficiency maps to a specific R-17(a) subcategory:  
              Ground A         (spouse-witness co-authorship) → R-17(a)(ii) (family relationships with witnesses; academic collaboration). Also implicates the affirmative-misstatement problem, which bears on the         “    diligent inquiry    ”         certification.  
              Ground B         (Whitmore board service) → R-17(a)(ii) (board memberships; relationships with a party/representative).  
              Ground C         (CLE co-panelist) → R-17(a)(ii) (professional association with counsel) and R-17(a)(iv) (public statements/views on issues relevant to the dispute).  
              Ground D         (indirect financial interest) → R-17(a)(i) (direct or indirect financial interest; interest in an entity affected by the result).  
              Ground E         (omitted             Halcyon         matter) → R-17(a)(iii) (pending cases with counsel).  
              Ground F         (understated repeat history) → R-17(a)(iii) (prior/pending cases with counsel); the statistics also bear on R-17(a)(ii)/(a)(iv) appearance concerns.  
              Ground G         (Lakeview financial terms) → R-17(a)(ii) (professional/financial association).  
      Rule R-17(d) expressly provides that failure to make complete and timely disclosures         “    may result in the arbitrator’s removal … and may constitute grounds for vacatur of any resulting award under applicable law, including … Section 10 of the Federal Arbitration Act.    ”         The rule also authorizes the AAA to         “    bring any disclosure deficiency to the attention of the arbitrator and request supplemental disclosure before acting on any challenge    ”         — a mechanism we recommend invoking (see Part VIII).  
        B. IBA Guidelines on Conflicts of Interest  
      Applying the IBA categorizations (persuasive, not binding):  
              Ground A         — relationship of the arbitrator’s spouse with a witness via academic collaboration on a topic relevant to the dispute: at least             Orange List    ; the subject-matter specificity and affirmative misstatement may warrant treatment as a             Waivable Red List         circumstance.  
              Ground B         — the arbitrator and a party’s managing director serving on governing/advisory bodies of the same organization:             Orange List         (Specific Situation 3.3.1), with the governance hierarchy (Trustees appoint/oversee the advisory board) arguably elevating it.  
              Ground C         — one-time co-panelist with counsel:             Green-to-Orange List    ; elevated by the directly germane topic and the lead-counsel identity.  
              Ground D         — indirect financial interest in an entity affected by the result:             Orange List         at minimum;             Waivable Red List         if the interest is confirmed and material.  
              Ground E         — omitted pending matter: not a listed IBA situation, but the underlying repeat-appointment relationship is             Green List    ; the             omission    , however, is a disclosure failure independent of the IBA list.  
              Ground F         — repeat appointments:             Green List         in isolation, but the concentration and the inaccurate certification create an appearance concern outside the Green List’s safe harbor.  
              Ground G         — institutional affiliation with referral dependency:             Orange List         territory where financial terms are material.  
      The IBA framework reinforces that the Orange List circumstances (A, B, D, and arguably G) carry a disclosure obligation and a deemed-acceptance mechanism that is             not         satisfied where the circumstance is never disclosed at all. Because the arbitrator did not disclose these circumstances, the parties never had the opportunity the IBA framework presumes.  
        C. FAA § 10(a)(2) — Evident Partiality and Vacatur Risk  
      The         “    evident partiality    ”         standard is the most consequential lens because it governs post-award vacatur and is not waivable by failure to object within the Rule R-18 window (Rule R-18(f) expressly preserves the right to seek vacatur on grounds of evident partiality not previously known). The Supreme Court has held that         “    evident partiality    ”         is established where an arbitrator fails to disclose a material relationship that would lead a reasonable person to doubt the arbitrator’s impartiality.             Commonwealth Coatings Corp. v. Continental Casualty Co.    , 393 U.S. 145 (1968).  
      The deficiencies most likely to support an evident-partiality vacatur motion are:  
              Ground A         — the spouse-witness co-authorship, compounded by the affirmative misstatement. This is the type of undisclosed family/witness relationship that courts have found to constitute evident partiality.  
              Ground D         — an indirect financial interest in an entity affected by the result, if confirmed. Undisclosed financial interests are the paradigmatic evident-partiality scenario.  
              Ground B         — the undisclosed shared board service with the respondent’s principal.  
      The practical implication is significant:             even if no party challenges now, and even if the arbitrator issues an award, the award remains vulnerable to vacatur         if any of these undisclosed relationships is later discovered. This vacatur risk is bidirectional and is addressed in the strategic analysis in Part VII.  
        D. AAA/ABA Code of Ethics  
      Canon II of the Code requires arbitrators to disclose         “    any fact that might lead a reasonable party to consider the arbitrator partial or biased.    ”         Canon I requires upholding the integrity and fairness of the process. The pattern of omissions identified here — particularly the affirmative misstatement in Ground A and the inaccurate         “    complete list    ”         certification in Grounds E and F — implicates both canons. The Code, like Rule R-17, construes the disclosure obligation broadly.

        VII. Strategic Considerations  
      Several strategic factors bear on the decision whether and how to proceed, and they cut in different directions.  
       A. The Respondent Selected This Arbitrator  
      Whitmore Capital Partners LLC ranked Judge Fairhaven as its             first choice         during the AAA ranking process; Cascade ranked her third. The appointment data suggests that, historically, the arbitrator’s awards have favored Barrow & Thistlewood LLP’s clients (a 77.8% prevailing rate) and that Barrow & Thistlewood matters account for a substantial share of the arbitrator’s income. On a pure         “    expected outcome    ”         basis, the respondent may have no desire to dislodge this arbitrator.  
        B. The Vacatur Risk Cuts Both Ways — and May Cut Against the Respondent  
      The most serious deficiency,             Ground A         (the spouse-witness co-authorship), suggests a relationship that runs             toward the claimant’s side         — the arbitrator’s husband collaborated with the claimant’s key expert witness on the very technology at issue. If the arbitrator issues an award             in the respondent’s favor    , the claimant could later discover this undisclosed relationship and move to vacate under FAA § 10(a)(2) on the theory that the arbitrator was, if anything, predisposed toward the claimant’s expert and yet still ruled against the claimant — or, more straightforwardly, that the nondisclosure itself constitutes evident partiality warranting vacatur regardless of the direction of any presumed bias.             An award in the respondent’s favor is therefore not safe so long as these relationships remain undisclosed and uncured.         This is the central strategic point: the respondent’s interest in a durable, enforceable award is best served by ensuring full disclosure             now    , before the arbitrator has invested in the matter and before any award issues.  
        C. The Direction of the Financial Interest (Ground D) Is Ambiguous  
      If the arbitrator holds an indirect interest in Whitmore Fund II LP, the direction of any resulting bias is not obvious. Rescission of the Volaris license would             harm         Whitmore Fund II LP’s portfolio (Volaris loses below-market access to ThermalCore technology), which could bias the arbitrator             against         rescission — i.e., toward the respondent’s position on that remedy. Conversely, a damages award against Whitmore Capital Partners LLC (the manager) would be borne by the manager, not Fund II directly. The point for present purposes is not the direction of the bias but the             existence         of a disclosable indirect interest in an entity affected by the result, which Rule R-17(a)(i) requires be disclosed regardless of direction.  
        D. Some Grounds Implicate Our Own Client or Counsel  
      Grounds B (Whitmore’s board service), C (Hargrave’s CLE co-panelist service), E (the omitted             Halcyon         matter), and F (the repeat-appointment history) all involve the respondent’s own principal or counsel. Raising these in a challenge would require us to surface facts about our own client and firm. This is not improper — the disclosure obligation runs to the arbitrator, not to us — but it bears on how we frame any challenge and whether we prefer to seek supplemental disclosure (which places the onus on the arbitrator to disclose) rather than to lead with a challenge that emphasizes our own relationships.  
        E. The Claimant May Challenge Independently  
      Cascade ranked the arbitrator third and may have its own reasons to scrutinize the disclosure. Ground A (spouse-witness) and Ground D (financial interest in Fund II) are grounds the claimant is particularly well-positioned to raise, as both could be argued to disfavor the claimant. We should be prepared for the possibility that the claimant challenges, and we should have a position on whether to agree, oppose, or stand neutral. If the claimant challenges on Ground A, the respondent’s strategic calculus changes: opposing the challenge would mean defending an arbitrator whose spouse has an undisclosed relationship with the claimant’s key witness — a difficult position to maintain and one that would itself create vacatur risk.  
        F. Waiver and Preservation  
      Under Rule R-18(f), a party that fails to challenge timely is deemed to have waived grounds         “    then known,    ”         but             not         grounds         “    that were not and could not reasonably have been known at the time of the deadline.    ”         Several of the grounds here (A, B, C, D, E) were not disclosed and were not reasonably knowable to the parties from the face of the Disclosure Statement; they became known through independent research completed June 19, 2024. Accordingly, even if we do not challenge by June 24, we are not foreclosed from challenging on those grounds (the         “    whichever is later    ”         rule in R-18(a)(ii) and the R-18(f) savings clause both apply), nor from raising evident partiality in a later vacatur proceeding. That said, challenging or seeking supplemental disclosure             now         is far cleaner than litigating waiver and timeliness after an award.

      We recommend a             measured, two-step approach         designed to cure the disclosure deficiencies (thereby protecting any eventual award from vacatur) while preserving all challenge rights:  
       Step 1 — Request Supplemental Disclosure Through the AAA (Immediately)

              Ground A:         Whether the arbitrator’s spouse, Dr. Alan Fairhaven, has co-authored any publication with Dr. Rita Peshkov (or has any other professional or personal relationship with her), and whether the arbitrator stands by the statement in Section V that her husband         “    has no … professional relationship with … any of [Cascade’s] officers, directors, or employees    ”    ;  
              Ground B:         Whether any party, party principal, or anticipated witness — including Garrett T. Whitmore — serves in any capacity (including on the Board of Trustees) with the Minnesota ADR Ethics Institute, and the nature of the governance relationship between the Board of Trustees and the advisory board;  
              Ground C:         Whether the arbitrator has participated in any CLE event, panel, or public presentation with either party’s counsel (including the October 14, 2023 Lakeview CLE with Dominic Hargrave), and whether she expressed any views on IP licensing valuation, arbitrator selection in technology cases, or expert testimony management at such events;

        Step 2 — Evaluate the Supplemental Disclosure and Decide on Challenge  
      Upon receiving the arbitrator’s supplemental disclosure (which the AAA is to transmit to the parties within two business days of receipt under Rule R-17(c)), we recommend evaluating whether to file a formal challenge under Rule R-18. Our preliminary view, subject to the content of the supplemental disclosure, is as follows:  
              If the arbitrator confirms the spouse-witness co-authorship (Ground A) and/or an indirect financial interest in Whitmore Fund II LP (Ground D):         these are, in our judgment, sufficient to sustain a challenge, and we would recommend challenging — particularly on Ground A, given the affirmative misstatement and the family/witness/direct-relevance combination. The challenge deadline would run from the date the supplemental disclosure is received (Rule R-18(a)(ii),         “    whichever is later    ”    ).  
              If the arbitrator’s supplemental disclosure is complete and candid, and confirms only the Orange List-type relationships (B, C, E, F, G) without confirming A or D:         the respondent may elect to waive those relationships (with the claimant’s position to be assessed) and proceed, having cured the vacatur risk by securing full disclosure on the record. The respondent would, however, need to weigh the claimant’s likely response.  
              If the arbitrator declines to supplement or provides an evasive response:         that itself would be a basis for challenge, as it would confirm the inadequacy of the original disclosure.  
        Step 3 — Preserve Rights and Prepare for the Claimant’s Position  
      Regardless of the path chosen, we recommend:  
              Preserving all rights in writing         by noting in any communication to the AAA that the firm does not waive any challenge ground by requesting supplemental disclosure, and that it reserves the right to challenge within seven days of receipt of any supplemental disclosure or by June 24, 2024, whichever is later;  
              Monitoring the claimant’s position    , as the claimant may challenge on Grounds A and/or D. If the claimant challenges, the respondent should be prepared to take a position (agree, oppose, or stand neutral) — and we note that opposing a claimant challenge based on Ground A would be difficult to justify given the affirmative misstatement; and  
              Documenting the basis for any eventual waiver    , so that the record reflects an informed decision to proceed (or not) with full knowledge of the disclosed relationships, which is the strongest defense against a later vacatur motion.  
      
         IX. Conclusion  
      The arbitrator’s June 17, 2024 Disclosure Statement is detailed in presentation but deficient in substance. Our review against the source documents identifies at least seven material deficiencies: an affirmative misstatement regarding the arbitrator’s spouse’s relationship with the claimant’s key expert witness (Ground A); the omission of the respondent’s principal’s board service at the same organization where the arbitrator serves (Ground B); the omission of a directly germane CLE co-panelist relationship with the respondent’s lead counsel (Ground C); a potential undisclosed indirect financial interest in Whitmore Fund II LP, the owner of the license beneficiary Volaris (Ground D); the omission of a pending arbitration involving the respondent’s counsel (Ground E); a materially inaccurate         “    complete list    ”         certification that understates the arbitrator’s repeat-appointment history and economic dependence on the respondent’s counsel (Ground F); and an incomplete disclosure of the financial terms of the arbitrator’s Lakeview affiliation (Ground G).  
      The two strongest grounds — the spouse-witness co-authorship (A) and the potential indirect financial interest (D) — each independently warrant action, and both create a material risk of vacatur under FAA § 10(a)(2) if left unaddressed. The remaining grounds, while individually moderate, collectively undermine the reliability of the arbitrator’s certification of completeness.  
      We recommend that the firm immediately request, through the AAA and with copy to opposing counsel, that the arbitrator supplement her disclosure to address all seven deficiencies, while expressly preserving all challenge rights. The firm should then evaluate whether to file a formal challenge under Rule R-18 based on the content of the supplemental disclosure, with particular attention to Grounds A and D. This approach best protects the respondent’s interest in a durable, enforceable award while preserving the full range of options should the supplemental disclosure confirm material conflicts.

       I. Executive Summary  
      We reviewed the draft Settlement Agreement and General Release of All Claims dated May 5, 2025 (the         “    Draft    ”    ), prepared by Engel & Associates, P.C. (Mr. Delano’s counsel), against Mr. Delano’s employment and equity records and the terms of the Greenleaf 2021 Equity Incentive Plan (the         “    Plan    ”    ). A redlined markup reflecting our recommended revisions accompanies this memo.  
      The Draft is a largely conventional separation release, but it contains several provisions that are             factually incorrect, legally non-compliant, or materially adverse to the Company    , and it omits standard protective provisions. The most important items are:  
             Material valuation error (§§ 3(d), 3(e)).         The Draft values the accelerated RSUs at $10.00/share and represents that figure as         “    the most recent independent valuation.    ”         It is not. The most recent 409A valuation (Ridgepoint Valuation Services, dated             January 15, 2025    ) is             $12.50/share    . The correct RSU component value is             $62,500         (not $50,000), and the correct aggregate settlement value is             $537,500         (not $525,000). The Draft therefore contains a false statement of fact that the Company should not execute.  
             The ADEA release is likely invalid as written (§§ 5, 16).         The Draft gives Mr. Delano only             14 days         to consider the agreement and contains             no revocation period    . The Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (    “    OWBPA    ”    ) requires             at least 21 days         to consider and a             7-day revocation period         for a knowing and voluntary waiver of Age Discrimination in Employment Act claims. Because age discrimination is a central asserted claim, an invalid ADEA waiver would defeat a primary purpose of the settlement and leave the Company exposed.  
             Significant pretext / retaliation exposure.         Company records show that             six days         after terminating Mr. Delano for         “    elimination of position due to restructuring of supply chain division,    ”         the Company hired             Raj Mehta         as Senior Director of Supply Chain & Logistics at $185,000. A near-instantaneous backfill into a substantially similar role directly undercuts the stated rationale and strongly supports Mr. Delano’s whistleblower-retaliation (ORS 654.062(5)) and age-discrimination claims. This bears on both litigation risk and settlement leverage.  
             No protected-activity carve-outs; pending OSHA investigation (§§ 6, 7, 15).         Read literally, the confidentiality, non-disparagement, and         “    no future filings    ”         provisions could be construed to bar Mr. Delano from cooperating with the             pending Oregon OSHA investigation         (Complaint No. OR-OSHA-2024-11872) or receiving any whistleblower award. We added standard government-cooperation carve-outs and a DTSA/ORS 646.573 trade-secret immunity notice. The OSHA investigation itself proceeds independently of this settlement and is not resolved by it.  
             Non-compete is largely unenforceable (§ 9).         The 18-month, four-state non-compete is             void as to California         (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600) and             likely voidable in Oregon         (ORS 653.295) because it is entered at separation rather than at hire or upon a bona fide advancement. We removed California; we recommend the Company consider relying on the (more enforceable) non-solicitation covenants instead.  
             Missing boilerplate.         The Draft has no choice-of-law/venue, notices, assignment, or survival provisions. We added them (§§ 20–22).  
             Items requiring GC coordination before execution:         (a) confirm             EPLI carrier consent         (Timberline Insurance Group, Policy No. TIG-EPL-2024-00391) and the coverage/allocation; (b) obtain             Compensation Committee approval         for the RSU acceleration (required under the Plan); and (c) verify             PTO payout and 2024 bonus         treatment to support the § 2 wage representation and the W-2 back-pay characterization.  
      We recommend returning the redlined version to Engel & Associates as the Company’s counter, treating the valuation correction, the OWBPA fixes, and the protected-activity carve-outs as             non-negotiable    , and the reference and non-compete provisions as             points for negotiation    .

        IV. Detailed Issues and Recommendations  
       A. RSU valuation error — §§ 3(d), 3(e) —             High priority / non-negotiable  
          Issue.         Section 3(d) states that the fair market value of each accelerated RSU is         “    $10.00 per share, based on the most recent independent valuation conducted by Ridgepoint Valuation Services,    ”         yielding a $50,000 component and a $525,000 aggregate. The employment/equity records show the $10.00 figure is the             July 1, 2024         annual valuation and that the             most recent         409A valuation, dated             January 15, 2025    , is             $12.50/share    . The Draft therefore (i) contains a false statement of fact (    “    most recent    ”    ), and (ii) understates the RSU component by $12,500 and the aggregate by $12,500.  
          Why it matters.         Signing an agreement containing a demonstrably false statement about the Company’s own valuation is a credibility and disclosure risk and could be cited against the Company in any later dispute. The understatement also misstates the total consideration, which matters for board/Compensation Committee approval, EPLI reporting, and the parties’ respective tax reporting.  
          Recommendation (reflected in redline).         Correct the per-share value to $12.50, cite the January 15, 2025 valuation, correct the component to $62,500, and correct the aggregate to $537,500. We also added an express condition that the acceleration is             subject to Compensation Committee approval         (see Item H).  
        B. ADEA / OWBPA release deficiencies — §§ 5, 16 —             High priority / non-negotiable  
          Issue.         Mr. Delano is 58 and asserts an ADEA claim, so the ADEA waiver is a centerpiece of the settlement. The Draft provides only a             14-day         consideration period (§§ 5(c), 16) and             no revocation period    , and it does not advise Mr. Delano to consult an attorney.  
          Why it matters.         Under the OWBPA, 29 U.S.C. § 626(f), a release of ADEA claims is         “    knowing and voluntary    ”         (and thus effective) only if, among other things, the individual is given             at least 21 days         to consider the agreement (§ 626(f)(1)(F)) and a             7-day revocation period         after signing (§ 626(f)(1)(G)), and is             advised in writing to consult an attorney         (a statutory factor, § 626(f)(1)(E)). A non-conforming waiver does not bar ADEA claims, so the Company could pay $537,500 and still be sued for age discrimination. The Draft’s other OWBPA elements (referral to ADEA/OWBPA, consideration in addition to anything already owed, written to be understood) are present.  
          Recommendation (reflected in redline).         (i) Change the consideration period to             21 days         (§§ 5(c), 16); (ii) add a             7-day revocation period         with revocation mechanics (new § 5(g) and § 16); (iii) add the             advice-to-consult-an-attorney         acknowledgment (§ 5(c)); and (iv) redefine the             “        Effective Date        ”         as the 8th day after signing (i.e., the day after the revocation window closes), so that all payment deadlines run from a date on which the release is actually effective. Note: because this is an individual (not a group/RIF) release, the OWBPA’s 45-day consideration period and         “    decisional unit    ”         disclosure requirements do not apply.  
        C. Pretext / retaliation exposure —             High priority (risk assessment, not a text fix)  
          Issue.         The Recitals recite the Company’s position that the termination was         “    a legitimate business reorganization unrelated to any complaint or protected activity.    ”         The records show the Company hired a replacement (Raj Mehta, Senior Director of Supply Chain & Logistics, $185,000) on             March 20, 2025 — six days         after the March 14, 2025 termination. The replacement salary is materially below Mr. Delano’s $218,000 base, which also supports an age-discrimination inference (a 58-year-old replaced by a lower-paid, presumably younger employee).  
          Why it matters.         A near-immediate backfill into a substantially similar role is strong evidence that the         “    elimination of position    ”         rationale is pretextual, which is the central issue for both the ORS 654.062(5) whistleblower-retaliation claim and the ADEA claim. Combined with the close temporal proximity between Mr. Delano’s protected activity (Nov. 2024 internal complaint; Dec. 2024 OSHA complaint) and his termination, Mr. Delano has a meaningful case. This (i) increases the value at risk if the matter is not settled, and (ii) means the Company should not over-rely on the restructuring narrative in negotiations or in any document that could be used as an admission.  
          Recommendation.         No change to the Recitals is required (they state the parties’ positions, not admissions), but the GC should be aware that Engel & Associates likely knows or will discover the Mehta hire. We recommend the Company (i) preserve all documents concerning the restructuring and the Mehta hire, (ii) be prepared to articulate a non-pretextual, documented business rationale if litigation resumes, and (iii) factor this exposure into the settlement value (the current economics may be reasonable given the exposure, but the Company should not assume its position is strong).  
        D. Protected activity and the pending OSHA investigation — §§ 6, 7, 15, and new § 19 —             High priority  
          Issue.         The confidentiality (§ 6), non-disparagement (§ 7), and         “    no future filings    ”         (§ 15(a)(ii)) provisions, read literally, could be construed to prohibit Mr. Delano from cooperating with the             pending Oregon OSHA investigation         or from receiving a whistleblower award. Provisions that chill or appear to chill safety whistleblowing are disfavored and, depending on framing, may be unenforceable or create separate retaliation exposure.  
          Why it matters.         The OSHA investigation (opened Jan. 22, 2025) is a government proceeding that continues regardless of this settlement; the Company cannot and should not try to shut it down through a private agreement with Mr. Delano. Standard, narrowly drawn carve-outs protect the Company by making clear the agreement does not impinge on protected activity.  
          Recommendation (reflected in redline).         Added (i) a government-cooperation carve-out and DTSA (18 U.S.C. § 1833(b)) / ORS 646.573 trade-secret immunity notice to § 6; (ii) a truthful-statement / government-cooperation carve-out to § 7; (iii) a carve-out to § 15(a)(ii) preserving the right to file with and cooperate with agencies and to receive awards, while waiving only the right to             personal         monetary recovery; and (iv) a consolidated             § 19 (Protected Activity and Government Cooperation)         confirming the OSHA investigation proceeds independently. We did             not         include any obligation that Mr. Delano withdraw or refrain from cooperating in the OSHA matter — such a term would be against public policy and create risk.  
        E. Non-competition — § 9 —             Medium-high priority  
          Issue.         The 18-month non-compete covers Oregon, Washington, Idaho, and California.  
          Why it matters.         (i)             California:         Non-competes are void except on narrow exceptions (sale of a business, etc.) under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600; the California prong is unenforceable and, worse, could be asserted against the Company in a California forum. (ii)             Oregon:         ORS 653.295 renders a non-compete voidable unless, among other requirements, the employee was informed in writing at least two weeks before the first day of work             or         the agreement is entered         “    upon a bona fide advancement.    ”         A non-compete first imposed             at separation         satisfies neither condition, so it is likely voidable even though Mr. Delano’s salary exceeds the statutory threshold and 18 months is the statutory cap. (iii) Including an overbroad, largely unenforceable covenant invites a challenge and, depending on the court, can put the entire agreement’s severability to the test.  
          Recommendation (reflected in redline).         Removed California from the geographic scope. We further recommend the Company             consider dropping the non-compete entirely         and relying on the non-solicitation (§ 10) and confidentiality (§ 6) covenants, which are exempt from ORS 653.295 and are materially more enforceable. If the Company wishes to keep a non-compete, it should be prepared to defend it and should narrow the duration/scope. The tolling provision in § 9 is aggressive and largely moot if the covenant is unenforceable; we left it but flag it for negotiation.  
        F. Employment reference — § 11 —             Medium priority / negotiation  
          Issue.         Section 11 obligates the Company to provide a         “    positive, neutral, or at minimum non-negative    ”         reference and to state that Mr. Delano         “    was a valued member of the Greenleaf team who made significant contributions.    ”  
          Why it matters.         Mandating specific laudatory statements (i) creates risk if the statements are not accurate and a prospective employer relies on them; (ii) is difficult to administer consistently across the named individuals (Wojcik, Huang, Engel, Foss); and (iii) is unusual — most employers provide only a neutral reference. The         “    shall not state he was terminated for cause    ”         portion is reasonable and we retained it.  
          Recommendation (reflected in redline).         Replaced with a             neutral reference         limited to dates of employment, final title, and a statement that Mr. Delano is             eligible for rehire    , with all inquiries directed to HR. This is defensible, easy to administer, and consistent with § 14 (re-employment eligibility).  
        G. Tax allocation and wage characterization — §§ 2, 3(b), 8 —             Medium priority  
          Issue.         The Draft allocates $275,000 to non-wage emotional-distress damages (1099-MISC) and $125,000 to         “    back pay … from the Separation Date through the Effective Date    ”         (W-2 wages). The back-pay period (Mar. 14 – May 5, 2025) is roughly 52 days, which at $218,000 annualized is only ~$31,000 of actual lost wages — far less than $125,000.  
          Why it matters.         The IRS scrutinizes settlement allocations. Characterizing $125,000 as wages when the actual wage loss for the stated period is ~$31,000 is hard to defend unless the back-pay bucket also covers other wage elements (e.g., the             2024 annual bonus    , which was         “    not determined    ”         but historically paid in March of the following year and for which Mr. Delano likely has a wage claim, and other incentive/benefits). Separately, § 2 represents that         “    no further wages … are owed … except as specifically provided    ”         — if the 2024 bonus or PTO was not in fact paid, that representation is inaccurate at signing.  
          Recommendation (reflected in redline).         Broadened the § 3(b) back-pay description to include         “    incentive and bonus compensation, and other compensation and benefits,    ”         which supports the W-2 treatment and reconciles with § 2.             Action item for GC/payroll:         (i) confirm whether the 2024 bonus and all accrued PTO were paid; (ii) if not, either pay them or ensure the $125,000 back-pay allocation is documented as covering them so the § 2 representation is accurate; (iii) coordinate the wage/non-wage split with tax counsel and the EPLI carrier (Item H). The Draft’s tax-disclaimer language (§ 8) is otherwise acceptable.  
        H. EPLI coverage and carrier consent —             High priority (action item)  
          Issue.         The Company carries EPLI through Timberline Insurance Group (Policy No. TIG-EPL-2024-00391). The settlement value is $537,500 (corrected).  
          Why it matters.         Most EPLI policies (i) require the insurer’s             consent to settle    , and (ii) may exclude or limit coverage for wage/back-pay components (EPLI typically covers non-wage damages and defense/fees, not wage items). Settling without carrier consent can forfeit coverage, and the wage/non-wage allocation can affect recovery.  
          Recommendation.         Before execution, the GC should (i) tender/notice the settlement to Timberline and obtain             written consent to settle    ; (ii) confirm the policy’s limits, retention/deductible, and whether the $125,000 back-pay component is covered; and (iii) align the settlement allocation with the carrier’s coverage position. The attorney-fee ($75,000) and emotional-distress ($275,000) components are more likely covered than the back-pay component.  
        I. Compensation Committee approval of RSU acceleration —             High priority (action item)  
          Issue.         The Plan provides that the             Compensation Committee         has discretion to approve accelerated vesting in connection with separation agreements. The Draft does not condition the § 3(d) acceleration on that approval, and § 15(b)(ii) represents only that the signatory is authorized to bind the Company generally.  
          Why it matters.         Without Compensation Committee approval, the Company may lack authority under the Plan to accelerate the RSUs, creating a performance risk (and a potential § 15(b) inaccuracy).  
          Recommendation (reflected in redline).         Added an express condition in § 3(d) that the acceleration is         “    subject in all respects to the approval of the Compensation Committee,    ”         with delivery timed to run from the later of the Effective Date or the approval date.             Action item:         obtain and document Compensation Committee approval before or contemporaneously with execution.  
        J. Missing boilerplate — new §§ 20–22 —             Medium priority  
          Issue.         The Draft contains no choice-of-law/venue, notices, assignment, further-assurances, headings/construction, or survival provisions.  
          Recommendation (reflected in redline).         Added:             § 20 (Choice of Law and Venue)         — Oregon law; exclusive jurisdiction/venue in Multnomah County, Oregon; jury-trial waiver;             § 21 (Notices)         — delivery methods and addresses;             § 22 (Miscellaneous)         — assignment (Delano may not assign; Company may assign to a successor), further assurances (including RSU/tax-withholding mechanics), headings/construction, and survival. The Company may also wish to add a             dispute-resolution/arbitration         clause to keep any enforcement dispute confidential; we did not insert one because arbitration is a policy call for the GC, but we recommend considering it.  
        K. Other items  
              Non-disparagement duration (§ 7).         The Draft makes non-disparagement perpetual. Perpetual mutual non-disparagement is generally enforceable (it is not a restraint on trade), but the Company may prefer a defined term (e.g., 3–5 years) to improve enforceability and reduce administrative burden. We retained perpetuity but added the carve-out (Item D); the GC may elect to limit the term.  
              Confidentiality clawback.         The Draft provides injunctive relief and damages for a confidentiality breach but no clawback of consideration. The Company may want a clawback/liquidated-damages remedy for material breach of §§ 6, 7, 9, or 10; we flag this as an optional negotiation item (clawbacks are sometimes challenged as penalties, so draft carefully).  
              Non-solicitation scope (§ 10(a)).         We narrowed the employee non-solicit to employees with whom Mr. Delano had material contact / confidential information, mirroring the customer non-solicit standard and improving enforceability.  
              Replacement hire / re-employment (§ 14).         § 14 requires removal of any         “    do-not-rehire    ”         flag and consideration for future openings. This is acceptable, but the Mehta hire into a similar role (Item C) makes the         “    elimination of position    ”         narrative internally inconsistent; the Company should ensure HR records and any rehire-eligibility coding are consistent with § 14.  
              Mutual release carve-outs (§ 12).         The Company’s release of Mr. Delano preserves claims for fraud/embezzlement/criminal conduct, breach of this Agreement, and surviving obligations under prior agreements. This is favorable and we left it as-is.  
      
         V. Items Requiring GC Action Before Execution  
              EPLI:         Tender to Timberline Insurance Group (Policy TIG-EPL-2024-00391); obtain written consent to settle; confirm limits, retention, and coverage of the back-pay component.  
              Compensation Committee:         Obtain and document approval of the RSU acceleration (5,000 RSUs) under the Plan.  
              Payroll / Tax:         Verify 2024 bonus and PTO payout; confirm the $125,000 back-pay allocation covers any unpaid wage items so § 2 is accurate; align wage/non-wage split with tax counsel and the carrier.  
              HR:         Confirm no         “    do-not-rehire    ”         flag exists and that records are consistent with §§ 11 and 14; prepare the written reference instructions required by § 11.  
              Document preservation:         Preserve all materials concerning the supply-chain restructuring and the Raj Mehta hire in light of the pretext risk (Item C).  
      
        VI. Recommended Negotiation Posture and Next Steps  
              Non-negotiable (Company must insist):         (i) RSU valuation correction to $12.50/share and aggregate to $537,500 (and removal of the false         “    most recent valuation    ”         statement); (ii) OWBPA fixes (21-day consideration, 7-day revocation, attorney-consultation advice, Effective Date redefinition); (iii) protected-activity carve-outs (§§ 6, 7, 15, 19).  
              Strongly recommended:         (iv) remove California from the non-compete (and consider dropping the non-compete in favor of non-solicitation); (v) neutral reference in lieu of mandated positive statements; (vi) add §§ 20–22 boilerplate.  
              Negotiation leverage we hold:         The valuation correction             increases         the stated value to Mr. Delano by $12,500, which gives us a concession to offer in exchange for accepting the OWBPA/carve-out/reference changes. We can frame the corrected $537,500 as the Company’s good-faith correction and condition acceptance of the higher figure on Delano’s acceptance of the protective revisions.  
              Leverage Delano holds:         The Mehta replacement hire and the temporal proximity of protected activity to termination give Delano a strong pretext case; the Company should not assume its litigation position is favorable and should value the settlement accordingly.  
              Next step:         Return the redline to Engel & Associates with a cover letter framing the corrections as necessary for enforceability and accuracy, and requesting a 21-day consideration clock from Delano’s receipt of the revised draft.  
      
        VII. Conclusion  
      The Draft is a workable framework but should not be signed in its current form. The RSU valuation is wrong and is mischaracterized as         “    most recent    ”    ; the ADEA release does not satisfy the OWBPA and is likely invalid; the confidentiality/non-disparagement/no-filing provisions lack protected-activity carve-outs in the shadow of a pending OSHA investigation; the non-compete is largely unenforceable; and standard boilerplate is missing. The accompanying redline addresses each of these. The GC should also complete the pre-execution coordination items in Part V — most importantly EPLI carrier consent and Compensation Committee approval — before the Company executes.  
      We are available to discuss any of the foregoing or to adjust the redline in light of the Company’s negotiating strategy.

       1. Introduction and Scope  
      This memorandum reports the results of a cross-referenced review of the H-1B petition package assembled on behalf of             Rajesh Venkataraman         (the         “    Beneficiary    ”    ) by             Brightfield Analytics Inc.         (the         “    Petitioner    ”    ) for the position of             Senior Machine Learning Engineer    . Issues are ranked             Critical    ,             Significant    ,             Moderate    , and             Minor/Clerical         based on their potential to trigger a Request for Evidence (RFE), a Notice of Intent to Deny/Revoke (NOID/NOIR), or a Department of Labor (DOL) compliance finding.

        3. Summary of Key Findings  
      The package is, on the whole, a competently assembled H-1B support file: the LCA is certified, the PWD is valid and supports the offered wage at the certified level, the SOC/NAICS coding is internally consistent, and the employer letter articulates a colorable specialty-occupation theory. However, the cross-reference review surfaced             one pivotal internal contradiction         that, if not resolved, is likely to drive the outcome of the case, together with several substantive and clerical issues.  
          Headline finding.         The employer letter represents that the Beneficiary         “    is currently maintaining valid F-1 Optional Practical Training (OPT) status.    ”         F-1 post-completion OPT, however, is available only on the basis of a degree earned at a U.S. SEVP-certified school. The only education documented anywhere in the package is a             foreign         Bachelor of Technology (B.Tech) in Electronics and Communication Engineering from JNTU Hyderabad, conferred in             May 2014    . A foreign degree cannot confer F-1 status or OPT, and even a U.S. degree from 2014 could not sustain OPT through 2025. The package therefore either (a) omits a U.S. degree that is the actual basis of the Beneficiary’s current status, or (b) misstates the Beneficiary’s nonimmigrant status. Either way the change-of-status request, the cap-category analysis, and the qualification analysis are all affected. This is addressed in Issue C-1.  
      Other notable findings include a             field-of-study gap         between the Beneficiary’s ECE degree and the CS/ML/AI requirement (Issue S-1); a             wage-level inconsistency         in which the duties described to USCIS read as DOL Level 3 while the LCA/PWD are certified at Level 2 (Issue S-2); an             LCA posting period of only 8 business days         where 10 are required (Issue M-1);             résumé date discrepancies         that mask a ~3.5-month employment gap (Issue M-2); and a cluster of             clerical inconsistencies         in employer contact telephone numbers, including a posting notice that lists the Beneficiary’s personal cell number as the employer contact (Issues M-7 and m-1).

         5. Critical Issues (resolve before filing)  
       C-1. F-1 OPT status claim is irreconcilable with the documented education  
      The employer letter states that the Beneficiary         “    is currently maintaining valid F-1 Optional Practical Training (OPT) status and will transition to H-1B nonimmigrant status effective October 1, 2025,    ”         framing the petition as a change of status.  
      This representation cannot be reconciled with the education record in the package:  
          F-1 post-completion OPT is available             only         on the basis of a degree earned at a U.S. SEVP-certified institution. A foreign degree — here, the B.Tech from JNTU Hyderabad — does not, and cannot, confer F-1 status or OPT.  
          The only degree documented anywhere in the package is the 2014 Indian B.Tech. No U.S. degree, I-20, EAD, or other F-1 evidence is included.  
          Even assuming arguendo a U.S. degree dated May 2014, standard post-completion OPT (12 months) plus a 24-month STEM extension would expire no later than approximately May 2017 — yet the package documents continuous U.S. employment from April 2017 (NovaStar) through the present (Brightfield), a span F-1 OPT based on a 2014 degree could not cover.  
          Implications.         This single inconsistency touches three distinct adjudication issues:  
              Change-of-status eligibility.         A change of status requires the Beneficiary to be in, and maintain, lawful nonimmigrant status through the requested H-1B start date. If the F-1 OPT representation is inaccurate, the I-539/I-129 change-of-status request is improper and the petition should be filed for consular notification instead.  
              Cap category.         The October 1, 2025 start date and the F-1→H-1B         “    cap gap    ”         framing indicate a cap-subject FY2026 petition. If the Beneficiary in fact holds a U.S. master’s degree (the most likely explanation for a current F-1 STEM OPT), he may be eligible for the             U.S. advanced-degree (master’s) cap exemption    , which materially changes the registration strategy and selection odds. The package is silent on cap category.  
              Qualification analysis.         A U.S. master’s in computer science, data science, or a related field would (a) squarely satisfy the position’s         “    CS/ML/AI or closely related field    ”         requirement, dissolving the field-of-study problem in Issue S-1, and (b) make the foreign B.Tech supplementary rather than central. Its absence from the package is therefore not merely an omission but a potential misframing of the entire qualification theory.  
          Required action.         Determine the Beneficiary’s actual current nonimmigrant status and the degree(s) underlying it. If a U.S. degree exists, add it (with credential evaluation if needed), the corresponding I-20(s), EAD card(s), and I-94, and re-cast the qualification and cap-category analysis accordingly. If no U.S. degree exists, correct the status representation and convert the petition to consular processing (and address the status-continuity question in C-2).  
        C-2. Undocumented nonimmigrant status / employment-authorization continuity (2017–2025)  
      Independent of the F-1 OPT label, the package documents roughly eight years of U.S. employment (NovaStar, April 2017–July 2020; self-employment, August 2020–August 2023; Brightfield, September 2023–present) but supplies             no evidence of the nonimmigrant status or employment authorization         that permitted any of that work. The self-employment period is especially sensitive: most nonimmigrant classifications that permit employment do not permit unrestricted self-employment, and F-1 OPT (if that were the basis) has strict self-employment limits and could not span 2020–2023 on a 2014 degree.  
          Implications.         An adjudicator examining the Beneficiary’s history may question whether he maintained lawful status throughout — a prerequisite for a change of status and a factor USCIS weighs under its discretion framework. Unexplained status gaps are a common RFE trigger.  
          Required action.         Provide a complete status timeline with documentary support (I-94s, approval notices, I-20s/EADs, or other status evidence) covering 2017–2025. If any period of unauthorized presence or status lapse exists, evaluate change-of-status vs. consular processing and any 3/10-year bar exposure before filing.  
        C-3. Missing H-1B registration selection notice (cap-subject filing)  
      The start date (October 1, 2025), the F-1→H-1B transition language, and the April 7, 2025 employer-letter date (the FY2026 cap filing window opened April 1, 2025) all indicate a             cap-subject         petition. Brightfield Analytics is a for-profit commercial employer and is not cap-exempt. A cap-subject H-1B petition may be filed only on the basis of a             selected         H-1B electronic registration. No registration selection notice appears in the package.  
          Required action.         Confirm cap subjectivity and include the USCIS H-1B registration selection notice. Confirm whether the Beneficiary is being filed under the regular cap or the U.S. master’s cap (see C-1) and ensure the registration was submitted in the correct category.  
      
         6. Significant Issues (substantive vulnerabilities)  
       S-1. Field-of-study mismatch — ECE degree vs. CS/ML/AI requirement  
      The position requires         “    a Bachelor’s degree or higher in Computer Science, Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence, or a closely related field.    ”         The Beneficiary’s degree is in             Electronics and Communication Engineering (ECE)    . The credential evaluation:  
          equates the B.Tech to a U.S.             B.S. in Electronics and Communication Engineering         (not in CS/ML/AI);  
          states that computer-science/IT coursework constitutes only             ~12 of ~160 credit hours (~7.5%)         of the program; and  
          expressly disclaims any opinion on         “    whether the applicant’s field of study satisfies the requirements of any particular position or petition.    ”  
      The employer letter attempts to bridge the gap by noting three relevant courses (C Programming, Data Structures, Introduction to Artificial Intelligence). That is a thin reed: three courses, ~7.5% of the curriculum, and the evaluation’s own figures undercut the         “    closely related    ”         argument. USCIS routinely challenges ECE→CS/ML qualification where the CS content is this limited and no expert opinion addresses field-of-study equivalence.  
          Mitigating factors.         The Beneficiary has substantial post-graduation ML experience (NovaStar, self-employment, Brightfield), and the employer letter offers an experience-based alternative qualification (see S-3). ECE is also generally treated as a STEM/engineering discipline, and several ECE courses (signals, DSP, probability, computer architecture) have analytical overlap with ML foundations.  
          Required action.         Strengthen the field-of-study bridge by one or more of: (a) obtaining an expert opinion / supplemental evaluation that specifically opines on ECE→CS/ML/AI equivalence (the current evaluation deliberately does not); (b) leaning on the experience-based alternative in S-3 with supporting documentation; or (c) if a U.S. qualifying degree exists (C-1), re-centering the qualification theory on it. As filed, the qualification theory leans on a degree the evaluation itself characterizes as only 7.5% computer-science content.  
        S-2. Wage-level inconsistency — duties read as Level 3; LCA/PWD certified at Level 2  
      The PWD and LCA are certified at             DOL Wage Level 2         ($113,734), and the offered wage ($128,000) satisfies Level 2. The problem is internal consistency: the job as described to USCIS exhibits the hallmark traits of a             higher         wage level.  
      DOL’s own level descriptions in the PWD are instructive:  
              Level 2 (Qualified):         “    moderately complex tasks requiring             limited judgment    .    ”  
              Level 3 (Experienced):         “    sound understanding of the occupation, may             exercise independent judgment    , and may have             supervisory responsibilities    .    ”         ($137,946)  
              Level 4 (Fully Competent):         “    advanced skills … exercise broad latitude …             supervise and train         other workers.    ”         ($162,157)  
      The employer letter describes the position as a             “        Senior        ”         Machine Learning Engineer who             “        leads and mentors a team        ”         of junior data scientists,             “        exercises independent judgment        ”         in designing ML solutions, and requires a             minimum of 3 years’         progressive experience. Those attributes map to Level 3 (independent judgment + supervisory responsibilities) and arguably Level 4 (supervise and train). The         “    Senior    ”         title itself is strongly associated with Level 3/4 in DOL practice.  
          Implications.         The offered wage of $128,000 sits             between         Level 2 ($113,734) and Level 3 ($137,946). If the position is properly classified at Level 3, the offered wage would be             deficient         by ~$10,000, creating a prevailing-wage compliance exposure under INA § 212(n)(1)(A) and a basis for DOL scrutiny. Even at the USCIS stage, the contrast between a         “    limited judgment    ”         Level 2 attestation made to DOL and a         “    senior, supervisory, independent-judgment    ”         description made to USCIS is an internal inconsistency an adjudicator may notice and use to question either the wage or the bona fides of the job description.  
          Required action.         Reconcile the description and the wage level. Either (a) moderate the employer-letter language to support Level 2 (de-emphasize supervisory/    “    independent judgment    ”    /    “    senior    ”         framing and align the duties with         “    moderately complex tasks, limited judgment    ”    ), or (b) raise the offered wage to at least $137,946 to cover Level 3 and obtain a re-determination if necessary. The current package tells DOL         “    Level 2 / limited judgment    ”         while telling USCIS         “    senior / leads a team / independent judgment    ”         — that divergence should be eliminated.  
        S-3.         “    Any engineering discipline + 5 years    ”         alternative — double-edged  
      The employer letter offers an alternative qualification:         “    a Bachelor’s degree in             any engineering discipline         combined with             5 years         of progressive experience in machine learning.    ”         This is both a potential saving grace for the ECE field-of-study problem (S-1) and a specialty-occupation vulnerability:  
              Vulnerability.         “    Any engineering discipline    ”         is broad. USCIS has repeatedly held that an employer’s willingness to accept             any         general degree can undercut the showing that the position             requires         a specialized body of knowledge in a specific specialty — i.e., it can weaken the § 214(i)(1) specialty-occupation analysis. The broader the acceptable field, the harder it is to prove the degree requirement is a true minimum.  
              Internal inconsistency.         The primary requirement demands             3 years’         experience; the alternative demands             5 years’    . A single position should have a coherent experience requirement; the 3-vs-5 split is awkward and invites the question which figure actually governs.  
              Reliance problem.         To rely on the alternative, the Beneficiary must show 5 years of             progressive ML         experience. His ML-specific experience (NovaStar + self-employment + Brightfield) plausibly exceeds 5 years, but the self-employment period (3 of those years) is undocumented (Issue M-5), so the         “    progressive    ”         showing is presently unsupported for a large slice of the period.  
          Required action.         Tighten the alternative (e.g.,         “    a Bachelor’s degree in Computer Science, Electrical Engineering, Computer Engineering, or a closely related discipline …    ”    ) to preserve specialty-occupation strength; reconcile the 3- vs. 5-year experience figures; and document the self-employment period (M-5) if the alternative will be relied upon.  
        S-4. Credential-evaluator credibility —         “    Halford University    ”         Ph.D.  
      The credential evaluation is signed by         “    Dr. Linda Farnsworth, Ph.D., Senior Credential Analyst,    ”         whose doctoral credential is listed as         “    Ph.D., Education —             Halford University    .    ”         Halford University is not a recognized, regionally accredited U.S. institution of higher education and appears to be either unaccredited or non-existent as a mainstream university. The credibility of a credential evaluation rests substantially on the evaluator’s own qualifications; a doctoral degree from a questionable institution is a red flag that an adjudicator (or, in litigation, opposing counsel) may seize upon to discount the evaluation.  
      Westbridge Credential Services claims membership in NACES and AICE. NACES membership is the practical gold standard for immigration evaluations and would substantially rehabilitate the evaluation’s weight even if the individual analyst’s credential is weak — but only if the membership is current and verifiable.  
          Required action.         Verify (a) Westbridge Credential Services’ current NACES membership (check the NACES member directory) and (b) Dr. Farnsworth’s actual qualifications. If either is questionable, replace the evaluation with one from a verifiable NACES-member agency and a credentialed analyst. At minimum, remove or correct the         “    Halford University    ”         line.  
      
         7. Moderate Issues (consistency / compliance)  
       M-1. LCA posting period is only 8 business days (10 required)  
      20 C.F.R. § 655.734(b)(1) requires that the notice of filing be posted for             10 consecutive business days    . The LCA (Section J) and the posting notice both state posting from             March 10, 2025 through March 19, 2025    . March 10, 2025 is a Monday and March 19, 2025 is a Wednesday; excluding the weekend of March 15–16, that span is             8 business days         — two short of the regulatory minimum. (The period is 10             calendar         days, suggesting the preparer may have confused calendar and business days.)  
          Implications.         An insufficient posting period is a DOL compliance deficiency that can undermine the LCA attestation and, in enforcement, expose the employer to back-wage and penalty liability. USCIS generally does not independently police posting duration, but DOL does, and a defective posting is a self-contained compliance risk.  
          Required action.         Re-post the notice for a full 10 consecutive business days and update the LCA/posting-notice records accordingly before relying on the LCA in the petition.  
        M-2. Résumé date discrepancies mask an employment gap  
      The résumé states Kiran employment as         “    June 2014 –             March 2017    ”         and NovaStar as         “    April 2017 –             August 2020    .    ”         Both the experience letters and the employer letter give different end dates:  
          Kiran: June 16, 2014 –             December 9, 2016         (résumé overstates by ~3 months).  
          NovaStar: April 2017 –             July 2020         (résumé overstates by ~1 month).  
      The résumé’s inflated Kiran end date (March 2017) abuts its NovaStar start date (April 2017), concealing an actual             ~3.5-month gap         (December 9, 2016 – April 2017) that the experience letters and employer letter reveal. Submitting a résumé that conflicts with the experience letters in the same package is both unprofessional and a credibility risk: an adjudicator comparing the documents may infer the Beneficiary inflated his tenure and obscured a gap.  
          Required action.         Correct the résumé to match the experience letters (Kiran through December 2016; NovaStar through July 2020) and, if relevant, briefly account for the January–March 2017 period (e.g., job search / relocation). Ensure every date in the package tells the same story.  
        M-3. Team-size discrepancy —         “    3 junior data scientists    ”         vs. 2 direct reports  
      The employer letter (duty 4) states the Senior ML Engineer will be         “    leading and mentoring a team of             3         junior data scientists.    ”         The organizational chart shows the proposed position with             2         direct reports (two         “    Data Scientist (Filled)    ”         positions). This numerical inconsistency directly affects the supervisory characterization of the role — the same characterization that drives both the specialty-occupation analysis and the wage-level question (S-2).  
          Required action.         Reconcile the headcount. If the team is two, correct the employer letter; if three, update the org chart (and confirm the third report is a real, funded position). The supervisory claim should be accurate and consistent across documents.  
        M-4. Worksite / LCA coverage — Denver satellite and remote team not covered  
      The employer letter describes Brightfield as operating across         “    its Austin headquarters, a satellite office in             Denver, Colorado    , and a small distributed             remote         team.    ”         The LCA, however, certifies a             single worksite         (Austin) and states         “    Additional Worksites: None.    ”         Under current DOL rules, an H-1B worker may telework from a home office within the             same area of intended employment (MSA)         as the certified worksite without a new LCA, but             Denver is a different MSA    , and any work there would require separate LCA coverage (or a new LCA / short-term placement compliance).  
          Implications.         If the Beneficiary will in fact work exclusively at the Austin HQ, the single-worksite LCA is fine and the employer letter’s multi-location description is merely background. But if any Denver or out-of-MSA remote work is contemplated, the LCA does not cover it.  
          Required action.         Confirm in writing that the Beneficiary will perform all duties at the Austin HQ (4200 Congress Avenue). If any off-site/out-of-MSA work is possible, obtain appropriate LCA coverage and a short-term-placement or new-worksites analysis. Consider aligning the employer letter’s         “    Denver/remote    ”         language with the single-worksite LCA to avoid an apparent conflict.  
        M-5. Independent-consultant period (Aug 2020 – Aug 2023) is undocumented  
      Three years of the Beneficiary’s experience — the self-employed         “    Independent Machine Learning Consultant    ”         period — are supported by             no documentation         in the package (no client letters, 1099s, contracts, business registration, invoices, or tax records). This period is relied upon both for the         “    over 8 years of progressive experience    ”         assertion and, potentially, for the 5-year alternative qualification (S-3). Self-employment is inherently harder to verify, and an adjudicator may discount undocumented self-employment when evaluating experience.  
          Required action.         Obtain documentary support for the self-employment period: 1099/1040 Schedule C records, letters from two or more clients describing the ML work performed and dates, and any business-registration evidence.

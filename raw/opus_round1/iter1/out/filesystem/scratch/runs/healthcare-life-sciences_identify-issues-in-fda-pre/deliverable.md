       I. Executive Summary  
      At Kate’s request, I have completed a comprehensive review of the draft Pre-Submission (Q-Sub) package for the VascuClear 3000 Thrombectomy System (    “    VascuClear 3000    ”         or         “    the Device    ”    ) in advance of the June 15, 2025 filing target. This memorandum identifies and prioritizes the regulatory, scientific, clinical, labeling, and strategic deficiencies I have identified, and recommends specific corrective actions.  
      The package is well-organized and professionally drafted. However, my review surfaces             three threshold (Tier 1) issues that, in my judgment, must be resolved before the package is filed    , because each has the potential to change the regulatory pathway, the review division, or the viability of the substantial equivalence (    “    SE    ”    ) determination on which the entire 510(k) strategy depends. These are: (1) the unresolved combination-product classification question; (2) the incorrect         “    no IDE / post-clearance    ”         characterization of the proposed clinical study; and (3) the substantial equivalence argument, which rests on a predicate (ThrombEx 200, K192847) that lacks both of the Device’s defining technological features — the rotating impeller and the integrated drug-delivery lumen.  
      Below these, I identify             eight high-priority (Tier 2) scientific and testing deficiencies         that FDA is likely to raise at the Pre-Submission meeting and that, if not addressed, will delay or destabilize the review. These include a biocompatibility contact-duration classification that is internally contradicted by the Device’s own labeling (72-hour indwell vs.     “    <24-hour limited    ”         contact), an impeller fatigue test of only 500 revolutions (≈2.5 seconds of operation), an omitted EMC test battery, a saline-only drug-delivery characterization, and a clinical study that is statistically underpowered relative to its stated performance goal.  
      I further identify             seven medium-priority (Tier 3) gaps         (MRI safety data, shelf-life validation, reprocessing validation, dynamic hemolysis, embolic-debris characterization, IFU deficiencies, and cross-document material/specification inconsistencies) and a short list of drafting items.  
      A consolidated issue table appears in Section VIII. My overarching recommendation is that the two questions Kate raised in her May 12 email — (a) whether FDA views the Device as a combination product, and (b) whether an IDE is required for the proposed study — be added expressly to the Pre-Submission as questions to FDA, and that the package be revised to resolve the internal contradictions identified below before filing. These steps are low-cost relative to the risk of committing to a pathway that FDA may reject at the meeting.

        III. Tier 1 — Threshold Issues (Resolve Before Filing)  
       Issue 1 — Combination-Product Classification Is Unaddressed in the Package  
          Where present:         Cover Letter §VII (Regulatory Pathway Statement); Device Description §§2.4, 5.1; Draft IFU §§2, 9; Clinical Synopsis §§2, 8.2; Email chain (Pressman, May 5 & May 12; Marsh, April 28 & May 9).  
          Observation.         The package commits the Device to a traditional 510(k) under product code GXD / 21 CFR 870.1250 and asserts, through Dr. Marsh’s analysis, that the Device is             not         a combination product because the thrombolytic agent is physician-supplied and not co-packaged. The proposed indication, however, expressly claims         “    simultaneous local delivery of physician-specified thrombolytic agents    ”         as a therapeutic function, the catheter incorporates a lumen         “    purpose-built    ”         for drug delivery, and the clinical protocol administers alteplase through that lumen at specified doses (0.5–1.0 mg/hr). Notwithstanding the internal debate reflected in the email chain,             the package as drafted does not include any question to FDA asking whether the Device should be classified as a combination product under 21 CFR Part 3.         The six questions in Cover Letter §VI are silent on the point.  
          Analysis.         The infusion-pump analogy on which the         “    not a combination product    ”         position rests is distinguishable. A general-purpose infusion pump is indicated for fluid delivery without reference to any particular drug or therapeutic effect; the VascuClear 3000’s indication ties the lumen to a specific therapeutic class (thrombolytics) and a specific clinical purpose (pharmacomechanical thrombectomy). Under 21 CFR 3.2(e), a combination product includes, among other things, a device         “    intended for use only with    ”         another regulated article where the proposed labeling so specifies. The Device’s labeling and design arguably satisfy that prong. More importantly, the proposed predicate (ThrombEx 200) has             no drug-delivery capability whatsoever    , so there is no predicate precedent for the pharmacomechanical approach within the GXD family. If FDA concludes the Device is a combination product, the consequences are material: a Request for Designation may be required (21 CFR 3.7), the lead review center and/or consultative review by the Office of Combination Products could change, and the review timeline and data expectations could expand well beyond a conventional 510(k). Dr. Marsh’s position may ultimately prove correct, but it is presently an             assumption    , not a confirmed determination, and it is the load-bearing assumption of the entire pathway.  
          Recommendation.         Add an express question to the Pre-Submission asking FDA whether the Device, as designed and labeled, should be classified as a combination product under 21 CFR Part 3, and if so, what pathway and review-division implications follow. This is the         “    cheap insurance    ”         Kate identified on May 12. I would also recommend that the package state Clearfield’s             position         (not a combination product) together with the             rationale    , so that FDA’s feedback is directed to a stated view rather than an open-ended inquiry. Finally, the indication language should be revisited in light of this issue (see Issue 4).  
      
        Issue 2 — The Clinical Study Is Mischaracterized as         “    Post-Clearance,    ”         and the IDE Question Is Unanswered  
          Where present:         Clinical Synopsis §§5, 6, 15; Cover Letter §VI(Q4); Email chain (Pressman, May 5 & May 12; Marsh, April 28 & May 9).  
          Observation.         The Clinical Synopsis (§6) states that         “    an Investigational Device Exemption (IDE) is not anticipated    ”         because         “    the clinical study is designed as a post-clearance data collection effort, and the VascuClear 3000 is expected to have received 510(k) clearance prior to study initiation.    ”         This is internally inconsistent with the package’s own timeline: the study is scheduled to begin enrolling in             Q4 2025         (Synopsis §15), while the 510(k) clearance target is             December 31, 2025         (Cover Letter; email chain), and the Synopsis elsewhere (§3) describes the study as generating data         “    to support the 510(k) submission.    ”         A study that begins before clearance and is intended to generate supportive data for the clearance decision is, by definition, not         “    post-clearance.    ”         The package nonetheless does not ask FDA whether an IDE is required.  
          Analysis.         Under 21 CFR 812.3(m), an         “    investigational device    ”         is a device that is the object of a clinical investigation; the VascuClear 3000 has not received 510(k) clearance, so its use in the proposed study is investigational. Whether an IDE is             required         turns on the significant-risk (    “    SR    ”    ) / non-significant-risk (    “    NSR    ”    ) determination. The Sponsor assumes NSR, but the SR/NSR determination is not the Sponsor’s alone — for an SR device, an FDA-approved IDE is required before enrollment (21 CFR 812.20), and even for an NSR device, the IRB makes the NSR determination subject to FDA oversight. Several features reasonably support an SR characterization: an uncleared device with a rotating 12,000-RPM impeller in the venous system, aspiration to −650 mmHg, and concurrent tPA infusion (itself a hemorrhagic-risk agent) over a potential 72-hour indwell. If FDA or an IRB determines the study is SR, the timeline and budget impact is substantial — an IDE application, FDA review, and approval must precede enrollment. Proceeding on the unverified assumption that no IDE is needed creates both a compliance risk (use of an investigational device without a required IDE) and a timeline risk. The combination-product question (Issue 1) compounds this: if the Device is a combination product, the tPA administration through the Device lumen may implicate drug-investigational requirements as well.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Add an express question to the Pre-Submission asking whether FDA considers the proposed study to require an IDE, and if so, whether FDA concurs with an SR or NSR characterization. (b) Correct the Synopsis to remove the         “    post-clearance    ”         characterization, which is contradicted by the package’s own timeline; describe the study accurately as a pre-clearance supportive study whose data may be included in the 510(k) or, alternatively, conducted post-clearance — but not both simultaneously. (c) Reconcile the role of the clinical data: the Cover Letter (§VI(Q4)) asks FDA whether the study         “    provides adequate supportive evidence for the 510(k) submission,    ”         while the Synopsis (§6) says the data         “    may be included in the 510(k) submission package             or         maintained as a post-market clinical study.    ”         The package should take a definitive position on whether clinical data is in the 510(k) critical path, because that position drives both the IDE analysis and the clearance-timeline feasibility (see Issue 11).  
      
        Issue 3 — The Substantial Equivalence Argument Is Weak; the Predicate Lacks Both Defining Technologies  
          Where present:         Cover Letter §III; Device Description §§3, 4, 5; ThrombEx 200 510(k) Summary (K192847).  
          Observation.         The sole proposed predicate, the ThrombEx 200 (K192847), is an 8 Fr / 55 cm catheter that removes thrombus by             Bernoulli-effect (hydrodynamic) aspiration    , has             no rotating impeller or mechanical maceration element    , has a             single lumen         with             no drug-delivery capability    , and is indicated for         “    percutaneous removal of thrombus from the peripheral vasculature    ”         (broad, mechanical only). The VascuClear 3000 is a 10 Fr / 65 cm catheter that removes thrombus by a             rotating helical impeller (mechanical maceration) plus aspiration    , has a             dedicated thrombolytic infusion lumen    , and is indicated for             pharmacomechanical thrombectomy         (mechanical removal             plus         drug delivery) in a defined venous segment. The package asserts these differences         “    do not raise new questions of safety and effectiveness    ”         and cites, in support, that         “    the rotating impeller mechanism has been employed in previously cleared thrombectomy devices    ”         —             without identifying a single such device by name, 510(k) number, or product code.         No reference predicate is proposed for either the impeller mechanism or the drug-delivery lumen.  
          Analysis.         SE requires the same intended use             and         either the same technological characteristics or different characteristics that do not raise new questions of safety and effectiveness. Here, both prongs are strained:  
              Intended use.         The predicate’s use is purely mechanical; the subject’s use adds local drug delivery as a co-primary therapeutic function stated in the indication. The package frames the narrower anatomy (iliofemoral vs.     “    peripheral    ”    ) as a favorable         “    narrowing,    ”         but the addition of a drug-delivery therapeutic claim is an             expansion         of intended use relative to the predicate, not a narrowing. The         “    key differentiating feature    ”         language in the Cover Letter (§II) confirms that drug delivery is positioned as a core, not ancillary, function.  
              Technological characteristics / new questions.         A rotating 12,000-RPM impeller in contact with blood and the vessel wall raises safety questions the Bernoulli-aspiration predicate does not address: vessel-wall injury/perforation/dissection, hemolysis, particulate and embolic debris generation, and impeller fracture/detachment with potential embolization. A drug-delivery lumen raises separate questions: drug-device material compatibility, dosing accuracy, drug adsorption/loss, air embolism, and infection risk during extended indwell. None of these is addressed by the predicate, and the bench program is under-scoped to address them (see Issues 5, 6, 9, 10, 16, 17).  
      The Cover Letter’s assertion that rotating-impeller thrombectomy devices exist in the cleared landscape is plausible, but it is presently             unsupported         — and an unsupported assertion is unlikely to survive FDA scrutiny. If FDA determines that the differences raise new questions of safety and effectiveness, the Device may be deemed not substantially equivalent (    “    NSE    ”    ), which could require a De Novo or PMA pathway with materially different timelines and data burdens. This is the existential risk of the program.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Identify and cite at least one specific, cleared             reference device employing a rotating/impeller-based thrombectomy mechanism         (with 510(k) number and product code) to support the         “    no new questions    ”         argument for the impeller. (b) Identify and cite a             reference device with catheter-based drug-delivery / infusion capability         (e.g., an infusion catheter or pharmacomechanical device) to bridge the drug-delivery gap. (c) Strengthen the SE discussion to address each new-question category (vessel injury, hemolysis, particulate/embolic, drug compatibility/dosing) with a specific pointer to the bench test that mitigates it — and ensure those bench tests are adequately scoped (see Tier 2). (d) Add a question to FDA asking whether additional reference predicates are recommended (the Device Description already poses this as Question 4, but it should be elevated and made concrete). (e) Consider whether the indication should be restructured so that mechanical thrombectomy is the primary indication and drug delivery is described as a device feature rather than a co-primary therapeutic claim — this would strengthen both the SE argument and the combination-product position (see Issue 4), though it should be adopted only after confirming it does not undermine the Device’s commercial value proposition.  
      
         IV. Tier 2 — High-Priority Scientific, Testing & Clinical Deficiencies  
       Issue 4 — Indication for Use: Drug Delivery as Co-Primary Claim (Strategic/SE/Combination-Product Nexus)  
          Where present:         Cover Letter §IV; Device Description §2.4; Draft IFU §2; Clinical Synopsis §4.1; Email chain (Marsh, May 9).  
          Observation.         The indication states the Device is         “    indicated for the percutaneous removal of thrombus             and simultaneous local delivery of physician-specified thrombolytic agents    .    ”         Drug delivery is thus a co-primary therapeutic claim. Dr. Marsh proposed (May 9) restructuring the indication so the primary claim is mechanical thrombectomy, with drug delivery described as a feature in the device description. The package as filed retains the co-primary formulation.  
          Analysis.         The co-primary drug-delivery claim is the single thread that ties together the combination-product risk (Issue 1), the SE weakness (Issue 3), and the IDE/drug-investigational question (Issue 2). Narrowing the indication to mechanical thrombectomy (with drug delivery as a described feature) would meaningfully reduce all three risks and is consistent with Dr. Marsh’s stated         “    standard practice for devices that have ancillary capabilities.    ”         The trade-off is commercial: the pharmacomechanical single-catheter value proposition is the Device’s differentiator. This is a business judgment for Clearfield, but the regulatory consequences of the current formulation should be presented to Dr. Okonjo and the board explicitly.  
          Recommendation.         Present Clearfield with a clear-eyed choice between (a) retaining the co-primary drug-delivery indication (with the attendant combination-product, SE, and IDE risks) and (b) restructuring to a mechanical-primary indication with drug delivery as a feature. Whichever is chosen, ensure the indication language is identical across the Cover Letter, Device Description, IFU, and Clinical Synopsis (it currently is consistent, which is good). Also harmonize the IFU’s         “    not intended for … pulmonary vasculature    ”         (IFU §2) with the Device Description’s         “    not intended for … pediatric patients    ”         (DD §2.4) — these are different exclusions stated in different documents and should be reconciled into a single, consistent set of contraindicated uses.  
      
        Issue 5 — Biocompatibility Contact-Duration Classification Is Internally Contradicted by the Device’s Own Labeling  
          Where present:         Device Description §4.2 (comparison table); Proposed Testing Plan §§2.1, 2.2, 6.2; Draft IFU §§1, 8.4.  
          Observation.         The Device Description comparison table states, in adjacent rows, that the Device’s         “    Device Contact Duration    ”         is         “        Up to 72 hours         (per draft IFU) for extended thrombolytic infusion therapy    ”             and         that         “    Biocompatibility … contact duration [is] classified as         ‘        limited    ’         (<24 hours).    ”         The IFU expressly permits the catheter to         “    remain in situ for up to 72 hours.    ”         The Testing Plan (§2.1) classifies the catheter as         “    limited contact duration (less than 24 hours)    ”         and, on that basis,             excludes         subchronic implantation (ISO 10993-6), subchronic systemic toxicity (ISO 10993-11), and genotoxicity (ISO 10993-3). The EtO residual acceptance limits (Testing Plan §6.2: EO ≤4 mg, ECH ≤9 mg) are likewise predicated on         “    limited exposure duration.    ”  
          Analysis.         Under ISO 10993-1:2018, Table A.1, contact duration categories are: limited (≤24 h), prolonged (>24 h to 30 d), and permanent (>30 d). A 72-hour indwell is             prolonged    , not limited. The contradiction is not merely a drafting error — it is set out in two adjacent rows of the same comparison table that FDA will read first. Misclassifying the contact duration (i) under-scopes the biocompatibility battery (prolonged blood contact may require additional endpoints), (ii) renders the exclusion of genotoxicity, subchronic toxicity, and implantation unjustified, and (iii) applies EtO residual limits that may be too permissive for a 72-hour exposure (ISO 10993-7 tolerable-contact limits are duration-dependent and generally more stringent for prolonged contact). FDA will almost certainly flag this.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Decide whether the 72-hour indwell is a labeled claim the company intends to support. If yes, reclassify the contact duration as             prolonged    , re-derive the biocompatibility endpoint matrix from ISO 10993-1:2018 Table A.1 for prolonged blood contact, and re-evaluate the excluded endpoints (genotoxicity, subchronic systemic toxicity, and possibly implantation/subchronic per the matrix). (b) Re-derive the EtO/ECH residual limits under ISO 10993-7 for the prolonged contact duration. (c) If the 72-hour indwell is             not         essential, consider removing it from the IFU and limiting the indication to acute procedural use, which would support the         “    limited    ”         classification — but note that the Clinical Synopsis (§8.2) and IFU (§8.4) both rely on extended infusion as a fallback for incomplete thrombus removal, so removing it has clinical-design consequences. (d) Correct the comparison table so the two rows are consistent.  
          Related material inconsistency (see also Issue 18).         The impeller’s material of construction is stated as             316L stainless steel         in the Device Description (§2.2) but as             nitinol (nickel-titanium)         in the Testing Plan (§2.3). This is not cosmetic: nitinol raises specific nickel-leaching and sensitization concerns that bear directly on the biocompatibility battery (and on MRI safety, Issue 13). The materials of construction must be reconciled across all documents, and the biocompatibility program must be designed against the             actual         material.  
      
        Issue 6 — Impeller Fatigue Testing Is Grossly Inadequate (500 Revolutions ≈ 2.5 Seconds)  
          Where present:         Proposed Testing Plan §3.3; Device Description §2.2.  
          Observation.         The Testing Plan (§3.3) specifies impeller fatigue testing of         “    a minimum of 500 rotational cycles at maximum rated speed (12,000 RPM),    ”         defining         “    one rotational cycle    ”         as         “    one complete revolution (360°).    ”         The Device Description (§2.2) represents this         “    500 complete activation cycles    ”         specification as validated and as confirming         “    adequate performance for clinical use.    ”  
          Analysis.         At 12,000 RPM, the impeller completes 12,000 revolutions per minute. Five hundred revolutions therefore equals             approximately 2.5 seconds         of continuous operation. A fatigue/durability test of 2.5 seconds does not credibly characterize the structural endurance of a rotating component that, in a single procedure, may operate for several minutes (i.e., hundreds of thousands of revolutions). The test is underspecified by roughly three to four orders of magnitude. The predicate (ThrombEx 200) had no rotating component and therefore offers no benchmark, but its own catheter flexural-fatigue test ran to 50,000 cycles — illustrating the expected order of magnitude. There is also a terminology ambiguity: the Device Description calls these         “    activation cycles,    ”         which could be misread as on/off events, while the Testing Plan defines them as revolutions; either reading is inadequate (500 on/off events is not a rotational-endurance test, and 500 revolutions is not a meaningful fatigue duration). FDA will not accept this specification as supportive of safety or SE.  
          Recommendation.         Redefine the fatigue test in terms of             worst-case cumulative impeller operating time         across the labeled use (accounting for multiple passes and any repeat-session use permitted by the IFU), convert to revolutions, and apply an appropriate safety factor (commonly ≥2×, and often higher for rotating surgical components). The resulting cycle count will be in the hundreds of thousands to millions. Specify the test duration, speed, and load conditions explicitly, and define         “    activation cycle    ”         unambiguously. Remove or revise the Device Description’s representation that 500 cycles         “    confirms adequate performance for clinical use.    ”  
      
        Issue 7 — Software Level of Concern Is Likely Underclassified; Framework Is Outdated; Key Documentation Withheld  
          Where present:         Proposed Testing Plan §5; Device Description §2.3; ThrombEx 200 510(k) Summary §5.6.  
          Observation.         The Testing Plan classifies the console firmware as             “        Minor        ”         level of concern, relying on independent hardware safety mechanisms (a mechanical over-speed governor and a hardware pressure-relief valve). The plan proposes to             withhold         the hazard analysis, detailed software architecture, and unresolved-anomaly list from the 510(k), offering them only         “    upon specific request.    ”         The framework cited is FDA’s 2005 software guidance. Notably, the             predicate’s own software was classified                 “        Moderate        ”         (K192847 §5.6) — and the predicate’s software controlled only an aspiration pump and saline flow, a less safety-critical function than the VascuClear’s 12,000-RPM impeller speed control.  
          Analysis.         Three concerns. First, the Minor classification is difficult to defend: the software directly controls a rotating impeller at 12,000 RPM in contact with blood and the vessel wall, and a software fault causing uncontrolled overspeed or failure to shut down could reasonably contribute to serious injury (vessel perforation, dissection, hemolysis, embolization of fractured impeller fragments). Reliance on hardware backups is a mitigation, but the 2005 guidance asks whether software failure         “    could    ”         result in serious injury — not whether it is the sole cause. A             Moderate         classification is more defensible and would align the subject device with (or above) its predicate. Second, FDA has for years expected IEC 62304 software safety classification (Class A/B/C) rather than the 2005         “    level of concern    ”         framework; the Device Description references IEC 62304 while the Testing Plan relies on the 2005 guidance — an internal inconsistency. Third, proactively withholding the hazard analysis and architecture is risky; FDA frequently requests these, and providing them upfront avoids a hold.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Reassess the software safety classification; I recommend             Moderate         (or IEC 62304 Class B) with a documented rationale, and I recommend             against         relying on the Minor classification given the predicate precedent and the impeller’s hazard profile. (b) Standardize on the IEC 62304 framework across all documents and provide the classification consistently. (c) Include the hazard analysis, architecture summary, and anomaly disposition in the 510(k) rather than withholding them. (d) Confirm that the hardware safety mechanisms (governor, relief valve) are themselves verified and that their independence from software is demonstrated.  
      
        Issue 8 — Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC) Testing Is Omitted  
          Where present:         Proposed Testing Plan §4; ThrombEx 200 510(k) Summary §5.3.  
          Observation.         The Testing Plan’s electrical-safety section (§4) addresses IEC 60601-1:2012 only. It does             not         include IEC 60601-1-2 (EMC) testing. The predicate (K192847 §5.3) performed a full IEC 60601-1-2:2014 EMC battery (radiated/conducted emissions; ESD, radiated-RF, EFT/burst, surge, conducted-RF, power-frequency magnetic-field, and voltage-dip immunity).  
          Analysis.         The VascuClear Console is a powered medical electrical device with a variable-frequency motor drive and a touchscreen; it both generates emissions and must demonstrate immunity in the cath-lab electromagnetic environment. IEC 60601-1-2 EMC testing is a standard expectation for any powered medical electrical equipment and was required for the predicate. Its omission is a clear gap that FDA will identify. Relatedly, the console incorporates safety alarms; IEC 60601-1-8 (alarm systems) and IEC 60601-1-6 (usability) may also apply and are not addressed.  
          Recommendation.         Add IEC 60601-1-2:2014 (Ed. 4) EMC testing to the plan, performed at the NRTL, with the full immunity and emissions battery. Evaluate applicability of IEC 60601-1-8 (alarms) and IEC 60601-1-6 (usability) and add as appropriate. Update the testing summary table (Testing Plan §9.1) and timeline/cost estimates accordingly.  
      
        Issue 9 — Drug-Delivery Characterization Uses Saline Only; IFU Concedes Compatibility Is Unverified  
          Where present:         Proposed Testing Plan §8; Draft IFU §9.  
          Observation.         The drug-delivery characterization (Testing Plan §8) verifies flow rates, infusion pressure, and lumen patency using             normal saline only    , at room temperature. The IFU (§9) expressly states that         “    drug compatibility with catheter lumen materials has not been independently verified by Clearfield Medical Devices    ”         and that         “    specific compatibility testing with individual thrombolytic agents, diluents, or drug formulations has not been performed.    ”         The Testing Plan’s own Question 6 asks FDA whether saline is         “    sufficient    ”         or whether tPA testing is needed.  
          Analysis.         Saline-only testing does not verify the function the Device is indicated to perform. tPA (alteplase) is known to adsorb to certain plastics, which can reduce delivered dose; viscosity and surface-tension differences between saline and a reconstituted thrombolytic can affect flow accuracy and pressure; and material compatibility over the labeled infusion duration (up to 72 hours) is unaddressed. Shipping an IFU that concedes compatibility is         “    not verified    ”         is both a labeling deficiency and an open invitation for FDA to require the testing. The Testing Plan correctly flags the question but should propose the testing proactively rather than defer to FDA.  
          Recommendation.         Add drug-delivery characterization with             actual thrombolytic agent(s)         — at minimum alteplase at clinically representative concentrations — including (a) drug-material compatibility (adsorption/recovery of tPA after transit through the lumen), (b) flow-rate accuracy with the drug (not just saline), (c) stability of the agent over the maximum labeled infusion duration, and (d) particulate/extractables introduced by drug contact. Remove or replace the IFU’s         “    not verified    ”         concession with the resulting data. (See also the infusion-lumen material inconsistency in Issue 18: the Testing Plan says the lumen liner is PTFE; the IFU says it is         “    medical-grade polyurethane    ”         — reconcile before designing the compatibility study.)  
      
        Issue 10 — Simulated-Use Bench Testing Does Not Test the Claimed Pharmacomechanical Function  
          Where present:         Proposed Testing Plan §7; Draft IFU §§2, 8.3; Clinical Synopsis §8.2.  
          Observation.         The Testing Plan (§7.2) expressly states that         “    the simulated-use bench testing protocol evaluates mechanical thrombectomy only. The thrombolytic infusion lumen is not activated during simulated-use bench testing. Combined pharmacomechanical testing (simultaneous mechanical thrombectomy with concurrent thrombolytic agent delivery) is not included.    ”         The primary endpoint is ≥80% thrombus removal by weight after a single pass.  
          Analysis.         The Device’s indication and         “    key differentiating feature    ”         is pharmacomechanical thrombectomy — yet the principal bench performance test does not exercise that function. FDA will expect bench evidence that reflects the labeled/claimed mode of operation, including the combined mechanical-plus-drug effect. Additionally, the bench protocol is single-pass, while the IFU (§8.3) and Clinical Synopsis (§8.2) permit multiple passes and extended infusion; the bench model does not reflect the full labeled use. Finally, the bench endpoint (≥80% removal by weight, single pass) and the clinical primary endpoint (≥50% removal by venographic score at 24 hours) use different metrics, thresholds, and timepoints, making it difficult to present a coherent performance story across bench and clinical domains.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Add a combined pharmacomechanical bench arm in which the infusion lumen is activated concurrently with impeller/aspiration operation, using a thrombus analog and a thrombolytic surrogate (or actual agent), to demonstrate the combined function the Device is indicated to perform. (b) Consider a multi-pass / extended-infusion bench condition reflecting the IFU’s permitted use. (c) Align the bench and clinical endpoint frameworks (metric, threshold, timepoint) so the bench data and clinical data tell a consistent story, or explain the relationship explicitly. (d) Justify the thrombus-analog selection and aging (24–48 h) against the clinical population (symptom onset ≤14 days) and against the predicate’s analog approach (bovine clot aged 1–7 days).  
      
        Issue 11 — Clinical Study Is Statistically Underpowered and Follow-Up Is Too Short for the Stated Rationale  
          Where present:         Clinical Synopsis §§3, 9, 10, 11.  
          Observation.         The study is single-arm, 60 patients, 5 sites, with a primary endpoint of ≥50% thrombus removal at 24-hour venography and a performance goal that the lower bound of the exact 95% CI exceed 70%. The Synopsis (§11.1) asserts that, assuming a true success rate of 80%, n=60 provides         “    >95% confidence that the lower bound … will exceed the … performance goal of 70%.    ”         Follow-up is 30 days (§10), described as the final visit.  
          Analysis.             Statistical power.         The claim is overstated. At the assumed true rate of 80% (48 of 60), the lower bound of the exact (Clopper-Pearson) 95% CI falls at or below 70% (approximately 0.68–0.70); the study would             fail         its primary endpoint if the observed rate merely equals the assumed true rate. For the lower bound to exceed 70%, the study must observe roughly 49–50 successes of 60 (≈82–83%), which — under a true rate of 80% — occurs with substantially less than 95% probability (on the order of 30–45% by exact binomial calculation). The study is therefore markedly underpowered relative to its stated performance goal; either the sample size must increase or the performance goal/threshold must be re-justified.  
          Follow-up duration.         The Synopsis’s own background (§3) identifies post-thrombotic syndrome (    “    PTS    ”    ) as the primary long-term morbidity of iliofemoral DVT and the central rationale for pharmacomechanical thrombectomy. PTS develops over months. A 30-day follow-up cannot assess PTS, the outcome most relevant to the Device’s clinical value proposition. FDA has, in analogous thrombectomy submissions, sought longer follow-up (e.g., 6–12 months) with a validated PTS instrument (e.g., Villalta score).  
          Other design gaps.         The study has no independent Data Safety Monitoring Board (DSMB) and no Clinical Events Committee (CEC) for MAE adjudication; the Synopsis references Sponsor/CRO monitoring only (§12). The primary endpoint (venographic thrombus removal) should be adjudicated by an independent core laboratory; the budget references core-lab fees but the Synopsis does not commit to core-lab adjudication of the primary endpoint. The upper age limit of 75 (inclusion criterion 1) is not justified.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Re-perform the sample-size calculation using exact binomial methods and report the true power at the assumed rate; increase n (or adjust the performance goal/threshold with justification) so the study is adequately powered. (b) Extend follow-up to at least 6 months and add a validated PTS assessment (Villalta score) as a secondary endpoint, consistent with the study’s own stated rationale. (c) Add an independent DSMB and a CEC for MAE adjudication. (d) Commit to independent core-laboratory adjudication of the primary venographic endpoint. (e) Justify or remove the age-75 upper limit. (f) Reconcile the IDE implications (Issue 2), which may themselves alter the study design.  
      
        Issue 12 — Package Timelines Are Internally Inconsistent and the Dec. 31, 2025 Clearance Milestone Is Not Achievable  
          Where present:         Cover Letter §§V, VIII; Proposed Testing Plan §9.2; Clinical Synopsis §15; Email chain (Fessenden, April 22; Marsh, May 9).  
          Observation.         Several timeline statements conflict. The Testing Plan (§9.2) sets         “    Target Testing Initiation: July 2025, following receipt of FDA feedback from the anticipated Q-Sub meeting (scheduled for August/September 2025)    ”         — i.e., testing is stated to begin             before         the meeting whose feedback is supposed to precede it. The Cover Letter (May 19, 2025) requests the meeting within the 75-day review window, placing the meeting in early August 2025. Testing is estimated at 16–20 weeks (completion November–December 2025), with 510(k) submission         “    as early as practical following completion of all testing.    ”         The clinical study begins Q4 2025 and completes Q2 2026, with the clinical study report finalized Q4 2026. The Braddock Ventures Series D term sheet requires 510(k) clearance by             December 31, 2025         (email chain).  
          Analysis.         The December 31, 2025 clearance milestone is not achievable on the package’s own facts. If the 510(k) is submitted in December 2025/January 2026 (after testing completion), FDA’s review (RTA plus substantive review, plus any additional information cycle) will not conclude by December 31, 2025. If clinical data is in the 510(k) critical path (Cover Letter §VI(Q4)), clearance cannot occur until the study report is finalized (Q4 2026) — a year or more after the milestone. If clinical data is             not         in the critical path (Synopsis §6’s         “    post-market    ”         framing), then the 510(k) could proceed on bench data alone, but that contradicts the Cover Letter’s presentation of the study as supportive evidence. The package cannot have it both ways. The testing-initiation date preceding the Q-Sub meeting is a separate, clear internal error.  
          Recommendation.         (a) Correct the testing-initiation date so it follows the Q-Sub meeting (e.g., September/October 2025), and recompute the testing-completion and submission dates consistently. (b) Take a definitive position on whether clinical data is in the 510(k) critical path; this single decision determines whether the December 31, 2025 milestone is even theoretically reachable. (c) Prepare a realistic clearance timeline and, per Kate’s May 12 note, engage Kendrick & Hale promptly to address the corporate consequences if the Braddock milestone is not achievable — particularly if the combination-product or IDE questions (Issues 1–2) shift the pathway. The current cash position ($18.3M) and burn rate ($1.52M/month) leave limited margin for slippage, and the timeline assumptions should not be allowed to drive the regulatory strategy into untenable positions.  
      
         V. Tier 3 — Medium-Priority Gaps and Inconsistencies  
       Issue 13 —         “    MR Conditional    ”         Claim Is Unsupported by Any Testing  
          Where present:         Draft IFU §6; Device Description §4.2 (comparison table); Proposed Testing Plan (no MRI section).  
          Observation.         The IFU labels the catheter         “    MR Conditional    ”         but leaves the temperature rise and field strength as         “    [TBD].    ”         The Testing Plan contains no MRI safety testing. The comparison table lists         “    MRI Status: MRI Conditional (per draft IFU).    ”  
          Analysis.         An MR Conditional claim must be supported by testing per the recognized ASTM standards (F2052 magnetically induced displacement force; F2213 magnetically induced torque; F2182 RF-induced heating; F2119 image artifact) and consistent with FDA’s MRI guidance and IEC 62570 nomenclature. A         “    [TBD]    ”         MR Conditional label with no test plan is not supportable. (The impeller-material inconsistency in Issue 5 also bears on MRI: nitinol and 316L stainless steel have different MR properties.)  
          Recommendation.         Either (a) add a MRI safety testing section (ASTM F2052/F2213/F2182/F2119) and populate the IFU with the tested conditions, or (b) remove the MR Conditional claim and label the catheter         “    MR Safety Not Evaluated    ”         (or MR Unsafe) until testing is complete. Do not file an MR Conditional claim with         “    [TBD]    ”         values.  
      
        Issue 14 — Shelf-Life / Sterile-Barrier Validation Is Omitted  
          Where present:         Proposed Testing Plan §6; Draft IFU §12; ThrombEx 200 510(k) Summary §5.4.  
          Observation.         The Testing Plan’s sterilization section (§6) covers EtO process validation (ISO 11135) and residuals (ISO 10993-7) but does not address shelf-life or sterile-barrier-system validation. The IFU references an expiration date but states no shelf-life duration. The predicate performed shelf-life validation (2-year claim) per ASTM F1980 (accelerated aging), ASTM D4169 (distribution simulation), and ASTM F2095 (seal strength).  
          Analysis.         Shelf-life/sterile-barrier validation is a standard expectation for a sterile single-use device and was part of the predicate’s clearance. Its omission is a gap.  
          Recommendation.         Add shelf-life and sterile-barrier validation to the plan (ASTM F1980 accelerated aging to support the claimed shelf life, ASTM D4169 distribution simulation, ASTM F2095 seal strength), state the target shelf-life claim in the IFU, and update the testing summary and timeline.  
      
        Issue 15 — Console Reprocessing Validation Is Referenced but Not Included  
          Where present:         Proposed Testing Plan §6.1; Draft IFU §11.  
          Observation.         The Testing Plan states that console         “    cleaning and disinfection instructions … will be validated separately under Clearfield’s reusable device reprocessing validation protocol,    ”         but no such validation is included in the plan. The IFU (§11) provides wipe-based cleaning instructions.  
          Analysis.         FDA’s 2015 reprocessing guidance expects validation of reprocessing instructions for reusable devices. While the console exterior contacts only intact skin (a lower burden), the drive-cable connector port and any components that enter the sterile field warrant validated instructions. The         “    validated separately    ”         deferral leaves a gap in the testing plan.  
          Recommendation.         Add a reprocessing validation section for the console (soiling, cleaning, disinfection, and verification of instructions) consistent with FDA’s reprocessing guidance, or explicitly scope it and commit to a timeline.  
      
        Issue 16 — Dynamic Hemolysis Under Operating Conditions Is Not Evaluated  
          Where present:         Proposed Testing Plan §§2.2, 3.3; Draft IFU §8.5.  
          Observation.         The biocompatibility plan includes ISO 10993-4 hemocompatibility (hemolysis by extract/direct-contact methods, complement, thrombogenicity). The IFU lists hemolysis as a post-procedure adverse event to monitor. No             dynamic         hemolysis test — operating the impeller and aspiration in blood or blood-analog at clinical speed and pressure — is included.  
          Analysis.         ISO 10993-4 hemolysis is a static/extract test; it does not capture shear-induced hemolysis from a 12,000-RPM impeller and −650 mmHg aspiration, which are the operationally relevant mechanisms. Dynamic hemolysis testing under worst-case operating conditions is typically expected for active blood-handling devices. The IFU’s own acknowledgment of hemolysis risk underscores the need.  
          Recommendation.         Add dynamic hemolysis testing (impeller + aspiration operating in blood or blood-analog at worst-case speed/pressure and flow) with a pre-specified acceptance threshold (e.g., normalized index of hemolysis below an established limit), and reference it in the SE/safety discussion.  
      
        Issue 17 — Macerated-Thrombus Particle Size / Embolic Potential Is Not Characterized  
          Where present:         Proposed Testing Plan §§3.3, 7.  
          Observation.         The plan includes particulate testing per USP <788> (fatigue-test filtrate and a 10-minute closed-loop run) and a 25 µm filter check in the fatigue test. It does not characterize the             size distribution of thrombus particles produced by impeller maceration    , nor assess embolic potential.  
          Analysis.         The Device’s mechanism is mechanical maceration of thrombus; the size of the resulting fragments determines whether they are safely aspirated or may embolize (with pulmonary-embolism risk, which the Clinical Synopsis lists as a MAE). USP <788> limits govern particulate in parenteral injections and are not a direct measure of embolic risk in the vasculature. A thrombus-particle size distribution and an assessment of capture/aspiration efficiency versus embolic escape would more directly address the safety question.  
          Recommendation.         Add characterization of macerated-thrombus particle size distribution (by imaging/coulter methods) under operating conditions, and an assessment of the fraction aspirated versus the fraction potentially escaping distally. Define acceptance criteria tied to embolic risk rather than (or in addition to) USP <788> limits.  
      
        Issue 18 — IFU Deficiencies and Cross-Document Material/Specification Inconsistencies  
          Where present:         Draft IFU (multiple sections); Device Description; Proposed Testing Plan; Clinical Synopsis.  
          Observation.         Several labeling and cross-document inconsistencies, in addition to those noted above:  
              Console dimensions/weight:         Device Description (§2.3) states ~40 × 35 × 20 cm, ~15 kg; IFU (§13) states 30 × 25 × 15 cm, 4.5 kg. These are materially different and appear in two documents FDA will compare.  
              Impeller material:         316L stainless steel (Device Description §2.2) vs. nitinol (Testing Plan §2.3). (See Issue 5.)  
              Infusion-lumen material:         PTFE liner (Testing Plan §2.3) vs.     “    medical-grade polyurethane    ”         (IFU §9). (See Issue 9.)  
              Allergen/material lists:         IFU contraindications list         “    polyurethane, stainless steel, and barium sulfate    ”    ; Clinical Synopsis exclusion criteria list         “    polyurethane and silicone.    ”         Barium sulfate was the             predicate’s         radiopaque marker (K192847), not a stated VascuClear material — a likely carryover error. Silicone is not a stated VascuClear material. The VascuClear’s own radiopaque-marker material is unspecified.  
              Display:         Device Description says         “    7-inch color touchscreen    ”    ; IFU says         “    LCD.    ”         Reconcile.  
              No Adverse Events section:         The IFU has no consolidated Adverse Events section (known/probable adverse events and, where available, rates). FDA expects this section.  
              No Clinical Studies section:         If clinical data supports clearance, the IFU should summarize it.  
              No maximum cumulative impeller operating time / pass limit:         The IFU permits multiple passes and extended infusion but sets no maximum cumulative impeller operating time, which bears on the fatigue/durability question (Issue 6).  
              Drug-delivery                 “        not verified        ”                 concession:         (See Issue 9.)  
          Recommendation.         Reconcile all material and specification inconsistencies across the five documents (a single controlled device-specifications table would help). Add an Adverse Events section and (as applicable) a Clinical Studies section to the IFU. Specify the radiopaque-marker material and correct the allergen lists to reflect actual VascuClear materials. Add a maximum cumulative impeller operating time/pass limit consistent with the (revised) fatigue test.

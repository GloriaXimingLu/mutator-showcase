## 1. Executive Summary

Greenfield Biosciences, Inc. ("Greenfield" or the "Company") holds three intellectual-property and software licenses from NovaTrait Genomics Corporation ("NovaTrait") that, together, underpin the Company's corn, soybean, cotton, and (proposed) wheat trait programs, its Prairielands Seed Cooperative sublicense, and its bioinformatics R&D pipeline. All three agreements are at or past their initial-term expiration dates, and NovaTrait has submitted a consolidated renewal proposal (cover letter dated October 14, 2024; refined terms reflected in the January 15, 2025 transmission analyzed by Ridgemont Advisory Group) (the "Proposal").

**Bottom line.** We recommend that Greenfield **pursue renewal of all three licenses**, but on **substantially revised terms**. As proposed, the Package would raise Greenfield's aggregate annual IP cost from approximately **\$18.6 million (4.81% of FY 2024 revenue)** to approximately **\$28.0 million (7.22% of FY 2024 revenue)** — an increase of roughly **\$9.3 million per year (+49.9%)** — while simultaneously **narrowing** Greenfield's rights in several material respects (removal of insect resistance from License A; elimination of source-code access and transfer of output-data ownership in License C; reversal of improvement ownership in Licenses A and B; shortened biological-materials wind-down in License B). The proposed rates and fees **exceed the upper end of market comparables in all three license categories**, and several proposed changes are **not justified by the underlying IP value**, which is **declining** for License A (foundational patent expires March 12, 2028) and **static-to-moderate** for License C (multiple substitutes exist).

**Negotiating posture by license.**

- **License A (CRISPR-Ag Suite):** Moderate leverage. The foundational patent (U.S. 9,234,117) expires mid-term (March 2028), the competitive landscape is expanding, and switching is uneconomic in the near term but credible as leverage. Target a rate of **5.0% on the original Net Sales definition** (or 4.75% on a redefined definition), a **post-2028 step-down**, retention of insect resistance, retention of sole improvement ownership, and a 35–40% sublicense pass-through.
- **License B (TraitForge):** Weak leverage but time-critical. No commercial alternative exists and internal build is 3–4 years and \$25–30M; however, the License B standstill **expires June 29, 2025**, creating a hard deadline. Target **4.25% on the original Net Sales definition**, a **180-day biological-materials wind-down**, retention of current audit frequency, and a **20% Prairielands pass-through with a 12-month phase-in** (versus the 40% NovaTrait seeks). Secure a written standstill extension to December 31, 2025 as a condition precedent to continued negotiation.
- **License C (GenoMap Pro):** Strong leverage. Multiple substitutes exist at \$1.8M–\$3.5M/year with break-even under three years. Target **\$3.75M with an 80-user cap**, **retention of source-code access**, and **Greenfield ownership of all output/derivative data**. Obtain competitive quotes from BioSphere Analytics, PlantLogic Suite, and TraitView Pro to support the counter.

**Two critical, time-sensitive actions** are required immediately: (i) **confirm in writing** that timely renewal notices were delivered for Licenses B and C (deadlines of September 30 and November 30, 2024, respectively, have passed) and (ii) **secure a written extension of the License B standstill** beyond June 29, 2025. The License A renewal-notice deadline (March 31, 2025) is approaching and must not be missed.

A prioritized counter-proposal framework and diligence items appear in Sections 9–11 below.

**Key procedural risks.**

1. **License B standstill (Sec 12.6).** License B expired December 31, 2024. If a notice was not timely delivered, NovaTrait could argue that Greenfield forfeited its renewal right under the relevant notice provisions, although the parties' ongoing good-faith negotiations and NovaTrait's own Proposal (which treats renewal as live) substantially mitigate that risk. Confirming delivery protects Greenfield's position.

3. **License A renewal notice (March 31, 2025).** This deadline has not yet passed and **must be met**. Delivery of a timely renewal notice preserves Greenfield's right to negotiate a renewal under Section 14.2 and triggers the 180-day good-faith negotiation period under Section 14.4.

4. **License C holdover (February 28, 2025).** License C's initial term expires imminently. The agreement contains no standstill provision analogous to License B's Section 12.6. Greenfield should ensure an interim access/holdover arrangement is in place to avoid any disruption to the bioinformatics pipeline on which Licenses A and B operations depend.

### 5.2 The Section 14.3 Rate Cap — A Key Lever

Section 14.3(a) provides that any royalty-rate increase for a renewal term **shall not exceed 150% of the then-current Royalty Rate** without completion of an independent fair-market-value ("IFMV") assessment by a mutually agreed valuation firm. The current rate is 4.5%, so the cap is **6.75%**. NovaTrait's proposed 6.25% is nominally within the cap, and NovaTrait asserts no IFMV assessment is required.

**Greenfield's argument:** The "Royalty Rate" should be construed as the **effective economic burden**, not merely the nominal percentage. When the Net Sales redefinition is taken into account, the effective rate on the original base is **6.70%** — i.e., **148.9% of the current rate**, which is within but *barely* within the 150% cap. If the redefinition adds even slightly more than estimated, the combined increase would exceed 150% and trigger the IFMV requirement. We recommend **arguing that the combined effect (rate + redefined base) must be measured against the 150% cap**, and that an updated IFMV assessment is warranted in any event (the Clarendon landscape analysis is dated September 2020 and predates material developments, including the post-2028 competitive landscape). Equally important, Section 14.3 requires that any increase be **"by mutual agreement"** — Greenfield is **not obligated to accept any increase**, cap or no cap. Licenses B and C contain no analogous rate-cap provision.

### 5.3 Removal of Insect Resistance from the Field of Use — A Material Scope Reduction

NovaTrait proposes to **remove insect resistance** (including Bt-derived and non-Bt insecticidal constructs) from the Field of Use, on the ground that it has granted an exclusive insect-resistance license to another agricultural biotechnology company. NovaTrait offers only to "discuss a transition mechanism" for Greenfield's existing insect-resistance product lines.

This is a **material adverse change**. Per the Ridgemont negotiation-priorities matrix, insect resistance represents approximately **30% of License A revenue**. Removing it mid-portfolio would force Greenfield to exit or re-license a core product line. **We recommend countering to retain insect resistance AND add wheat**, accepting at most a modest rate premium for the expanded crop scope. If NovaTrait is contractually bound to a third party and cannot retain insect resistance, Greenfield should demand (a) a **transitional non-exclusive license with a defined sunset period** sufficient to wind down or migrate existing product lines, and (b) a **corresponding reduction in the royalty rate and MAR** reflecting the narrowed scope. Greenfield should also request disclosure of the third-party exclusive arrangement to assess its bona fides and scope.

### 5.4 Wheat Expansion — Value Must Be Tied to Actual Patent Coverage

The addition of wheat is presented as a concession, but the Clarendon landscape analysis (Section 6, Recommendation 2) cautions that **field-of-use expansions must be evaluated against the actual scope of the underlying patents**. NovaTrait's CRISPR-Ag patents are anchored in corn and soybean embodiments; if the claims do not specifically cover wheat or do not block third-party competition in wheat, the "expanded" scope confers little incremental exclusivity and should not command a higher royalty. Greenfield should require NovaTrait to **demonstrate meaningful patent coverage and blocking position in wheat** before accepting any rate premium for the expansion.

### 5.5 Improvement Ownership — Do Not Concede Sole Ownership

Current Section 8.1 provides that **Greenfield solely owns** its improvements, with a non-exclusive license back to NovaTrait at a commercially reasonable royalty to be negotiated. The Proposal would reverse this to **joint ownership** with an **irrevocable, perpetual, royalty-free, sublicensable, transferable license to NovaTrait** for any purpose (inside or outside the Field of Use), while Greenfield would receive only a non-exclusive, non-sublicensable, royalty-bearing license for the renewal term.

This is a significant give-back of Greenfield-generated IP and is **asymmetric** (NovaTrait gets a perpetual, royalty-free, sublicensable grant; Greenfield gets a term-limited, royalty-bearing, non-sublicensable grant). **We recommend retaining sole Greenfield ownership** of Greenfield improvements, with a non-exclusive license back to NovaTrait at a reduced (not "commercially reasonable" open-ended) royalty. The same joint-ownership/royalty-free-grant-back structure is proposed for License B and should be resisted there as well.

### 5.6 Sublicense Pass-Through and Anti-Stacking Offset

- **Pass-through (35% → 50%).** The current 35% is already above the market median (comparable gene-editing transactions show 30–40% pass-through). We recommend **retaining 35%**, or accepting **40% only with a volume discount** above a \$2M pass-through threshold. Note this provision governs License A sublicenses; the Prairielands pass-through issue arises under License B (Section 6.4).
- **Anti-stacking offset (new).** The proposed 50% credit for third-party freedom-to-operate royalties, capped at 25% of quarterly royalties, with a 4.75% effective-rate floor, is **net beneficial** and we recommend accepting it **with modification**: the floor should be measured against **75% of the *calculated* royalty** (not the minimum annual royalty), so the offset retains practical value when running royalties are high.

### 5.7 Patent Expiry and the Case for a Post-2028 Step-Down

The Clarendon analysis identifies **U.S. Patent No. 9,234,117** as the foundational method patent representing approximately **60% of the License A portfolio's commercial value**, with an expiration date of **March 12, 2028** — i.e., roughly **two years into the proposed five-year renewal term** (July 1, 2025 – June 30, 2030). Upon expiry:

- The core CRISPR-based gene-editing methods enter the public domain;
- The residual portfolio (Nos. 9,234,118; 9,651,002; 10,112,445) retains only an estimated **35–40% of current aggregate value**;
- Competitors (notably AgriGenome Solutions, with seven improvement patents expected in force through ~2037, and CropTech Innovations, pursuing base-editing alternatives) will be positioned to offer commercially viable alternative platforms, exerting **downward pressure on market royalty rates** (Clarendon projects equilibrium rates gravitating toward the lower end of, or below, the current 3.5–5.5% range post-2028).

**Recommendation:** Any License A renewal extending beyond March 2028 must incorporate a **royalty step-down** (or a rate-renegotiation trigger) effective upon expiry of the '117 patent. Clarendon suggests a post-2028 rate in the **2.0–3.5%** range reflecting residual improvement-patent and know-how value. A flat 6.25% across the full term would have Greenfield **overpaying for the final ~2.25 years** relative to the IP value received.

### 5.8 Patent-Term Discrepancy Requiring Diligence

There is a **material discrepancy** in the Licensed Patents schedule across documents:

| Patent | Original Exhibit A (2015) | Clarendon (Sept 2020) | Renewal Proposal Appendix A (Oct 2024) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 9,234,117 | Mar 12, 2028 | Mar 12, 2028 | Mar 12, 2028 |
| 9,234,118 | Sep 22, 2033 | 2033 (est.) | **Dec 19, 2028** |
| 9,651,002 | Mar 8, 2034 | 2034 (est.) | **Mar 8, 2029** |
| 10,112,445 | Oct 6, 2035 | Oct 6, 2035 | **Nov 3, 2031** |

The renewal Proposal shows **substantially shorter terms** for three of the four patents. This is consistent with **terminal disclaimers** having been filed (e.g., to overcome obviousness-type double-patenting rejections), which would tie those patents' terms to an earlier-expiring parent. If terminal disclaimers were filed, the portfolio value is **even more concentrated** in the '117 patent (expiring March 2028), which **strengthens** Greenfield's argument for a post-2028 step-down. Greenfield should **reconcile this discrepancy** by obtaining current patent-status confirmations (file histories, any terminal disclaimers, family relationships) and updating the Licensed Patents schedule accordingly. This is also relevant to the IFMV question in Section 5.2.

### 5.9 Most-Favored-Licensee ("MFL") Reference — Verify

The Proposal (Part I, Item 13) states that NovaTrait "proposes to maintain existing Section 14.5 (MFL) with no changes." However, **the License A agreement as executed contains no Section 14.5** — Article 14 comprises only Sections 14.1–14.4. Either (a) an amendment adding an MFL clause was executed and is not in the materials reviewed, or (b) NovaTrait's reference is erroneous. Greenfield should **verify whether an MFL clause exists** (and, if so, obtain its exact terms, including the carve-outs NovaTrait references for academic/non-profit licenses and litigation settlements). If no MFL exists, NovaTrait's "no change" representation is meaningless; if one does exist, Greenfield should understand and potentially invoke it.

### 6.2 Royalty Rate — Above Market, but Leverage Is Limited

The proposed 5.0% **exceeds the upper end of the market range for trait-expression platform licenses (3.0–4.5%)** in every growth scenario modeled by Ridgemont. We recommend countering at **4.25% on the original Net Sales definition** (within market), supported by the comparables. However, Greenfield's leverage is constrained: the Clarendon analysis rates the TraitForge portfolio **VERY STRONG**, identifies **no commercial alternatives**, and estimates **3–4 years and \$25–30M** to build a comparable platform internally (with no guarantee of success). The biological materials (proprietary expression vectors) are not available from any other source. Accordingly, the realistic objective is to **negotiate the rate down toward the market range** rather than to credibly threaten switching. Securing the **longest practical renewal term** at a locked-in rate is advisable to forestall further increases, given NovaTrait's very high leverage on this asset.

### 6.3 Biological-Materials Return and Wind-Down — Operational Continuity

The Proposal would replace the current **90-day wind-down period** (during which Greenfield may continue using materials in already-initiated breeding programs and field trials, subject to ongoing royalties) with a **30-day return/destroy obligation and no wind-down**. This is operationally inadequate: ongoing breeding programs, field trials, and regulatory submissions cannot be wound down in 30 days, and destruction of materials would destroy years of investment. **We recommend countering for a 180-day wind-down** with the right to continue using remaining materials in ongoing trials, consistent with the current structure and with market comparable 5 (180-day wind-down for biological materials).

### 6.4 Audit Rights — Resist the Expansion

The Proposal doubles audit frequency (to 2x/year), shortens notice to 15 business days, and extends the look-back to five years. This materially increases compliance burden and audit risk. **We recommend retaining the current 1x/year frequency**, accepting at most a reduction in notice to 20 business days. The five-year look-back should be resisted as disproportionate.

### 6.5 Prairielands Pass-Through — A New Obligation Where None Exists

This is one of the most significant economic changes in the Package. **No pass-through obligation exists today** under License B — the NovaTrait consent letter (April 15, 2021) expressly states that "the Master License does not currently require the remittance by Greenfield to NovaTrait of any portion of royalties received by Greenfield from sublicensees." NovaTrait now seeks to introduce a **40% pass-through** on Prairielands sublicense revenue. At an estimated \$12M base growing at 4%, this is approximately **\$5.0M in Year 1, rising to ~\$5.8M by Year 5** — a cumulative ~\$28M over the renewal term, on top of the direct License B increase.

**We recommend countering at 20% with a 12-month phase-in**, and structuring any pass-through to apply **prospectively only** (not to royalties already received). Greenfield should also note that the Prairielands sublicense itself **expired on December 31, 2024** (co-terminus with License B) and must be renewed/amended in parallel, which requires NovaTrait's consent under Section 12.4 of License B — giving Greenfield a reciprocal consent lever in the negotiation.

### 6.6 Improvements — Resist Joint Ownership / Royalty-Free Grant-Back

As with License A (Section 5.5), the Proposal would convert the current split-ownership structure to joint ownership with a royalty-free, perpetual, sublicensable license to NovaTrait. We recommend retaining the current structure (Greenfield owns Greenfield Improvements; NovaTrait owns NovaTrait Improvements; Joint Improvements jointly owned), with the license-back to NovaTrait on Greenfield Improvements at a defined (not open-ended) royalty.

| Derivative/output-data ownership | Silent | **NovaTrait owns; Greenfield non-exclusive license** | **Material adverse** |
| Term | 5 years (ends 2/28/2025) | 3 years (ends 2/28/2028) | Shorter |

### 7.2 Source-Code Access — A Non-Negotiable Retention

Current Section 5.2 grants Greenfield the right to access, review, and **internally modify the source code** to adapt the software for its internal use. Greenfield has built custom analytics pipelines on this access. The Proposal would **eliminate source-code access entirely** (object code only), consistent with NovaTrait's enterprise distribution model for Version 5.x. This is a **material adverse change** that would eliminate Greenfield's ability to customize its analytics pipelines — a significant R&D impact. **We recommend retaining source-code access** as a non-negotiable, accepting reasonable enhanced security/escrow provisions (the existing Section 10.3 enhanced source-code protections and Section 5.3 escrow can be carried forward). If NovaTrait will not budge, this materially weakens the case for renewing at a *higher* fee and strengthens the switching case (Section 8.3).

### 7.3 Derivative-Data Ownership — A Critical IP Protection Issue

The Proposal adds a new Section 9.3 providing that **all Derivative Data** (outputs, analyses, visualizations, results generated by the software from Greenfield's Input Data) **shall be the sole and exclusive property of NovaTrait**, with Greenfield receiving only a non-exclusive, non-transferable, royalty-free license for internal use — and that license **terminates upon expiration of the renewal term**, with no sublicensing and no publication rights.

This is **inconsistent with the existing Section 9.2 principle** that Greenfield owns its data, and it could **jeopardize Greenfield's proprietary trait-mapping datasets and analytical outputs** — which are themselves valuable IP generated from Greenfield's own Input Data and R&D. Allowing NovaTrait to own the outputs of Greenfield's proprietary genomic data, and to cut off Greenfield's use of those outputs at term end, is unacceptable. **We recommend countering that all output and derivative data be solely owned by Greenfield**, consistent with Section 9.2. This is a **CRITICAL** priority (Ridgemont Priority #2).

### 7.4 Annual Fee and User Cap — Above Market; Strong Switching Leverage

The proposed effective annual cost (~\$5.28M) is **above the market range** for comparable bioinformatics platforms. Clarendon identifies three commercial alternatives (BioSphere Analytics, PlantLogic Suite, TraitView Pro) at approximately **\$1.8M–\$3.5M/year**; Ridgemont's comparable transactions show a market range of **\$2.9M–\$3.2M** (mean \$3.05M). The proposed \$4.85M base fee alone exceeds the market high by ~21%, and the all-in cost exceeds it by ~32%. **We recommend countering at \$3.75M with an 80-user cap** and overage at \$12,000/user, and **obtaining competitive proposals** from the three alternative providers to support the counter (and to maintain a credible Plan B). The Version 5.x conversion tool (\$175K) should be **included at no cost** as a condition of renewal.

### 7.5 Version 5.x Upgrade — Value, but Not at Any Price

Version 5.x offers genuine improvements (machine-learning trait prediction with ~22% accuracy gains, multi-omic database schemas, native cloud deployment, enterprise SSO/RBAC). These are valuable, but the upgrade is being used to justify both a higher fee *and* the elimination of source-code access and the data-ownership transfer. Greenfield should treat the upgrade as a value-add to be priced separately and conditionally, not as a package that bundles adverse term changes.

## 14. Conclusion

NovaTrait's consolidated renewal Proposal would increase Greenfield's aggregate annual IP cost by roughly 50% (to ~\$28M, or 7.22% of FY 2024 revenue) while narrowing Greenfield's rights in several material respects — and it does so against IP value that is **declining** for License A (foundational patent expiring mid-term), **irreplaceable but already above-market** for License B, and **substitutable** for License C. Greenfield should **pursue renewal of all three licenses** — switching is not economic for A and B in the near term, and the technology stack is operationally interdependent — but should do so on **substantially revised terms** anchored to (i) the Section 14.3 rate-cap and effective-rate arguments, (ii) the '117 patent expiry and post-2028 step-down, (iii) market comparables, (iv) the FOV scope reductions, and (v) Greenfield's IP-ownership and data-ownership protections. The two most urgent actions are securing the License B standstill extension and confirming the missed renewal-notice deliveries; the License A renewal-notice deadline of March 31, 2025 must be met.

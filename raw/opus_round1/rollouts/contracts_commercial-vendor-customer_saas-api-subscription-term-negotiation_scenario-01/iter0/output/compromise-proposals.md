## I. PURPOSE AND SCOPE

This memorandum sets forth compromise proposals for each of the seven contested terms remaining in the Master AI Services Agreement (the "Agreement") between Meridian Health Systems, Inc. ("Meridian" or "Customer") and Cognitive Foundry, Inc. ("Cognitive Foundry" or "Vendor") for the ClinIQ™ Platform (v4.2). The proposals are prepared for delivery to the Meridian deal team in advance of the July 18, 2025 joint negotiation session with Cognitive Foundry's team — Sandra Hu (VP, Commercial Legal) and Rafael Bowen (Partner, Kessler Pratt & Lowe LLP).

Each proposal follows the four-component format directed by Elaine Sutter on July 3, 2025:

1. **Proposed Contract Clause Language** — fully drafted, redline-ready language for insertion into the Agreement;
2. **Negotiation Rationale and Fallback Ladder** — mapping of both parties' positions and red lines, the recommended landing zone, and the documented fallback ladder for real-time adjustment at the table;
3. **Acceptability Analysis for the Vendor Team** — anticipated objections from Sandra Hu (commercial flexibility and precedent) and Rafael Bowen (liability exposure and IP protection), and how each proposal accommodates or neutralizes them; and
4. **Regulatory and Compliance Constraints** — HIPAA, the New Jersey AI in Healthcare Transparency Act (effective March 1, 2025) (the "NJ Act"), and FDA clinical decision-support guidance.

The proposals are calibrated to Meridian's board-approved priority ranking and the strategic guidance set forth in the Meridian AI/Technology Vendor Negotiation Playbook (the "Playbook"), the July 1 Contested Terms Matrix, and the intelligence memoranda of July 3, 2025.

## II. NEGOTIATION STRATEGY FRAMEWORK

### A. Priority Ranking and the Critical Rule

Meridian's priority ranking for the seven contested terms, approved by the Office of General Counsel and the Board Risk Committee, governs the allocation of negotiating capital:

1. **Patient-safety liability** (super-cap structure)
2. **Audit rights** (AI model transparency)
3. **Termination flexibility** (ETF and transition)
4. **Data usage** (aggregated/de-identified data)
5. **SLA accuracy** (binding benchmarks)
6. **IP ownership** (model weights and fine-tuned adaptations)
7. **Indemnification scope**

**Critical Rule.** No concession on a higher-priority item may be made to gain advantage on a lower-priority item without the express approval of the General Counsel. The proposals below hold firm on Priorities 1 and 2 (where Meridian's red lines and the NJ Act are non-negotiable), accept the Playbook fallback on Priorities 5, 6, and 7 (where the practical value to Meridian is captured without expending capital), and deploy calibrated concessions on Priorities 3 and 4 (where the strategic guidance identifies both flexibility and bargaining value).

### B. The Package Approach and Concession Currency

Two asymmetries drive the concession strategy:

- **Data usage (Priority 4) is Cognitive Foundry's highest-priority commercial term.** The aggregated, de-identified data flowing from Meridian's 14-hospital network (~2.1 million annual patient encounters) is highly valuable to Cognitive Foundry's model-training pipeline. This gives Meridian a natural bargaining chip: calibrated flexibility on data usage can be exchanged for movement on Priorities 1–3.
- **Cognitive Foundry has already agreed to more protective terms in a comparable deal.** Market intelligence confirms that Cognitive Foundry recently closed a comparable multi-hospital deal with a general liability cap of 1.5× ACV and a super-cap of 3× ACV covering IP infringement and data breach. This directly undermines Cognitive Foundry's posture that 1× ACV is its "standard" cap and that 3× ACV super-caps are unavailable, and it anchors our positions on Terms 1 and 7.

Accordingly, the proposals are designed to move as a coherent package: Meridian concedes on data usage (Priority 4) and accepts the Playbook fallback on IP (Priority 6), and in exchange secures (i) a patient-safety-inclusive super-cap at 3× ACV (Priority 1), (ii) the Langford tiered audit framework at Tier 2 with Tier 3 for cause (Priority 2), and (iii) a graduated ETF that declines to zero by Year 3 paired with robust transition and data-export obligations (Priority 3).

### C. Comparable-Deal Intelligence — Tactical Use

The comparable-deal intelligence should be used **indirectly** at the table. Rather than disclosing the source, Meridian should anchor positions on "market practice for enterprise healthcare AI deployments of comparable scope," noting that caps in the 1.5×–2× ACV range and super-caps at 3× ACV (including data-breach coverage) are consistent with deals Cognitive Foundry has concluded for similarly situated health systems. If Cognitive Foundry holds at 1× ACV or resists a 3× super-cap including patient-safety, the team may escalate to a more direct reference. Elaine Sutter will make the tactical call on timing.

## III. CROSS-CUTTING ISSUE — INSURANCE COVERAGE GAP

A single issue cuts across the liability (Term 1) and indemnification (Term 7) proposals and must be resolved as part of the liability package: **the insurance collectability gap.**

- **Vendor's current cyber/E&O coverage:** Northfield Mutual Insurance Co., Policy NM-CY-2025-04417 — $25M per occurrence / $50M aggregate.
- **Meridian's requested minimum:** $30M per occurrence / $60M aggregate.
- **Delta:** +$5M per occurrence / +$10M aggregate.
- **Vendor posture:** evaluating a coverage increase in the 2026 renewal cycle; resists a binding contractual floor.

**Why it matters.** The compromise super-cap proposed in Term 1 is 3× ACV (~$28.8M). The Vendor's current per-occurrence limit is $25M. If a patient-safety or data-breach claim approaches the super-cap, the excess over $25M per occurrence is, at present, unbacked by insurance — a collectability problem that undermines the entire liability structure and will not satisfy the Board Risk Committee. The liability package is therefore incomplete without a binding insurance commitment.

**Recommended resolution (reflected in Term 1, Component (iv), and Term 7):** Vendor covenants to increase cyber/E&O coverage to $30M per occurrence / $60M aggregate no later than the earlier of (a) the next policy renewal date or (b) March 31, 2026; provides broker written confirmation during the gap period that the increase has been requested and is reasonably expected to be obtained; and grants Meridian step-in rights (supplemental coverage at Vendor's expense, fee escrow, or termination without penalty) if the increase is not achieved by the deadline. This is the Playbook's approved approach to a vendor coverage shortfall and converts the Vendor's "commercially reasonable efforts" posture into a binding commitment with a date-certain and remedies.

## TERM 1 — PATIENT-SAFETY LIABILITY / SUPER-CAP STRUCTURE

**Priority: 1 (highest). Agreement Sections: §12.1–12.4; §15.1 (insurance). Related: Term 7 (Indemnification).**

This is the single most important term in the deal. The Board Risk Committee has classified AI-related liability as a Tier 1 enterprise risk; the final liability structure must be defensible in that context. Patient-safety liability must have a separate, higher cap or remain uncapped, and may never be capped below 3× ACV (~$28.8M).

### (i) Proposed Contract Clause Language

**§12.2 General Liability Cap.** EXCEPT AS SET FORTH IN SECTION 12.3, EACH PARTY'S TOTAL CUMULATIVE LIABILITY ARISING OUT OF OR RELATED TO THIS AGREEMENT, WHETHER IN CONTRACT, TORT (INCLUDING NEGLIGENCE), STRICT LIABILITY, OR OTHERWISE, SHALL NOT EXCEED AN AMOUNT EQUAL TO ONE AND ONE-HALF TIMES (1.5×) THE TRAILING TWELVE (12)-MONTH FEES PAID OR PAYABLE BY CUSTOMER UNDER THIS AGREEMENT (THE "GENERAL CAP"). AS OF THE FIRST CONTRACT YEAR, THE GENERAL CAP IS FOURTEEN MILLION FOUR HUNDRED THOUSAND DOLLARS ($14,400,000).

**§12.3 Super-Cap / Carve-Out Liability.** THE FOLLOWING CATEGORIES OF LIABILITY SHALL NOT BE SUBJECT TO THE GENERAL CAP AND SHALL INSTEAD BE SUBJECT TO A SEPARATE AGGREGATE LIABILITY CAP EQUAL TO THREE TIMES (3×) THE TRAILING TWELVE (12)-MONTH FEES PAID OR PAYABLE BY CUSTOMER (THE "SUPER-CAP"). AS OF THE FIRST CONTRACT YEAR, THE SUPER-CAP IS TWENTY-EIGHT MILLION EIGHT HUNDRED THOUSAND DOLLARS ($28,800,000):

> (a) **Patient-Safety Liability** (as defined in Section 1.22) — any liability arising from bodily injury, clinical misdiagnosis, treatment error, or other patient harm attributable to Outputs, recommendations, alerts, or clinical decision-support results generated by the Platform;
>
> (b) **PHI / Data-Breach Liability** — any liability arising from a breach of either Party's obligations under the Business Associate Agreement (Exhibit F), including any unauthorized access to, use of, or disclosure of Protected Health Information;
>
> (c) **Vendor's IP Indemnification Obligations** under Section 13.1(a); and
>
> (d) **Regulatory-Penalty Liability** — any liability for civil penalties, fines, or enforcement costs arising from Vendor's breach of its documentation, audit, or regulatory-cooperation obligations under Article 10 or Section 5.7, including penalties assessed under the New Jersey AI in Healthcare Transparency Act.

**§12.4 Uncapped Liabilities.** THE FOLLOWING SHALL NOT BE SUBJECT TO THE GENERAL CAP, THE SUPER-CAP, OR ANY OTHER CAP OR LIMITATION:

> (a) Either Party's liability arising from willful misconduct, gross negligence, or fraud;
>
> (b) Either Party's breach of Article 11 (Confidentiality); and
>
> (c) Customer's obligation to pay all Fees due under this Agreement.

**§15.1(c) Cyber/E&O Insurance (binding increase).** Cyber Liability and Technology Errors & Omissions Insurance in an amount not less than Thirty Million Dollars ($30,000,000) per occurrence and Sixty Million Dollars ($60,000,000) in the aggregate (current policy: Northfield Mutual Insurance Co., Policy No. NM-CY-2025-04417, $25M/$50M). Vendor covenants to increase such coverage to the foregoing minimums no later than the earlier of (i) the next renewal date of such policy or (ii) March 31, 2026. During the period prior to such increase, Vendor shall provide Customer with written confirmation from its broker that the coverage increase has been requested and is reasonably expected to be obtained. If Vendor has not obtained the required coverage by such date, Customer may, at its election and upon written notice, (x) procure supplemental coverage at Vendor's expense, (y) withhold up to ten percent (10%) of each quarterly invoice in escrow until the coverage requirement is met, or (z) terminate this Agreement for convenience without payment of any early termination fee.

### (ii) Negotiation Rationale and Fallback Ladder

| Step | General Cap | Patient-Safety Super-Cap | Other Carve-Outs | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vendor opening (June 6) | 1× ACV (~$9.6M) | 2× ACV (~$19.2M); IP + willful misconduct only | Patient-safety & HIPAA excluded | Rejected |
| Customer opening (June 20) | 2× ACV (~$19.2M) | Uncapped | Uncapped | Opening |
| Vendor counter (June 30) | 1.5× ACV (~$14.4M) | 3× ACV (~$28.8M); IP + willful misconduct only | Patient-safety & HIPAA excluded | Non-starter on scope |
| **Compromise proposal** | **1.5× ACV (~$14.4M)** | **3× ACV (~$28.8M), included** | **3× ACV super-cap (PHI, IP, regulatory); willful misconduct uncapped** | **Recommended landing zone** |
| Fallback (if Vendor refuses patient-safety at 3×) | 1.5× ACV (~$14.4M) | 4× ACV (~$38.4M), all carve-outs | 4× ACV super-cap | Playbook second fallback |
| Absolute floor | Never below 1.5× ACV | Never below 3× ACV | Escalate to GC + Board | Non-negotiable |

**Rationale.** The compromise accepts the Vendor's counter on the **dollar amounts** (1.5× general cap; 3× super-cap) — both of which the comparable-deal intelligence confirms Cognitive Foundry has agreed to elsewhere — while holding firm on **scope**: patient-safety and PHI/data-breach liability must be inside the super-cap, not left at the general cap. The Vendor's June 30 counter is a non-starter precisely because it excludes patient-safety and HIPAA from the super-cap, which would leave patient-safety liability capped at the general cap of 1.5× ACV (~$14.4M) — below Meridian's absolute floor of 3× ACV. The compromise cures that defect by moving patient-safety and PHI into the 3× super-cap at the same dollar figure the Vendor already proposed.

The structure is deliberately clean: a single 3× super-cap covering the four highest-severity categories, with willful misconduct/gross negligence and confidentiality breaches remaining uncapped (industry-standard positions the Vendor is unlikely to contest). This gives the Board Risk Committee a defensible, easily explained liability architecture and gives the Vendor a single, familiar super-cap number it has already accepted in a comparable deal.

**Why concede the general cap to 1.5× ACV.** The Vendor's 1.5× counter lands exactly at Meridian's Playbook floor, and the comparable-deal intelligence confirms it is achievable. Accepting it here converts the general cap into a bargaining chip: Meridian concedes on the general cap (a commercial-claims bucket) in exchange for the Vendor's agreement to include patient-safety and PHI in the 3× super-cap (the Tier 1 risk bucket). This is the correct trade under the priority ranking — we give on a lower-severity dollar figure to hold the line on the highest-severity scope.

**Fallback if the Vendor refuses to include patient-safety at 3× ACV.** Move to the Playbook second fallback: 1.5× general cap with a 4× ACV super-cap (~$38.4M) across all carve-out categories. This preserves the absolute floor (patient-safety never below 3× ACV) while giving the Vendor a higher aggregate ceiling in exchange for scope inclusion. If the Vendor refuses both, the matter must be escalated to Dr. Narayanan and, if unresolved, to the Board Risk Committee before Meridian may proceed — patient-safety below 3× ACV is not within the deal team's authority to accept.

### (iii) Acceptability Analysis for the Vendor Team

**Sandra Hu (commercial flexibility / precedent).** Hu's likely objection is that a patient-safety-inclusive super-cap exceeds Cognitive Foundry's "standard" and creates precedent risk across its 40+ enterprise deployments. The response: (a) the dollar figures (1.5× general; 3× super-cap) are exactly what Cognitive Foundry proposed on June 30 and what it accepted in a comparable deal — there is no dollar precedent break; (b) the only change is **scope** (adding patient-safety and PHI to a 3× super-cap the Vendor already offered for IP and willful misconduct), which is a modest, defensible expansion, not a new dollar exposure; (c) the super-cap remains capped (not uncapped), preserving the Vendor's board- and insurer-mandated prohibition on uncapped liability; and (d) Meridian is simultaneously conceding the general cap to the Vendor's own counter and offering meaningful data-usage flexibility (Term 4) — this is a package, not an a la carte demand. Framing the patient-safety inclusion as a scope adjustment to the Vendor's own 3× number, rather than a new ask, is the key to neutralizing the precedent objection.

**Rafael Bowen (liability exposure / IP).** Bowen's likely objection is that patient-safety and PHI exposure at 3× ACV (~$28.8M) is unquantifiable and threatens collectability and insurability. The response: (a) the 3× figure is capped and finite, consistent with the Vendor's own insurers' requirements; (b) the binding insurance increase to $30M/$60M (Term 1, §15.1(c)) directly addresses collectability — the super-cap will be backed by adequate per-occurrence and aggregate coverage; (c) willful misconduct and gross negligence remain the only uncapped categories, which is standard; and (d) the indemnification proposal (Term 7) pairs this cap with a shared-defense structure that allocates AI-output claims to the Vendor and clinician-override claims to Meridian, so the Vendor is not absorbing all patient-harm exposure unallocated. The insurance commitment is the linchpin: it converts Bowen's collectability concern into a solvable, date-certain obligation.

### (iv) Regulatory and Compliance Constraints

- **HIPAA / HITECH.** The BAA (Exhibit F, executed June 2, 2025) governs PHI obligations. Carving PHI/data-breach liability into the super-cap (rather than the general cap) is consistent with the BAA's breach-notification and safeguarding framework and with the Playbook's treatment of PHI breaches as a highest-severity category. The 24-hour breach-notification standard (Meridian's BAA position) is unaffected.
- **NJ AI in Healthcare Transparency Act.** Civil penalties up to $50,000 per violation, with each day of a continuing violation constituting a separate violation, are assessed against Meridian as the covered entity — not against Cognitive Foundry. Where penalties arise from the Vendor's breach of its documentation, audit, or cooperation obligations, Meridian's only recourse is contractual. The compromise therefore places regulatory-penalty liability (to the extent attributable to Vendor non-compliance) inside the 3× super-cap, consistent with the Whitfield Ames compliance brief's recommendation that regulatory-penalty indemnification be carved out from the general cap or subject to a separate, higher cap.
- **FDA clinical decision-support guidance.** The ClinIQ™ modules (sepsis, radiology triage, medication interaction) may be subject to FDA oversight depending on the 21st Century Cures Act § 3060 four-part exemption analysis. The radiology module's interaction with medical imaging raises a particular question under the first exemption criterion. Patient-safety liability flowing from AI outputs is the precise risk the FDA framework is designed to address; a patient-safety super-cap at 3× ACV is the minimum structure consistent with responsible deployment of a clinical decision-support tool that directly influences ~2.1 million annual encounters.
- **Board Risk Committee (Tier 1).** The Tier 1 enterprise-risk designation requires a pre-execution briefing on the liability structure. The compromise (1.5× general / 3× super-cap including patient-safety / uncapped willful misconduct / binding insurance increase) is designed to be defensible to the Board: it meets every absolute floor in the Playbook, is backed by adequate insurance, and is consistent with market practice the Vendor has accepted elsewhere.

## TERM 2 — AUDIT RIGHTS / AI MODEL TRANSPARENCY

**Priority: 2. Agreement Sections: §10.1–10.4; Exhibit H (Independent Auditor Engagement Terms). Related: NJ Act.**

Meaningful audit rights over AI model behavior are both a regulatory mandate (the NJ Act requires an independent annual audit for Meridian's three New Jersey facilities) and a core risk-management function. Summary reports alone are insufficient. The Langford AI Assurance Group tiered framework is the compromise architecture.

### (i) Proposed Contract Clause Language

**§10.1 Transparency Documentation (affirmative, non-discretionary).** Vendor shall provide Customer, on an initial basis (within thirty (30) days following the Go-Live Date) and on an ongoing basis (updated within thirty (30) days following any material change to the Platform, including model updates, retraining, or changes to training data), with the following transparency documentation for each ClinIQ™ module: (a) training data sources, provenance, demographic composition, and representativeness analysis; (b) validation methodology, including validation datasets, performance metrics, and statistical methods; (c) known limitations, failure modes, edge cases, and population subgroups for which performance may be degraded; and (d) bias-testing results across race, ethnicity, sex, age, socioeconomic status, language, and disability status. This right is affirmative and non-discretionary and is necessary for Customer's compliance with the NJ Act and applicable FDA guidance.

**§10.3 Independent Audit Rights (tiered).**

> (a) **Annual Audit (Tier 2).** Customer shall have the right, at Customer's expense, to engage the Independent Auditor — initially Langford AI Assurance Group, LLC, under the direction of Dr. Anil Cherian — to conduct one (1) annual audit of the Platform's AI/ML models. Vendor shall cooperate with each such audit and shall provide the Independent Auditor with Tier 2 access within twenty (20) business days of Customer's written audit request, consisting of: (i) model architecture documentation (network topology, layer specifications, activation functions); (ii) training data provenance records; (iii) validation and test datasets and results, including performance metrics by subpopulation; (iv) the ability to submit test queries to the Platform in a controlled, sandboxed environment; (v) bias and fairness testing results and methodologies; and (vi) change logs and version history for model updates deployed during the audit period. Tier 2 access does not require disclosure of raw model weights or proprietary source code.
>
> (b) **For-Cause Audit (Tier 3).** Notwithstanding the annual cadence, Customer may request an additional audit at any time upon the occurrence of any of the following triggers: (i) a documented patient-safety event at any Covered Facility potentially attributable, in whole or in part, to a Platform Output; (ii) a formal regulatory inquiry or investigation by the New Jersey Department of Health, the FDA, or other governmental authority concerning the Platform; (iii) two (2) or more consecutive quarterly misses of a Performance Benchmark under Section 9.3; or (iv) mutual written agreement of the Parties. A for-cause audit shall be conducted at Tier 3 access: model weights and embeddings shall be made available for review solely within a Vendor-controlled secure enclave environment, with no data extraction, copying, photographing, or removal of model weights, embeddings, or source code from the secure environment permitted.
>
> (c) **Auditor Qualifications and Confidentiality.** The Independent Auditor shall (i) possess recognized expertise in healthcare AI/ML auditing; (ii) execute a confidentiality agreement with Vendor, in a form reasonably acceptable to Vendor, prior to commencing any audit; (iii) conduct all Tier 3 reviews solely within Vendor's designated secure enclave; (iv) not extract, copy, or remove any Vendor Confidential Information from the secure environment; and (v) destroy all working papers containing Vendor Confidential Information within thirty (30) days of delivering the audit report and certify such destruction in writing to Vendor within five (5) business days.
>
> (d) **Audit Reports.** The Independent Auditor shall deliver a written audit report to both Parties. Vendor shall provide a written response and remediation plan within thirty (30) days of receipt of any audit report identifying material findings. Audit reports shall be maintained by Customer in a form suitable for production to the New Jersey Department of Health upon request.
>
> (e) **Regulatory Cooperation.** Vendor shall cooperate fully with Customer and the Independent Auditor in responding to any inquiry or investigation by the New Jersey Department of Health or other governmental authority under the NJ Act or comparable law, including making personnel available, producing documentation within statutory timeframes (not to exceed fifteen (15) business days), and providing technical assistance. Vendor shall not seek protective orders or confidential treatment that would prevent Customer from satisfying its regulatory obligations, except with Customer's prior written consent.
>
> (f) **Substitute Auditors.** If Langford is unable or unwilling to serve, Customer may propose an alternative Independent Auditor, subject to Vendor's reasonable approval (not to be unreasonably withheld, conditioned, or delayed), to be agreed within thirty (30) days.

**§10.4 No Discretionary Limitation.** Nothing in this Article 10 shall be construed to limit Customer's statutory rights under the NJ Act, and no term of this Agreement shall waive, limit, or condition Customer's ability to satisfy its obligations under the NJ Act.

### (ii) Negotiation Rationale and Fallback Ladder

| Step | Frequency | Access Level | Auditor | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vendor opening (June 6) | Annual summary report only | No model internals | Vendor-selected | Rejected |
| Customer opening (June 20) | Quarterly | Full model internals + weights | Customer's chosen auditor | Opening |
| Vendor counter (June 30) | Annual | Severely limited (no architecture, no provenance, no SHAP, no weights); Vendor may redact | Vendor-approved | Insufficient for NJ Act |

**Rationale.** The compromise is the Playbook's second-fallback position, selected deliberately because (a) it satisfies the NJ Act's mandatory independent annual audit at the scope Langford has confirmed is sufficient (Tier 2), and (b) the Vendor has already signaled — through Sandra Hu's private acknowledgment — that a qualified third-party audit is acceptable. Spending negotiating capital to push for quarterly or semi-annual audits would be inefficient when annual Tier 2 access satisfies the regulatory mandate and Meridian's risk-management needs, and when concessions on audit frequency can be preserved for higher-priority terms.

The Tier 3 for-cause mechanism is the critical addition over the Vendor's June 30 counter, which stripped out architecture, provenance, and any weight access. Tier 3 is structured to neutralize the CTO's "hard no" on raw model weights: it is (i) triggered only by defined cause events (patient-safety incident, regulatory inquiry, persistent benchmark failure, or mutual agreement), not routine; and (ii) enclave-only with no extraction — the Vendor's model IP never leaves its controlled environment. This distinction (extractable access = hard no; enclave review for cause = acceptable) is the bridge that makes meaningful audit rights compatible with the Vendor's IP concerns.

**Fallback if the Vendor refuses Tier 3.** Hold at annual Tier 2 (which still satisfies the NJ Act) and route cause-based escalations through the SLA Governance Committee with a right to escalate to Tier 3 by mutual agreement or upon a formal NJ DOH investigation. Do not surrender Tier 2 scope (architecture, provenance, validation, test queries) under any circumstances — Tier 2 is the statutory minimum and Langford's confirmed floor for NJ Act compliance.

### (iii) Acceptability Analysis for the Vendor Team

**Sandra Hu (commercial flexibility / precedent).** Hu has already privately acknowledged that a qualified third-party audit is acceptable, conditioned on defined scope and a mutually agreed auditor. The compromise delivers both: Langford is pre-approved by both sides, the scope is defined by tier, and the annual cadence (not quarterly) minimizes operational burden. The objection to anticipate is the breadth of Tier 2 scope (architecture, provenance, test queries). The response: Tier 2 is the minimum scope Langford has identified as sufficient for NJ Act compliance, it does not require raw weight disclosure, and it is conducted under NDA with secure-environment protocols and mandatory work-product destruction. Meridian is not asking for anything the Vendor's own comparable-deal customers have not received in substance.

**Rafael Bowen / CTO (IP protection).** The CTO's "hard no" is on **extractable** access to raw model weights. The compromise honors that: Tier 2 (routine) requires no weight access at all; Tier 3 (cause-based) permits weight review only inside a Vendor-controlled secure enclave with no extraction, no copying, no network egress, and cryptographic destruction of all enclave data after the audit, with destruction certified in writing. The Vendor retains the right to monitor and log all enclave access. This is the architecture Langford designed specifically to permit the technical review a regulatory investigation may require without creating model-replication or reverse-engineering risk. Bowen should be shown the enclave protocol in detail; it is the single most important document for resolving the audit impasse.

### (iv) Regulatory and Compliance Constraints

- **NJ AI in Healthcare Transparency Act (statutory mandate).** The Act requires an independent annual audit of each covered AI system deployed in clinical decision-making at Meridian's three New Jersey facilities (Meridian Atlantic Medical Center, Meridian South Jersey Regional Hospital, Meridian Princeton Medical Center), with audit reports available to the NJ DOH upon request (production within 15 business days) and civil penalties up to $50,000 per violation per day. The Act's anti-waiver provision (§ 7(b)) prohibits contractual terms that restrict Meridian's audit or documentation access below the level required for compliance. The Vendor's June 30 counter — which conditions model-level audit access on Vendor consent and excludes architecture, provenance, and test queries — is, as a matter of New Jersey law, insufficient to enable Meridian's compliance and is unenforceable to the extent it conflicts with the Act. The compromise must be presented to the Vendor as a **legal compliance baseline**, not a preferred commercial position. The first mandatory audit must be completed by January 6, 2027 (12 months after go-live); audit fieldwork should commence no later than Q4 2026.
- **HIPAA.** Audit access must be conducted in a manner consistent with the BAA; the Langford NDA and secure-environment protocols satisfy PHI-protection requirements. De-identified validation datasets used in testing must meet Safe Harbor or Expert Determination standards (45 C.F.R. § 164.514(b)).
- **FDA clinical decision-support guidance.** The audit framework must accommodate evolving FDA requirements. The radiology module's potential device-status question (Cures Act § 3060, first criterion) makes independent validation of imaging-module performance especially important. The change-of-law provision (Playbook § 10.4) should be incorporated so that audit scope can expand to meet new FDA requirements without amendment.
- **Enterprise-wide application.** While the NJ Act applies to three facilities, Whitfield Ames recommends applying the audit framework uniformly across all 14 Covered Facilities to avoid dual-framework operational complexity and to position Meridian for anticipated comparable legislation in Delaware (H.B. 412) and Pennsylvania. The clause language is drafted to apply enterprise-wide.

## TERM 3 — TERMINATION FOR CONVENIENCE / ETF / TRANSITION

**Priority: 3. Agreement Sections: §14.3–14.6; §2.5 (portability). Related: Term 6 (IP portability).**

Meridian must retain the right to terminate for convenience without an early termination fee that makes exit economically prohibitive. The graduated ETF structure identified through CFO intelligence is the compromise architecture, paired with robust transition and data-export obligations.

### (i) Proposed Contract Clause Language

**§14.3 Termination for Convenience.**

> (a) **Customer Termination for Convenience.** Customer may terminate this Agreement for convenience at any time upon one hundred fifty (150) days' prior written notice to Vendor, subject to the graduated Early Termination Fee set forth in subsection (b).

The perpetual license is a use right, not a transfer of title, and is limited to the fine-tuned layer generated from Meridian's data (not the base model). This is consistent with the Vendor's own June 30 counter, which offered a "perpetual output and data set license" — the compromise extends that concept to the fine-tuned model layer, which is the incremental ask. Hu should view this as the Vendor keeping its IP while granting a bounded, customer-specific license.

**Rafael Bowen / CTO (IP protection).** The CTO's concern is model replication and reverse-engineering. The response: (a) the license is to the **fine-tuned layer** (the delta from Meridian's data), not the base model — the Vendor's foundational IP is expressly excluded (§6.2(e)); (b) the license is non-exclusive, so it does not prevent the Vendor from using the same fine-tuned adaptations elsewhere; (c) portability is for **operational continuity** (running the fine-tuned model on alternative infrastructure), not for reverse-engineering the base model — the reverse-engineering prohibitions in §2.6 remain in force; and (d) the functional-portability mechanism (continued API access or transition service) is available if technical inseparability makes weight export infeasible, so the Vendor is not required to do something technically impossible. The Langford audit framework (Term 2) provides the independent verification Meridian needs without weight extraction, further reducing IP-exposure concerns.

### (iv) Regulatory and Compliance Constraints

- **NJ Act.** The NJ Act does not require Meridian to own model weights — it requires access to documentation and audit rights, which are addressed in Term 2. The IP compromise is therefore regulatorily neutral; it does not impair NJ Act compliance.
- **HIPAA.** Fine-tuned model artifacts that incorporate Customer Data must be handled consistent with the BAA and Exhibit D de-identification standards. The portability export is limited to fine-tuned model artifacts and Customer Data in interoperable formats; PHI handling during portability remains governed by the BAA.
- **FDA.** If the fine-tuned model is subject to FDA regulation, portability to a successor platform may require regulatory re-validation; the change-of-law and cooperation provisions (Term 2) accommodate this.
- **Vendor lock-in (strategic).** The irrevocable perpetual license with portability is the structural protection against vendor lock-in — the Playbook's core concern in Section 4. Without it, the cost of switching vendors could become prohibitive, converting a contractual relationship into a dependency. The survival-for-any-reason provision is the non-negotiable element.

## TERM 7 — INDEMNIFICATION SCOPE AND STRUCTURE

**Priority: 7 (lowest). Agreement Sections: §13.1–13.7; §15.1 (insurance). Related: Term 1 (super-cap).**

Indemnification is the most readily tradeable of the seven categories, but it intersects heavily with Term 1 and carries Board Risk Committee implications. The Playbook fallback (broad scope; shared defense with Vendor primary for AI-output claims; subject to super-cap) is the landing zone, drafted to operate coherently with the Term 1 super-cap.

### (i) Proposed Contract Clause Language

**§13.1 Vendor Indemnification (scope).** Vendor shall indemnify, defend, and hold harmless Customer and its Affiliates and their respective officers, directors, employees, agents, and medical staff members (collectively, "Customer Indemnitees") from and against any and all third-party claims, governmental investigations, regulatory enforcement actions, demands, judgments, damages, liabilities, penalties, fines, losses, costs, and expenses (including reasonable attorneys' fees) (collectively, "Losses") arising out of or related to:

> (a) **IP Infringement Claims** — any allegation that Customer's authorized use of the Platform infringes or misappropriates any third-party patent, copyright, trademark, or trade secret;
>
> (b) **Regulatory Claims** — any investigation, enforcement action, or proceeding by a governmental authority (including HHS-OCR, the New Jersey Department of Health, the FDA, or any state attorney general) arising from Vendor's acts or omissions in connection with the Platform, including non-compliance with HIPAA, HITECH, the NJ Act, or applicable FDA regulations;
>
> (c) **Patient-Harm Claims** — any claim by or on behalf of a patient alleging bodily injury, adverse clinical outcome, wrongful death, or emotional distress arising from or related to any Output, recommendation, alert, risk score, or clinical decision-support result generated by the Platform that is alleged to be inaccurate, misleading, biased, delayed, or otherwise deficient; and
>
> (d) **Data-Breach Claims** — any claim, regulatory action, or proceeding arising from a Security Incident or Breach of Unsecured PHI attributable to Vendor, its subprocessors, or its systems, including notification costs, credit monitoring, forensic investigation, and regulatory fines.

**§13.5 Defense Control — Shared Defense for Patient-Safety Claims.**

> (a) **General.** Except as provided in subsection (b), the Indemnifying Party shall have primary control of the defense and settlement of indemnified claims; provided that no settlement shall impose liability or admit fault on behalf of the Indemnified Party without its prior written consent (not to be unreasonably withheld).
>
> (b) **AI-Output Patient-Safety Claims.** With respect to Patient-Harm Claims under Section 13.1(c): (i) **Vendor shall be primarily responsible** for the defense and indemnification of claims directly attributable to AI-output errors — i.e., claims where the proximate cause of patient harm is an erroneous, misleading, or defective Output generated by the Platform; (ii) **Customer shall be primarily responsible** for claims arising from clinician override or independent clinical judgment — i.e., claims where a clinician received a correct Output but chose not to follow it, or where the clinician's independent decision-making was the proximate cause; (iii) for **mixed-causation claims**, the Parties shall cooperate in good faith and allocate defense costs and indemnification obligations proportionately, and failing agreement, through the dispute resolution process in Section 16.2; and (iv) Vendor shall not settle any Patient-Harm Claim without Customer's prior written consent where the settlement could adversely affect Customer's clinical reputation, regulatory standing, or patient relationships.
>
> (c) **Customer Participation.** Customer shall have the right, at its own expense, to participate in the defense of any Patient-Harm Claim with counsel of its own choosing, given the direct impact on Customer's clinical reputation.

**§13.7 Indemnification Cap.** Vendor's aggregate liability under this Article 13 shall be subject to the Super-Cap set forth in Section 12.3 (3× trailing twelve-month Fees, ~$28.8M) and shall not be subject to the General Cap. For the avoidance of doubt, indemnification obligations and other super-cap liabilities share the single 3× Super-Cap aggregate.

**§13.8 NJ Act Regulatory-Penalty Indemnification.** To the extent any civil penalty is assessed against Customer under the NJ Act as a result of Vendor's breach of its documentation, audit, or regulatory-cooperation obligations under Article 10 or Section 5.7, Vendor shall indemnify Customer for such penalties, subject to the Super-Cap. Penalties attributable to Customer's own operational failures (not to Vendor's breach) are not indemnifiable by Vendor.

### (ii) Negotiation Rationale and Fallback Ladder

| Step | Scope | Defense Control | Cap | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vendor opening/counter (June 6/30) | IP infringement only | Vendor controls all; shared for complex | General cap (then super-cap) | Rejected |
| Customer opening (June 20) | Broad (IP, regulatory, patient harm, data breach) | Customer controls patient-safety defense | Excluded from cap | Opening |
| **Compromise proposal** | **Broad (IP, regulatory, patient harm, data breach)** | **Shared; Vendor primary for AI-output; Customer primary for clinician override** | **Subject to Super-Cap (3×), not General Cap** | **Recommended landing zone (Playbook fallback)** |
| Floor | Must include patient harm + IP at minimum | — | Never less than Super-Cap floor | Non-negotiable |

**Rationale.** The compromise restores the indemnification scope the Vendor stripped out (regulatory, patient-harm, and data-breach categories) while conceding on defense control — moving from Customer-controlled defense of all patient-safety claims (Customer opening) to the Playbook's shared-defense structure with Vendor primary for AI-output claims. This is the correct trade for a Priority 7 term: Meridian gets the scope it needs (the Vendor stands behind its product's clinical outputs and regulatory compliance) while giving the Vendor a defense-control structure it can accept (the Vendor leads on claims attributable to its AI outputs; Meridian leads on claims attributable to clinician judgment).

The cap is the key structural decision: indemnification is placed **above the general cap but within the 3× super-cap** (Term 1), not excluded from all caps (Customer opening) and not buried in the general cap (Vendor position). This is the Playbook fallback and is coherent with the Term 1 super-cap architecture — indemnification obligations and the other super-cap categories (patient-safety, PHI, regulatory) share the single 3× aggregate, backed by the binding insurance increase. The NJ Act regulatory-penalty indemnification (§13.8) is carved out as a specific obligation consistent with the Whitfield Ames compliance brief, subject to the super-cap.

**Why this is the right concession level.** Indemnification is Priority 7 — the most tradeable category. The compromise gives the Vendor the defense-control structure it wants (a real concession) while holding the scope line (patient-harm and regulatory indemnification must remain). Per the Playbook trade-off guidance, moderate flexibility on defense control is acceptable in exchange for the Vendor's agreement on the broader scope and on the Term 1 super-cap. Any trade-off that eliminated patient-harm indemnification entirely would require GC escalation and is not contemplated here.

**Fallback.** Hold at broad scope with shared defense. The only further concession available is on the consent-to-settle standard (could soften Customer's consent right on non-reputational settlements), but the scope (patient harm + IP minimum) and the super-cap floor are non-negotiable.

### (iii) Acceptability Analysis for the Vendor Team

**Sandra Hu (commercial flexibility).** Hu's objection is that broad indemnification (regulatory, patient-harm, data-breach) exceeds the Vendor's "IP-only" standard. The response: (a) the Vendor's own June 30 counter already moved to add data-breach indemnity to IP — the compromise extends that logic to regulatory and patient-harm, which are the categories a clinical AI vendor must stand behind when its outputs directly influence patient care across 14 hospitals; (b) the shared-defense structure (Vendor primary for AI-output errors, Customer primary for clinician-override errors) is a fair allocation that does not make the Vendor the guarantor of all clinical outcomes — where the clinician's independent judgment caused the harm, Meridian bears the defense; (c) the cap is the 3× super-cap the Vendor has already agreed to in substance (Term 1), not an uncapped or general-cap arrangement; and (d) the package gives the Vendor meaningful data-usage value (Term 4) and IP ownership (Term 6) in exchange. Framed as "the Vendor stands behind its AI outputs and regulatory compliance, with a fair defense split and a capped exposure it has already accepted," this should be acceptable.

**Rafael Bowen (liability exposure).** Bowen's concern is unbounded patient-harm indemnification. The response: (a) the indemnification is capped at the 3× super-cap (~$28.8M), not uncapped — the only uncapped categories are willful misconduct and confidentiality (Term 1), which are standard; (b) the shared-defense structure means the Vendor indemnifies AI-output-error claims (its domain) while Meridian indemnifies clinician-override claims (Meridian's domain) — the Vendor is not absorbing clinician-malpractice exposure; (c) the binding insurance increase to $30M/$60M (Term 1) backs the indemnification with adequate coverage; and (d) the NJ Act penalty indemnification is limited to penalties attributable to the Vendor's own breach of its documentation/audit/cooperation obligations — penalties from Meridian's operational failures are not shifted to the Vendor. The indemnification package is bounded, fairly allocated, and insured.

### (iv) Regulatory and Compliance Constraints

- **NJ Act.** Civil penalties (up to $50,000 per violation per day) are assessed against Meridian as the covered entity, not the Vendor. Where penalties arise from the Vendor's failure to provide required documentation, audit access, or regulatory cooperation, Meridian's only recourse is contractual. The §13.8 NJ Act penalty indemnification (subject to the super-cap) implements the Whitfield Ames compliance brief's recommendation and is essential to Meridian's risk allocation. The anti-waiver provision (NJ Act § 7(b)) ensures these contractual rights cannot be waived below the level required for compliance.
- **HIPAA / HITECH.** Data-breach indemnification (§13.1(d)) is consistent with the BAA's breach framework and the Playbook's treatment of PHI breaches as a highest-severity category. The 24-hour breach-notification standard (BAA) is unaffected.
- **FDA.** Regulatory indemnification (§13.1(b)) extends to FDA enforcement actions attributable to the Vendor's failure to maintain required clearances or to platform defects — consistent with the Playbook's § 10.3 framework. The Vendor's obligation to notify Meridian of FDA adverse actions (within 24 hours) and to cooperate with FDA inquiries supports the indemnification.
- **Board Risk Committee (Tier 1).** The indemnification structure is part of the liability package the Board will review. The combination of broad scope, shared defense, super-cap backing, and the binding insurance increase is designed to be defensible: the Vendor stands behind its clinical outputs and regulatory compliance, exposure is capped and insured, and defense is allocated to the party best positioned to control the relevant risk.
- **Insurance collectability.** The indemnification cap (3× super-cap, ~$28.8M) is backed by the binding insurance increase to $30M/$60M (Term 1, §15.1(c)). Without the insurance commitment, the indemnification would present a collectability gap; the two terms must close together.

The Board Risk Committee has designated AI-related liability as a Tier 1 enterprise risk and requires a pre-execution briefing for any agreement with TCV exceeding $10 million (this Agreement's TCV is $38.4 million). The compromise package is designed to be defensible in that briefing:

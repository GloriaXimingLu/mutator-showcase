       I. Executive Summary  
      We reviewed the draft Settlement Agreement and General Release of All Claims dated May 5, 2025 (the         “    Draft    ”    ), prepared by Engel & Associates, P.C. on behalf of Marcus R. Delano, against Greenleaf’s employment records and the company policies referenced in the Draft (the Greenleaf 2021 Equity Incentive Plan and the RSU Grant Agreement dated July 1, 2022). A redlined markup reflecting our drafted revisions accompanies this memo as              settlement-agreement-markup.docx    .  
      The Draft is substantively negotiable but, as written, contains several provisions that are             legally defective or unenforceable         and would expose Greenleaf to avoidable risk if executed without correction. The most significant are:  
             OWBPA noncompliance (critical).         The Draft gives Delano only             14 days         to consider the release and provides             no seven-day revocation period    . Because Delano is 58 and is releasing Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA) claims, the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA) requires             at least 21 days         to consider and             7 days         to revoke. Without these, the ADEA release is invalid and Delano could sue for age discrimination after keeping the settlement proceeds.  
             RSU valuation error (critical).         The Draft values the 5,000 accelerated RSUs at             $10.00/share         ($50,000), citing the         “    most recent    ”         Ridgepoint valuation. The most recent 409A valuation is dated             January 15, 2025 at $12.50/share    . The correct value is             $62,500    , and the correct total settlement value is             $537,500    , not $525,000 — a             $12,500 understatement    , and a misstatement of fact in the agreement.  
             Non-compete is likely void (critical).         The 18-month, four-state non-compete was entered into at termination, not upon hire or a bona fide advancement, and Delano was terminated by the Company. Under             ORS 653.295         it is almost certainly void and unenforceable; the California portion is independently void under California Business & Professions Code § 16600. We have struck it.  
             Confidentiality / non-disparagement chill protected whistleblower activity (critical).         The Draft contains no carve-out for communications with government agencies or cooperation with the             pending Oregon OSHA investigation    . As written, these clauses are unenforceable as to protected activity and could themselves support a retaliation claim. We added robust carve-outs and a new Section 19.  
      We also identified a strong underlying             retaliation risk         that the settlement is meant to resolve: Delano engaged in protected activity (internal complaint November 8, 2024; Oregon OSHA complaint December 3, 2024), was placed on leave six weeks later, terminated two months after that, and             a replacement (Raj Mehta) was hired six days after the termination         — squarely contradicting the stated         “    elimination of position    ”         rationale. This dynamic affects litigation leverage and should inform final positioning, as discussed in Part VI.

        V. Issues and Recommended Revisions  
       A. Critical  
          A1. OWBPA — consideration period and revocation period (§5, §16, §1(b)).         Delano is 58 and is releasing ADEA claims. The OWBPA, 29 U.S.C. § 626(f), requires that a release of ADEA claims be         “    knowing and voluntary,    ”         which requires, at minimum,             (i) a reasonable period of at least 21 days         to consider the agreement and             (ii) a seven-day revocation period    , during which the agreement is not effective. The Draft provides only             14 days         and             no revocation period    , and defines the         “    Effective Date    ”         as May 5, 2025 (a fixed date unrelated to execution or revocation).             Effect:         the ADEA release is likely invalid; Delano could accept the money and later sue for age discrimination, and the Company would have difficulty enforcing the release.             Fix (in redline):         21-day consideration period; 7-day revocation right with mechanics in §16;         “    Effective Date    ”         redefined as the 8th day after signing (post-revocation).             Note:         if the         “    restructuring    ”         is part of a group termination or exit-incentive program, OWBPA requires             45 days         and disclosure of eligibility data — confirm this was an individual termination (see Part VII).  
          A2. RSU valuation uses an outdated 409A valuation and misstates it as                 “        most recent        ”                 (§3(d), §3(e)).         The Draft states the 5,000 accelerated RSUs are valued at         “    $10.00 per share, based on the most recent independent valuation conducted by Ridgepoint Valuation Services.    ”         That is the             July 1, 2024         valuation. The             most recent         409A valuation is dated             January 15, 2025 at $12.50/share    . The correct RSU value is             $62,500    , and the correct aggregate is             $537,500         (not $525,000) — a             $12,500 understatement    .             Effect:         the Company would under-deliver value and the agreement would contain a factual misstatement that Delano’s counsel could later challenge (and that is inconsistent with the Company’s own equity records).             Fix (in redline):         correct to $12.50/share and $62,500; reference the January 15, 2025 valuation; correct the total.             Recommendation:         confirm with finance/stock administration that the January 15, 2025 valuation remains the current 409A valuation as of the vesting/delivery date.  
          A3. Non-competition covenant is likely void under Oregon law (§9).         The 18-month non-compete covering Oregon, Washington, Idaho, and California was entered into in connection with Delano’s             termination    , not upon his initial employment or a bona fide advancement. Under             ORS 653.295(1)(d)    , a non-compete is void unless entered into upon initial employment or a bona fide advancement. Separately, under             ORS 653.295(2)    , a non-compete may not be enforced against an employee             terminated by the employer         (Delano was terminated, not for cause) unless a qualifying severance package is provided — which the Draft does not clearly satisfy. The             California         portion is independently void under California Business & Professions Code § 16600.             Effect:         the provision is almost certainly unenforceable; keeping it creates false expectations, invites a challenge, and — given the retaliation context — looks punitive.             Fix (in redline):         strike the non-compete and replace with an acknowledgment that no non-compete is imposed, with post-employment conduct governed by the non-solicitation in §10 (which is excluded from ORS 653.295 and is generally enforceable if reasonable).             Recommendation:         if the Company wants post-termination protection, rely on §10 (non-solicitation) and confidentiality; do not rely on a non-compete.  
          A4. Confidentiality and non-disparagement chill protected whistleblower activity; pending OSHA investigation (§6, §7).         The Draft’s confidentiality clause permits disclosure only to spouse/counsel/tax advisor or         “    as required by … compulsory legal process,    ”         and the non-disparagement clause is broad and perpetual. Neither carves out             communications with government agencies, cooperation with investigations, or receipt of whistleblower awards.         The Oregon OSHA investigation (Complaint No. OR-OSHA-2024-11872) is             pending    .             Effect:         as written, these clauses (a) are unenforceable as to protected activity under ORS 654.062(5), federal OSHA § 11(c), the Defend Trade Secrets Act (18 U.S.C. § 1833(b)), and EEOC/NLRB guidance; and (b) could themselves be alleged as further retaliation.             Fix (in redline):         add agency-communication and DTSA carve-outs to §§6–7; add a consolidated             Section 19 (Protected Activity and Government Agency Communications)         that also acknowledges the OSHA investigation may proceed independently; shorten non-disparagement from         “    in perpetuity    ”         to             3 years    .  
        B. High  
          B1. Back-pay amount inconsistent with the stated period (§3(b)).         The Draft characterizes $125,000 as         “    back pay … from the Separation Date through the Effective Date    ”         (March 14 – May 5, 2025 = 52 days). At $218,000/year, 52 days of wages ≈             $31,000    , not $125,000. $125,000 corresponds to roughly             6.9 months         of salary.             Effect:         the characterization does not match the amount, creating a tax-allocation and factual-consistency risk (the IRS and a court look to the nature of the claims).             Fix (in redline):         recharacterize as         “    back pay and front pay    ”         for approximately seven months following the Separation Date (front pay remains wage income, W-2).             Recommendation:         have tax counsel confirm the W-2/1099 allocation across the $275,000 / $125,000 split is defensible given the claims released.  
          B2. No governing law, venue, or dispute-resolution provision.         The Draft contains no governing-law clause, no forum/venue selection, and no fee-shifting or equitable-relief mechanism for enforcing the agreement.             Fix (in redline):         new             Section 20         — Oregon law; exclusive venue in state/federal courts in Multnomah County, Oregon; prevailing-party fees; preserved right to seek injunctive relief to enforce §§6, 7, and 10.  
          B3. Entire-agreement clause would supersede the Equity Plan and RSU Grant (§18).         §18 states the agreement         “    supersedes all prior … agreements … including … employment agreements … or term sheets.    ”         Read literally, this could supersede the             2021 Equity Incentive Plan and the RSU Grant Agreement    , which must continue to govern vesting, forfeiture, and tax treatment of the RSUs.             Fix (in redline):         carve out the Plan and RSU Grant from the integration clause, except as expressly modified by §3(d).  
          B4. Release of workers’ compensation claims is likely ineffective (§4(xii)).         The general release purports to release         “    workers’ compensation claims under ORS Chapter 656.    ”         Claims under ORS Chapter 656 generally cannot be extinguished by a general release absent a Board-approved compromise and release.             Fix (in redline):         qualify the release to the extent permitted by law and subject to required Board approval.  
          B5. Accelerated vesting requires Compensation Committee approval (§3(d), §15(b)).         The equity record states the             Compensation Committee has discretion to approve accelerated vesting in connection with separation agreements.         The Draft does not condition the acceleration on that approval or represent that it has been obtained.             Fix (in redline):         add the approval as a condition in §3(d) and a corresponding representation in §15(b).             Action item:         obtain and document Comp Committee approval before the vesting/delivery date (Part VII).  
        C. Moderate  
          C1. Mandated                 “        positive        ”                 reference and required laudatory language (§11).         §11 requires a         “    positive    ”         reference and a scripted statement that Delano         “    was a valued member … who made significant contributions.    ”         Mandating specific praise the Company may not endorse creates misrepresentation risk if a prospective employer relies on it.             Fix (in redline):         “    neutral, non-negative    ”         reference; replace the scripted praise with a confirmation of rehire eligibility (consistent with §14). The prohibition on stating Delano was terminated         “    for cause    ”         is retained (consistent with the record — he was not).  
          C2. No cooperation clause; OSHA matter proceeds independently.         The Draft does not address Delano’s cooperation with the pending OSHA investigation.             Fix (in redline):         Section 19 acknowledges the OSHA investigation may proceed independently and preserves Delano’s right to cooperate.             Recommendation:         consider whether the Company also wants an affirmative cooperation obligation (separate from protected-activity rights) — a point to confirm with the GC.  
          C3. Re-employment eligibility vs. replacement hire (§14).         §14 makes Delano eligible for rehire and bars a         “    do-not-rehire    ”         designation. This is fine on its own, but it sits uneasily beside the fact that a replacement was hired six days after the         “    elimination of position.    ”         The rehire eligibility is retained; the tension is a litigation-leverage point, not a drafting defect (see Part VI).  
      
         VI. Principal Litigation and Regulatory Risks (Not Fully Resolved by the Agreement)  
      These bear on whether the settlement value and structure are appropriate. They are not         “    drafting    ”         fixes but should inform the GC’s decision to sign.  
             Strong retaliation inference.         The timeline is adverse to the Company: protected activity (Nov. 8, 2024 internal complaint; Dec. 3, 2024 OSHA complaint) → paid administrative leave (Feb. 10, 2025) → termination (Mar. 14, 2025) →             replacement hired six days later (Mar. 20, 2025)    . The         “    elimination of position due to restructuring    ”         rationale is directly contradicted by the prompt hiring of Raj Mehta into a substantially similar role (Sr. Director, Supply Chain & Logistics) at a lower salary ($185,000 vs. $218,000). This is powerful circumstantial evidence of pretext under ORS 654.062(5) and supports the ADEA/ORS 659A claims.             Recommendation:         preserve a defensible (and consistent) record of the reorganization; ensure the decisional documents do not contradict the         “    restructuring    ”         rationale; and weigh this exposure in the settlement value.  
             ADEA / age exposure.         Delano is 58; the replacement’s age is not in the records. If the replacement is materially younger, the ADEA claim strengthens.             Action:         confirm the replacement’s age and any other comparators. (The OWBPA fixes in Part V.A are essential regardless.)  
             Pending Oregon OSHA investigation (regulatory exposure independent of the settlement).         A private settlement does not stop an OSHA investigation; Oregon OSHA may cite the Company for the cold-chain/food-safety issues regardless of this agreement.             Recommendation:         coordinate the food-safety response with operations and regulatory counsel; do not assume the settlement closes the OSHA matter.  
             EPLI coordination.         The settlement is potentially covered under the EPLI policy (Timberline Insurance Group, Policy No. TIG-EPL-2024-00391).             Recommendation:         tender/notice to the carrier before execution; confirm coverage, consent-to-settle, and whether the carrier must approve the allocation and amount. Failure to comply with policy notice/consent conditions could forfeit coverage.  
             2024 bonus / wage exposure.         The 2024 bonus was         “    not determined prior to termination.    ”         §2 represents that all wages (including bonuses) have been paid and none are owed. If a pro-rated 2024 bonus was contractually earned, the release should clearly cover it and the Company should confirm no separate wage claim survives under ORS 652.610 / 653.045.

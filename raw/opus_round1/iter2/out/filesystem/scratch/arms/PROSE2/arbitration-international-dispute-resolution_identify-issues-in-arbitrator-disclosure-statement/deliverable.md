       I. Introduction and Scope  
      This memorandum assesses whether grounds exist to challenge the appointment of Hon. Margaret R. Fairhaven (Ret.) as Sole Arbitrator in AAA Case No. 01-24-0003217. The assessment compares the arbitrator’s Disclosure Statement dated June 17, 2024 against the other source documents in the record: (a) the Arbitration Demand and Statement of Claims filed March 15, 2024; (b) the AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules excerpts (Rules R-16, R-17, R-18, and related provisions); (c) the arbitrator appointment data workbook (case list, fee summary, and pending-matters tabs); and (d) the entity and relationship profiles memorandum dated June 19, 2024.  
          Source provenance.         The entity-relationship-profiles memorandum was prepared by Catherine M. Aldridge, an associate at Barrow & Thistlewood LLP (counsel for Respondent Whitmore Capital Partners LLC) and is labeled attorney-client privileged / work product. It is advocacy-oriented research. The underlying facts it reports are stated to be drawn from public records (SEC EDGAR/Form ADV/Form D filings, IRS Form 990 filings, AAA directories, academic publication databases, CLE archives, and public award databases) and are, in principle, independently verifiable. The arbitrator-appointment-data workbook is an unattributed data file that corroborates and, in certain respects, extends the relationship memorandum. Where the two sources disagree, or where a source’s summary figure does not match its underlying line-item data, the discrepancy is flagged below rather than papered over.  
          Bottom line.         The disclosure statement is materially incomplete. Comparison with the source documents reveals             eight         distinct disclosure concerns, of which             five         are outright non-disclosures of facts within the arbitrator’s knowledge or readily available to her,             one         is a partial disclosure that omits material detail, and             two         are aggregate/structural concerns that aggravate the others. Several of the non-disclosures implicate specific subparagraphs of AAA Rule R-17 and contradict the arbitrator’s own certification that her list of prior/pending matters is         “    complete.    ”         The issues do not all point in the same direction: some create an appearance of partiality toward the Claimant’s side (notably the arbitrator’s spouse’s undisclosed academic collaboration with the Claimant’s key expert witness), while others create an appearance of partiality toward the Respondent’s side (notably undisclosed relationships with the Respondent’s lead counsel, the Respondent’s principal, and an indirect financial interest in a fund affiliated with the Respondent). Under the governing         “    justifiable doubt    ”         standard, that bidirectional pattern is itself significant: a reasonable party on either side could question the arbitrator’s ability to render a fair determination. The challenge deadline is             June 24, 2024    .  
      
        II. Procedural Framework and Governing Standards  
          Governing rules.         The arbitration is administered by the AAA under its Commercial Arbitration Rules (as amended effective September 1, 2022), with the Large Complex Case Supplementary Procedures invoked by virtue of the amount in controversy ($46.9 million in compensatory damages alone). The seat is Minneapolis, Minnesota; the substantive law is Delaware; the Federal Arbitration Act, 9 U.S.C. §§ 1–16, governs the arbitration agreement.  
          The disclosure obligation (Rules R-16 and R-17).         Rule R-16 requires disclosure of         “    any circumstance likely to give rise to justifiable doubt as to the arbitrator’s impartiality or independence, including any bias or any financial or personal interest in the result of the arbitration, or any past or present relationship with the parties, their representatives, or their counsel,    ”         and states that the obligation         “    shall be construed broadly.    ”         Rule R-17(a) enumerates four non-exclusive disclosure categories:  
              R-17(a)(i):         any known direct or indirect financial or personal interest in the outcome,         “    including any interest, whether legal or beneficial, in the subject matter of the dispute, in any party, or in any entity that may be affected by the result of the proceeding    ”    ;  
              R-17(a)(ii):         any known existing or past financial, business, professional, family, or social relationship with any party, its counsel, representatives,             or witnesses         likely to affect impartiality or that might reasonably create an appearance of partiality —         “    including, without limitation, relationships arising from employment, professional association, joint ventures, board memberships,             academic collaboration    , or regular social contact    ”    ;  
              R-17(a)(iii):         any prior or pending cases in which the arbitrator has been involved         “    with any party to the arbitration, its counsel, or representatives,    ”         in any capacity, before the AAA or any other institution, neutral or non-neutral; and  
              R-17(a)(iv):         a catch-all covering         “    any other circumstances that might cause a reasonable party to question the arbitrator’s ability to render a fair and impartial determination,    ”         expressly         “    including but not limited to the arbitrator’s prior publications, public statements, or expressed views on legal or factual issues relevant to the dispute.    ”  
      Rule R-17(b) makes disclosure a             continuing obligation    ; the arbitrator         “    shall not await inquiry by a party or by the AAA before making any required supplemental disclosure.    ”         Rule R-17(d) provides that failure to make complete and timely disclosure         “    may result in the arbitrator’s removal in accordance with Rule R-18, and may constitute grounds for vacatur of any resulting award under applicable law, including but not limited to Section 10 of the Federal Arbitration Act.    ”  
          The challenge procedure (Rule R-18).         A challenge must be made by written notice to the AAA and the other party,         “    stating with specificity the grounds for the challenge,    ”         within             seven (7) calendar days         after the later of (i) the appointment being communicated to the challenging party, or (ii) the circumstances giving rise to the challenge becoming known or reasonably should have become known to the challenging party. If the other party does not agree and the arbitrator does not withdraw, the AAA decides the challenge (R-18(d)). A party that fails to challenge timely is deemed to have waived grounds then known to it,             except         grounds that         “    were not and could not reasonably have been known at the time,    ”         and nothing in the rule precludes a later evident-partiality vacatur motion under FAA § 10 for issues not previously known and subject to waiver (R-18(f)).  
          Related authorities referenced in the rules excerpts.         The AAA/ABA Code of Ethics for Arbitrators in Commercial Disputes (Canon I — integrity/fairness; Canon II — disclosure; Canon IV — fair conduct); the IBA Guidelines on Conflicts of Interest in International Arbitration (General Standards; Non-Waivable Red List, Waivable Red List, Orange List, Green List); and FAA § 10(a)(2), which permits vacatur         “    where there was evident partiality or corruption in the arbitrators.    ”         The IBA Guidelines are not binding AAA rules, but they are expressly referenced in the rules excerpts as interpretive guidance and are widely used to evaluate disclosure adequacy. The IBA Orange List is particularly relevant here: item 3.3.3 calls for disclosure where an arbitrator has, within the past three years, received more than three appointments from the same counsel or law firm; items 3.3.5 and 3.3.6 call for disclosure of past or present relationships through shared professional-association or board membership.  
          Deadline.         The arbitrator’s disclosure is dated June 17, 2024. Seven calendar days thereafter is             June 24, 2024    , which the arbitrator herself identifies as the objection deadline. The safest course is to file any challenge on or before that date. The         “    whichever is later    ”         prong of R-18(a)(ii) may afford modest additional time for facts that first became known to a party after June 17 (e.g., facts surfaced in the June 19, 2024 research memorandum), but because most of the relevant facts are drawn from public records that         “    should reasonably have become known    ”         earlier, reliance on that extension is risky and should not be assumed.

        IV. Assessment of Specific Disclosure Issues  
       Issue 1 — Undisclosed academic collaboration between the arbitrator’s spouse and the Claimant’s key expert witness (Dr. Rita Peshkov)  
          The fact (source: relationship memorandum §§III.2, 1.2; demand §VIII.B.2).         Dr. Alan Fairhaven, the arbitrator’s husband, co-authored a peer-reviewed paper with Dr. Rita S. Peshkov, Cascade’s Vice President of R&D and the demand’s designated key expert witness for the Claimant:  
          Title:         “    Optimization of Phase-Change Heat Exchangers in High-Load Industrial Applications    ”  
          Journal:             Journal of Advanced Thermal Engineering    , Vol. 42, Issue 3 (September 2022)  
          Authors: Alan R. Fairhaven, Ph.D.; Rita S. Peshkov, Ph.D.  
      The paper’s subject — optimization of phase-change heat exchangers in high-load industrial applications — is directly on point with the ThermalCore technology at the center of this arbitration (described in the demand as a phase-change heat-exchange system for heavy industrial environments). Co-authorship of a peer-reviewed article in a specialized field ordinarily reflects a sustained professional collaboration (joint research design, data collection, drafting, peer-review response) extending over months, with a relationship that may continue beyond publication.  
          What the disclosure said (Section V).         The arbitrator identifies her husband and his former University of Minnesota faculty position, then states that he         “    does not currently serve in any consulting or advisory capacity to any party,    ”         is         “    not currently engaged in any active research collaborations with industrial partners,    ”         and,         “    [t]o the best of my knowledge, he has no financial or professional relationship with Cascade Thermal Systems Inc., Whitmore Capital Partners LLC, or any of their officers, directors, or employees.    ”         The disclosure does not mention Dr. Peshkov, does not mention the co-authored paper, and does not address any connection between the spouse and any witness.  
          Obligation implicated.         R-17(a)(ii) squarely requires disclosure of         “    professional … relationships with any party … its counsel, representatives,             or witnesses    ,    ”         expressly including         “        academic collaboration    .    ”         R-17(a)(iv) (catch-all) is also implicated. The AAA/ABA Code Canon II imposes a corresponding disclosure duty. Under the IBA Guidelines, the arbitrator’s family members’ relationships with witnesses fall within the General Standard 3 duty to disclose facts that might give rise to doubts.  
          Why it matters.         This is one of the strongest grounds in the record. Three features compound it. First, the relationship is with a             witness         — and not a peripheral witness but the Claimant’s designated             key expert witness         on the central technical issue (the nature, uniqueness, and value of the ThermalCore technology). Second, the collaboration is on the             exact subject matter         of the dispute (phase-change heat exchangers for high-load industrial use), so the spouse’s expertise and the witness’s expertise overlap precisely with the technical questions the arbitrator must decide. Third, the arbitrator was on notice: the demand (which she says she reviewed) names Dr. Peshkov as a key expert witness, and the arbitrator’s own husband is a retired mechanical engineering professor in the same field. A diligent arbitrator in those circumstances should have inquired whether her spouse had any professional relationship with the identified witness. The disclosure’s affirmative statement that the spouse         “    has no … professional relationship with Cascade … or any of their officers, directors, or employees    ”         is, if the co-authorship is accurate,             inaccurate         (Dr. Peshkov is a Cascade employee), or at minimum reflects a failure of the         “    diligent inquiry    ”         the arbitrator certifies she performed. The         “    to the best of my knowledge    ”         qualifier does not cure a failure to inquire into an obvious and checkable connection.  
          Severity: HIGH.             Favors a challenge by the Respondent         (it suggests potential favorability toward the Claimant’s key expert), though it is also relevant to the Claimant to the extent it bears on the arbitrator’s ability to evaluate Dr. Peshkov’s testimony independently of her husband’s collaboration.  
          Recommendation.         This issue likely supports a successful challenge on its own. The challenging party should (a) verify the co-authorship via the cited journal citation, (b) demand that the arbitrator supplement her disclosure to address the spouse–Peshkov relationship and the scope/timing of the collaboration, and (c) if the co-authorship is confirmed, seek the arbitrator’s withdrawal or AAA disqualification under R-18(d). The relationship between the arbitrator’s spouse and a central expert witness on the precise subject matter of the dispute creates justifiable doubt that supplementation alone may not cure.  
        Issue 2 — Undisclosed potential indirect financial interest in Whitmore Fund II LP (via the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund)  
          The fact (source: relationship memorandum §§III.4, 4.3).         The arbitrator’s personal investment portfolio is managed on a discretionary basis by Northbridge Wealth Advisors. Northbridge also manages a fund-of-funds product, the             Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund    , which per its most recent public filing (March 2024) holds a             2.3% limited partner interest in Whitmore Fund II LP    . Whitmore Fund II LP owns Volaris Manufacturing Group LLC, the direct beneficiary of the challenged below-market license. The economic outcome of this arbitration — any award rescinding, renegotiating, repricing, or imposing damages with respect to the Volaris license — could affect the value of Whitmore Fund II LP’s portfolio and, by extension, the value of the LP interest held by the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund. The chain of potential indirect interest is:  
      Arbitrator’s portfolio (managed by Northbridge Wealth Advisors) → possible allocation to the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund (managed by the same firm) → 2.3% LP interest in Whitmore Fund II LP → owns Volaris → beneficiary of the disputed $2.8M/yr license (FMV $9.5M/yr).  
          What the disclosure said (Section IV).         The arbitrator states she holds         “    no direct equity positions in any party to this arbitration,    ”         that she has         “    not undertaken a position-by-position review of each fund or sub-investment held within my managed accounts,    ”         that she relies on Northbridge to manage the portfolio within her investment policy statement, and that she is         “    not aware of any … investment that would constitute a material financial interest in the outcome of this proceeding.    ”  
          Obligation implicated.         R-17(a)(i) requires disclosure of         “    any known             direct or indirect         financial or personal interest in the outcome … including any interest, whether legal or beneficial, in the subject matter of the dispute, in any party,             or in any entity that may be affected by the result of the proceeding    .    ”         Whitmore Fund II LP is an         “    entity that may be affected by the result    ”         — it owns Volaris, the beneficiary of the license whose rescission/repricing is the core remedy sought. FAA § 10(a)(2) (evident partiality) is implicated to the extent an indirect financial interest is confirmed.  
          Why it matters.         The disclosure’s         “    no direct equity positions    ”         framing is technically accurate but does not address the             indirect         interest that R-17(a)(i) expressly reaches. Critically, the arbitrator admits she has not reviewed the underlying holdings of her managed accounts, so she cannot in fact certify the absence of an indirect interest — she can only say she is         “    not aware    ”         of one. Where her own investment adviser manages a fund-of-funds that holds a direct LP interest in a fund affiliated with the Respondent and directly affected by the remedy, the         “    not aware    ”         statement is not a disclosure; it is an acknowledgment of an unexamined risk. The interest, if held, is aligned with the             Respondent         (a Claimant victory that rescinds or reprices the license reduces Volaris’s benefit and, derivatively, Whitmore Fund II LP’s portfolio value).  
          Important caveat.         The public record does             not         confirm that the arbitrator’s personal portfolio includes an allocation to the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund. The interest is therefore             potential         rather than established. The point nonetheless stands as a disclosure deficiency: the arbitrator should have made inquiry of Northbridge (or disclosed the limits of her knowledge and the specific exposure pathway) rather than relying on a         “    no direct equity    ”         formulation that sidesteps the indirect-interest obligation.  
          Severity: MEDIUM-HIGH as a disclosure deficiency (potentially HIGH if the allocation is confirmed).             Favors a challenge by the Claimant.  
          Recommendation.         Demand that the arbitrator make inquiry of Northbridge Wealth Advisors and supplement her disclosure to state whether her portfolio (or any pooled vehicle in which she participates) holds the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund or any other vehicle with exposure to Whitmore Fund II LP, Whitmore Fund III LP, or any Whitmore-managed fund or portfolio company. If any such exposure is confirmed, disqualification is warranted under R-17(a)(i) and R-18. Even if no exposure is confirmed, the arbitrator’s initial framing was inadequate and the supplementation should be on the record.  
        Issue 3 — Undisclosed CLE co-panelist relationship with Dominic Hargrave (Respondent’s lead counsel) on a directly germane topic  
          The fact (source: relationship memorandum §§III.6, 4.1).         On October 14, 2023, the Lakeview Dispute Resolution Center hosted a CLE panel titled             “        Arbitrating Complex IP Licensing Disputes,        ”         at Lakeview’s offices (750 Nicollet Mall, Suite 3200, Minneapolis). The listed panelists were Hon. Margaret R. Fairhaven (Ret.);             Dominic Hargrave         (lead partner for Whitmore/Respondent in this arbitration); Professor Naomi J. Strickland (University of Minnesota Law School); and Marcus DelVecchio (Henning & Burwell P.A.). The panel addressed valuation methodologies, arbitrator selection in technology-intensive cases, and the management of expert testimony — topics directly germane to this arbitration. The event occurred roughly five months before the demand was filed and eight months before the arbitrator’s appointment was confirmed.  
          What the disclosure said.         The disclosure statement does not mention the CLE, does not identify the arbitrator and Mr. Hargrave as co-panelists, and does not otherwise disclose any professional relationship with Mr. Hargrave beyond the arbitration matters in which Barrow & Thistlewood has appeared as counsel.  
          Obligation implicated.         R-17(a)(ii) (professional relationships with counsel, including         “    professional association    ”    ); R-17(a)(iv) (the catch-all expressly reaches         “    public statements, or expressed views on legal or factual issues relevant to the dispute    ”         — a CLE panel on arbitrating IP licensing disputes qualifies). IBA Orange List items 3.3.5/3.3.6 (shared professional-association activity) are interpretively relevant. AAA/ABA Code Canon II.  
          Why it matters.         Co-paneling a single CLE is not per se disqualifying, and the arbitrator did disclose her repeat arbitration appointments with Barrow & Thistlewood. But four aggravating features make this non-disclosure material: (a) the co-panelist was the             lead counsel for the Respondent specifically         (not merely a member of the firm); (b) the topic was             directly germane         to this arbitration (IP licensing disputes, valuation, expert testimony); (c) the event was hosted at             Lakeview    , the arbitrator’s own affiliated institution, tying the CLE to her institutional affiliation (Issue 8); and (d) the arbitrator disclosed other, less direct professional relationships (e.g., Ellen Wu’s advisory-board service) but omitted this one. The combination creates an appearance of professional familiarity with the Respondent’s lead counsel on the very subject matter of the dispute.  
          Severity: MEDIUM-HIGH.             Favors a challenge by the Claimant.  
          Recommendation.         Disclosable and should have been disclosed. Standing alone it may not mandate disqualification, but combined with the Lakeview affiliation (Issue 8) and the repeat-player pattern (Issue 7) it meaningfully supports a challenge. Demand supplementation; the arbitrator should disclose the event, the co-panelists, the topic, and any substantive interaction with Mr. Hargrave.  
        Issue 4 — Undisclosed shared organizational service with Garrett T. Whitmore (Respondent’s principal and anticipated witness) at the Minnesota ADR Ethics Institute  
          The fact (source: relationship memorandum §§1.1, III.3, 4.2).         Per IRS Form 990 filings for the Minnesota ADR Ethics Institute (fiscal year 2023),             Garrett T. Whitmore         — Whitmore Capital Partners’ founder and Managing Partner, and an anticipated adverse fact witness in this arbitration — serves on the             Board of Trustees         of the Institute. The Board of Trustees is the Institute’s governing body, responsible for strategic direction, budgetary oversight, and appointments to leadership positions, including the advisory board. The arbitrator serves on the             advisory board         (since 2021). The Institute is described as a small nonprofit; its bylaws and posted minutes indicate that both bodies attend annual meetings and hold periodic joint sessions, increasing the likelihood of direct interaction between the arbitrator and Mr. Whitmore.  
          What the disclosure said (Section VI).         The arbitrator disclosed her own advisory-board service and disclosed that             Ellen Wu         (Barrow & Thistlewood) also serves on the advisory board. The disclosure does             not         mention Garrett T. Whitmore’s service on the Board of Trustees, and does not address whether any party or party principal serves the Institute in any capacity.  
          Obligation implicated.         R-17(a)(ii) (relationships with parties, representatives,             or witnesses    , including         “    board memberships    ”    ); R-17(a)(iv). IBA Orange List 3.3.6 (shared board/professional-association membership). AAA/ABA Code Canon II.  
          Why it matters.         Three features elevate this above ordinary shared-board service. First, Mr. Whitmore is not merely a party principal; he is also an             anticipated witness         (the demand identifies him as an adverse fact witness on the central conduct allegations). Second, the relationship is             hierarchical    , not merely peer service: the Board of Trustees governs the Institute and appoints/oversees the advisory board on which the arbitrator sits, so the Respondent’s principal sits on the body that appoints and can remove the arbitrator from her advisory-board role. Third, the disclosure is             asymmetric    : the arbitrator disclosed the less significant connection (Ellen Wu, a Barrow & Thistlewood partner who is not lead counsel and who serves on the same advisory board as the arbitrator) while omitting the more significant one (the Respondent’s principal and witness, on the governing board). That asymmetry itself creates an appearance of selective disclosure.  
          Severity: MEDIUM-HIGH.             Favors a challenge by the Claimant.  
          Recommendation.         Disclosable and should have been disclosed. Demand supplementation identifying all parties, counsel, witnesses, and their principals who serve the Institute in any capacity, and describing the governance relationship between the Board of Trustees and the advisory board and any interaction between the arbitrator and Mr. Whitmore in that context.  
        Issue 5 — Omission of a pending arbitration (        Halcyon Biotech v. Stonewall Partners    ) in which Barrow & Thistlewood LLP appears as counsel

The arbitrator is currently serving as sole arbitrator in             Halcyon Biotech v. The arbitrator has a             third concurrent pending matter         with the Respondent’s counsel — one worth approximately $175,000 in remaining fees — that she did not disclose, despite certifying her list complete. Whether the omission was inadvertent or deliberate, the result is the same: the parties were deprived of information they were entitled to receive within the objection window.  
          Severity: HIGH (as a disclosure violation).             Favors a challenge by the Claimant.  
          Recommendation.         A clear basis to challenge. The challenging party should (a) cite the specific case number and appointment date, (b) note the contradiction with the completeness certification, and (c) request that the AAA direct a complete, verified supplemental list of all pending matters involving either firm. The omission itself is grounds to question the arbitrator’s diligence and the reliability of her disclosure.

        VI. Aggregate Analysis  
          The standard.         Rule R-16 defines the test as         “    justifiable doubt as to the arbitrator’s impartiality or independence,    ”         intended to         “    encompass all circumstances that a reasonable person would consider relevant in evaluating an arbitrator’s ability to render a fair and unbiased determination.    ”         Rule R-17(d) ties disclosure failure to both removal under R-18 and post-award vacatur under FAA § 10. The AAA/ABA Code and the IBA Guidelines reinforce a broad, err-on-the-side-of-disclosure approach.  
          The pattern is bidirectional, and that matters.         The undisclosed relationships do not all lean one way. Issue 1 (the spouse–Peshkov co-authorship) creates an appearance of potential favorability toward the             Claimant’s         key expert witness. Issues 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and 8 create appearances of potential favorability toward the             Respondent’s         side (an indirect financial stake in a Whitmore fund; professional familiarity with the Respondent’s lead counsel; shared governance with the Respondent’s principal; and economic dependence on the Respondent’s counsel). Under the         “    justifiable doubt    ”         standard, this bidirectional pattern is itself significant: it means a reasonable party on             either         side could question the arbitrator’s ability to render a fair determination, and the arbitrator’s own certification of completeness and impartiality is undermined from both directions. A challenge is therefore not contingent on identifying bias toward one party; the disclosure deficiencies independently support justifiable doubt.  
          The disclosure-completeness failures are the clearest violations.         Issues 5 and 6 are not close calls on the merits of partiality — they are direct contradictions of R-17(a)(iii) and of the arbitrator’s express certification that her list of prior/pending matters is         “    complete.    ”         The arbitrator omitted one pending matter and seven completed matters involving the parties’ counsel. Whatever the reason, the parties were deprived of disclosable information within the seven-day objection window, which is precisely the harm R-17 and R-18 are designed to prevent.  
          The spouse–witness collaboration is the strongest single substantive ground.         Issue 1 combines an undisclosed relationship with a             witness         (expressly within R-17(a)(ii)),             academic collaboration         (expressly within R-17(a)(ii)), a connection to the             exact subject matter         of the dispute, and an affirmative disclosure statement that appears inaccurate given that Dr. Peshkov is a Cascade employee. The arbitrator was on notice of Dr. Peshkov’s witness status from the demand. This ground would likely support disqualification (or at minimum a successful challenge) if the co-authorship is verified.  
          The indirect financial interest is the highest-stakes open question.         Issue 2 is currently         “    potential    ”         because the public record does not confirm the arbitrator’s portfolio holds the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund. But the arbitrator’s own disclosure concedes she has not reviewed her underlying holdings, so she cannot rule out the interest — and her         “    no direct equity positions    ”         framing does not satisfy the             indirect    -interest obligation of R-17(a)(i). This is the issue most likely to support a post-award FAA § 10(a)(2) evident-partiality vacatur if it later emerges that the interest existed and was not disclosed.  
          Aggregate severity.         Taken together, the issues describe an arbitrator who (a) certified a         “    complete    ”         matter list that omitted eight matters involving the parties’ counsel; (b) framed her financial disclosure to address only         “    direct    ”         interests while conceding she had not examined indirect ones, in a case where her own adviser manages a fund with a direct interest in a Respondent-affiliated fund; (c) disclosed a peer’s advisory-board service while omitting the Respondent’s principal’s service on the governing board of the same organization; (d) omitted a co-panelist relationship with the Respondent’s lead counsel on the very subject matter of the dispute; and (e) omitted her spouse’s academic collaboration with the Claimant’s key expert witness on the exact technology in dispute. This is not a case of a single arguable omission; it is a pattern of under-disclosure across every category R-17 enumerates.  
      
        VII. Procedural Recommendation and Next Steps  
             File a challenge on or before June 24, 2024.         The deadline is seven calendar days from the June 17, 2024 disclosure. The challenge must be in writing to the AAA, with a copy to opposing counsel, and must         “    stat[e] with specificity the grounds for the challenge    ”         (R-18(a)). Given that the strongest grounds cut in different directions, each party should evaluate its own challenge independently; the Claimant has the larger cluster of grounds (Issues 2–8), while the Respondent has a strong standalone ground (Issue 1). Either party’s timely challenge preserves the record.  
             Verify the public-record facts before filing.         The co-authorship (Issue 1) should be confirmed against the cited             Journal of Advanced Thermal Engineering         citation; the Whitmore trustee role (Issue 4) against the IRS Form 990; the CLE panel (Issue 3) against the Lakeview/MSBA CLE archives; and the omitted matters (Issues 5–6) against AAA case records. The relationship memorandum is advocacy work product; its factual assertions should be independently corroborated before they are asserted to the AAA.  
             Request that the AAA direct supplemental disclosure (R-17(d)).         Before ruling on any challenge, the AAA may in its discretion         “    bring any disclosure deficiency to the attention of the arbitrator and request supplemental disclosure.    ”         The challenging party should ask the AAA to direct supplementation on each open item: (a) the spouse–Peshkov co-authorship and the scope/timing of that collaboration; (b) whether the arbitrator’s portfolio holds the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund or any vehicle with exposure to any Whitmore fund or portfolio company; (c) a complete, verified list of all prior and pending matters (AAA and other institutions) involving either firm, with outcomes and the arbitrator’s role; (d) all parties, counsel, witnesses, and their principals who serve the Minnesota ADR Ethics Institute in any capacity, and the governance relationship between the Board of Trustees and the advisory board; (e) the October 14, 2023 CLE and any substantive interaction with Mr. Hargrave; and (f) the financial structure of the Lakeview affiliation and the volume of referrals from each firm.  
             Seek withdrawal or disqualification if supplementation confirms any potential interest.         If the arbitrator confirms the indirect fund interest (Issue 2), the spouse–witness collaboration (Issue 1), or materially expands the matter list (Issues 5–6), the challenging party should seek the arbitrator’s voluntary withdrawal or AAA disqualification under R-18(d). Replacement would proceed under R-19.  
             Preserve the FAA § 10(a)(2) record.         Even if the AAA declines to disqualify, the disclosure deficiencies should be documented on the record for any later motion to vacate on evident-partiality grounds. R-18(f) preserves a party’s right to seek vacatur for issues         “    not previously known and subject to waiver,    ”         and the indirect-interest and spouse–witness issues in particular are the kind of undisclosed interests that support evident-partiality vacatur under             Commonwealth Coatings Corp. v. Continental Casualty Co.    , 393 U.S. 145 (1968), and its progeny, which require disclosure of even modest relationships that might create an impression of partiality.  
             Do not waive by inaction.         A party that does not challenge by June 24, 2024 is deemed to have waived grounds then known to it (R-18(f)). The cost of a timely challenge is low; the cost of waiver — combined with the difficulty of unwinding an award after a 15-day evidentiary hearing — is high. If there is any doubt, file.  
      
        VIII. Conclusion  
      The arbitrator’s June 17, 2024 disclosure statement is materially incomplete when measured against the source documents. Eight distinct concerns are identified, five of them outright non-disclosures and two of them direct contradictions of the arbitrator’s certification that her list of prior and pending matters is         “    complete.    ”         The non-disclosures implicate each enumerated category of AAA Rule R-17(a): an indirect financial interest in an entity affected by the result (R-17(a)(i)); professional and family relationships with counsel and witnesses, including academic collaboration and board membership (R-17(a)(ii)); prior and pending cases with the parties’ counsel (R-17(a)(iii)); and public statements/views on germane issues (R-17(a)(iv)). The strongest single substantive ground is the arbitrator’s spouse’s undisclosed co-authorship with the Claimant’s key expert witness on the precise subject matter of the dispute (Issue 1); the clearest procedural violations are the omissions of one pending and seven completed matters involving the parties’ counsel (Issues 5 and 6); and the highest-stakes open question is the potential indirect financial interest in a Whitmore-affiliated fund (Issue 2). Because the undisclosed relationships create justifiable doubt from the perspective of both the Claimant and the Respondent, a timely challenge is warranted for whichever party elects to pursue it. The challenge deadline is             June 24, 2024    .

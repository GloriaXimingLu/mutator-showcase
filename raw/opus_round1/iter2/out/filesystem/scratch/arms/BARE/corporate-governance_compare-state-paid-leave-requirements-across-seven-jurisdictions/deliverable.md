       1. Executive Summary  
      Hartwell & Cabot LLP (    “    H&C    ”    ) has completed a comprehensive review of Vantage Logistics Group, Inc.’s (    “    Vantage    ”         or the         “    Company    ”    ) paid leave policies and practices as set forth in the Employee Handbook (Rev. January 15, 2022) and as implemented in practice, measured against the statutory and regulatory requirements of all seven jurisdictions in which the Company operates or plans to operate: New Jersey, New York, Connecticut, Massachusetts, Colorado, Oregon, and Washington. The detailed state-by-state comparison appears in the accompanying Matrix, which employs color-coded gap indicators (red / yellow / green) across five categories: Paid Sick Leave, Paid Family Leave, Paid Medical Leave, Employer Obligations, and PTO/Vacation Payout on Separation.  
      The review identifies a pattern of systemic, company-wide non-compliance that predates and is independent of the planned expansion. The Company’s leave policies were drafted in January 2022 primarily around New Jersey and New York requirements, with only informal attention to Connecticut, and have not been substantively updated since. The policies do not reflect the current law of any of the seven jurisdictions in several material respects. The most serious deficiencies are not marginal or technical; they are structural and affect the entire workforce.  
          Five findings are rated Critical.         First, the paid sick leave policy contains a family-member definition and a set of covered uses that are narrower than the law of every jurisdiction in which the Company operates — the precise defects that gave rise to the pending New Jersey Department of Labor complaint (        Carla Muñoz v. Vantage Logistics Group, Inc.    , Case No. NJ-ESL-2025-04117). Second, the Company’s self-funded paid family leave benefit (50% of average weekly wage, capped at $850/week, for up to eight weeks) falls below the statutory minimum of all seven states on rate, weekly cap, and duration, omits qualifying military exigency in every state, omits the employee’s own medical condition in five states, and has never been approved as a private-plan substitute in any jurisdiction; in Colorado, no private-employer opt-out exists at all. Third and fourth, the Company has not registered with the state paid leave programs in any of the four expansion states, with Massachusetts on the critical path for its August 1, 2025 opening and Colorado’s obligation likely already triggered by three remote employees hired in April 2025. Fifth, the Company is withholding employee contributions for New Jersey TDI, New York DBL, and Connecticut Paid Leave but is directing employees who experience their own serious health conditions to a voluntary, employee-paid short-term disability plan instead of the state programs they are funding — creating exposure to retroactive claims across the existing 2,140-employee footprint.  
          Six findings are rated High         and             five are rated Moderate.         The High findings include a sick-leave accrual rate (1 hour per 40 hours worked) that is too slow for five of seven states; a uniform 120-day waiting period that is unlawful in five states; a no-carryover rule that is non-compliant in all seven states; a no-PTO-payout-on-separation rule that violates the wage laws of five states (with treble-damages exposure in Massachusetts); a paid family leave notice policy that fails to address unforeseeable leave; and a manual spreadsheet leave-tracking system that is not scalable to seven jurisdictions and has already produced documented errors.  
      The good news is that all of these deficiencies are remediable, and the Company has allocated a $175,000 compliance remediation budget and targeted July 1, 2025 for issuance of a revised Employee Handbook. The challenge is sequencing: the Massachusetts facility opens August 1, 2025 — only one month after the handbook target date — and the registration and payroll-configuration work for Massachusetts must begin immediately. Section 8 sets out a prioritized remediation plan with specific action items, owners, and deadlines tied to the facility-opening schedule.

The following are expressly outside scope and are not addressed here: representation in the pending NJ DOL complaint (handled under the separate February 15, 2025 engagement); federal FMLA compliance; local/municipal paid leave ordinances; drafting of the revised Handbook; and negotiation of vendor agreements.

H&C also relied on the Company’s representations regarding headcount, payroll, the self-funded benefit structure, the HRIS/payroll/time-attendance technology stack, and the expansion timeline.  
        2.3 Methodology and Severity Framework  
      For each jurisdiction and each dimension, H&C compared the Company’s written policy and documented practice against the controlling statute, regulations, and agency guidance in effect as of May 19, 2025, and assigned a color-coded indicator in the Matrix:             red         (non-compliant),             yellow         (partially compliant or at risk), and             green         (compliant). Each material deficiency is then categorized by severity in this memorandum using the framework prescribed by Engagement § 2.2:  
              Critical         — active legal exposure exists, an imminent regulatory deadline is at risk, or the deficiency has already resulted in, or is likely to imminently result in, regulatory action, penalties, or litigation.  
              High         — non-compliance is likely to result in enforcement action, monetary penalties, or employee claims if not remediated before operations commence in the applicable jurisdiction or within the near term.  
              Moderate         — technical non-compliance, best-practice gaps, or policy ambiguities that may not give rise to immediate enforcement but expose the Company to risk over time or in the event of a complaint or audit.  
      State paid leave is a rapidly evolving area. Statutory contribution rates, wage bases, and benefit caps are adjusted annually. The 2025 figures cited herein and in the Matrix should be re-verified against current agency postings before implementation.

        4. Critical Findings  
       4.1 Critical Finding C-1 — Systemic Paid Sick Leave Policy Deficiencies (All Seven States); Active NJ DOL Complaint  
          Affected jurisdictions:         All seven (NJ, NY, CT, MA, CO, OR, WA).  
          The deficiency.         The Company’s paid sick leave policy (Handbook § 6.2) contains multiple provisions that are non-compliant in every jurisdiction:  
              Family-member definition.         The policy limits covered family members to         “    spouse, child, or parent.    ”         Every state’s sick leave law requires a materially broader definition — including domestic partners, children of domestic partners, grandparents, grandchildren, siblings, other blood relatives, and individuals whose close association is the equivalent of family. This is the precise defect alleged in the Muñoz complaint: Ms. Muñoz was denied sick leave to care for her domestic partner’s child, a relationship expressly covered by N.J.S.A. 34:11D-1.  
              Covered uses.         The policy permits sick leave only for the employee’s own illness, injury, or medical appointment. It omits care for a family member, preventive care, absences related to domestic or sexual violence (    “    safe leave    ”    ), public-health-emergency closures, and school-related conferences/functions — all of which are required uses under the sick leave laws of all seven states.  
              Accrual rate.         The policy accrues sick leave at 1 hour per 40 hours worked. New Jersey, New York, Massachusetts, Colorado, and Oregon all require 1 hour per 30 hours worked. (Connecticut and Washington require 1 hour per 40 hours.) The Company is therefore under-accruing sick leave for employees in five of seven states.  
              Part-time cap.         The policy caps part-time employees at 24 hours per year. No state distinguishes between full-time and part-time employees for sick leave accrual/cap purposes; all require 40 hours (or 48 in Colorado, or no cap in Washington). The 24-hour part-time cap is non-compliant everywhere.  
              No carryover.         The policy resets balances to zero on January 1. All seven states require carryover of unused sick leave (40 hours in most states; 48 in Colorado; all unused in Washington).  
              120-day waiting period.         The policy imposes a uniform 120-day waiting period before use. This is permissible in New Jersey but non-compliant in New York (no waiting period), Massachusetts, Oregon, and Washington (90 days), and Colorado (no waiting period).  
          Risk.         This finding is Critical because the family-member and covered-use defects have already resulted in an active, pending enforcement matter (Case No. NJ-ESL-2025-04117). The NJ DOL expressly reserved the right to         “    conduct a broader investigation of the Employer’s sick leave practices, policies, and records to determine whether additional violations… have occurred.    ”         Because the same defective policy language applies company-wide, the exposure is not limited to Ms. Muñoz or to New Jersey; it extends to every employee in every state. The accrual-rate, part-time-cap, no-carryover, and waiting-period defects mean the Company is systematically providing less sick leave than the law requires, which is the kind of systemic, records-based violation a state agency can quantify across the workforce and assess penalties and restitution for. Under N.J.S.A. 34:11D-7 and analogous provisions in other states, violations can yield fines, administrative penalties, and orders of restitution, plus (in several states) private rights of action and attorney’s fees.  
          Context.         The Company’s February 21, 2025 response to the NJ DOL acknowledged that the policy         “    may not fully reflect    ”         the Act’s requirements and committed to a review by July 1, 2025. That commitment is appropriate but does not eliminate exposure for the period during which the non-compliant policy remains in effect. The internal email thread (March 2025) confirms that HR staff have been applying the written policy as drafted, including denying sick leave to employees within the 120-day waiting period (e.g., the Newark employee denied on March 18, 2025 and the Queens employee denied on January 28, 2025). The leave-tracking spreadsheet itself flags that         “    NJ law requires 40 hrs for all employees,    ”         indicating the HR team is aware of at least part of the problem.

First, employees who take family leave are receiving materially less than the law entitles them to — a benefits shortfall that is quantifiable per employee and, in the aggregate, substantial. Second, the Company’s Handbook represents to employees that the self-funded benefit         “    meets or exceeds the requirements of applicable state paid family leave programs    ”         and is         “    intended to satisfy any applicable state mandate.    ”         That representation is incorrect. H&C recommends (a) for Colorado (mandatory) and as the default for the expansion states, and a state-by-state cost/benefit analysis of (a) versus (b) for New Jersey, New York, and Connecticut. This decision should be made before the revised Handbook is issued on July 1, 2025, because the Handbook’s description of the benefit must be accurate.  
        4.3 Critical Finding C-3 — Massachusetts PFML Registration (Critical Path)  
          Affected jurisdiction:         Massachusetts.  
          The deficiency.         As of the date of this memorandum, the Company has not registered with the Massachusetts Paid Family and Medical Leave (MA PFML) program administered by the Department of Family and Medical Leave (DFML). MA PFML requires employer registration and contribution remittance from the first day any covered employee works in Massachusetts. The Worcester facility is scheduled to open August 1, 2025 with 130 projected employees.  
          Risk.         This finding is Critical on a pure deadline basis. PinnacleHR has confirmed (per Priya Samuelson, April 10, 2025) that it cannot configure withholding until it receives the Company’s registration confirmation, and that its configuration lead time is six to eight weeks. Working backward from August 1, 2025, registration must be completed no later than approximately             June 1, 2025         — roughly two weeks from the date of this memorandum. Any slippage cascades directly into a failure to withhold and remit MA PFML contributions on the facility’s opening day, which is itself a violation. MA PFML also covers the employee’s own serious health condition (up to 20 weeks), so non-registration means Massachusetts employees will have no access to state medical-leave benefits. Separately, Massachusetts requires payout of accrued unused vacation at separation under the Wage Act (see H-4), and its sick-time law requires a 90-day use threshold (see H-2) — both of which the current Handbook violates.  
          Context.         The HR Expansion Plan Memorandum flagged this as a         “    critical path    ”         item on March 28, 2025. The window has since narrowed. H&C provided (or will provide) an early-action notification on Massachusetts registration in advance of the interim status update, as contemplated by Engagement § 5.  
        4.4 Critical Finding C-4 — Colorado FAMLI Registration (Obligation Likely Already Triggered)  
          Affected jurisdiction:         Colorado.  
          The deficiency.         The Company has not registered with the Colorado Family and Medical Leave Insurance (FAMLI) program. FAMLI is mandatory for employers with 10 or more employees and does not permit private-employer opt-out. Critically, three Colorado-based employees — a regional operations manager and two logistics coordinators — were hired to work remotely from the Aurora/Denver area in April 2025, approximately seven months before the Aurora facility opens on November 1, 2025.  
          Risk.         This finding is Critical because the registration obligation is most defensibly tied to the first date an employee performs work in Colorado, not to the opening of a physical facility. On that reading, the Company’s FAMLI registration obligation was triggered in April 2025 and may already be overdue. Even under the more favorable reading that the obligation attaches to the November 1, 2025 facility opening, the six-to-eight-week PinnacleHR lead time requires registration no later than approximately mid-August 2025. The Company cannot self-fund in lieu of FAMLI, so registration and participation are unavoidable. Colorado also requires payout of all accrued unused vacation at separation (see H-4) and paid sick leave of 48 hours per year with no waiting period (see H-2) — both violated by the current Handbook. The three early hires have already raised PTO-payout questions (see the March 2025 email thread), and the Company’s offer-letter language must not state a blanket         “    no payout    ”         rule for Colorado.  
          Context.         The HR Expansion Plan Memorandum labeled Colorado         “    the single most urgent registration item.    ”         H&C concurs and recommends treating the April 2025 hires as the trigger date unless and until a contrary position is validated.  
        4.5 Critical Finding C-5 — Paid Medical Leave Gap and State-Program Misdirection (NJ, NY, CT)  
          Affected jurisdictions:         New Jersey, New York, Connecticut (current footprint); the same gap is unaddressed in MA, CO, OR, WA (see C-3/C-4 and the Matrix).  
          The deficiency.         The Company provides no employer-funded paid medical leave for an employee’s own serious health condition beyond accrued sick leave (capped at 40 hours) and a voluntary, employee-paid short-term disability plan through Keystone Benefit Administrators (in which fewer than 30% of Connecticut hourly staff are enrolled). Meanwhile, the Company is withholding employee contributions for three state programs that cover the employee’s own condition: New Jersey Temporary Disability Insurance (NJ TDI, up to 26 weeks at 85% of AWW), New York Disability Benefits Law (NY DBL, up to 26 weeks at $170/week), and Connecticut Paid Leave (CT PL, up to 12 weeks at the 95%/60% tiered rate). According to the March 2025 email thread, HR staff at multiple sites have been directing employees who experience their own serious health conditions to the voluntary, employee-paid Keystone plan rather than to the state programs those employees are funding. At least seven such inquiries were identified across Connecticut, New Jersey, and New York in the prior twelve months (four in Bridgeport, two in Edison, one in Queens), and the HR Director confirmed that NJ HR staff         “    don’t even know that NJ TDI exists as a separate benefit.    ”  
          Risk.         This finding is Critical because it combines a benefits gap with an active misdirection practice across the existing 2,140-employee footprint. Employees are paying into state programs (via PinnacleHR withholdings) but are being steered away from claiming the benefits those programs provide. The Muñoz complaint is a concrete reminder that Vantage employees are willing to escalate to state agencies when they believe they have not received what they are entitled to. Employees who were misdirected to Keystone may have grounds for retroactive claims against the state programs, and the state agencies may treat the pattern as a systemic practice rather than isolated errors. The exposure scales with headcount and tenure. The General Counsel has already directed (March 17, 2025) that HR staff         “    not turn away employees who ask about paid medical leave for their own conditions without at least checking whether a state program applies,    ”         and has requested a full list of inquiries going back twelve months; that list should be compiled and provided to H&C.  
          Context.         The self-funded plan was, per the General Counsel,         “    supposed to supplement, not replace, the state programs,    ”         but it is no longer clear that is how it was implemented. H&C’s review confirms the structural ambiguity: the self-funded plan covers only family leave, not the employee’s own condition, so there is no employer-funded medical-leave benefit at all. The state programs (NJ TDI, NY DBL, CT PL) are the employees’ primary entitlement for their own conditions and must be administered as such.  
         5. High Findings  
       5.1 High Finding H-1 — Sick Leave Accrual Rate (1:40) Non-Compliant in Five States  
          Affected jurisdictions:         NJ, NY, MA, CO, OR (compliant in CT and WA).  
      The policy accrues 1 hour per 40 hours worked. New Jersey, New York, Massachusetts, Colorado, and Oregon all require 1 hour per 30 hours worked. The Company is therefore under-accruing sick leave for the majority of its workforce. This is a quantifiable, records-based violation: for a full-time employee working 2,080 hours per year, the Company accrues 52 hours (before the 40-hour cap) versus the 69.3 hours the law requires in five states. Remediation requires changing the accrual rate to 1:30 in those states (or adopting a uniform 1:30 company-wide, which would also exceed the 1:40 minimums in CT and WA). This must be reflected in the revised Handbook and configured in whatever leave-tracking system is in place by the time each state’s employees are onboarded.  
        5.2 High Finding H-2 — 120-Day Waiting Period Non-Compliant in Five States  
          Affected jurisdictions:         NY, MA, CO, OR, WA (compliant in NJ; at risk in CT).  
      The uniform 120-day waiting period is permissible in New Jersey but unlawful in New York (no waiting period), Massachusetts, Oregon, and Washington (90 days each), and Colorado (no waiting period). The leave-tracking spreadsheet documents at least two employees denied sick leave within the waiting period in early 2025 (Newark, March 18; Queens, January 28). In Connecticut, the law uses an hours-based threshold (680 hours) rather than calendar days, so the 120-day rule is misaligned for part-time employees (see M-3). Remediation requires reducing the waiting period to 90 days (or eliminating it) in the non-compliant states. The interim guidance issued by the General Counsel (do not deny leave for unforeseeable situations) does not cure the waiting-period defect for ordinary sick leave use.  
        5.3 High Finding H-3 — No Carryover Non-Compliant in All Seven States  
          Affected jurisdictions:         All seven.  
      The policy resets sick leave balances to zero on January 1. Every state requires carryover of unused sick leave (40 hours in NJ, NY, CT, MA, OR; 48 hours in CO; all unused in WA). The leave-tracking spreadsheet documents multiple employees who forfeited unused balances at year-end 2024 (e.g., 12 hours, 18 hours, 14 hours, 8 hours). Forfeiture of accrued sick leave is a records-based violation in every jurisdiction. Remediation requires implementing carryover (or frontloading) consistent with each state’s requirement.  
        5.4 High Finding H-4 — PTO/Vacation Payout on Separation Non-Compliant in Five States  
          Affected jurisdictions:         NY, MA, CO, OR, WA (compliant in CT; at risk in NJ).  
      The Handbook provides that unused PTO is forfeited on separation except in Connecticut. This is non-compliant in five states that treat accrued vacation/PTO as wages that must be paid at termination: New York (NY Labor Law), Massachusetts (Wage Act, M.G.L. c. 149 § 148), Colorado (C.R.S. § 8-4-101 et seq.), Oregon (ORS 652.200), and Washington (RCW 49.48.010). Massachusetts is especially hazardous because the Wage Act imposes             treble damages         and mandatory attorney’s fees for violations, and the Massachusetts facility opens August 1, 2025 — meaning the first Massachusetts separations could generate treble-damages exposure almost immediately if the policy is not fixed first. Colorado is also acute because the three early hires have already raised the issue and Colorado prohibits vacation-forfeiture agreements as to wages already earned. New Jersey is rated at risk (yellow) rather than red because New Jersey permits a clearly communicated         “    use-it-or-lose-it    ”         policy, but the Company’s bi-weekly accrual vests PTO as earned, so the no-payout rule is vulnerable. The five-day carryover cap and March 31 forfeiture deadline (see M-4) compound the wage-protection risk in CO, MA, OR, and WA. Remediation requires paying out accrued unused PTO at separation in NY, MA, CO, OR, and WA, and revising the carryover/forfeiture provisions.  
        5.5 High Finding H-5 — Paid Family Leave Notice Policy Fails to Address Unforeseeable Leave  
          Affected jurisdictions:         All seven.  
      The Handbook requires 30 calendar days’ advance notice for paid family leave but is silent on unforeseeable leave. Every state’s program (and the FMLA) requires only         “    as soon as practicable    ”         notice when the need for leave is unforeseeable. The March 2025 email thread documents a Connecticut employee who gave 10 days’ notice for a parent’s unexpected hospitalization and was initially pushed back under the 30-day rule; HR approved the leave only after the employee objected. The General Counsel has issued interim guidance not to deny unforeseeable leave, but the written policy remains non-compliant and creates inconsistency risk: the NJ and NY HR leads acknowledged they have been         “    making judgment calls without a framework,    ”         which itself raises disparate-treatment exposure. Remediation requires rewriting the notice provision to distinguish foreseeable (30 days) from unforeseeable (as soon as practicable) leave, state by state.  
        5.6 High Finding H-6 — Manual Spreadsheet Leave Tracking Is Not Scalable and Creates Recordkeeping Risk  
          Affected jurisdictions:         All seven.  
      The Company tracks sick leave accruals, usage, and balances in manual Excel workbooks maintained by two HR coordinators, one workbook per state. The HR Expansion Plan Memorandum and the sample spreadsheet identify six specific deficiencies: no state-specific rule differentiation; no automated alerts for caps, waiting-period eligibility, or carryover deadlines; no audit trail for corrections; no integration with WorkBridge Pro (HRIS) or ClockSync Enterprise (time and attendance); limited scope (sick leave only — paid family leave is tracked on paper, PTO in the HRIS); and a projected 50–75% workload increase to scale from three to seven states. The sample spreadsheet contains documented errors, including a negative balance (James Kowalski, −2.5 hours), a part-time employee capped at 24 hours in New Jersey where 40 hours is required (Marcus Pham), and inconsistent application of the 120-day waiting period. Several states impose specific recordkeeping-retention obligations (e.g., New Jersey requires retention of sick leave records for at least five years), and the absence of an audit trail means the Company cannot reliably demonstrate how any given balance was calculated in the event of an audit or complaint — a direct vulnerability given the pending NJ DOL matter. Remediation requires a dedicated leave-management system configured with state-specific rules and integrated with the HRIS and time-and-attendance systems (see Section 8).  
         6. Moderate Findings  
       6.1 Moderate Finding M-1 — Medical Documentation Threshold (All Seven States)  
      The policy requires a doctor’s note after two consecutive scheduled workdays. Most states permit an employer to require documentation only after three or more consecutive days. Requiring documentation earlier than the statute contemplates is not expressly prohibited in every state, but it can be challenged as an unreasonable burden that discourages or interferes with the lawful use of sick leave, particularly in states with anti-interference provisions. H&C recommends aligning the documentation threshold to three consecutive days (or the state-specific threshold) to eliminate this risk.  
        6.2 Moderate Finding M-2 — New Jersey PTO Payout Nuance  
      New Jersey treats earned vacation as wages but permits a         “    use-it-or-lose-it    ”         policy if it is clearly communicated in writing. The Company’s no-payout rule is therefore potentially enforceable in New Jersey, but the risk is non-trivial because the Company accrues PTO bi-weekly (vesting it as earned) and the policy’s forfeiture-on-separation language could be challenged. H&C recommends clarifying the policy to expressly state the vesting/forfeiture mechanism and confirming it is communicated in writing at hire, or — more conservatively — paying out accrued PTO at separation in New Jersey as well.  
        6.3 Moderate Finding M-3 — Connecticut Sick Leave Waiting Period Misalignment  
      Connecticut’s sick leave law uses an hours-based use threshold (680 hours of employment) rather than a calendar-day waiting period. The Company’s 120-day rule is roughly equivalent for full-time employees but misaligned for part-time employees, for whom 120 calendar days elapses at far fewer than 680 work hours. The practical effect is ambiguous (the Company’s rule may be more generous for some part-time employees and less generous for others), but the misalignment creates inconsistency and audit risk. H&C recommends adopting the state’s hours-based threshold for Connecticut, or a uniform 90-day threshold that is clearly more generous than 680 hours for all classifications.  
        6.4 Moderate Finding M-4 — PTO Carryover Cap and Year-End Forfeiture (Wage-Protection States)  
      The policy caps PTO carryover at five days and forfeits any excess on December 31, with carryover PTO forfeited if unused by March 31. In the wage-protection states (CO, MA, OR, WA, and to a lesser extent NY and NJ), year-end forfeiture of accrued PTO and a tight carryover cap can be scrutinized as a mechanism to minimize the separation-payout obligation. Colorado is the clearest case: its statute prohibits forfeiture of earned vacation. Remediation requires revising the carryover/forfeiture provisions to preserve earned PTO through separation in the wage-protection states.  
        6.5 Moderate Finding M-5 — Workplace Posting and Individual Employee Notice Obligations (All Seven States)  
      Every state’s sick leave and paid family/medical leave law requires the employer to display an official workplace poster and provide a written notice to each employee at hire. The Company relies on the Handbook alone and has not posted the state-specific notices or provided the hire-time notices. These are technical, citable violations that state agencies routinely check first in an audit or complaint investigation. Remediation requires obtaining and posting the current official poster for each state and incorporating the required written notices into the onboarding packet for each jurisdiction.  
         7. Cross-Cutting Observations  
          The                 “        uniform policy        ”                 approach is the root cause.         Most of the deficiencies above arise from a single design choice: applying one company-wide policy across jurisdictions with materially different requirements. A uniform policy can be compliant only if it is set at the level of the most protective state on every dimension. The current policy is instead set near the least protective level on most dimensions (1:40 accrual, 24-hour part-time cap, no carryover, 120-day waiting period, narrow family-member definition, narrow covered uses, no PTO payout). The revised Handbook should either (a) adopt a single         “    highest-common-denominator    ”         policy that satisfies the most protective state on each dimension, or (b) adopt state-specific addenda keyed to each jurisdiction. H&C recommends approach (b) for the dimensions where states diverge most (accrual rate, caps, waiting period, carryover, PTO payout, family-member definition) and approach (a) where a uniform generous rule is simpler (e.g., covered uses, documentation threshold).  
          The self-funded plan decision gates the Handbook.         The Company cannot accurately describe its paid family leave benefit in the revised Handbook until it decides, state by state, whether to participate in the state programs, seek private-plan approval, or discontinue the self-funded plan (see C-2). That decision should be made immediately so the Handbook can be drafted accurately by July 1, 2025.  
          The pending NJ DOL complaint should inform, and be informed by, this review.         The compliance review findings support the Company’s February 21, 2025 representation to the NJ DOL that it is undertaking a good-faith, comprehensive remediation by July 1, 2025. Conversely, the resolution strategy for the Muñoz matter should be calibrated to the scope of the systemic defects: a narrow settlement that addresses only Ms. Muñoz’s individual claim will not resolve the broader exposure, and the NJ DOL has expressly reserved the right to investigate company-wide. H&C is available to advise on resolution posture under the separate engagement, but the remediation timeline in Section 8 is designed to support a credible         “    good-faith remediation    ”         position in that proceeding.

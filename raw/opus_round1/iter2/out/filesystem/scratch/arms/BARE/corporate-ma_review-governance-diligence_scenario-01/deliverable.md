       I. Executive Summary  
      This memorandum summarizes our review of the governance documents of Terravox Systems, Inc. (    “    Terravox    ”         or the         “    Company    ”    ) in connection with the proposed acquisition of 100% of the Company’s outstanding equity by Hargrove Capital Partners III, L.P. (    “    Hargrove    ”         or         “    Buyer    ”    ), structured as a reverse triangular merger at an enterprise value of $485 million (the         “    Transaction    ”    ). The Transaction is being documented by Whitfield & Crane LLP as counsel to Hargrove; the Company is represented by Avery, Pollard & Glenn LLP (partner: Nathaniel Rourke). This memo is delivered to inform deal structuring and merger agreement negotiation and is subject to the privilege and confidentiality protections noted above.  
          The central finding of this diligence is that Stonemill Capital Partners Fund VI, L.P. (        “        Stonemill        ”        ) holds a hard, charter-level blocking right over the Transaction that cannot be circumvented cleanly through the contractual drag-along mechanism.         Stonemill holds 100% of the outstanding Series C Preferred Stock (7,800,000 shares). The Restated Certificate of Incorporation requires the affirmative consent of holders of at least 66⅔% of the Series C Preferred Stock, voting as a             separate class    , to effect any         “    Liquidation Event    ”         — and the reverse triangular merger squarely qualifies as a Liquidation Event. Because no other stockholder holds any Series C, this separate-class consent cannot be obtained without Stonemill. This is the single most important governance fact in the deal.  
      Our principal conclusions are as follows:  
             Stonemill has a hard veto.         The Series C separate-class protective provision (Restated Certificate §4.3.6(b)(i)) gives Stonemill an absolute blocking right over the Transaction. The companion all-Preferred protective provision (§4.3.6(a)(vi))             can         be satisfied without Stonemill (Ridgeline + Northbridge = 8,700,000 shares, exceeding the 8,250,000-share threshold), but the Series C separate-class vote cannot.             (Critical — Section 1.)  
             The drag-along is a contingent, legally uncertain fallback — not a clean override.         The Stockholders’ Agreement drag-along can be triggered only if all three prongs of Section 3.1(a) are met. Prongs (ii) (majority of Preferred, as-converted) and (iii) (at least two of three Investor Stockholders) can be satisfied without Stonemill. Prong (i) (majority of Common, as-converted) is mathematically uncertain and depends on the composition of the option/other-common pool and the treatment of unexercised options. Even if triggered, whether a contractual drag-along can compel Stonemill to vote its Series C in favor             in the face of a charter separate-class protective provision         is a contested question of Delaware law. The drag-along preserves the charter waterfall (so Stonemill would receive its full $18.50/share liquidation preference even if dragged), meaning the drag-along strips Stonemill of             leverage    , not             economics    .             (Critical — Section 2.)  
             At the current price, Stonemill receives only its 1x liquidation preference — which explains its posture.         The $473 million equity value is insufficient for Series C conversion to be economic ($13.95/share as-converted vs. $18.50/share preference). Stonemill will take its $144.3 million preference (1.0x its invested capital) and receives no premium. This is the economic engine of the client’s concern: Stonemill has every incentive to withhold consent to extract a side payment, a higher allocation, or post-closing management rights. Notably, the September 25, 2024 board minutes record an analysis suggesting all Preferred would convert — that analysis is incorrect as to Series C and should not be relied upon.             (Critical — Sections 1, 2, 9.)  
             A potential live exclusivity obligation to SensorLink Technologies, Inc. may conflict with signing the Hargrove merger agreement.         The SensorLink LOI contains an automatic-renewal exclusivity provision, and the September 25, 2024 minutes reflect uncertainty as to whether a termination notice was sent before the September 8, 2024 expiration. If the exclusivity auto-renewed, Terravox may be contractually barred from signing a definitive agreement with Hargrove. This is time-sensitive and must be resolved immediately.             (Critical — Section 8.)  
             The $12 million Clearfield National Bank term loan appears to have been authorized without the required Preferred Stockholder consent.         The Restated Certificate (§4.3.6(a)(v)) requires majority-of-Preferred consent for borrowed-money indebtedness exceeding $5 million. The October 11, 2023 board minutes reflect Board approval but no Preferred consent. This is a potential protective-provision violation that Stonemill can exploit as leverage.             (Critical — Section 8.)  
             The Special Committee is not fully independent, creating fiduciary-duty exposure.         Committee member Sandra Okafor is Northbridge’s designee and a Principal at Northbridge, which holds Series B Preferred with a material liquidation preference. Her presence compromises the committee’s independence and may defeat reliance on the             MFW         framework, exposing the Transaction to entire-fairness review. Julian Trask’s absence from and objection to the April 17, 2024 meeting at which Hargrove engagement was authorized is an additional process vulnerability.             (Significant — Section 9.)  
             The Registration Rights Agreement may not cleanly terminate at closing.         The RRA terminates upon         “    a Deemed Liquidation Event,    ”         but that term is             undefined         in the RRA and is not cross-referenced to the charter’s         “    Liquidation Event.    ”         The merger agreement should include an express termination/waiver of the RRA at closing.             (Significant — Sections 6, 10.)  
             We have not received the 2021 Equity Incentive Plan or any award agreements.         We therefore cannot assess change-of-control acceleration, the single-/double-trigger mechanics, or the dollar impact on deal economics. Notably, employee options appear to be out of the money at the deal price (409A value $14.30 vs. ~$12.59–$13.95 per Common share), and the July 2023 share-reserve increase (8.0M → 9.5M) shows only Board approval in the minutes.             (Significant — Section 7.)  
          Bottom line for the client:         There is no clean, certain path to closing over Stonemill’s objection. The Series C separate-class veto is a hard block as a matter of charter law. The drag-along is a theoretically available but legally contested and mathematically contingent lever that, even if successful, would likely require litigation and would not improve Hargrove’s economics (Stonemill keeps its full preference). The pragmatic path is to negotiate Stonemill’s consent while (a) preserving the drag-along as negotiating leverage, (b) cleaning up the SensorLink exclusivity and the term-loan consent issues, (c) reconstituting the Special Committee, and (d) building express RRA termination into the merger agreement. The SensorLink and term-loan items should be escalated immediately and should not await further diligence.

        1. Change-of-Control Consent Requirements  
       1.1 Charter protective provisions  
      The Restated Certificate contains two layers of protective provisions that apply to a         “    Liquidation Event,    ”         both of which are triggered by the Transaction.  
          Definition of                 “        Liquidation Event        ”                 (Charter §4.3.2(d)).         A         “    Liquidation Event    ”         includes:  
        
          any merger, consolidation, or other business combination of the Company with or into another entity (whether or not the Company is the surviving entity),             other than         one in which the Company’s pre-transaction stockholders continue to hold at least 50% of the voting power of the surviving entity;             or  
        
          a sale, lease, transfer, exclusive license, or other disposition of all or substantially all assets;             or  
        
          any transaction or series of related transactions resulting in the transfer or issuance of equity representing more than 50% of the voting power (other than a capital-raising equity financing);             or  
        
          any voluntary or involuntary liquidation, dissolution, or winding up.  
      A Liquidation Event is expressly         “    treated as a liquidation, dissolution, or winding up of the Corporation for all purposes of this Section 4.3.2 and the protective provisions set forth in Section 4.3.6    ”         (§4.3.2(d), final paragraph). The Company must give Preferred holders at least 20 days’ written notice of any proposed Liquidation Event (§4.3.2(d), notice clause).  
          Application to the Transaction.         The reverse triangular merger with Hargrove results in Terravox’s pre-transaction stockholders holding 0% of the surviving entity’s voting power (they are cashed out). The Transaction therefore falls squarely within §4.3.2(d)(i) and is a         “    Liquidation Event.    ”         The 20-day pre-closing notice obligation to Preferred holders must be observed.  
          (a) All-Preferred protective provision (Charter §4.3.6(a)(vi)).         So long as any Preferred Stock is outstanding, the Company may not         “    effect any Liquidation Event    ”         without the prior written consent or affirmative vote of holders of at least a             majority (greater than 50%)         of the then-outstanding Preferred Stock, voting together as a             single class         on an as-converted basis. The Charter specifies that this requires the consent of holders of             more than 8,250,000 shares         of Preferred (based on 16,500,000 shares outstanding).  
              Can this be satisfied without Stonemill?             Yes.         Ridgeline (3,500,000) + Northbridge (5,200,000) = 8,700,000 shares, which exceeds 8,250,000. The margin is thin (only ~450,000 shares), so the vote of             both         Ridgeline and Northbridge is required; the loss of either defeats this threshold.  
          (b) Series C separate-class protective provision (Charter §4.3.6(b)(i)).         So long as any Series C Preferred is outstanding, the Company may not         “    effect any Liquidation Event    ”         without the prior written consent or affirmative vote of holders of at least             66⅔%         of the then-outstanding Series C Preferred, voting as a             separate class    . The Charter specifies that this requires the consent of holders of             at least 5,200,000 shares         of Series C (based on 7,800,000 shares outstanding).  
              Can this be satisfied without Stonemill?             No.         Stonemill holds 7,800,000 shares — i.e.,             100% of the outstanding Series C Preferred Stock    . No other stockholder holds any Series C. The 66⅔% separate-class threshold (5,200,000 shares) therefore             cannot be met without Stonemill’s affirmative consent    . This is a             hard, charter-level blocking right    .  
      The Charter expressly states that the §4.3.6(b) consent is         “    separate from and in addition to    ”         the §4.3.6(a) consent, and that         “    both consents must be independently obtained    ”         (§4.3.6(b), penultimate paragraph). Accordingly,             both         the all-Preferred vote             and         the Series C separate-class vote are required to effect the Transaction. The all-Preferred vote is achievable without Stonemill; the Series C separate-class vote is not.  
        1.2 DGCL requirements  
      Under DGCL §251, a merger requires (i) approval of the merger agreement by the board of directors, and (ii) approval by the affirmative vote of the holders of a majority of the outstanding stock entitled to vote (unless a higher vote is required by the Charter or another provision of the DGCL). The Charter does not impose a supermajority stockholder vote for a merger             as such    , but the protective provisions described above effectively impose supermajority/class-vote requirements that exceed the DGCL baseline. Action by written consent in lieu of a meeting is permitted under DGCL §228 and Charter Article VI, §6.2, and the Bylaws (§2.6) — a mechanism the Company’s counsel flagged as available for the consent solicitation (Sept. 25, 2024 minutes).  
        1.3 Stockholders’ Agreement and Voting Agreement  
      The SHA’s         “    Sale of the Company    ”         definition (§1.1) and the Voting Agreement’s         “    Sale of the Company    ”         definition (§1) each capture a merger in which the Company’s pre-transaction stockholders do not retain a majority of the surviving entity’s voting power. The Transaction qualifies as a         “    Sale of the Company    ”         under both. These definitions trigger (i) the SHA drag-along (Section 2 below) and (ii) termination of the SHA and the Voting Agreement upon consummation (Sections 4 and 5 below). Neither the SHA nor the Voting Agreement imposes an             additional         standalone consent requirement to             authorize         the merger beyond the Charter protective provisions and the DGCL §251 stockholder vote; their principal governance effect is the drag-along obligation and the termination consequences.

         2. Drag-Along Analysis  
      The SHA (Article 3) contains drag-along rights. The client has asked us to determine (a) whether the drag-along can be triggered             without         Stonemill, and (b) whether the drag-along can override the Charter-level Series C separate-class protective provision. Both questions are addressed below.  
       2.1 The three-prong trigger (SHA §3.1(a))  
      A         “    Drag-Along Approval    ”         requires the satisfaction of             all three         of the following conditions (SHA §3.1(a)–(b)):  
              (i)         holders of at least a             majority of the outstanding shares of Common Stock    , calculated on an as-converted-to-Common-Stock basis (treating each share of Preferred as converted) (the         “    Common Prong    ”    );  
              (ii)         holders of at least a             majority of the outstanding shares of Preferred Stock    , as-converted, voting together as a             single class         (the         “    Preferred Prong    ”    ); and  
              (iii)         at least             two (2) of the three (3) Investor Stockholders         (the         “    Investor Prong    ”    ).  
      If a Drag-Along Approval is obtained,             every         Restricted Stockholder — including any that did not vote in favor — must (A) vote all of its Shares in favor of the Sale of the Company, (B) refrain from exercising appraisal/dissenters’ rights, (C) take all actions necessary to consummate the sale, and (D) refrain from impeding the sale (SHA §3.1(a)). The obligation is backed by an irrevocable proxy coupled with an interest (SHA §3.3) and a specific-performance remedy (SHA §7.8).  
        2.2 Can the drag-along be triggered without Stonemill?  
          Preferred Prong (ii) — YES, achievable without Stonemill.         The threshold is a majority of 16,500,000 Preferred shares (as-converted) = more than 8,250,000 shares. Ridgeline (3,500,000) + Northbridge (5,200,000) = 8,700,000 shares, exceeding the threshold. As with the Charter all-Preferred vote, the margin is thin and requires both Ridgeline and Northbridge.  
          Investor Prong (iii) — YES, achievable without Stonemill.         Two of three Investor Stockholders (Ridgeline and Northbridge) suffice.  
          Common Prong (i) — UNCERTAIN; likely cannot be satisfied by the SHA parties alone.         This is the binding constraint. The Common Prong requires a majority of the outstanding Common Stock on an as-converted basis (i.e., a majority of the entire equity capitalization treated as Common). Against the fully diluted base of 33,900,000 shares, a majority is             more than 16,950,000 shares    . The Founders (7,300,000) + Ridgeline (3,500,000) + Northbridge (5,200,000) =             16,000,000 shares — approximately 950,000 shares short         of the threshold.  
      Whether the Common Prong can be cleared without Stonemill therefore depends on two unresolved questions:  
             The denominator.         “    Outstanding shares of Common Stock (as-converted)    ”         most naturally means issued-and-outstanding Common plus Preferred-as-converted,             excluding         unexercised options and unvested RSUs (which are not         “    outstanding shares    ”    ). If a meaningful portion of the 10,100,000-share         “    various Common / option pool    ”         consists of unexercised options/unvested RSUs, the denominator shrinks and the threshold falls — potentially below 16,000,000, which would allow the Founders + Ridgeline + Northbridge to clear the Common Prong without any other holders. If, conversely, the denominator is the full 33,900,000 fully diluted figure, the parties fall short and would need the support of additional Common holders (employees, advisors) who are             not         parties to the SHA and therefore             not         subject to the drag-along.  
             Support of non-party Common holders.         If the denominator is the full fully-diluted figure, the Company would need ~950,000+ additional shares of Common-holder support. Those holders are not bound by the drag-along (only         “    Restricted Stockholders    ”         — parties and joinders — are). Their willingness to approve is not assured, particularly because, as discussed in Section 7, employee options appear to be             out of the money         at the deal price (409A value $14.30 vs. ~$12.59–$13.95 per Common share in the waterfall), which may dampen employee enthusiasm for the Transaction.  
          Conclusion on trigger:         Prongs (ii) and (iii) can be satisfied without Stonemill. Prong (i) is the gating item and             cannot be confirmed without (a) the current capitalization table, (b) a breakdown of the 10,100,000-share pool between issued Common and outstanding options/RSUs, and (c) a legal determination of the proper                 “        outstanding shares        ”                 denominator.         We flag this as a priority open item (Appendix C).  
        2.3 Can the drag-along override the Charter Series C separate-class protective provision?  
      This is the central legal question and, in our view, the answer is             not cleanly — the drag-along is a contested and contingent lever, not a reliable override.         The analysis:  
          Mechanical interaction.         If the drag-along is properly triggered, Stonemill — as a party to the SHA and therefore a         “    Restricted Stockholder    ”         — would be contractually obligated to vote             all         of its Shares (including its 7,800,000 Series C shares) in favor of the Sale of the Company (SHA §3.1(a)(A)). If Stonemill so voted, the Series C separate-class vote would be satisfied (100% > 66⅔%), and the Charter protective provision would be respected — not         “    overridden.    ”         On this mechanical view, the drag-along does not dispense with the separate-class vote; it compels Stonemill to cast its separate-class vote in favor.  
          The legal contest.         Stonemill will argue that the drag-along cannot be used to circumvent a Charter protective provision that the Series C holders specifically negotiated as a veto over a Liquidation Event. The arguments in Stonemill’s favor include:  
          The Certificate of Incorporation is the supreme corporate document under Delaware law; its protective provisions bind the corporation and all stockholders and may not be waived by a private stockholder agreement to which the protective-protection beneficiary did not intend to subordinate its veto.  
          The drag-along’s own Preferred Prong (ii) requires only an             all-Preferred single-class         majority — it does             not         require the             Series C separate-class         vote that the Charter demands. This textual gap supports an inference that the drag-along was designed to operate             alongside    , not             in lieu of    , the Charter protective provisions; the drafters did not build the separate-class vote into the drag-along trigger, suggesting they did not intend the drag-along to override it.  
          Delaware public policy protects the integrity of class-voting rights created by the charter.  
      The arguments in the Company’s/Buyer’s favor include:  
          Delaware law (DGCL §218(c)) generally enforces stockholder voting agreements, including drag-alongs, as valid contracts; a stockholder that voluntarily agreed to be dragged has contractually committed to vote in favor, which is a permissible waiver of its own protective-provision veto in the specified circumstances.  
          The drag-along here             preserves the Charter waterfall    : SHA §3.2(a) expressly provides that consideration is distributed         “    in accordance with the liquidation waterfall provisions of the Restated Certificate as if the Sale of the Company were a Liquidation Event.    ”         Thus, even if dragged, Stonemill receives its full $18.50/share Series C liquidation preference — the drag-along strips Stonemill of             leverage    , not             economics    . This undercuts any argument that the drag-along breaches Stonemill’s economic rights or fiduciary protections.  
          The irrevocable proxy         “    coupled with an interest    ”         (SHA §3.3) and specific-performance remedy (SHA §7.8) strengthen enforceability.  
          Our assessment.         The drag-along is a             colorable         mechanism to compel Stonemill’s vote, and the waterfall-preservation feature meaningfully weakens Stonemill’s equitable objections. However, the question whether a stockholder-agreement drag-along can compel a separate-class Charter vote over the beneficiary’s objection is not free from doubt under Delaware law, and Stonemill is likely to seek injunctive relief to enjoin closing pending adjudication. Even a successful enforcement outcome would therefore likely involve             litigation, delay, and closing uncertainty         — outcomes inconsistent with a Q1 2025 target and a clean financing/close. Moreover, the entire analysis is moot unless the Common Prong (i) can be satisfied, which is itself unconfirmed.  
          Practical implication.         The drag-along is best understood as             negotiating leverage    , not a closing plan. It allows the Company/Buyer to tell Stonemill:         “    you will receive your full $18.50 preference whether or not you consent; your only leverage is the delay and litigation you can cause, and the drag-along limits even that.    ”         That is a meaningful negotiating posture, but it does not produce a clean, certain close over Stonemill’s objection. The reliable path to closing runs through Stonemill’s actual consent.  
          Severity: Critical         (the combination of the hard veto in Section 1 and the drag-along’s contingency/contestability).  
      
         3. Impediments to Closing a Reverse Triangular Merger  
       3.1 Does the structure clearly fall within the operative defined terms?  
      We confirmed that the reverse triangular merger is captured by every operative change-of-control definition in the governance documents:  
              “        Liquidation Event        ”                 (Charter §4.3.2(d)(i)):         captures         “    any merger, consolidation, or other business combination of the Corporation with or into another entity (whether or not the Corporation is the surviving entity)    ”         where pre-transaction stockholders do not retain ≥50% voting power. The reverse triangular merger (Terravox survives but is 100%-owned by Hargrove; stockholders cashed out) is captured. ✓  
              “        Sale of the Company        ”                 (SHA §1.1; VA §1):         captures a merger in which pre-transaction stockholders do not retain a majority of the surviving entity’s voting power. Captured. ✓  
              “        Deemed Liquidation Event        ”                 (RRA §9(c)):             undefined         — see Section 6 and Section 10. This is the one structural ambiguity; it does not impede             closing         the merger but creates ambiguity about RRA termination.  
        3.2 Reverse-triangular-merger-specific considerations  
      The reverse triangular merger structure (Merger Sub merges into Terravox; Terravox survives) is generally the cleanest structure for preserving the Company’s entity-level assets, contracts, and licenses, because the Company does not change its legal identity. However, the following items warrant attention in the merger agreement:  
              Contractual change-of-control consents.          4. Transfer Restrictions  
       4.1 Does the merger trigger ROFR, co-sale, or tag-along?  
      The SHA imposes a general transfer restriction (§2.1), a right of first refusal (§2.2), a co-sale right (§2.3), and a tag-along right (§2.4) on         “    Transfers    ”         of Shares by         “    Restricted Stockholders.    ”         “    Transfer    ”         is broadly defined (§1.1) to include         “    any direct or indirect sale, assignment, transfer, pledge, hypothecation, encumbrance, or other disposition of Shares or any interest therein, whether voluntary or involuntary, by operation of law or otherwise.    ”  
          ROFR (§2.2) and co-sale (§2.3).         These are triggered by a Selling Stockholder’s receipt of a bona fide third-party offer to             purchase         Shares and the Selling Stockholder’s desire to accept it. In a statutory merger, shares are not         “    sold    ”         by stockholders to a third party; they are cancelled and converted into the right to receive merger consideration by operation of the merger statute. Delaware law generally treats a merger as outside the scope of stockholder-level transfer restrictions absent express language to the contrary. The SHA contains             no express merger carve-out    , but the structure of §2.1 — which permits Transfers         “    in connection with a Sale of the Company consummated in accordance with Article 3    ”         (§2.1(c)) — functions as the operative carve-out. Because the Transaction is a         “    Sale of the Company,    ”         Transfers of Shares in the merger fall within §2.1(c) and are not subject to the ROFR/co-sale regime. We do not expect ROFR or co-sale to be triggered by the merger.  
          Tag-along (§2.4).         Tag-along is triggered by a             Founder’s         proposed Transfer of more than 5% of the outstanding Shares to a non-affiliate third party. The merger is not a         “    Founder Transfer    ”         to a third party; it is a statutory merger affecting all stockholders. Tag-along should not apply. Moreover, all stockholders receive merger consideration in the same transaction, so the pro-rata participation that tag-along is designed to ensure is inherent in the merger structure.  
        4.2 The         “    consummated in accordance with Article 3    ”         qualifier — a minor ambiguity  
      Section 2.1(c) permits Transfers         “    in connection with a Sale of the Company consummated in accordance with Article 3.    ”         Article 3 is the drag-along article. A literal reading could suggest that the §2.1(c) carve-out applies only where the Sale of the Company is effected             through         the drag-along mechanism (i.e., with non-consenting stockholders compelled). If the Transaction is instead consummated by             unanimous         written consent (including Stonemill’s), it is not strictly         “    in accordance with Article 3    ”         (which concerns compelling non-consenting stockholders), creating a theoretical argument that the transfer restrictions are not cleanly carved out.  
      We view this as a             low-probability but real ambiguity.         The better reading is that §2.1(c) carves out any Sale of the Company (however consents are assembled), and the merger-as-statutory-event analysis independently takes the merger outside the transfer restrictions. Nonetheless, to eliminate any argument, the merger agreement and the stockholder consent/waiver documentation should include an express acknowledgment that the merger constitutes a Sale of the Company and that the transfer restrictions do not apply. A clean legal opinion on transfer restrictions at closing would further insulate the Transaction.  
        4.3 Termination of the SHA upon the Sale of the Company  
      The SHA terminates upon the earliest of (a) an IPO (≥$75 million proceeds), (b) a             Sale of the Company         (§1.1), or (c) written consent of the Company, the Founders, and holders of a majority of the Investor Stockholder Shares (SHA §6.1). The Transaction is a Sale of the Company, so the SHA             terminates upon consummation of the merger         (§6.1(b)). Upon termination, all transfer restrictions, ROFR, co-sale, tag-along, and drag-along rights         “    cease to be of any further force and effect,    ”         and the Company must remove related legends (§6.3). Article 7 (miscellaneous) and accrued-liability provisions survive (§6.2).  
          Severity: Moderate         (the transfer restrictions should not impede the merger, but the §2.1(c)         “    Article 3    ”         qualifier should be cleaned up in the transaction documents).

         9. Fiduciary Duty / Sale Process Concerns  
       9.1 Special Committee composition — independence concern (SIGNIFICANT)  
      The Special Committee, formed April 12, 2023 to evaluate strategic alternatives and oversee any sale process, consists of             Rebecca Lau (Chair), Thomas Garrett, and Sandra Okafor    .  
          Sandra Okafor’s dual role.         Ms. Okafor is (i) the Series B Director designated by Northbridge Growth Equity Fund IV, LP pursuant to the Voting Agreement (§2.2(b)), and (ii) a Principal at Northbridge Growth Advisors, LLC (Northbridge’s general partner), as reflected in the SHA signature blocks and the Voting Agreement. Northbridge holds 5,200,000 shares of Series B Preferred with a $48.1 million liquidation preference. Ms. Okafor is therefore             not disinterested and not independent         of a significant stockholder with a material economic stake in the Transaction’s outcome.  
          Delaware law implications.         In a change-of-control transaction, the             Kahn v. M&F Worldwide Corp.         (    “        MFW    ”    ) framework provides that a transaction negotiated and approved by (1) a special committee of             independent, disinterested         directors and (2) a majority-of-the-minority stockholder vote receives business-judgment review rather than entire-fairness review. The presence of Ms. Okafor — a designee of a controlling-significant stockholder with a liquidation preference — on the Special Committee             compromises the committee’s independence         and likely             defeats reliance on the                   MFW                 framework    . If             MFW         is unavailable, the Transaction could be exposed to             entire-fairness review    , under which the Company/Buyer would bear the burden of demonstrating fair price and fair dealing. This is a meaningful litigation risk, particularly if Stonemill (or another stockholder) challenges the Transaction.  
          Mitigations to consider:  
              Reconstitute the Special Committee         to exclude Ms. Okafor (or to add additional truly independent directors and have Ms. Okafor recuse from substantive votes). The two         “    Independent Directors    ”         (Lau and Garrett) appear independent under the Voting Agreement’s definition (not employees; not affiliates of any Investor Stockholder), but a two-person committee is thin, and Lau’s role as Chair of both the Special Committee and the Compensation Committee concentrates authority.  
              Obtain a robust majority-of-the-minority vote         as a closing condition, to invoke             MFW         even if the committee is reconstituted. Note, however, that the         “    minority    ”         for             MFW         purposes must exclude any controlling stockholder; the analysis of who constitutes the         “    minority    ”         is complicated here by the multiple Preferred classes and their economic interests.  
              Ensure Thornbury & Associates’ fairness opinion is thoroughly documented         and that the Special Committee’s deliberations are carefully recorded, to support a fair-dealing record under entire fairness.  
              Address the waterfall-analysis error         (Section 3.3) in the fairness materials — the recorded statement that all Preferred converts is incorrect as to Series C and could undermine the fairness record if not corrected.  
        9.2 Trask’s absence and objection — process vulnerability (SIGNIFICANT)  
      At the April 17, 2024 meeting, the Board authorized the Special Committee to engage with Hargrove and provide data-room access — the decision that effectively launched the Hargrove process. Julian Trask (Stonemill’s designee) was absent (scheduling conflict; no proxy provided), and Thomas Garrett was absent (family medical matter). The resolution passed 5-0 among the directors present. On April 19, 2024, Trask emailed a written objection, stating that Stonemill’s         “    perspectives should have been sought before authorizing engagement with a potential acquirer    ”         and requesting that         “    no further substantive steps be taken regarding a potential Hargrove transaction without Stonemill’s participation.    ”         The objection was appended to the April 17 minutes.  
          Implications:  
              Procedural validity.         The Board had a quorum (5 of 7) and could act; the resolution was validly adopted. The absence of a single director does not invalidate Board action where a quorum is present.  
              Process-fairness vulnerability.         Notwithstanding procedural validity, the optics are poor: the pivotal decision to engage with Hargrove was made without the participation of the designee of the Series C lead investor — the very stockholder whose consent is ultimately required and whose economic interest (1x preference) gives it the greatest incentive to scrutinize the process. Stonemill can and will argue that the sale process was structured to marginalize it, supporting a narrative of unfair dealing. This is ammunition for any Revlon-duty or entire-fairness challenge.  
              Consistency of Trask’s opposition.         Trask has voted against or objected to every major Hargrove-process milestone (the July 2023         “    Beta    ”         exclusivity (6-1); the April 2024 Hargrove engagement (objected via email); the July 2024 Hargrove exclusivity (6-1); the September 2024 authorization of definitive-document negotiation (6-1); and abstentions on minute approvals). He has consistently advocated a broader auction. This record supports both (a) Stonemill’s narrative that it has been excluded and (b) a potential Revlon argument that the Board narrowed the process prematurely — though the Board did initially run a three-party process (Alpha, Beta, Gamma) with a financial advisor, which provides some process protection.  
        9.3 Sale-process observations  
          The Board engaged Meridian Partners as financial advisor (January 2023) and ran an initial process yielding three indications of interest (Alpha: $350–400M EV all-cash; Beta: $450–525M EV LBO; Gamma: $380–420M EV stock-for-stock).           The engagement of Thornbury & Associates as an independent valuation advisor (July 2024) is a positive process feature that should be leveraged in the fairness record.  
          Severity: Significant         (the committee-composition and process-fairness vulnerabilities create real litigation exposure and should be addressed before signing/closing).  
      
         10. Registration Rights Post-Transaction  
       10.1 RRA termination provisions  
      The RRA terminates a Holder’s registration rights upon the earliest of (RRA §9):  
        
          the date the Holder can sell all its Registrable Securities in a single three-month period under Rule 144 without volume/manner-of-sale restrictions and without current-public-information requirements;  
        
          the fifth anniversary of the Company’s IPO; or  
        
              “        a Deemed Liquidation Event.        ”  
      The RRA terminates in its entirety upon (i) termination as to all Holders per (a)–(c), or (ii) written consent of the Company + Holders of a majority of the then-outstanding Registrable Securities (RRA §9, second paragraph). Section 7 (indemnification) and accrued-liability provisions survive termination (RRA §9, final paragraph).  
        10.2 The         “    Deemed Liquidation Event    ”         problem  
      As detailed in Section 6.2,             “        Deemed Liquidation Event        ”                 is undefined in the RRA         and is not cross-referenced to the Charter’s         “    Liquidation Event.    ”         It is therefore ambiguous whether the merger — a Liquidation Event under the Charter and a Sale of the Company under the SHA/VA — constitutes a         “    Deemed Liquidation Event    ”         that triggers RRA termination under §9(c).  
          Practical effect.         Because the merger consideration is all-cash and all Preferred is cancelled and converted into cash, the former Holders will hold no Registrable Securities post-closing (Registrable Securities are defined as Common issued/issuable upon conversion of Preferred). With no Registrable Securities outstanding, the registration rights are largely moot as a practical matter. However:  
          The RRA is a contract that, on its face, may survive the merger if         “    Deemed Liquidation Event    ”         is not satisfied, creating a technical post-closing obligation on Hargrove (as successor) that should not be left open.  
          If any element of the consideration were to include Hargrove equity (e.g., a rollover, earnout, or escrow shares issued to former stockholders), registration rights could attach to those securities, reviving a live registration obligation — an outcome Hargrove would not want.  
          A surviving RRA could also entangle future Hargrove capital-markets activity (e.g., a future IPO or follow-on) with Holder piggyback/demand rights.  
        10.3 Recommended clean-up  
      The merger agreement should include, as a             closing condition and/or a closing covenant    , an             express termination of the RRA         effected by either:  
        
          a written consent of the Company and Holders of a majority of the Registrable Securities (RRA §9, second paragraph) — which can be obtained             without Stonemill         (Ridgeline + Northbridge = 8,700,000 > 8,250,000 threshold); and/or  
        
          a provision in the merger agreement deeming the merger to constitute a         “    Deemed Liquidation Event    ”         for all purposes of the RRA, coupled with each Holder’s written acknowledgment/consent to the RRA’s termination at closing.  
      Do             not         rely on the ambiguous         “    Deemed Liquidation Event    ”         definition alone. The clean-up should be documented and delivered at closing, and the Company’s RRA-termination representation in the merger agreement should be backed by the consent/deeming mechanics.  
          Severity: Significant         (the undefined term creates a real post-closing liability risk that is readily curable but must be built into the merger agreement).

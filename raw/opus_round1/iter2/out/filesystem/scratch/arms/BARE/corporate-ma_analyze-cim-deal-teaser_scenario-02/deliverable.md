       1. Executive Summary and Recommendation  
       1.1 Purpose  
      This memorandum presents the deal team’s comprehensive buy-side analysis of Cascade Environmental Solutions, Inc. (    “    CES    ”         or the         “    Company    ”    ), a Portland-based environmental services platform offered for sale by Greenleaf Advisory Group, LLC (    “    Greenleaf    ”    ). It is prepared in response to Sarah Lindgren’s sector memorandum of June 12, 2025, which directed that a full buy-side CIM analysis be completed by June 25, 2025 using the sector benchmarks and Thornfield portfolio experience therein as the evaluative framework. Sources reviewed: (i) the CIM dated June 9, 2025; (ii) the Greenleaf process letter; (iii) the Customer Summary and Backlog Report; and (iv) the Financial Exhibits workbook (Income Statement, Balance Sheet, Cash Flow, EBITDA Bridge, Revenue by Segment, and Capex Schedule).  
        1.2 Recommendation  
          Proceed to submit an Indication of Interest, but at a disciplined valuation that reflects a series of material financial and commercial issues identified in the CIM package.         CES is a genuinely attractive platform — a 16-year-old, integrated, regionally-scaled environmental services business with real regulatory tailwinds (notably PFAS) and a defensible competitive position. However, the sell-side materials contain multiple financial inconsistencies, aggressive growth projections, customer-concentration and contract-renewal exposures, and EBITDA adjustments of questionable defensibility that, in aggregate, warrant a valuation meaningfully below the Greenleaf-implied range and a heavily conditional IOI.

        3. Business Overview and Investment Thesis  
       3.1 What CES Is  
      CES is a regional environmental services platform generating $87.3M of FY2024 revenue across three segments — Environmental Remediation ($48.2M, 55.2%), Industrial Cleaning ($27.8M, 31.8%), and Emergency Response ($11.3M, 12.9%) — serving 150+ customers across Oregon, Washington, Idaho, and Northern California from three facilities (Portland HQ, Seattle office, Boise depot). The Company employs 412 people, operates an 82+ unit specialized fleet (replacement value >$22M), and holds hazardous waste transporter permits in all four states plus an Oregon DEQ contractor license. Two licensed Professional Engineers provide technical sign-off on remediation work.  
        3.2 Genuine Strengths (Buy-Side Acknowledged)  
              Integrated, regulated platform.         The remediation/industrial-cleaning/emergency-response combination creates cross-selling and share-of-wallet dynamics; the four-state permit infrastructure and DEQ license are multi-year barriers to entry.  
              Real regulatory tailwinds.         EPA’s 2024 PFAS drinking-water rule and state-level PFAS initiatives in OR/WA create durable, multi-decade demand for investigation and remediation. CES is an early mover with a 3-year-old PFAS practice.  
              Recurring revenue characteristics.         MSA-based industrial cleaning on scheduled turnaround cycles, multi-year remediation project lifecycles, and standby emergency-response retainers provide a recurring base (~60% of revenue under MSAs).  
              Track record.         12.5% revenue CAGR (FY21–24) with reported EBITDA margin expansion from 12.1% to 13.9% — within sector norms and indicative of a reasonably well-managed operation.  
              Platform fit.         CES is a credible consolidation platform in a fragmented Pacific Northwest market, consistent with Thornfield’s sector thesis and Fund II experience (GreenWorks, Allied Waste).  
        3.3 Thesis in One Sentence  
      CES is an attractive, regulation-backed environmental services platform whose headline growth and margin story rests disproportionately on an unproven PFAS ramp and a set of EBITDA adjustments and customer relationships that do not survive close scrutiny — making it a         “    good business, careful price    ”         opportunity rather than a         “    pay the ask    ”         opportunity.

      Synthesizing the above, buy-side Adjusted EBITDA is estimated at             ~$15.5–16.5M         (centered ~$16.0M), versus the CIM’s $17.1M — a reduction of roughly $0.6–1.6M, before considering any downward revision to reported EBITDA from the reconciliation problems in Section 4.2.         5.3 Free Cash Flow and Leverage Capacity  
      FCF is thin and a leveraged transaction is constrained:  
          Reported FCF is only ~$2.0–2.8M (CIM vs Excel) on $12.1M EBITDA, because capex ($7.2M) and working-capital absorption (AR +$2.1M, etc.) consume most of EBITDA.  
          The Company has been run for owner cash extraction: distributions to shareholders were $1.5M / $2.7M / $1.5M / $1.04M in FY21–24 — roughly the entirety of FCF — consistent with under-reinvestment and the deferred-maintenance capex concern.  
          If true maintenance capex is ~$5.5M (sector midpoint) rather than $3.0M, FCF falls by ~$2.5M to near zero on a CIM basis. This severely limits debt service capacity and argues for an equity-heavy capital structure (likely ≤2.0–2.5x EBITDA of leverage), which in turn drives a large equity check and the fund-concentration issue in Section 8.6.

        6.5 Capacity Constraint: Professional Engineer Headcount  
      PFAS site assessments and remediation designs require PE oversight.             CES’s PE headcount fell from three to two in January 2025         following an unreplaced retirement — a one-third reduction in the credential that underpins the growth thesis. Supporting the projected PFAS ramp would likely require hiring two to three additional PEs, and the environmental PE labor market is extremely tight (6–12 month recruitment cycles per the sector memo). The deal team should obtain management’s PE hiring plan and assess whether the lost capacity creates a near-term execution bottleneck. This is a concrete, quantifiable constraint on the projections.  
        6.6 Margin Expansion Is Contingent on the PFAS Ramp  
      Adjusted EBITDA margin is projected to expand from 19.6% to 21.5% over FY24–FY27E, explicitly attributed to         “    operating leverage… favorable mix shift toward higher-margin remediation and PFAS services.    ”         If PFAS growth disappoints, the mix shift does not materialize and margins do not expand — indeed, without the PFAS mix benefit and with normalized maintenance capex, the FCF profile is unattractive. The margin expansion should be treated as a contingent outcome, not a base case.

        8.2 Regulatory and Compliance Risk  
              August 14, 2024 Oregon DEQ enforcement action:         improper storage of contaminated soils at the Portland HQ facility; $875K settlement + $325K legal fees. The fact that the violation occurred             at the operational core         (not a remote field site) is a yellow flag for systemic compliance posture, not just an isolated incident. A full 10-year enforcement history (notices of violation, permit actions, complaints) must be obtained.  
              Recurring compliance cost reality:         as noted in Section 5, environmental services companies routinely incur $0.2–0.5M/yr of regulatory/compliance expense; treating the full $1.2M as non-recurring is not defensible QoE practice.  
              Permit currency:         all listed permits are stated         “    current,    ”         but renewal cycles and any conditions/restrictions should be verified, particularly the DEQ contractor license given the recent enforcement action.  
        8.3 Real Estate and Related-Party Risk  
      The Portland HQ (8.5 acres, purpose-built environmental operations center) is             owned personally by Mr. Oakes         and leased to CES at $1.4M/yr through 2029 — ~$0.8M above estimated market rent. This creates three intertwined issues: (i) the EBITDA add-back is conditional on a post-close negotiation with the seller (Section 5); (ii) the buyer must decide whether to acquire the property, renegotiate the lease, or relocate — but the property is customized for environmental staging (permitted wash rack, secondary containment, soil stockpiles), making relocation costly and disruptive; and (iii) Mr. Oakes, as 68% seller and landlord, has leverage on both the equity price and the rent. The process letter explicitly asks bidders to address the real estate, signaling that Greenleaf expects this to be a negotiated term. The Boise depot ($180K/yr through 2028) and Seattle office ($85K/yr through 2027) are third-party leases and present no related-party issue.  
        8.4 Key-Person and Management-Depth Risk  
              Randall Oakes (CEO, 61, 68% owner):         intends to transition out of day-to-day operations within 18–24 months post-close. He has been the sole strategic, financial, and operational decision-maker for 16 years. A replacement CEO             will         be required (which is why the owner-comp add-back is conceptually valid), but the $0.5M replacement assumption is light and the transition risk is real.  
              Janet Prewitt (VP Operations, 18-year tenure):         holds key remediation client relationships — a critical key person. Retention agreement should be a closing condition (the GreenWorks experience, where a lead PM departed within six months of close, is a direct cautionary parallel).  
              No CFO:         the Company has a Controller (Lisa Fontaine) but no CFO. For an $87M platform being integrated into a sponsor portfolio, a CFO will likely need to be installed post-close — an additional cost not reflected in the projections.  
              Professional Engineer capacity:         down to two from three (Section 6.5), constraining the growth thesis.  
        8.5 Capital Expenditure and Fleet Condition  
      CIM maintenance capex of $3.0M (3.4% of revenue) is             ~150–200 bps below the 5–7% sector floor         and below Thornfield’s actual portfolio experience (GreenWorks 6.2%, Allied 5.8%). The GreenWorks lesson — where pre-acquisition maintenance capex was understated at ~3.5% and actual post-close was ~6.2% as deferred maintenance surfaced — is directly on point. The fleet is reported at an average age of ~4.5 years with >$22M replacement value, which sounds reasonable, but a detailed fleet age-and-condition assessment is essential early diligence. If normalized maintenance capex is ~$5.5M, the ~$2.5M/yr difference directly reduces FCF (to near zero on a CIM basis) and weakens the leverage/return profile. Note also that FY2023–24 growth capex was elevated ($3.0M/$4.2M) including $0.7M/$1.2M of PFAS equipment — some         “    growth    ”         fleet additions may in fact be replacement in disguise.  
        8.6 Strategic Fit and Fund Concentration  
      The sector memo states Fund III         “    targets platforms in the $30M–$100M enterprise value range    ”         and that CES         “    fits squarely within these parameters.    ”         However, Greenleaf’s own implied EV range is             $136.8M–$171.0M         (trailing) — materially above the stated target. At a buy-side EV of ~$130–150M, less ~$13–15M net debt, the phantom-unit cash settlement, and any HQ property acquisition, the equity check is likely             ~$110–135M    , or roughly             26–32% of Fund III’s $425M         in a single platform. This is a meaningful concentration that warrants explicit IC discussion: either the         “    $30–100M    ”         target is defined on a different basis (e.g., revenue, where CES’s $87.3M does fit), or CES represents a larger-than-typical platform requiring IC approval and possibly a co-invest. The deal team should align with the IC on fund-concentration limits before the IOI.  
      
         9. Transaction Structure Considerations  
      The IOI must address the structurally sensitive items Greenleaf flagged. Recommended positions:  
              Real estate (HQ).         Propose to acquire the HQ property at appraised fair market value (eliminating the related-party lease and the add-back debate entirely)             or         negotiate a new long-term lease at market rent ($0.6M/yr) as a condition to closing. Do not assume the $0.8M add-back is free; price the EV on a basis that reflects the likely outcome.  
              Existing debt.         Refinance/repay the $17.3M Cascadia facility at close (term loan matures Aug 2026 in any event). Confirm no prepayment penalties or change-of-control triggers.  
              Phantom units.         Treat the cash settlement of all outstanding phantom units as a             debt-like closing obligation         reducing equity proceeds; obtain the current liability balance and unit agreements. Do not double-count the $0.3M annual add-back against the closing payment.  
              Oakes transition / rollover / earnout.         Accommodate the 18–24 month advisory transition. Consider a modest seller rollover (5–10%) to align Oakes on the PFAS execution and PNR renewal, and/or an earnout tied to PFAS revenue and PNR contract renewal — directly bridging the two largest value uncertainties. An earnout is well-suited here because the principal risks (PFAS ramp, PNR renewal) are measurable post-close.  
              Working capital peg.         Establish a normalized NWC peg via QoE; the DSO discrepancy (Section 4/8) and the reconciliation issues mean the peg must be set from audited data, not the CIM.  
              Tax structure.         CES is a Delaware C-corp and the process letter anticipates a stock purchase. A stock purchase of a C-corp generally provides             no tax basis step-up         absent a Section 338(h)(10)/(g) election. Buyer’s counsel (Calloway, Barrett & Sims) should evaluate 338(h)(10) feasibility and the net tax benefit versus the cost, in coordination with tax advisors, as this can materially affect after-tax returns.  
              Representations & warranties / indemnity.         Given the DEQ enforcement history, customer-contract status, and related-party lease, insist on strong reps specifically covering regulatory compliance history, material contract status/renewals, and the lease; consider R&W insurance with specific exclusions carved back to seller indemnity.  
      
        10. Recommended IOI Position and Diligence Priorities  
       10.1 Recommended IOI  
              Enterprise value range: ~$130M–$150M         on a cash-free, debt-free basis with normalized NWC, valued on trailing (FY2024A) buy-side Adjusted EBITDA of ~$15.5–16.5M (≈8.1x–9.4x). State that the range is trailing-based and contingent on confirmatory diligence.  
              Structure:         100% stock purchase; refinance existing debt at close; acquire or re-lease HQ at market; accommodate 18–24 month Oakes transition with proposed modest rollover and/or PFAS/PNR-tied earnout.  
              Financing:         sponsor equity from Fund III (indicate anticipated leverage of ~2.0–2.5x EBITDA given the thin FCF profile) plus senior debt from a lender to be identified; note that final leverage is subject to QoE and FCF confirmation.  
              Conditions / value drivers:         explicitly state that the upper end of the range is contingent on (i) confirmation of PFAS contracted backlog supporting FY2025E, (ii) PNR contract renewal/extension, and (iii) validation of the EBITDA adjustments via QoE. This signals seriousness while preserving downside protection.  
        10.2 Prioritized Diligence Requests (Phase II / VDR)  
              Reconciled, audited financial statements         with a complete EBITDA build that ties to the line items; QoE provider to resolve the ~$3.2M Excel footing gap and the CIM-vs-Excel differences.  
              PNR:         current contract status, renewal correspondence, PNR’s share of backlog, and any rebid/competitive activity.  
              PFAS:         contracted backlog vs. pipeline breakout for the PFAS practice; signed PFAS contracts supporting FY2025E; PE staffing/hiring plan.  
              Customer list:         a single reconciled top-customer list with contract copies, tenure, and segments (resolve the #6–#10 discrepancy).  
              Regulatory:         10-year enforcement/compliance history; DEQ consent agreement and any ongoing obligations; permit renewal schedule.  
              Capex/fleet:         independent fleet age-and-condition assessment; maintenance vs. growth capex detail; deferred-maintenance confirmation.  
              Real estate:         HQ appraisal, lease terms, environmental condition of the property, and Oakes’s willingness to sell/re-lease.  
              Phantom equity:         outstanding unit count, vesting, and cash settlement amount at close.  
              Management:         retention terms for Prewitt and other key personnel; CFO plan; Oakes transition/consulting terms.  
              Working capital:         monthly NWC and AR aging to set the peg and resolve the DSO discrepancy (52 days stated vs. ~71 days implied).  
        10.3 Walk-Away / Repricing Triggers  
      The IOI range should be explicitly revisitable downward if: (a) PNR is not renewed or is competitively rebid at reduced scope; (b) PFAS contracted backlog does not meaningfully support FY2025E; (c) QoE reduces reported/Adjusted EBITDA; (d) maintenance capex is confirmed materially above $3.0M; or (e) the regulatory history reveals systemic rather than isolated compliance issues. Conversely, the range could move toward/above $150M if PFAS backlog and PNR renewal are confirmed and adjustments validate.  
      
         11. Conclusion  
      CES is a high-quality environmental services platform with genuine regulatory tailwinds and a defensible regional competitive position, and it aligns with Thornfield’s sector thesis and Fund II pattern-recognition. However, the CIM package exhibits a pattern of issues that Thornfield’s own portfolio experience predicts in this sector: understated maintenance capex, regulatory costs presented as fully non-recurring, and emerging-service-line growth projected well above achievable rates — compounded here by financial statements that do not reconcile, an EBITDA bridge with conditional and overstated add-backs, a largest customer whose contract has already expired, and customer disclosures that are internally inconsistent.  
          Recommendation: submit a disciplined IOI at ~$130–150M EV, heavily conditioned on the diligence items above, with an earnout/rollover structure bridging the PFAS and PNR uncertainties.         This positions Thornfield as a credible, serious bidder while preserving the downside protection that the identified risks demand. The deal team should obtain IC alignment on the fund-concentration implication before submission. Target IC pre-read ahead of the July 14, 2025 IOI deadline.

            III.  Critical Compliance & Enforceability Issues  
            A.  OWBPA / ADEA Release (Sections 5, 16) — Critical  
             Delano is 58 years old and his claims include age discrimination under the ADEA. For an individual (non-group) waiver of ADEA rights to be “knowing and voluntary” under the OWBPA, 29 U.S.C. § 626(f), the agreement must give the claimant at least 21 days to consider and a 7-day revocation period after signing. The Draft allows only 14 days (Sections 5(c) and 16) and provides no revocation period. As written, the ADEA release is defective and Delano could later challenge it. We corrected the consideration period to 21 days and added a 7-day revocation right (new Section 5(g)). Because payments should not be owed until the agreement is final, we redefined “Effective Date” as the eighth day after Delano signs (the day after the revocation period expires), provided he does not revoke; all Section 3 payment deadlines now run from that date. This also protects Greenleaf from funding the settlement before the revocation window closes.  
            B.  RSU Valuation (Sections 3(d), 3(e), 8(d)) — Critical  
             The Draft values the 5,000 accelerated RSUs at $10.00 per share, citing “the most recent independent valuation conducted by Ridgepoint Valuation Services.” That is incorrect. The employment records reflect a more recent 409A valuation by Ridgepoint dated January 15, 2025, at $12.50 per share. The correct value of 5,000 RSUs is $62,500, not $50,000, and the aggregate settlement value is $537,500, not $525,000. We corrected the per-share value, the RSU component, and the total. This is not merely cosmetic: the taxable income Delano recognizes on vesting will be based on fair market value at the vesting date, so the Draft’s figures would conflict with Greenleaf’s own tax reporting. We also added a clarification in Section 8(d) that taxable income is determined by FMV at vesting. Separately, the records note that accelerated vesting requires Compensation Committee approval under the 2021 Equity Incentive Plan; we added a representation to that effect in Section 15(b)(iii). The GC should confirm that approval has been (or will promptly be) obtained and documented.  
            C.  Whistleblower / Government-Reporting Carve-Outs (Sections 6, 7) — Critical  
             The confidentiality and non-disparagement clauses, as drafted, sweep broadly enough to prohibit Delano from communicating with government agencies. That is unlawful and, in this matter, dangerous: Delano filed the internal complaint (Nov. 8, 2024) and the Oregon OSHA complaint (Dec. 3, 2024; No. OR-OSHA-2024-11872) that underlie this dispute, and the OSHA investigation opened January 22, 2025 remains pending. Provisions that gag whistleblowers from reporting to or participating in government investigations are unenforceable and, under some statutes, can themselves create liability. We added (i) a government-agency reporting exception in Section 6(v) (EEOC, DOL, Oregon OSHA, NLRB, SEC, and comparable agencies); (ii) a DTSA / whistleblower notice tracking 18 U.S.C. § 1833(b); and (iii) a parallel carve-out in the non-disparagement clause (Section 7). We also made clear that the notice obligation does not apply to voluntary agency reports. These carve-outs are protective: they preserve the release of Delano’s civil claims while removing provisions a court would likely strike down anyway.  
            D.  Governing Law / Dispute Resolution (new Section 19) — Important  
             The Draft contains no governing-law, venue, or dispute-resolution provision at all — a significant omission. We added Section 19: Oregon governing law; binding arbitration before JAMS in Portland under its Employment Arbitration Rules; a prevailing-party attorneys’-fee provision for enforcement actions; a carve-out permitting either party to seek injunctive or equitable relief in Multnomah County, Oregon to enforce the confidentiality, non-disparagement, non-compete, and non-solicitation provisions; an express carve-out preserving the right to file with government agencies; and a jury-trial waiver. The GC may prefer litigation with a jury waiver rather than arbitration; we can adjust.  
            E.  Integration Clause (Section 18) — Important  
             The integration clause could be read to supersede the 2021 Equity Incentive Plan and the RSU Grant Agreement, which would undermine the very acceleration it contemplates in Section 3(d). We carved both instruments out so they remain in effect to the extent not modified by the Agreement.  
            F.  Non-Competition (Section 9) — Important / Likely Unenforceable  
             Under ORS 653.295, a non-competition agreement is voidable unless it is entered into at the time of initial employment or upon a bona fide advancement and the employee is informed in writing in advance. A non-compete first imposed in a separation/settlement agreement almost certainly does not satisfy this requirement, so the provision is likely voidable in its entirety regardless of its terms. In addition, the California portion is void per se under California Business & Professions Code § 16600 (subject to narrow exceptions not applicable here). We narrowed the term from 18 to 12 months, limited the geography to Oregon and Washington (removing Idaho and California), and added a savings clause referencing ORS 653.295. The core timing defect, however, cannot be cured by redrafting. We recommend the GC consider whether to retain the non-compete at all; the non-solicitation (Section 10) and confidentiality (Section 6) covenants, which have materially better enforceability prospects, may provide adequate protection. We reduced the non-solicitation term to 12 months for consistency.  
            G.  Employment Reference (Section 11)  
             The Draft obligates Greenleaf to provide a “positive” reference and to state that Delano “was a valued member of the Greenleaf team who made significant contributions.” Mandated laudatory statements over-obligate the Company and can create misrepresentation exposure if they are not accurate. We revised this to a neutral reference confirming dates of employment, final title, and eligibility for rehire — the market standard and sufficient to support Delano’s job search.  
            IV.  Litigation Risk & Strategy Considerations  
            A.  Replacement Hire — High Exposure  
             The employment records show that Greenleaf hired Raj Mehta as Senior Director of Supply Chain & Logistics on March 20, 2025 — six days after terminating Delano on March 14, 2025 — at a base salary of $185,000 (versus Delano’s $218,000). This fact directly contradicts the stated termination rationale (“elimination of position due to restructuring of supply chain division”) and supplies powerful evidence of pretext for both (i) retaliation under ORS 654.062(5) (the whistleblower claim, tied to Delano’s Nov. 8, 2024 internal complaint and Dec. 3, 2024 OSHA complaint) and (ii) age discrimination under the ADEA (Delano is 58; the replacement was hired at a lower salary). Implications:  
           Greenleaf’s litigation exposure is high, and a jury would likely find the timing and salary differential compelling.  
           The corrected settlement value of $537,500 is reasonable in light of that exposure; this is not a case where Greenleaf is overpaying.  
           It is essential that the release be broad and — critically — enforceable. The OWBPA corrections in Part III.A are therefore the single most important set of revisions; an unenforceable ADEA release would leave Greenleaf paying $537,500 without obtaining the release it is buying.  
           The GC should confirm the contemporaneous business record supporting the “restructuring” rationale and assess whether any portion of the consideration should be specifically allocated to the retaliation/whistleblower claims for allocation-of-damages and tax purposes.  
            B.  Pending Oregon OSHA Investigation  
             This settlement resolves Delano’s civil retaliation claim against Greenleaf. It does not resolve the pending Oregon OSHA investigation (opened Jan. 22, 2025) arising from Delano’s complaint. Greenleaf remains exposed to citations, penalties, and adverse findings regardless of this Agreement. The carve-outs we added preserve Delano’s right to participate in that investigation (which the law requires), so Greenleaf cannot prevent his cooperation with OSHA. We recommend that Greenleaf’s operations and food-safety teams prepare for the OSHA matter independently; the value of this settlement is the civil release, not regulatory peace.  
            C.  Key-Witness Considerations  
             Leanne Foss (HR Director) is both a named Releasee and a central witness: she received Delano’s internal complaint, placed him on administrative leave, and prepared the employment summary on which this review relies. Her inclusion as a Releasee is appropriate, but the GC should be aware that her testimony and the records she prepared will be relevant if the release is ever challenged or if the OSHA matter proceeds.  
            V.  Items to Verify Before Execution  
           PTO / Final Wages (Section 2): The Draft represents that Delano received all wages and accrued PTO. The employment records do not confirm a PTO payout. Under ORS 652.610, accrued vacation/PTO must be paid at termination; if it was not paid, the wage release could be challenged. HR should confirm payment before execution.  
           2024 Bonus: The 2024 performance bonus (20% target = $43,600) was “not determined” before termination. The $125,000 back-pay component appears intended to cover unpaid wages including any bonus, but the GC should confirm the wage/non-wage allocation is defensible.  
           Compensation Committee Approval: Confirm the Committee has approved the RSU acceleration (Section 3(d) / 15(b)(iii)).  
           Tax Allocation (Section 8): The $275,000 as 1099-MISC non-wage (emotional distress / reputational) and $125,000 as W-2 back pay is generally defensible, but confirm the split is supportable on audit.  
           Non-Compete Decision: Decide whether to retain the (likely voidable) non-compete or rely on non-solicitation plus confidentiality.  
           Execution Mechanics: After Delano’s counsel accepts the redline, ensure Delano signs, the 21-day consideration period is honored, and the 7-day revocation period expires without revocation before the Effective Date and before any payment is released.  
            VI.  Recommendation  
             Proceed to negotiate the redline with Delano’s counsel. Do not execute until: (a) the OWBPA revisions (21-day consideration plus 7-day revocation) are accepted; (b) the RSU valuation is corrected and Compensation Committee approval is documented; (c) HR confirms the PTO payout; (d) the GC has decided whether to retain the non-compete; and (e) both parties have signed and the revocation period has expired. Given the replacement-hire exposure, a clean, enforceable release is the primary value Greenleaf obtains from this settlement, and the Part III.A revisions are essential to securing it.  
             We are available to discuss any of the foregoing or to adjust the redline — for example, substituting a litigation/jury-waiver structure for arbitration, or revisiting the non-compete.

       I. Executive Summary  
      This memorandum assesses the sufficiency of the Arbitrator Disclosure Statement dated June 17, 2024, submitted by Hon. Margaret R. Fairhaven (Ret.) in the above-captioned matter. The assessment measures the disclosure against the AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules (Rules R-16, R-17, and R-18), the Federal Arbitration Act, the IBA Guidelines on Conflicts of Interest in International Arbitration, and the AAA/ABA Code of Ethics for Arbitrators in Commercial Disputes, and cross-checks it against the arbitration demand, the entity-relationship research memorandum dated June 19, 2024, and the compiled arbitrator appointment data.  
          Bottom line.         The disclosure is materially incomplete. Our review identifies             eight categories of concern    . Of these,             five represent connections or interests that were entirely omitted         from the disclosure,             one involves an affirmative misstatement         that is contradicted by the public record, and             two involve disclosures that are partial or incomplete    . Several of these constitute clear violations of Rule R-17’s disclosure obligations and, independently, create a credible risk of vacatur of any resulting award under the         “    evident partiality    ”         standard of Section 10(a)(2) of the Federal Arbitration Act, 9 U.S.C. § 10(a)(2).  
      The two strongest grounds are:  
             An undisclosed indirect financial interest in Whitmore Fund II LP         — the fund that owns Volaris Manufacturing Group LLC, the direct beneficiary of the disputed below-market license — held through a fund-of-funds product (the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund) managed by the same firm (Northbridge Wealth Advisors) that manages the arbitrator’s personal portfolio. The arbitrator’s statement that she holds         “    no direct equity positions in any party    ”         is technically accurate but materially misleading by omission, because Rule R-17(a)(i) expressly reaches             “        direct or indirect        ”         interests in         “    any entity that may be affected by the result of the proceeding.    ”  
             An undisclosed professional relationship between the arbitrator’s spouse, Dr. Alan Fairhaven, and the claimant’s key expert witness, Dr. Rita Peshkov    , evidenced by their co-authorship of a peer-reviewed paper on phase-change heat exchangers (the precise technology at issue) published in September 2022. The disclosure affirmatively states that the arbitrator’s husband         “    has no financial or professional relationship with Cascade … or any of their officers, directors, or employees    ”         — a statement that the publication record appears to contradict.  
      Either ground, standing alone, is sufficient to give rise to justifiable doubt as to the arbitrator’s impartiality. Together with the additional omissions described below — an undisclosed CLE co-panelist relationship with respondent’s lead counsel on a directly germane topic; an undisclosed shared organizational affiliation with the respondent’s principal, Garrett T. Whitmore; an undisclosed pending arbitration involving respondent’s counsel; and a pronounced repeat-player/fee-dependency pattern — they present a compelling case for disqualification.  
          Deadline.         Under Rule R-18(a), a challenge must be filed within seven (7) calendar days after the appointment was communicated or the challenging party learned of the grounds, whichever is later. The arbitrator’s disclosure was submitted June 17, 2024, yielding a             deadline of June 24, 2024    . We recommend that the arbitrator be asked immediately to supplement her disclosure and to consider withdrawal; if she does not withdraw, a formal challenge should be filed before the deadline, and all grounds should be preserved for any later motion to vacate.  
          A note on perspective.         This memorandum is written from the perspective of counsel for the respondent, Whitmore Capital Partners LLC, which ranked Judge Fairhaven as its first choice for sole arbitrator. The assessment is nevertheless objective: it identifies grounds that would support a challenge by             either         party, indicates which party each ground most naturally favors, and candidly evaluates the strategic implications — including the risk that an award rendered by an arbitrator with undisclosed conflicts could be vacated, an outcome that is not in any party’s interest.  
      
        II. Procedural Framework and Governing Standards  
       A. The AAA disclosure duty (Rules R-16 and R-17)  
      Rule R-16 establishes the governing standard: an arbitrator         “    shall disclose to the AAA any circumstance likely to give rise to             justifiable doubt         as to the arbitrator’s impartiality or independence.    ”         The rule further provides that the obligation         “    shall be construed broadly to further the goal of ensuring that arbitrators are and remain impartial and independent,    ”         and that the standard         “    is intended to encompass all circumstances that a reasonable person would consider relevant in evaluating an arbitrator’s ability to render a fair and unbiased determination.    ”  
      Rule R-17(a) enumerates four non-exhaustive categories of required disclosure:  
              (i)         Any known             direct or indirect         financial or personal interest in the outcome,         “    including any interest, whether legal or beneficial, in the subject matter of the dispute, in any party, or in             any entity that may be affected by the result of the proceeding    ”    ;  
              (ii)         Any known existing or past financial, business, professional, family, or social relationships with any party, its counsel, representatives, or witnesses —         “    including … employment, professional association, joint ventures, board memberships,             academic collaboration    , or regular social contact    ”    ;  
              (iii)         Any prior or pending cases in which the arbitrator has been involved         “    with any party to the arbitration, its counsel, or representatives,    ”         in any capacity and before any institution; and  
              (iv)         A broad catch-all covering         “    any other circumstances that might cause a reasonable party to question the arbitrator’s ability to render a fair and impartial determination,    ”         expressly including         “    the arbitrator’s prior publications, public statements, or expressed views on legal or factual issues relevant to the dispute.    ”  
      Rule R-17(b) makes disclosure a             continuing obligation    : the arbitrator         “    shall not await inquiry by a party or by the AAA before making any required supplemental disclosure.    ”         Rule R-17(d) provides that failure to make complete and timely disclosures         “    may result in the arbitrator’s removal … and may constitute grounds for vacatur of any resulting award under applicable law, including … Section 10 of the Federal Arbitration Act.    ”  
        B. The challenge procedure (Rule R-18)  
      Rule R-18(a) permits any party to challenge an arbitrator         “    on grounds that circumstances exist that give rise to justifiable doubt as to the arbitrator’s impartiality or independence.    ”         The challenge must be made by written notice to the AAA and the other party,         “    stating with specificity the grounds for the challenge,    ”         within             seven (7) calendar days         after (i) the appointment was communicated to the challenging party, or (ii) the circumstances giving rise to the challenge         “    became known or should reasonably have become known to the challenging party,             whichever is later    .    ”  
      Two features of Rule R-18 are important here:  
          The         “    whichever is later    ”         language means that the seven-day clock may run from the date a party learns of an             undisclosed         circumstance, not merely from the date of the disclosure. This is significant because several of the grounds below were not disclosed and came to light only through independent research.  
          Rule R-18(f) provides that a party that fails to make a timely challenge         “    shall be deemed to have waived its right to challenge the arbitrator on the grounds then known to the party,    ”             except         for         “    grounds that were not and could not reasonably have been known at the time of the deadline.    ”         Critically, the rule also preserves the right to seek vacatur:         “    Nothing in this Rule shall preclude a party from raising issues of evident partiality in a proceeding to confirm, modify, or vacate an award under applicable law, provided that such issues were not previously known and subject to waiver.    ”  
        C. The Federal Arbitration Act —         “    evident partiality    ”  
      Section 10(a)(2) of the FAA, 9 U.S.C. § 10(a)(2), authorizes vacatur of an arbitral award         “    where there was             evident partiality or corruption         in the arbitrators.    ”         The Supreme Court has instructed that arbitrators must avoid not only actual bias but the             appearance         of bias, and that nondisclosure of relationships bearing on impartiality can itself constitute evident partiality.             See Commonwealth Coatings Corp. v. Continental Casualty Co.    , 393 U.S. 145 (1968). The FAA standard operates independently of the AAA’s administrative challenge process: even if no timely Rule R-18 challenge is filed, undisclosed conflicts creating evident partiality may support vacatur after an award is rendered. This is the central strategic consideration discussed in Part VI.  
        D. The IBA Guidelines and the AAA/ABA Code of Ethics  
      The AAA rules expressly direct attention to two additional authorities (see the Editor’s Note to the rules excerpts):  
          The             IBA Guidelines on Conflicts of Interest in International Arbitration         (2014), which categorize potential conflicts into a Non-Waivable Red List, a Waivable Red List, an Orange List (circumstances that should be disclosed and that, depending on the parties’ response, may be waivable), and a Green List. The General Standard requires disclosure of any facts that         “    may, in the eyes of the parties, give rise to justifiable doubts as to the arbitrator’s impartiality or independence.    ”         Although not binding in a domestic AAA proceeding, the IBA lists are widely consulted as a benchmark for disclosure adequacy.  
          The             AAA/ABA Code of Ethics for Arbitrators in Commercial Disputes    , Canon II, which imposes a duty to disclose         “    any relationship … that might affect impartiality or lack of independence,    ”         including relationships of the arbitrator’s family members.  
        E. The governing standard in summary  
      For purposes of a Rule R-18 challenge, the test is whether the circumstances give rise to             justifiable doubt         as to impartiality or independence — a standard that the rules instruct be         “    construed broadly.    ”         For purposes of post-award vacatur, the test is             evident partiality         under FAA § 10(a)(2). The former is broader and more readily satisfied; the latter is the more demanding standard that nonetheless is implicated here because the most serious omissions involve financial interests and witness relationships of the type courts have treated as supporting vacatur.

        IV. Detailed Analysis of Each Ground  
       Ground 1 — Undisclosed indirect financial interest in Whitmore Fund II LP  
          Facts.         The arbitrator’s disclosure states that her personal investment portfolio is managed on a discretionary basis by Northbridge Wealth Advisors and that she holds         “    no direct equity positions in any party to this arbitration.    ”         She further states that she         “    has not undertaken a position-by-position review of each fund or sub-investment held within my managed accounts    ”         and that she         “    rel[ies] on Northbridge Wealth Advisors to manage my portfolio.    ”  
      Public SEC filings and fund disclosure documents reveal that Northbridge Wealth Advisors manages a fund-of-funds product, the             Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund    , which (per its most recent public filing, dated March 2024) holds a             2.3% limited partner interest in Whitmore Fund II LP    . Whitmore Fund II LP is the Delaware limited partnership that owns             Volaris Manufacturing Group LLC         — the direct beneficiary of the disputed below-market ThermalCore technology license that is the subject of this arbitration. Whitmore Capital Partners LLC (the respondent) serves as general partner and investment manager of Whitmore Fund II LP.  
      The economic chain is as follows:  
      Arbitrator’s portfolio (managed by Northbridge Wealth Advisors) → potential allocation to the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund (managed by the same firm) → 2.3% LP interest in Whitmore Fund II LP → Whitmore Fund II LP owns Volaris → Volaris is the beneficiary of the disputed $2.8M/year license (independently valued at $9.5M/year).  
          Disclosure status.         Not disclosed. The arbitrator’s         “    no direct equity positions    ”         formulation is technically accurate but does not address             indirect         interests, fund-of-funds exposure, or the specific holdings of any pooled vehicle. The disclosure’s acknowledgment that the arbitrator has not reviewed her holdings is, paradoxically, an admission that she has not performed the inquiry Rule R-17 requires.  
          Governing standard.         Rule R-17(a)(i) requires disclosure of         “    any known             direct or indirect         financial or personal interest in the outcome of the arbitration, including any interest, whether legal or beneficial, in the subject matter of the dispute, in any party, or in             any entity that may be affected by the result of the proceeding    .    ”         (Emphasis added.) The         “    direct or indirect    ”         and         “    any entity that may be affected    ”         language is squarely on point.  
          Analysis.         Three points bear emphasis.  
      First, Whitmore Fund II LP is         “    an entity that may be affected by the result of the proceeding    ”         within the plain meaning of Rule R-17(a)(i). The claimant seeks rescission of the Volaris license, disgorgement of Volaris’s profits, and damages against Whitmore Capital Partners. Each form of relief would reduce the value of Volaris and, by extension, the portfolio value of Whitmore Fund II LP. An award in favor of the claimant would therefore diminish the value of the 2.3% LP interest held by the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund. The arbitrator thus has a potential             indirect financial interest that would be harmed by a claimant victory         — i.e., an interest aligned with the respondent.  
      Second, the         “    no direct equity positions    ”         language is the kind of technically-true-but-evasive formulation that courts have criticized in the evident-partiality context. The duty is to disclose             indirect         interests; a statement limited to             direct         equity does not satisfy it. The arbitrator’s own concession that she has not reviewed her holdings does not cure the deficiency — it compounds it, because Rule R-17 imposes a duty of             diligent inquiry    , and the arbitrator’s portfolio is managed by the very firm that manages the fund holding an interest in a party-related entity. At a minimum, the arbitrator was obligated to disclose the Northbridge relationship and the existence of the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund, and to inquire whether her portfolio included an allocation to it.  
      Third, under the IBA Guidelines, an arbitrator’s financial interest in a party or in the outcome falls on the             Red List         (non-waivable where the interest is direct; waivable in certain indirect configurations). Even if the interest here is too attenuated to be non-waivable, it is at minimum an Orange-List circumstance requiring disclosure and party consideration.  
          Caveat.         The public record does not confirm that the arbitrator’s personal portfolio includes an allocation to the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund. The significance of this ground therefore depends on facts within the arbitrator’s knowledge (or that she could ascertain by inquiry of her portfolio manager). This is precisely why the appropriate first step is to demand that the arbitrator make the inquiry and supplement her disclosure. The             failure to disclose the potential exposure and the failure to inquire         are themselves the violation; confirmation of an actual allocation would elevate the ground to the most serious in this matter.  
          Strength: Strong.         This is a direct hit on Rule R-17(a)(i)’s         “    direct or indirect    ”         /         “    any entity that may be affected    ”         language and creates independent FAA § 10(a)(2) evident-partiality exposure.             Favors the claimant    , because the interest is aligned with the respondent.  
        Ground 2 — Undisclosed relationship between the arbitrator’s spouse and the claimant’s key expert witness  
          Facts.         The arbitrator’s husband, Dr. Alan Fairhaven, is a retired professor of mechanical engineering at the University of Minnesota. Academic publication databases reveal that Dr. Alan Fairhaven co-authored a peer-reviewed paper with             Dr. Rita S. Peshkov    , Cascade’s Vice President of Research & Development and the claimant’s anticipated key expert witness on the ThermalCore technology:  
              Title:         “    Optimization of Phase-Change Heat Exchangers in High-Load Industrial Applications    ”  
              Journal:             Journal of Advanced Thermal Engineering    , Vol. 42, Issue 3  
              Date:         September 2022  
              Authors:         Alan R. Fairhaven, Ph.D.; Rita S. Peshkov, Ph.D.  
      The paper’s subject — optimization of phase-change heat exchangers in high-load industrial applications — is the precise technical subject of the ThermalCore technology portfolio at the center of this arbitration. Dr. Peshkov is identified in the arbitration demand as the claimant’s foremost technical authority and a key expert witness on (among other things) the technical basis for the valuation methodology employed by Ridgeline Advisory Group.  
          Disclosure status.         Not disclosed — and, more seriously,             affirmatively contradicted    . The disclosure states that Dr. Alan Fairhaven         “    has no financial or professional relationship with Cascade Thermal Systems Inc., Whitmore Capital Partners LLC, or any of their officers, directors, or employees,    ”         and that he         “    is not currently engaged in any active research collaborations with industrial partners.    ”         The co-authorship record appears to contradict the first of these statements: Dr. Peshkov is an officer (Vice President of R&D) and employee of Cascade, and the co-authored paper reflects a professional relationship with her.  
          Governing standard.         Rule R-17(a)(ii) requires disclosure of relationships with         “    witnesses,    ”         expressly including relationships arising from         “    academic collaboration.    ”         The AAA/ABA Code of Ethics, Canon II, extends the disclosure duty to relationships of the arbitrator’s family members. Rule R-17(a)(iv) (the catch-all) independently reaches circumstances that might cause a reasonable party to question impartiality.  
          Analysis.         Co-authorship of a peer-reviewed journal article in a specialized field ordinarily reflects a sustained professional collaboration — joint research design, data collection, drafting, and revision over many months. The September 2022 publication date indicates that the collaboration was active well before the arbitrator’s June 2024 appointment, and the relationship may have continued beyond publication. The subject-matter nexus is striking: the paper concerns the very technology (phase-change heat exchangers for high-load industrial applications) whose valuation is the central dispute in this arbitration. The arbitrator’s spouse is therefore not merely acquainted with the claimant’s key expert; he is a             technical peer and collaborator in the same narrow field    , and may hold views on the witness’s methodology, credibility, or expertise that bear on the arbitrator’s assessment of the witness’s testimony.  
      The affirmative misstatement is the most troubling feature. A disclosure that says         “    no professional relationship    ”         when a discoverable publication record shows the contrary is worse than a mere omission: it suggests either that the arbitrator did not perform the inquiry the rules require (her husband’s publication record is readily ascertainable) or that she made an inaccurate representation to the parties. Under Rule R-17(d), failure to make complete disclosures         “    may constitute grounds for vacatur    ”    ; an affirmative misstatement is, if anything, more serious.  
          Strength: Strong.         The witness relationship, the subject-matter nexus, and the affirmative misstatement combine to make this a powerful ground.             Favors the respondent    , because the relationship is with the claimant’s expert witness and thus suggests potential bias toward the claimant. This is the ground most naturally available to Whitmore.  
        Ground 3 — Undisclosed CLE co-panelist relationship with respondent’s lead counsel  
          Facts.         On October 14, 2023, the Lakeview Dispute Resolution Center hosted a Continuing Legal Education event titled             “        Arbitrating Complex IP Licensing Disputes.        ”         The listed panelists were:  
          Hon. Margaret R. Fairhaven (Ret.) — the sole arbitrator in this matter;  
              Dominic Hargrave         — Lead Partner, Barrow & Thistlewood LLP, and lead counsel for the respondent in this arbitration;  
          Professor Naomi J. Strickland, University of Minnesota Law School; and  
          Marcus DelVecchio, Partner, Henning & Burwell P.A.  
      The event occurred approximately five months before the arbitration demand was filed (March 15, 2024) and approximately eight months before the arbitrator’s appointment was confirmed (June 10, 2024). The topic — arbitrating complex IP licensing disputes — is             directly germane         to this arbitration, which concerns the valuation and enforceability of a technology license for Cascade’s proprietary ThermalCore heat-exchange technology.  
          Disclosure status.         Not disclosed. The arbitrator disclosed prior and pending arbitrations in which Barrow & Thistlewood LLP appeared as counsel, but she did not disclose this personal, extra-arbitral professional interaction with Mr. Hargrave.  
          Governing standard.         Rule R-17(a)(ii) reaches         “    social    ”         and         “    professional    ”         relationships with counsel, including those arising from         “    professional association.    ”         Rule R-17(a)(iv) reaches         “    expressed views on legal or factual issues relevant to the dispute    ”         (see Ground 8). Under the IBA Guidelines, recent professional contact with counsel — particularly collaborative contact such as co-panelist service — falls within the Orange List (disclose).  
          Analysis.         Co-panelist service at a CLE event involves shared preparation, coordination of presentation materials, interaction during the panel, and typically informal professional interaction before and after the event. This is a personal relationship with lead counsel — not merely a firm-level repeat-appointment relationship of the kind the arbitrator did disclose. Two features elevate it beyond a routine Green-List organizational co-membership: (a) the             directly germane topic    , which means the arbitrator and Mr. Hargrave likely discussed and presented views on the very subject of this dispute (IP licensing valuation, arbitrator selection in technology-intensive cases, management of expert testimony); and (b) the             recency         (eight months before appointment) and the fact that the event was hosted at Lakeview, the arbitrator’s own affiliated institution. The arbitrator’s disclosure of firm-level repeat appointments, while omitting this personal interaction with the specific lead partner, is inconsistent.  
          Strength: Moderate–Strong.         A single CLE co-panelist event might, in isolation, be characterized as routine professional contact. The directly germane topic, the personal (not merely firm) relationship with lead counsel, the recency, and the failure to disclose combine to raise justifiable doubt.             Favors the claimant    , because the relationship is with respondent’s lead counsel.  
        Ground 4 — Undisclosed shared organizational service with respondent’s principal  
          Facts.         The arbitrator has served on the             advisory board         of the Minnesota ADR Ethics Institute since 2021 (disclosed). Ellen Wu, a Senior Partner at Barrow & Thistlewood LLP and head of that firm’s national arbitration practice, also serves on the advisory board (disclosed). Public IRS Form 990 filings for fiscal year 2023 reveal that             Garrett T. Whitmore         — the Founder and Managing Partner of Whitmore Capital Partners LLC (the respondent) and an anticipated adverse fact witness — serves on the             Board of Trustees         of the same Institute. The Board of Trustees is the Institute’s governing body, responsible for strategic direction, budgetary oversight, and appointments to leadership positions, including the advisory board on which the arbitrator sits. The Institute is described as a small nonprofit; its bylaws and posted minutes indicate that the Board of Trustees and the advisory board hold joint sessions and attend annual meetings together.  
          Disclosure status.         Partial / incomplete. The arbitrator disclosed her own advisory board service and Ms. Wu’s advisory board service, but she did             not         disclose Mr. Whitmore’s service on the Board of Trustees. Notably, Ms. Wu is not lead counsel on this matter; Mr. Whitmore is the respondent’s principal and a witness.  
          Governing standard.         Rule R-17(a)(ii) reaches relationships with parties and their representatives, including those arising from         “    board memberships.    ”         Rule R-17(a)(iv) (catch-all) independently applies. Under the IBA Guidelines, an arbitrator’s organizational co-membership with a party or party representative is generally an Orange-List item requiring disclosure, particularly where the organization is small and the bodies interact.  
          Analysis.         The most telling feature is the             inconsistency         of the disclosure. Having identified the Minnesota ADR Ethics Institute as a relevant affiliation and disclosed one party-connected individual (Ms. Wu) associated with it, the arbitrator was obligated to disclose the other — Mr. Whitmore — who is more central to this proceeding (the respondent’s principal and a witness) than Ms. Wu (a non-lead partner). The governance structure compounds the concern: the Board of Trustees appoints and oversees the advisory board, so there is a hierarchical relationship between Mr. Whitmore’s body and the arbitrator’s. The small size of the organization and the joint meeting structure increase the likelihood of direct personal interaction. The arbitrator’s disclosure expressly states that she has         “    not socialized with, and do[es] not maintain a personal friendship with, any counsel of record or any individual identified in the pleadings as a party representative or anticipated witness    ”         — a statement that, at minimum, does not address her shared organizational service with the respondent’s principal.  
          Strength: Moderate–Strong.         The partial disclosure (Wu but not Whitmore) and the hierarchical governance relationship elevate this above ordinary organizational co-membership.             Favors the claimant    , because the relationship is with the respondent’s principal and witness.  
        Ground 5 — Undisclosed pending arbitration involving respondent’s counsel  
          Facts.         The arbitrator’s disclosure lists seven prior or pending arbitrations involving Barrow & Thistlewood LLP or Fielding Marsh & Collier LLP, and certifies that the list is         “    complete.    ”         The compiled appointment data identifies an additional             pending         arbitration —             Halcyon Biotech v. Stonewall Partners         (AAA Case No. 01-24-0003105) — in which Barrow & Thistlewood LLP represents a party and in which the arbitrator serves as sole arbitrator (appointed April 22, 2024; pre-hearing phase; estimated remaining fees $175,000). This matter does not appear in the disclosure.  
          Disclosure status.         Not disclosed. The arbitrator disclosed only two pending matters involving Barrow & Thistlewood LLP: the instant case and             Orion Fabrication v. Mountain West Supply    . The             Halcyon Biotech         matter is a third.  
          Governing standard.         Rule R-17(a)(iii) expressly requires disclosure of         “    any prior or pending cases in which the arbitrator has been or is involved … with any party to the arbitration, its counsel, or representatives,    ”         including matters before any institution and in any capacity. Pending matters are particularly significant because they reflect an             ongoing         relationship with counsel.  
          Analysis.         The omission of a pending matter is a direct, unambiguous violation of Rule R-17(a)(iii). The arbitrator’s certification that her list is         “    complete    ”         (    “    to the best of my recollection, the foregoing represents a complete list    ”    ) is contradicted by the record. Whether the omission was inadvertent or deliberate, the result is that the disclosure understates the number of concurrent pending matters in which the arbitrator and respondent’s counsel are engaged — a fact material to the parties’ evaluation of the arbitrator’s independence, particularly in light of the repeat-player pattern discussed in Ground 6. Pending matters are more consequential than completed ones for purposes of apparent partiality, because the arbitrator has a continuing professional and economic relationship with the firm in those matters.  
          Strength: Moderate–Strong.         The express enumeration of pending cases in Rule R-17(a)(iii) and the         “    complete list    ”         certification make this a clear deficiency.             Favors the claimant    , because the undisclosed relationship is with respondent’s counsel.

The estimated remaining fees in the three Barrow & Thistlewood pending matters total approximately             $560,000    , or approximately             67%         of the arbitrator’s total estimated pending fees ($835,000). The concentration of pending fee income in Barrow & Thistlewood matters is even higher than the concentration in completed matters.  
              Selection dynamics.         During the arbitrator-selection ranking process, Whitmore Capital Partners LLC ranked Judge Fairhaven as its             first choice    , while Cascade Thermal Systems Inc. ranked her             third    .  
          Disclosure status.         The arbitrator disclosed 6 of the 13 completed matters involving the parties’ counsel and 2 of the 3 pending matters involving Barrow & Thistlewood. She did not disclose the broader repeat-appointment pattern, the fee concentration, or the prevailing-rate disparity.  
          Governing standard.         Rule R-17(a)(iii) requires disclosure of prior and pending cases with counsel — a duty the seven omitted completed matters and the one omitted pending matter directly violate. Rule R-16 and Rule R-17(a)(iv) (the catch-all) reach the broader pattern: circumstances that         “    a reasonable person would consider relevant in evaluating an arbitrator’s ability to render a fair and unbiased determination.    ”         The IBA Guidelines and the AAA/ABA Code of Ethics both recognize that a pattern of repeat appointments and economic dependency on a particular firm or party can give rise to justifiable doubt, even where no single appointment is disqualifying.  
          Analysis.         This ground has two components, of different force.  
      The             non-disclosure of specific matters         (seven completed, one pending) is a clear, repeated violation of Rule R-17(a)(iii). Each omitted matter is, in itself, a disclosure failure; the aggregate is more serious because it indicates a systemic under-disclosure of the arbitrator’s relationship with respondent’s counsel. The arbitrator’s         “    complete list    ”         certification is contradicted by the data.  
      The             structural pattern         — 45% of completed appointments and 48% of income from one firm, with that firm’s clients prevailing at a reported rate of 77.8% (against 25% for the claimant’s counsel), and 67% of pending fees concentrated in that firm’s matters — is not, by itself, proof of actual bias. Statistical patterns are generally treated with caution in challenge analysis, because outcomes reflect the merits of individual cases. But the pattern is relevant to the             appearance         of partiality under the         “    justifiable doubt    ”         standard, particularly when combined with (a) the firm’s #1 ranking of the arbitrator in this matter, (b) the undisclosed CLE and Institute connections to the firm and its client’s principal, and (c) the undisclosed pending matter. The economic dependency is concrete: approximately $1.02 million in completed-matter fees and approximately $560,000 in pending-matter fees from Barrow & Thistlewood over a 36-month window, against total estimated income of approximately $2.125 million. A reasonable party could conclude that the arbitrator has a structural incentive to maintain the favor of respondent’s counsel, on whom a substantial portion of her arbitration practice depends.  
          Strength: Moderate–Strong.         The specific non-disclosures are strong (clear Rule R-17(a)(iii) violations); the structural-bias argument is moderate but materially reinforced by the surrounding omissions.             Favors the claimant    , because the pattern favors respondent’s counsel.  
        Ground 7 — Incomplete disclosure of the Lakeview affiliation  
          Facts.         The arbitrator is affiliated with the Lakeview Dispute Resolution Center as a neutral. Lakeview receives case referrals from both Barrow & Thistlewood LLP and Fielding Marsh & Collier LLP. Lakeview also hosted the October 14, 2023 CLE event at which the arbitrator and Mr. Hargrave served as co-panelists (Ground 3). The public record does not reveal the financial structure of the arbitrator’s Lakeview affiliation — whether it is employment, independent contracting, or equity/membership; whether Lakeview takes a percentage of the arbitrator’s fees; or whether Lakeview’s revenue depends on maintaining referral relationships with the firms.  
          Disclosure status.         Partial. The arbitrator disclosed the affiliation and the fact of referrals from both firms, and stated her view that the referrals         “    do not influence my decision-making.    ”         She did not disclose the financial terms of the affiliation or the extent of the referral dependency.  
          Governing standard.         Rule R-17(a)(i) (financial interest) and R-17(a)(ii) (business relationships with counsel). The IBA Guidelines address institutional affiliations and fee-sharing arrangements between arbitrators and arbitral institutions.  
          Analysis.         The arbitrator’s disclosure acknowledges the referral relationships but resolves the conflict by assertion rather than by disclosure of the underlying financial facts. If Lakeview’s revenue model depends on referrals from Barrow & Thistlewood and Fielding Marsh, and if the arbitrator’s compensation is tied (directly or indirectly) to Lakeview’s revenue or referral flow, then the arbitrator has an indirect financial interest in maintaining the firms’ favor — an interest that overlaps with and reinforces the repeat-player concern in Ground 6. The significance of this ground depends on facts not in the public record; the appropriate response is to demand that the arbitrator disclose the financial terms of her Lakeview affiliation.  
          Strength: Moderate.         The affiliation is disclosed; the financial terms are not. This is a         “    disclose more    ”         deficiency rather than a complete non-disclosure, but it is non-trivial given the referral dependency and the CLE event hosted at Lakeview.             Favors the claimant    , because Barrow & Thistlewood is the more frequent referrer.  
        Ground 8 — Prior expressed views on a directly germane subject  
          Facts.         As described in Ground 3, the arbitrator co-panelled with respondent’s lead counsel at an October 14, 2023 CLE event titled         “    Arbitrating Complex IP Licensing Disputes,    ”         addressing (among other topics) valuation methodologies, arbitrator selection in technology-intensive cases, and the management of expert testimony. The valuation methodologies at issue in this arbitration — comparable transactions and discounted cash flow — are precisely the methodologies employed by Ridgeline Advisory Group in arriving at the $9.5 million fair-market-value figure that the claimant advances and that the respondent disputes.  
          Disclosure status.         Not disclosed.  
          Governing standard.         Rule R-17(a)(iv) expressly requires disclosure of         “    the arbitrator’s prior publications, public statements, or expressed views on legal or factual issues relevant to the dispute.    ”  
          Analysis.         If the arbitrator expressed views at the CLE event on IP licensing valuation methodologies, arbitrator selection in technology-intensive cases, or the management of expert testimony, those views could bear on her assessment of the Ridgeline valuation and of Dr. Peshkov’s expert testimony. The catch-all provision is intended to capture exactly this kind of circumstance. This ground overlaps with Ground 3 but is analytically distinct: Ground 3 concerns the             relationship         with counsel; Ground 8 concerns the arbitrator’s             substantive views         on issues central to the dispute. The significance depends on what the arbitrator actually said, which can be ascertained from the CLE recording or materials.  
          Strength: Moderate.         Depends on the content of the arbitrator’s remarks, but the directly germane topic and the express terms of Rule R-17(a)(iv) make inquiry warranted.             Favors the claimant         to the extent the arbitrator’s expressed views align with the respondent’s valuation position; potentially favors either party depending on the content.  
      
         V. Cumulative Assessment  
      The eight grounds above should not be evaluated in isolation. The cumulative effect of multiple, overlapping non-disclosures is the central feature of this matter, and it is what elevates the case for challenge from arguable to compelling.  
          1. The disclosure is internally inconsistent.         The arbitrator disclosed her advisory board service alongside Ms. Wu (a Barrow & Thistlewood partner) but not alongside Mr. Whitmore (the respondent’s principal and a witness) on the same organization’s governing board. She disclosed firm-level repeat appointments with Barrow & Thistlewood but not her personal co-panelist appearance with the firm’s lead partner on a directly germane topic. She disclosed         “    no direct equity positions    ”         but not the indirect exposure running through her portfolio manager’s fund-of-funds product. She certified her arbitration list as         “    complete    ”         but omitted one pending and seven completed matters involving the parties’ counsel. Each inconsistency independently undermines confidence in the diligence of the disclosure.  
          2. The undisclosed connections run in both directions, but predominantly favor the respondent.         Ground 1 (the indirect financial interest in Whitmore Fund II LP) and Grounds 3, 4, 5, 6, and 7 (relationships with and economic dependency on respondent’s counsel and principal) all suggest potential bias             toward the respondent    . Ground 2 (the spouse’s relationship with the claimant’s expert witness) suggests potential bias             toward the claimant    . The fact that undisclosed connections point in both directions does not neutralize them; it means that             both parties have grounds to challenge    , and that the arbitrator’s impartiality is questionable from either perspective. This is significant because it reduces the likelihood that any award will be insulated from vacatur: a party that prevails before this arbitrator faces a credible motion to vacate by the losing party on evident-partiality grounds.  
          3. The                 “        justifiable doubt        ”                 standard is readily satisfied; the                 “        evident partiality        ”                 standard is implicated.         Under the broad Rule R-16/R-17         “    justifiable doubt    ”         standard, the combination of an indirect financial interest in an entity affected by the result, an affirmative misstatement regarding a witness relationship, and a pattern of non-disclosure of counsel relationships is more than sufficient. Under the more demanding FAA § 10(a)(2)         “    evident partiality    ”         standard, the financial interest (Ground 1) and the affirmative misstatement (Ground 2) are the categories most likely to support vacatur, particularly given the Supreme Court’s instruction in             Commonwealth Coatings         that arbitrators must avoid even the appearance of bias and that nondisclosure of relationships bearing on impartiality can itself constitute evident partiality.  
          4. The arbitrator’s own concessions aggravate the deficiencies.         The disclosure’s acknowledgments that the arbitrator         “    has not undertaken a position-by-position review    ”         of her investments and that her arbitration list is         “    complete    ”         “    to the best of my recollection    ”         are, in context, admissions that the inquiry underlying the disclosure was less than diligent. Rule R-17 imposes a duty of diligent and continuing inquiry; reliance on a portfolio manager and on recollection does not satisfy that duty where the relevant facts are ascertainable.  
      
        VI. Procedural Options and Recommendation  
       A. The available options  
          Option 1 — Request supplemental disclosure and invite withdrawal.         Under Rule R-17(b) and (c), the arbitrator has a continuing duty to disclose, and the AAA may bring disclosure deficiencies to the arbitrator’s attention and request supplemental disclosure before acting on a challenge. The most efficient first step is to request that the arbitrator supplement her disclosure to address each of the eight categories above — specifically, to (a) inquire of Northbridge Wealth Advisors whether her portfolio includes an allocation to the Northbridge Select Alternatives Fund and disclose any indirect interest in Whitmore Fund II LP; (b) address her spouse’s co-authorship with Dr. Peshkov and correct the         “    no professional relationship    ”         statement; (c) disclose the October 14, 2023 CLE co-panelist appearance with Mr. Hargrave and any views expressed on IP licensing valuation; (d) disclose Mr. Whitmore’s service on the Institute’s Board of Trustees; (e) disclose the             Halcyon Biotech         pending matter and the seven omitted completed matters; and (f) disclose the financial terms of her Lakeview affiliation. A candid supplemental disclosure may lead the arbitrator to conclude that withdrawal is appropriate.  
          Option 2 — File a formal challenge under Rule R-18.         If the arbitrator does not withdraw (or if the supplemental disclosure is inadequate), a formal written challenge should be filed with the AAA, with copy to opposing counsel,         “    stating with specificity the grounds for the challenge,    ”         before the             June 24, 2024         deadline. The challenge should plead the strongest grounds first (Grounds 1 and 2), followed by the cumulative effect of Grounds 3–8. Under Rule R-18(d), if the other party does not agree to the challenge and the arbitrator does not withdraw, the AAA will decide the challenge on its merits.  
          Option 3 — Proceed and preserve vacatur grounds.         Rule R-18(f) preserves the right to seek vacatur under FAA § 10(a)(2) for grounds that         “    were not and could not reasonably have been known    ”         at the time of the deadline. Because several grounds (the indirect financial interest, the spouse’s co-authorship, the CLE event, Mr. Whitmore’s trustee role, and the             Halcyon Biotech         matter) were not disclosed and came to light only through independent research, a party may argue that the seven-day waiver does not foreclose a later vacatur motion even if no timely Rule R-18 challenge is filed. This option is the riskiest: it contemplates proceeding through a 15-day evidentiary hearing and a final award, then seeking to vacate — a path that is expensive, uncertain, and destructive to the finality that arbitration is meant to provide.  
        B. Strategic considerations for the respondent  
      Whitmore Capital Partners ranked Judge Fairhaven as its first choice for sole arbitrator, and several of the undisclosed connections (Grounds 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7) might superficially appear to favor the respondent. The temptation, from the respondent’s perspective, is to defend the arbitrator against any claimant challenge and to proceed. We strongly caution against that approach, for three reasons.  
          First, the vacatur risk is symmetric and serious.         An award rendered by an arbitrator with undisclosed financial interests and an affirmative misstatement about a witness relationship is a candidate for vacatur under FAA § 10(a)(2). If the respondent prevails before this arbitrator, the claimant will have strong grounds to move to vacate on evident-partiality grounds — and may well succeed, given the financial-interest and misstatement issues. A vacated award wastes the substantial fees (approximately $340,000 in arbitrator fees alone, plus AAA administrative fees and party costs) and time (a 15-day hearing plus pre- and post-hearing work) invested in the proceeding, and requires a rehearing before a replacement arbitrator. The respondent’s interest in a             final and enforceable         award counsels in favor of a clean arbitrator now, not a tainted award later.  
          Second, the respondent has its own ground to challenge.         Ground 2 (the spouse’s co-authorship with the claimant’s expert witness, and the affirmative misstatement) is a strong ground that favors the respondent. If the respondent has doubts about the arbitrator’s ability to evaluate the claimant’s expert testimony impartially, it has an independent basis to seek disqualification — and doing so before the hearing is far more efficient than seeking vacatur after an adverse award.  
          Third, the ethical and reputational dimensions.         Proceeding with an arbitrator whose disclosure is known to be materially incomplete — particularly where an affirmative misstatement has been identified — exposes the proceeding to after-the-fact criticism and undermines the legitimacy of the result regardless of outcome. The AAA itself may, under Rule R-17(d), bring the disclosure deficiencies to the arbitrator’s attention and request supplemental disclosure; it is preferable for the parties to surface these issues promptly and cooperatively.  
        C. Recommendation  
             Immediately         request that the arbitrator supplement her disclosure to address each of the eight categories identified in this memorandum, and invite her withdrawal in light of the indirect financial interest (Ground 1) and the affirmative misstatement regarding the spouse-witness relationship (Ground 2). This request should be made in writing, with simultaneous copy to opposing counsel, and should be framed as a Rule R-17(b)/(c) supplemental-disclosure request rather than as a challenge, to preserve the option of a cooperative resolution.  
             In parallel    , prepare a formal Rule R-18 challenge to be filed before             June 24, 2024    , in the event that the arbitrator does not withdraw or does not adequately supplement. The challenge should be specific (as Rule R-18(a) requires), should plead Grounds 1 and 2 as the primary bases, and should plead Grounds 3–8 as cumulative support. Coordinate with the client on whether to lead with the respondent-favorable Ground 2 or to support a claimant-initiated challenge on the respondent-favorable Grounds 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, and 7.  
             Preserve all grounds         for any later motion to vacate under FAA § 10(a)(2). Document the dates on which each undisclosed circumstance was discovered (principally via the June 19, 2024 entity-relationship memorandum and the compiled appointment data), to support any argument under Rule R-18(f) that the seven-day waiver does not foreclose vacatur for grounds not reasonably knowable at the deadline.  
             Consider a joint approach.         Given that both parties have grounds to challenge, the most efficient resolution may be a joint request to the AAA that the arbitrator be replaced under Rule R-19, obviating the need for an adversarial challenge and preserving the scheduling order (preliminary hearing August 5, 2024; evidentiary hearing January 13–February 7, 2025) to the extent practicable. A replacement arbitrator appointed under Rule R-12 would determine whether any portion of the (still-early) proceedings must be repeated.  
      
         VII. Conclusion  
      The arbitrator’s June 17, 2024 disclosure statement is materially incomplete. It omits an indirect financial interest in an entity directly affected by the result of the proceeding (Whitmore Fund II LP); it affirmatively misrepresents the arbitrator’s spouse’s relationship with the claimant’s key expert witness; and it omits a co-panelist relationship with respondent’s lead counsel on a directly germane topic, a shared organizational affiliation with the respondent’s principal, a pending arbitration involving respondent’s counsel, and a substantial pattern of repeat appointments and fee dependency favoring respondent’s counsel. Several of these omissions constitute clear violations of AAA Rule R-17, and the combination creates a credible risk of vacatur under FAA § 10(a)(2).  
      We recommend prompt action before the June 24, 2024 deadline: a written request for supplemental disclosure and the arbitrator’s withdrawal, backed by a prepared Rule R-18 challenge, with all grounds preserved for any later vacatur proceeding. The objective — shared, we expect, by both parties — should be a proceeding before an arbitrator whose independence is beyond justifiable doubt, so that the resulting award is final and enforceable.

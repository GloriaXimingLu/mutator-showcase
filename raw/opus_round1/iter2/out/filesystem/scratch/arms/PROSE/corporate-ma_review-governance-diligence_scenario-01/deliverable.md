        I. Executive Summary  
      This memorandum summarizes our governance diligence review of Terravox Systems, Inc. (    “    Terravox    ”         or the         “    Company    ”    ) in connection with the proposed acquisition by Hargrove Capital Partners III, L.P. (    “    Hargrove    ”         or         “    Buyer    ”    ). We reviewed the Company’s Third Amended and Restated Certificate of Incorporation (the         “    Restated Certificate    ”    ), Amended and Restated Bylaws (the         “    Bylaws    ”    ), Stockholders’ Agreement, Voting Agreement, Registration Rights Agreement (the         “    RRA    ”    ), and Board minutes for the period January 2023 through September 2024. The transaction is structured as a reverse triangular merger at an enterprise value of $485 million (equity value approximately $473 million), with a target closing in Q1 2025.  
          The central finding of this review is that Stonemill Capital Partners Fund VI, L.P. (        “        Stonemill        ”        ) holds a hard, charter-level blocking right over the merger.         Because Stonemill holds 100% of the outstanding Series C Preferred Stock (7,800,000 shares), and the Restated Certificate requires the affirmative consent of holders of at least 66⅔% of the Series C Preferred Stock voting as a separate class to effect any         “    Liquidation Event    ”         (Restated Certificate § 4.3.6(b)(i)), the merger cannot be approved without Stonemill’s consent. The reverse triangular merger squarely qualifies as a         “    Liquidation Event    ”         (§ 4.3.2(d)(i)). This is the single most important governance fact in the transaction and the throughline of this memorandum.  
      A secondary, closely related question is whether the drag-along in the Stockholders’ Agreement can compel Stonemill to vote its Series C shares in favor of the merger, thereby satisfying the Series C separate-class vote over Stonemill’s objection. We conclude this is a             contested question under Delaware law         and that the drag-along should             not         be relied upon as a clean path to closing. The drag-along likely             can         be triggered without Stonemill’s affirmative support (the three-prong trigger can be satisfied by the Founders, Ridgeline, and Northbridge), but whether triggering it overrides the charter protective provision is unresolved and litigable. We recommend obtaining Stonemill’s consent as the primary path, while preserving the drag-along as negotiating leverage and a litigation backstop.  
      We also identified several additional issues that warrant attention before signing and closing, summarized by severity below and developed in the body of this memorandum.

         IV. Area 2 — Drag-Along Analysis  
       A. The Three-Prong Trigger (Stockholders’ Agreement § 3.1(a))  
      A         “    Drag-Along Approval    ”         requires the satisfaction of             all three         of the following:  
              Prong (i):         holders of at least a majority of the outstanding shares of Common Stock (on an as-converted basis, treating Preferred as converted);  
              Prong (ii):         holders of at least a majority of the outstanding Preferred Stock (on an as-converted basis, voting as a single class); and  
              Prong (iii):         at least two (2) of the three (3) Investor Stockholders.  
      Once triggered, every         “    Restricted Stockholder    ”         (including any that voted against) must (A) vote all of its Shares in favor of the Sale and         “    any other matter required to be approved in connection with such Sale,    ”         (B) refrain from exercising appraisal/dissenters’ rights, (C) take all actions necessary to consummate the Sale, and (D) not impede the Sale (§ 3.1(a)). Each party also granted an irrevocable proxy to the holders constituting the Drag-Along Approval (§ 3.3). Consideration is distributed under the charter’s liquidation waterfall         “    as if the Sale of the Company were a Liquidation Event    ”         (§ 3.2(a)).

        C. The Critical Legal Question: Can the Drag-Along Override the Series C Protective Provision?  
      Even if the drag-along is triggered without Stonemill, the decisive question is whether triggering it can compel Stonemill to vote its 7,800,000 Series C shares in favor of the merger — which, because Stonemill holds 100% of the Series C, would satisfy the 66⅔% Series C separate-class vote (§ 4.3.6(b)(i)). This is a             contested question under Delaware law    , and we recommend against relying on it as a clean path to closing.  
          Arguments that the drag-along overrides the protective provision (enforceable):  
          Stonemill is a party to the Stockholders’ Agreement and contractually agreed to the drag-along, which expressly requires it to vote in favor of         “    any other matter required to be approved in connection with such Sale of the Company    ”         (§ 3.1(a)(A)). The Series C separate-class vote is, by its terms,         “    a matter required to be approved in connection with such Sale.    ”  
          Delaware generally enforces stockholder voting agreements and drag-alongs (DGCL § 218(c)), and specific performance is an available remedy (§ 3.3; § 7.8 of the Stockholders’ Agreement).  
          The drag-along expressly preserves the charter waterfall (§ 3.2(a)), indicating the parties contemplated interaction with the charter.  
          Arguments that the protective provision is not overridden (Stonemill’s position):  
          The Series C separate-class protective provision (§ 4.3.6(b)(i)) was specifically negotiated to give Series C a veto over any         “    Liquidation Event    ”         — the very category into which this merger falls. Reading the drag-along to override that veto would substantially nullify the protective provision for the transaction it was designed to block.  
          The drag-along trigger requires only two of three Investor Stockholders (§ 3.1(a)(iii)), so Stonemill can be dragged without its own consent to the trigger. Allowing that to defeat a separately negotiated class veto raises serious contract-interpretation and fairness concerns.  
          The two instruments were executed simultaneously on November 3, 2021 and cross-reference each other; an ambiguity about whether the drag-along defeats the protective provision should, under standard interpretive principles, be resolved against the override, or at least treated as creating a triable issue.  
          The drag-along does not expressly state that it overrides charter protective provisions or class voting rights. Where a stockholder agreement would abrogate a charter-based class right, Delaware courts look for clear expression of that intent.  
          Practical assessment.         This is a close and litigable question. Even under the more aggressive reading, Stonemill would have colorable grounds to seek injunctive relief, which could delay or derail a Q1 2025 closing. The drag-along is therefore best understood as (i)             negotiating leverage         — it raises the cost and uncertainty of Stonemill’s obstruction — and (ii) a             litigation backstop         if negotiations fail,             not         a reliable substitute for Stonemill’s consent.  
          Recommendation.         Treat the Series C separate-class vote as a hard blocking right requiring Stonemill’s consent. Pursue Stonemill’s consent as the primary path to closing (anticipating that Stonemill may seek economic concessions — see Appendix B, which shows Stonemill already receives its full $144.3 million preference). Preserve the drag-along argument as a fallback and, if negotiations break down, be prepared to litigate specific performance — but do not underwrite a Q1 2025 close on the assumption that the drag-along overrides the protective provision.  
        D. Additional Drag-Along Mechanics  
              Irrevocable proxy (§ 3.3):         each party granted an irrevocable proxy to the Drag-Along Approval holders, supporting specific performance (but not resolving the override question).  
              No restrictive covenants (§ 3.2(d)):         no party can be required to enter a non-compete/non-solicit or to indemnify beyond its consideration — relevant to merger-agreement indemnity scope for dragged stockholders.  
              Several (not joint) indemnity (§ 3.2(c)):         dragged stockholders indemnify on a several basis, capped at their consideration — favorable for the buyer’s indemnity stack.  
              Amendment protection (§ 7.4):         any amendment to Article 3 requires the consent of each adversely affected Investor Stockholder — Stonemill cannot be subjected to a broadened drag-along without its consent.  
      
         V. Area 3 — Impediments to Closing a Reverse Triangular Merger  
       A. Defined-Term Capture  
      The reverse triangular merger is captured by all three operative defined terms:  
              “        Liquidation Event        ”                 (Restated Certificate § 4.3.2(d)(i)):         a merger/consolidation/business combination in which the Company’s pre-transaction stockholders do not retain at least 50% of the voting power of the surviving entity. Here, Terravox stockholders receive cash and retain 0% of the surviving entity (Hargrove owns 100% of the surviving subsidiary).             Captured.  
              “        Sale of the Company        ”                 (Stockholders’ Agreement § 1.1(i); Voting Agreement § 1(a)):         a merger/consolidation/reorganization in which stockholders do not retain a majority of the voting power of the surviving entity.             Captured.  
              “        Deemed Liquidation Event        ”                 (RRA § 9(c)):         undefined in the RRA — see Section VIII. If construed to mean the charter’s         “    Liquidation Event,    ”         the merger is captured; if not, registration rights may survive.             Ambiguous — must be addressed in the merger agreement.  
        B. Structural Considerations Specific to a Reverse Triangular Merger  
              Surviving subsidiary / contracts.         Because Terravox survives (rather than assigning contracts by merger), anti-assignment provisions that fire on         “    assignment by operation of law    ”         typically do not trigger.             However    , many commercial contracts (including the Permian Basin MSA and the Clearfield term loan) contain change-of-control triggers that fire regardless of structure.             Recommendation:         obtain a contracts list with change-of-control/assignment provisions and confirm consents; specifically confirm whether the Clearfield term loan requires lender consent to a change of control.  
              No charter amendment expected.         A reverse triangular merger typically does not amend the target’s charter, avoiding the additional § 8.1 protective consents. Confirm the merger agreement does not include a charter amendment that would trigger § 4.3.6(b)(ii).  
              20-day Liquidation Event notice (§ 4.3.2(d)).         The Company must give each Preferred holder written notice of the proposed Liquidation Event             not less than 20 days before the anticipated closing date    , with material terms, the anticipated closing date, and a good-faith per-share estimate.             Closing-condition/timing item:         build the notice into the signing-to-closing timeline and ensure the per-share estimates reflect the actual waterfall (Appendix B), not the $13.95 all-convert figure.  
              Merger consideration allocation.         The merger agreement must allocate consideration consistent with the charter waterfall. Because Series C will take its $18.50 preference rather than convert (Appendix B), the per-share consideration to Common (~$12.59) will be lower than the $13.95         “    average    ”         in the Board minutes; the consideration mechanics and disclosure-schedule estimates should match the actual waterfall.  
      
         VI. Area 4 — Transfer Restrictions (ROFR / Co-Sale)  
       A. ROFR and Co-Sale Do Not Apply to the Merger  
      The Stockholders’ Agreement’s transfer restrictions (ROFR (§ 2.2), co-sale (§ 2.3), tag-along (§ 2.4)) apply to a         “    Transfer    ”         of Shares by a stockholder to a third party. The merger is not a voluntary Transfer by an individual stockholder; it is a corporate transaction in which shares are cancelled for merger consideration. Two provisions confirm that a Sale of the Company is outside the ROFR/co-sale regime:  
              § 2.1(c):         the general transfer restriction does not apply to a Transfer         “    in connection with a Sale of the Company consummated in accordance with Article 3.    ”  
              § 2.7(e):         a         “    Permitted Transfer    ”         includes         “    Transfers in connection with a Sale of the Company consummated in accordance with Article 3.    ”         Permitted Transfers are exempt from ROFR and co-sale (§ 2.3(d)).  
          Conclusion:         The merger does not trigger ROFR or co-sale, provided it is consummated as a Sale of the Company         “    in accordance with Article 3    ”         (i.e., via the drag-along) or otherwise qualifies as a Sale of the Company.  
        B. The         “    In Accordance with Article 3    ”         Qualifier — Potential Ambiguity  
      Both exemptions are conditioned on the Sale being         “    consummated in accordance with Article 3    ”         (the drag-along article). If the merger proceeds by stockholder written consent             without         the drag-along being triggered (e.g., because Stonemill consents to the Series C vote but the drag-along’s prong (i) is not separately satisfied), a party could argue that the Sale was not         “    consummated in accordance with Article 3    ”         and that the ROFR/co-sale exemptions do not apply. The better reading is that a merger qualifying as a         “    Sale of the Company    ”         is not a         “    Transfer    ”         subject to ROFR at all (the ROFR is triggered by a stockholder’s receipt of a third-party offer to purchase its Shares, not by a corporate merger), so the qualifier is immaterial. But the ambiguity should be eliminated.  
          Recommendation.         Structure the transaction so that the drag-along is triggered (satisfying the         “    in accordance with Article 3    ”         condition)             or         obtain an interpretive waiver/confirmation from the Investor Stockholders that ROFR and co-sale do not apply to the merger. The merger agreement should also include a bring-down confirmation that the Stockholders’ Agreement terminates at closing.  
        C. Termination of the Stockholders’ Agreement  
      The Stockholders’ Agreement terminates upon a         “    Sale of the Company    ”         (§ 6.1(b)). The merger fits the definition (§ 1.1). Upon termination, all transfer restrictions, ROFR, co-sale, tag-along, and drag-along rights cease (§ 6.3), and the Company must remove the related legends.             Confirmed: the Stockholders’ Agreement terminates at closing.

          Key conclusions:  
              Series C (Stonemill) takes its $144.3 million preference ($18.50/share)         — it does             not         convert. The Board minutes’ statement that         “    all holders of Preferred Stock would receive amounts in excess of their respective liquidation preferences on an as-converted basis    ”         is             incorrect as to Series C    .  
              Common and option/RSU holders receive ~$12.59/share    , not the $13.95         “    all-convert    ”         figure in the minutes.  
              Stonemill is the most economically protected holder    : it receives its full $144.3 million preference regardless of the deal value, which exceeds both the $13.95 average and the $12.59 Common value. Any         “    concession    ”         Stonemill might seek would be upside above its already-full preference — relevant to the negotiation strategy.  
          The 20-day Liquidation Event notice (§ 4.3.2(d)) and the merger agreement’s per-share estimates should reflect this waterfall, not the $13.95 figure.

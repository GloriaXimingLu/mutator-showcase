       1. Executive Summary and Recommendation  
          Recommendation: Proceed to submit a non-binding Indication of Interest by the July 14, 2025 deadline, at a disciplined enterprise value of approximately $135–$150 million, contingent on confirmatory due diligence and a quality-of-earnings (        “        QoE        ”        ) review.         The asset is an attractive, on-strategy Pacific Northwest environmental services platform with genuine regulatory tailwinds and platform-for-consolidation characteristics, but the sell-side materials contain material financial inconsistencies and aggressive assumptions that must be normalized before any binding bid.  
          Why we like it.         CES is a 16-year-old, founder-built, integrated environmental services platform (remediation, industrial cleaning, emergency response) across a four-state territory with hard-to-replicate permits and licensed Professional Engineer (    “    PE    ”    ) credentials. FY2024 revenue of $87.3M grew at a 12.5% CAGR (FY2021–FY2024); reported EBITDA margin of 13.9% is within the sector range; the PFAS practice is a real, early-mover growth vector backed by federal/state regulatory mandates; and the fragmented market supports a buy-and-build thesis consistent with Fund III’s sector focus.  
          Why we must be disciplined.         Five categories of issue reduce confidence in the headline sell-side figures:  
              The FY2024 Adjusted EBITDA bridge does not foot.         The six listed adjustments sum to $4.7M, which applied to reported EBITDA of $12.1M yields $16.8M — not the $17.1M stated. The $0.3M overstatement flows directly into the valuation the seller is marketing ($2.4M–$3.0M of enterprise value at 8–10x). FY2021–FY2023 foot correctly; only FY2024 does not.  
              The CIM and the Financial Exhibits do not reconcile.         The balance sheets differ by $3.9M in total assets, $1.7M in equity, $1.8M in cash, and $1.8M in total debt; the income statements differ by $1.7M in net income; and the Exhibits contain an internal inconsistency in which operating income ($11.1M) plus D&A ($4.2M) does not equal EBITDA ($12.1M).  
              Several EBITDA add-backs are overstated or indefensible.         The $1.2M DEQ legal/regulatory add-back appears to include an $875K settlement that the Exhibits classify             below         the EBITDA line (in Other Income/Expense), meaning it was never deducted from EBITDA; and regulatory/compliance costs are recurring in this sector (CES itself recorded $0.2M of         “    one-time    ”         legal/regulatory costs in FY2023 as well). The owner-compensation add-back assumes a $500K replacement CEO, which is low for an $87M+ revenue, 412-employee platform.  
              Stated DSO of 52 days does not reconcile to the balance sheet.         Year-end accounts receivable of $16.9M implies ~71 days (or ~82 days including $2.7M of unbilled revenue), not 52.  
              The growth story is almost entirely PFAS, which is aggressively projected and capacity-constrained.         Traditional remediation (the largest sub-segment) is projected to grow only ~1–6% per year; PFAS is projected to more than double in FY2025 (+125.8%) despite the Company having lost one of three PEs in January 2025 and providing no contracted PFAS backlog breakout.

We should propose either (i) an option to purchase the property at independently appraised fair market value at/close to closing, or (ii) a new market-rate lease with Mr. Oakes. Either path removes the above-market rent but creates a continuing related-party dependency if leased.

              Debt.         Refinance/repay Cascadia facilities at closing (see Section 4.4 for the debt-balance discrepancy).  
              Transition.         Accommodate Mr. Oakes’s 18–24 month advisory transition, but pair it with a professional CEO search and retention agreements for the operating leadership (see Section 8).

        4.7 Capital expenditures and free cash flow — maintenance capex looks understated; FCF is thin

The Sector Memo (Section 4.2) benchmarks maintenance capex at             5–7% of revenue         for environmental services companies of CES’s size and service mix ($4.4M–$6.1M for CES), corroborated by Thornfield’s own portfolio data (GreenWorks actual 6.2% vs. sell-side 3.5%; Allied Waste 5.8%). The fleet is ~4.5 years old on average with a >$22M replacement value (CIM, Appendix C).             If true maintenance capex is ~$5.5M (midpoint) rather than $3.0M, free cash flow is reduced by ~$2.5M per year         — a material value driver. A detailed fleet age and condition assessment is a first-order diligence item; the GreenWorks experience (deferred maintenance surfaced only post-close) is directly on point.

         7. PFAS Growth Assessment  
          The projection is aggressive relative to achievable company-level growth.         PFAS revenue: FY2022 $1.1M → FY2023 $3.4M (+209.1%) → FY2024 $6.2M (+82.4%) → FY2025E $14.0M (+125.8%) → FY2026E $22.0M (+57.1%) → FY2027E $27.0M (+22.7%). The early trajectory is encouraging but from a small base. The Sector Memo (Section 3.2) benchmarks             company-level         PFAS growth at 30–50% annually once a practice exceeds ~$5M, based on Thornfield’s direct GreenWorks experience (vapor intrusion mitigation projected at 80%+ in years two/three; actual ~35% as pent-up demand was worked through and competition intensified).             The CIM’s 125.8% FY2025E growth is roughly three times the achievable company-level rate.         Sector-wide PFAS market growth of 25–35% should not be conflated with any single company’s capture rate.  
          Capacity constraint — PE headcount.         CES’s licensed PE headcount declined from three to two in January 2025 (one retired, not yet replaced) (CIM, Section II.D, item 6). PFAS site assessments and remediation designs require PE oversight. Growth at the projected pace would likely require hiring two to three additional PEs, and the environmental PE market is tight (6–12 month recruitment timelines per the Sector Memo, Section 3.2).             The loss of one-third of PE capacity is a near-term execution bottleneck that directly threatens the PFAS projection.         Management’s PE hiring plan must be vetted.  
          Regulatory tailwind is real but timing-dependent.         The EPA’s April 2024 national drinking water MCLs for six PFAS compounds and Oregon/Washington state initiatives are genuine demand drivers (CIM, Section IV.B; Sector Memo, Section 3.1). However, funded remediation work (vs. investigation/assessment) lags regulatory mandates, and larger national firms are entering the PFAS market with greater resources. The tailwind supports a             directional         PFAS thesis but not the specific FY2025E magnitude.  
          Net.         We credit PFAS as a real, attractive growth vector but model it at a moderated trajectory (~30–50% company-level growth) and bridge the gap to the seller’s projection with an earnout rather than paying for it upfront.

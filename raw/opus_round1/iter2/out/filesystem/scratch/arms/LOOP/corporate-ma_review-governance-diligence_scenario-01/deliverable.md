       I. Executive Summary  
      This memorandum summarizes our review of the governance documents of Terravox Systems, Inc. (    “    Terravox    ”         or the         “    Company    ”    ) in connection with the proposed acquisition of 100% of the Company’s outstanding equity by Hargrove Capital Partners III, L.P. (    “    Hargrove    ”         or         “    Buyer    ”    ), structured as a reverse triangular merger at an enterprise value of $485 million (equity value of approximately $473 million, net of approximately $12 million of outstanding indebtedness). The transaction is being led on the Company side by a Special Committee of the Board of Directors, with Meridian Partners LLC serving as financial advisor and Thornbury & Associates serving as independent valuation advisor. Target closing is Q1 2025.  
      We reviewed the Company’s Third Amended and Restated Certificate of Incorporation (the         “    Restated Certificate    ”    ), Amended and Restated Bylaws (the         “    Bylaws    ”    ), Stockholders’ Agreement, Voting Agreement, Registration Rights Agreement (the         “    RRA    ”    ), and compiled Board minutes covering January 2023 through September 2024 (eight meetings). Our review is organized around the ten diligence areas you identified and is anchored throughout by the client’s principal concern: the leverage held by Stonemill Capital Partners Fund VI, L.P. (    “    Stonemill    ”    ), the Series C lead investor.  
       Headline Findings  
             Stonemill holds a hard, charter-level blocking right over the transaction.         The Restated Certificate grants the holders of Series C Preferred Stock a separate-class veto over any         “    Liquidation Event,    ”         requiring the affirmative consent of holders of at least 66⅔% of the outstanding Series C Preferred Stock (Section 4.3.6(b)(i)). Stonemill holds 100% of the outstanding Series C Preferred Stock (7,800,000 shares). The reverse triangular merger is unambiguously a         “    Liquidation Event.    ”         Accordingly,             the transaction cannot close without either (a) Stonemill’s affirmative consent or (b) a successful judicial enforcement of the drag-along to compel Stonemill’s Series C vote.         This is the single most important governance fact in the deal.             (Critical)  
             The drag-along is a potential—but legally uncertain—override of the Series C veto.         The Stockholders’ Agreement drag-along can be             triggered         without Stonemill (two of its three prongs are satisfied by Ridgeline and Northbridge alone), and if enforced it would purport to compel Stonemill to vote its Series C shares in favor of the merger—mechanically satisfying the separate-class vote. However, whether a contractual drag-along can override a charter-level separate-class protective provision is an unsettled question under Delaware law, and Stonemill would almost certainly litigate. The drag-along is therefore a meaningful             negotiating lever    , not a clean path to closing.             (Critical / Significant)  
             A live SensorLink exclusivity obligation may conflict with signing a definitive agreement with Hargrove.         The SensorLink LOI contains an automatic-renewal exclusivity provision. The September 25, 2024 Board minutes reflect uncertainty as to whether a termination notice was sent before the September 8, 2024 expiration, and outside counsel flagged the auto-renewal risk. If no notice was sent, the exclusivity may have renewed and could continue renewing. This is time-sensitive and must be resolved before signing.             (Critical)  
             The $12 million Clearfield National Bank term loan appears to have been incurred without the Preferred Stockholder consent required by the charter.         Section 4.3.6(a)(v) of the Restated Certificate requires the consent of a majority of the Preferred Stock for indebtedness for borrowed money exceeding $5 million. The October 2023 Board minutes reflect Board approval of the $12 million facility but do not reflect any Preferred Stockholder consent. If consent was not obtained, this is a charter compliance failure that Stonemill can weaponize.             (Critical — pending confirmation)  
             The Special Committee includes an interested director.         Sandra Okafor—Northbridge’s board designee and a Principal at Northbridge Growth Equity—sits on the three-member Special Committee evaluating the sale. Northbridge (Series B) benefits economically from the deal (its shares convert above preference), while Stonemill (Series C) does not. Okafor’s participation creates a fiduciary-process vulnerability that Stonemill can exploit in any challenge to the sale process.             (Significant)  
             The Board minutes contain an apparently incorrect waterfall characterization.         The September 25, 2024 minutes state that         “    all holders of Preferred Stock would receive amounts in excess of their respective liquidation preferences on an as-converted basis,    ”         implying all preferred should convert. Our analysis shows the opposite for Series C: at $473 million equity value, the as-converted value (~$12.59/share) is well below the Series C $18.50 liquidation preference, so Series C takes its preference ($144.3 million) and does             not         convert. This bears directly on Stonemill’s economic motivation to block.             (Significant)

We have not received the 2021 Equity Incentive Plan, individual award agreements, the SensorLink LOI, the Clearfield loan agreement, stockholder consent records, or a current cap table. Several conclusions in this memo are necessarily preliminary pending these items.             (Significant)

Each Restricted Stockholder also granted an irrevocable proxy coupled with an interest to the holders constituting the Drag-Along Approval (§3.3).         “    Shares    ”         is defined broadly to include all capital stock held by a party, including Preferred Stock on an as-converted basis—so             Stonemill’s Series C shares are subject to the drag-along.

          Prong (i) analysis.         The drag-along measures         “    a majority of the outstanding shares of Common Stock (calculated on an as-converted to Common Stock basis, treating each share of Preferred Stock as having been converted).    ”         The natural reading is that the denominator is             issued and outstanding         Common Stock plus Preferred as-converted—i.e.,             unexercised options and unvested RSUs are excluded         (they are not         “    outstanding shares of Common Stock    ”    ). On that reading, the denominator is meaningfully below the 33.9 million fully diluted count, and Founders + Ridgeline + Northbridge (16.0 million as-converted) likely exceed the majority. If, however, the denominator is read as fully diluted (33.9 million), the majority threshold is greater than 16.95 million, and Founders + Ridgeline + Northbridge (16.0 million) fall short by approximately 950,000 shares—requiring support from a portion of the         “    other    ”         common holders (employees, ex-employees, and other option/common holders, who are generally not parties to the Stockholders’ Agreement and therefore cannot themselves be dragged, but who may voluntarily support a cash-out).  
          Conclusion on trigger:         The drag-along             can likely be triggered without Stonemill    , but prong (i) depends on (a) the correct interpretation of         “    outstanding shares of Common Stock    ”         and (b) the actual issued-and-outstanding Common share count.             We cannot confirm prong (i) without a current cap table.         This is a Significant open item. Even under the fully-diluted reading, only ~950,000 additional common-holder shares are needed—a low bar in a cash-out transaction where employee optionholders generally favor liquidity—but it is not automatic.  
        C. If triggered, can the drag-along override the Series C separate-class veto?  
      This is the central legal question of the deal. If the drag-along is triggered, it purports to compel Stonemill to vote its 7,800,000 Series C shares in favor of the merger. Because Stonemill holds 100% of the Series C, a compelled affirmative vote would mechanically satisfy the 66⅔% separate-class threshold under §4.3.6(b)(i). The question is whether a Delaware court would specifically enforce the drag-along against Stonemill notwithstanding the charter-level separate-class protective provision.  
          Arguments that the drag-along can override the veto (enforceable):  
          The drag-along is a valid voting agreement authorized by DGCL §218(c), which expressly permits stockholders to agree in writing to vote their shares in a specified manner and makes such agreements specifically enforceable.  
          Stonemill is a party to the Stockholders’ Agreement and voluntarily agreed to be bound by the drag-along, including the irrevocable proxy (§3.3) and specific-performance remedy (§7.8). The Series C protective provision is a             right held by Stonemill    ; a holder may contractually pre-commit to waive or exercise its own protective rights in a specified way. The drag-along is, in substance, a pre-agreed consent mechanism.  
          The drag-along expressly requires stockholders to vote         “    in favor of any other matter required to be approved in connection with such Sale of the Company    ”         (§3.1(a)(A)), which plausibly encompasses the separate-class vote.  
          The drag-along respects the liquidation waterfall (§3.2(a)): a dragged Stonemill still receives its $144.3 million preference. The drag-along does not strip Stonemill of its economic preference; it compels only the             vote    , not a worse economic outcome. This weakens any argument that enforcement is inequitable.  
          Arguments that the drag-along cannot override the veto (Stonemill’s likely position):  
          The Series C protective provision is a charter right with statutory force, negotiated separately and described in the charter as         “    separate from and in addition to    ”         the all-Preferred consent (§4.3.6(b)). Stonemill may argue the parties did not intend a contractual drag-along to override a senior preferred’s charter-level separate-class veto, and that the two provisions must be harmonized rather than the contract silently repealing the charter right.  
          Stonemill may invoke the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, particularly if the deal terms disproportionately favor classes whose designees drove the process (e.g., Northbridge, whose designee sits on the Special Committee).  
          The protective provision requires the consent of         “    holders of at least 66⅔% of the then-outstanding shares of Series C Preferred Stock, voting as a separate class.    ”         Stonemill may argue that a compelled vote is not a genuine         “    consent    ”         and that the charter’s protective-protection scheme cannot be satisfied by contractually coerced votes.  
          Even if ultimately enforceable, specific performance is not guaranteed; a court may decline to compel a vote where damages are adequate or where the equities favor the veto holder.  
          Assessment.         This is a genuinely unsettled question. We are not aware of Delaware authority directly deciding whether a stockholders’-agreement drag-along can override a charter separate-class protective provision. The better view is that the drag-along is             likely enforceable    —Stonemill voluntarily assumed the obligation, the statute authorizes voting agreements, and the economic preference is preserved—but the outcome is far from certain, and Stonemill would almost certainly seek a preliminary injunction or temporary restraining order to block the vote pending litigation.             Litigation would likely delay closing beyond Q1 2025 and create material outcome risk.  
        D. Practical limitations of the drag-along path  
              Trigger uncertainty (prong (i)):         As noted, triggering without Stonemill depends on the outstanding-share count, which we cannot yet confirm.  
              Litigation and delay:         Even a successful drag-along enforcement would require litigation, likely in the Delaware Court of Chancery (per the Bylaws’ exclusive-forum provision, Article VIII), with a preliminary-injunction fight that could delay closing by months.  
              Fiduciary overlay:         The Board owes fiduciary duties to all stockholders. Using the drag-along to override a senior preferred’s veto is defensible if the deal is fair and the process is sound, but the Special Committee composition issue (Section XII) gives Stonemill ammunition to attack the process.  
              Stonemill’s economic indifference:         Because Stonemill receives its full $144.3 million preference whether or not it is dragged, the drag-along does not impose economic pain on Stonemill—it merely forces the vote. Stonemill’s incentive to litigate is therefore high (it risks little by fighting, and stands to gain a higher price or concessions if it succeeds in delaying).  
        E. Drag-along as leverage, not a closing plan  
      The drag-along is most valuable as a             negotiating lever    . The credible threat—    “    we can trigger the drag-along and seek to compel your vote in Chancery    ”    —materially changes the dynamic, because it shifts some delay and litigation cost onto Stonemill and creates a non-trivial probability that Stonemill is dragged at its preference with no upside. But relying on the drag-along as the             primary         closing mechanism is imprudent given the legal uncertainty, the trigger ambiguity, and the delay risk.             Recommendation: prepare the drag-along for use (confirm the share count, line up common-holder support for prong (i), draft the consent solicitation and specific-performance complaint), but pursue a negotiated resolution with Stonemill in parallel.  
      
         VI. Impediments to Closing a Reverse Triangular Merger             (Diligence Area 3)  
      In the proposed structure, Hargrove forms a wholly owned acquisition subsidiary (    “    Merger Sub    ”    ); Merger Sub merges with and into Terravox; Terravox survives as a wholly owned subsidiary of Hargrove; and all outstanding Terravox equity is cancelled and converted into the right to receive cash merger consideration.  
       A. The defined terms clearly capture the structure  
              “        Liquidation Event        ”                 (Restated Certificate §4.3.2(d)):         Subsection (i) captures any merger or business combination in which the Company’s pre-transaction stockholders do not retain at least 50% of the voting power of the surviving or resulting entity. In a reverse triangular merger, Terravox’s stockholders retain 0% of the voting power of either Terravox (now wholly owned by Hargrove) or Hargrove (the parent). Subsection (iii) independently captures any transaction resulting in the transfer of more than 50% of the Company’s voting power to any person or group.             The merger is unambiguously a Liquidation Event.         No structural ambiguity.  
              “        Sale of the Company        ”                 (Stockholders’ Agreement §1.1; Voting Agreement §1):         Both define the term to include any merger or reorganization in which the Company’s stockholders do not retain a majority of the voting power of the surviving or resulting entity, and any transaction transferring more than 50% of the voting power.             The merger is unambiguously a Sale of the Company under both agreements.  
        B. Reverse-triangular-merger-specific considerations  
              Surviving-entity continuity.         Because Terravox survives as a subsidiary, Terravox’s charter, bylaws, and contracts technically continue in existence (the entity is not dissolved). This has two consequences:  
          The merger agreement will typically amend the surviving entity’s certificate of incorporation at the effective time, triggering the §8.1 amendment consents (subsumed within the change-of-control consents above).  
          Terravox’s indemnification obligations (charter Article VII; Bylaws Article VI) and D&O insurance survive with the entity. Hargrove should address indemnification survival and a D&O tail policy in the merger agreement (see Section XV).  
              Change-of-control in contracts.         A reverse triangular merger is generally treated as a change of control for contractual purposes because 100% of Terravox’s stock changes hands to Hargrove. The Clearfield term loan and material commercial contracts (e.g., Permian Basin MSA) must be reviewed for change-of-control triggers. The Clearfield facility is secured by substantially all assets (including intellectual property); a change-of-control default could require lender consent or trigger mandatory prepayment, affecting the $12 million debt bridge in the economics.  
              Appraisal rights (DGCL §262).         In a cash-out merger, Terravox stockholders generally have statutory appraisal rights. Holders of Preferred Stock may also have appraisal rights under §262(b)(2) because their shares are being cancelled for cash (not remaining outstanding and not converted into surviving-entity stock). The drag-along (§3.1(a)(B)) requires dragged stockholders to refrain from exercising appraisal rights. If the drag-along is             not         enforced against Stonemill, Stonemill could pursue appraisal as an alternative lever—though appraisal yields         “    fair value    ”         (likely at or below the deal value for Series C, given its preference), is slow and expensive, and is a weaker lever than the veto. We note it for completeness.

The principal residual risk is the survival of the RRA’s indemnification provisions (§7, which expressly survives termination under §9), though no registration was effected in connection with the merger, so indemnification exposure is unlikely.  
        D. Recommended treatment  
      To eliminate the ambiguity from the undefined         “    Deemed Liquidation Event,    ”         the merger agreement should include an             express termination of the RRA at the effective time    , supported by the §9 majority-Holder written consent. Because Ridgeline + Northbridge hold 8,700,000 of 16,500,000 Registrable Securities (52.7%),             the RRA can be terminated by the Company plus Ridgeline and Northbridge even without Stonemill’s consent.         A dual approach—(i) deeming the merger a Deemed Liquidation Event under §9(c) and (ii) obtaining the §9 majority-Holder written consent—provides belt-and-suspenders certainty.

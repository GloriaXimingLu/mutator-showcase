        1. Executive Summary  
          Recommendation: Proceed to a disciplined Indication of Interest, but anchor valuation on independently adjusted trailing EBITDA and use a PFAS-linked earnout to bridge the gap between the seller’s growth narrative and a defensible underwriting case.         CES is a genuinely attractive lower-middle-market environmental services platform that fits Fund III’s sector and geographic focus, and the core business (excluding the disputed PFAS ramp) exhibits real, durable growth. However, the CIM and supporting exhibits contain a pattern of financial inconsistencies, aggressive PFAS projections, and EBITDA adjustments that are likely overstated — issues that are consistent with the pattern observed in our GreenWorks Remediation and Allied Waste Systems investments and that materially affect both earnings quality and valuation.  
          Key findings:  
             The financial exhibits do not reconcile to the CIM, and contain internal inconsistencies.         The balance sheet in the financial exhibits shows total assets of $50.0M versus $46.1M in the CIM (a $3.9M variance), equity of $18.8M versus $17.1M, and cash of $4.1M versus $2.3M. The income statement exhibits report operating income of $11.1M that does not tie to the stated Reported EBITDA of $12.1M (a $3.2M internal gap). Segment splits, the customer list, and geographic mix also differ between documents. These are not rounding items; they must be reconciled in QoE before any number in the CIM can be relied upon.  
             The stated DSO of 52 days does not reconcile to the balance sheet.         Year-end accounts receivable of $16.9M against FY2024 revenue of $87.3M implies DSO of approximately 71 days (82 days if unbilled receivables are included) — roughly 19–30 days longer than represented. This suggests working capital is heavier than presented and that the NWC peg and cash-conversion profile need independent verification.  
             PFAS projections are aggressive and underpin the growth story.         The CIM projects PFAS revenue growing 125.8% in FY2025E (to $14.0M) and 57.1% in FY2026E (to $22.0M), with PFAS driving ~64% of incremental revenue through FY2026. Sector data and our GreenWorks experience indicate 30–50% annual growth is the realistic ceiling once a practice exceeds $5M. The August 2024 departure of one of three Professional Engineers (PE headcount now two) creates a near-term capacity bottleneck for the PE-oversight-intensive PFAS work.  
             Maintenance capex is materially understated versus sector norms.         CIM maintenance capex of $3.0M (3.4% of revenue) is 150–200 bps below the 5–7% sector range ($4.4M–$6.1M). Our GreenWorks experience — where pre-close maintenance capex was understated at ~3.5% and ran at 6.2% post-close — is directly on point. A $2.5M annual understatement reduces free cash flow and the implied return.  
             The $1.2M regulatory/legal add-back is likely overstated.         The August 2024 Oregon DEQ enforcement action (improper storage of contaminated soils at CES’s own Portland facility) is presented as fully non-recurring. Sector run-rates of $200K–$500K and our portfolio experience (GreenWorks $275K/yr; Allied $310K/yr) indicate only the amount above a normalized run-rate is defensible — a $300K–$500K haircut, or $2.4M–$5.0M of enterprise value at the guided multiple.  
             Customer concentration is high and the largest contract has already expired.         PNR represents 21.4% of revenue; the top 5 represent 52.5%; the top 10 represent 71.0%. The PNR master service agreement expired March 31, 2025 — before the CIM was distributed — and renewal status is not disclosed. This is the single most important commercial diligence item.  
             Real estate and related-party arrangements require structural resolution.         The Portland HQ is owned personally by the founder/CEO and leased to CES at $1.4M/yr versus an estimated $0.6M market rate. The $0.8M normalization is defensible only if the buyer either acquires the property at fair market value or executes a new arm’s-length lease; the structure of this resolution affects both EBITDA quality and transaction mechanics.  
          Preliminary valuation view.         On a buyer-adjusted trailing basis, FY2024 EBITDA is approximately $16.4M (versus the CIM’s $17.1M), implying an enterprise value of $131M–$164M at 8.0x–10.0x. On a forward basis with a haircut PFAS case, FY2025E EBITDA is approximately $17.2M (versus the CIM’s $19.7M), implying $138M–$172M. We recommend an IOI enterprise value range of             $145M–$165M    , structured as approximately             $140M–$150M of cash consideration at close         plus a             contingent earnout of up to ~$15M–$20M (total consideration ~$140M–$170M depending on earnout achievement)         tied to FY2025–FY2026 PFAS revenue and adjusted EBITDA thresholds. This bridges the seller’s ~$171M+ expectation to a defensible underwriting case while preserving upside if the PFAS ramp is realized.

        3. Company Overview and Investment Thesis  
      CES is a Delaware C-corporation founded in 2009 and headquartered in Portland, Oregon, with additional facilities in Seattle, WA and Boise, ID. The Company operates an integrated environmental services platform across a four-state Pacific Northwest territory (Oregon, Washington, Idaho, Northern California) through three segments: Environmental Remediation (FY2024 revenue $48.2M, 55.2%), Industrial Cleaning ($27.8M, 31.8%), and Emergency Response ($11.3M, 12.9%). Total FY2024 revenue was $87.3M (12.5% CAGR FY2021–FY2024). The Company employs 412 people, holds hazardous waste transporter permits in all four states plus an Oregon DEQ contractor license, and reports an EMR of 0.87.  
          Genuinely attractive attributes (independently assessed):  
              Integrated, regulated platform with a real moat.         Four-state hazmat transporter permits, the Oregon DEQ contractor license, and PE-stamped remediation capabilities take years to replicate and create meaningful barriers to entry. The integrated remediation/cleaning/emergency-response model drives cross-selling and share-of-wallet expansion.  
              Durable core growth, even excluding PFAS.         Traditional remediation grew from $34.8M (FY2021) to $42.0M (FY2024) and industrial cleaning from $19.2M to $27.8M over the same period — respectable organic growth in a market that generally grows 5–10% annually.  
              Regulatory tailwinds are real.         EPA’s 2024 PFAS drinking water rule, CERCLA designation activity, and active Oregon/Washington state enforcement underpin multi-year demand for investigation, remediation, and compliance work.  
              Revenue visibility.         $42.6M contracted backlog plus $31.4M pipeline ($74.0M total) as of 12/31/2024; ~77.5% of backlog expected to convert within twelve months. Reported 90% MSA renewal rate and no top-10 customer losses in five years.  
              Recurring revenue characteristics.         MSA-based industrial cleaning on scheduled turnaround cycles, multi-year remediation lifecycles, and standby emergency-response retainers provide a predictable base.  
              Platform for consolidation.         A fragmented Pacific Northwest market with 5–7 identified tuck-in targets supports a buy-and-build thesis consistent with Fund III’s strategy.  
              Strong safety record.         EMR of 0.87 is a meaningful commercial qualifier for access to refining and industrial facilities.  
      These attributes support proceeding. The remainder of this memo focuses on the issues that affect price, structure, and diligence — not on whether the asset is worth pursuing.

         6. Key Risk Factors and Diligence Priorities  
      The following are prioritized by impact on valuation and deal certainty. Each maps to a specific diligence request in Section 10.  
       6.1 PFAS Revenue Projections (HIGH)  
      The CIM projects PFAS revenue of $14.0M in FY2025E (125.8% growth) and $22.0M in FY2026E (57.1% growth), with PFAS providing ~64% of incremental revenue and underpinning the projected margin expansion to 21.0%+. Sector analysts project 25–35% growth for the overall PFAS market, but company-level growth is constrained by (a) licensed-engineer and trained-technician availability, (b) specialized treatment-equipment capital, (c) competitive entry by larger national firms, and (d) the pace of enforcement that actually triggers             funded         remediation (as opposed to investigation/assessment).  
      Our GreenWorks experience is directly analogous: an emerging service line (vapor intrusion mitigation) projected at 80%+ growth in years two and three moderated to ~35% once pent-up demand was worked through. Applying a 30–50% company-level growth assumption to CES’s $6.2M FY2024 PFAS base yields FY2025E PFAS of ~$8.1M–$9.3M — roughly $5M–$6M below the CIM’s $14.0M. If PFAS carries above-average margins (as the CIM implies), this shortfall could reduce FY2025E EBITDA by ~$1.5M–$2.0M and undermine the projected margin expansion.  
          PE capacity constraint.         PE headcount fell from three to two in January 2025 (one retired, unreplaced). PFAS site assessments and remediation designs require PE oversight. Sustaining the projected ramp likely requires hiring two to three additional PEs; environmental PE recruitment typically runs six to twelve months. Management’s PE hiring plan — and whether the loss of one-third of PE capacity creates a near-term execution bottleneck — is a critical diligence question.  
        6.2 Maintenance Capex (HIGH)  
      CIM maintenance capex of $3.0M (3.4% of revenue) is 150–200 bps below the 5–7% sector range ($4.4M–$6.1M for CES’s revenue). Our portfolio data confirms the range: GreenWorks ran at 6.2% post-close (after a pre-close understatement to ~3.5%), and Allied at 5.8%. If true maintenance capex is ~$5.5M (sector midpoint), the ~$2.5M annual difference directly reduces free cash flow — and at CES’s already-thin FCF (Section 4.4), this could push normalized FCF near breakeven. A detailed fleet age and condition assessment (the fleet is ~4.5 years old, replacement value >$22M) is essential early diligence. This is the single largest potential swing factor in the underwriting model after PFAS.  
        6.3 Regulatory Cost Add-Back (MEDIUM-HIGH)  
      The $1.2M add-back for the August 2024 DEQ enforcement action is presented as fully non-recurring. Two concerns: (i) the action concerned             improper storage of contaminated soils at CES’s own Portland facility         — an operational compliance failure at the Company’s primary site, not a client-site issue — which raises the question of whether it reflects systemic internal compliance weakness rather than an isolated event; and (ii) sector and portfolio data show a baseline of $200K–$500K/yr in regulatory and compliance cost is inherent to the business model. Best-practice QoE normalizes to an ongoing run-rate and adds back only the excess. At a $300K run-rate, the defensible add-back is ~$0.9M (a $0.3M haircut); at $500K, ~$0.7M (a $0.5M haircut). The full 10-year regulatory enforcement history must be reviewed to assess whether prior actions suggest a pattern.  
        6.4 Customer Concentration and PNR Contract Renewal (HIGH)  
      Top-customer concentration (PNR 21.4%; top 5 52.5%; top 10 71.0%) is typical for project-based environmental services but is a real risk.             The PNR master service agreement expired March 31, 2025 — before the CIM was distributed on June 9, 2025.         The CIM and backlog report describe the PNR relationship as         “    deeply embedded    ”         with on-site personnel and preferred-provider status, but neither document confirms renewal. Whether PNR has renewed (and on what terms), the renewal cadence of the other top-10 contracts (several expire in 2025–2026), and the realistic revenue-replacement capacity if PNR scope is reduced are the most important commercial diligence items. The concentration trend is favorable (top-10 share declining from 74.3% to 71.0% over FY2021–FY2024), but the absolute level remains high and the largest contract is in renewal at the moment of sale.  
        6.5 Founder Transition and Key-Person Risk (MEDIUM-HIGH)  
      Mr. Oakes (age 61, 68% owner, 30+ years’ experience) intends to exit within 18–24 months post-close. He is the central strategic, customer, and regulatory-relationship figure. The GreenWorks experience — where a key project manager departed within six months of close — is a cautionary precedent. Janet Prewitt (VP Operations, 18-year tenure, holds key remediation client relationships) is the critical retention target and should be covered by a retention agreement as a closing condition. The broader management bench (VP Sales since 2014; Controller since 2016; Safety Director since 2012) is reasonably tenured but has not operated the company without Mr. Oakes. A credible CEO succession plan — including the $0.5M replacement-compensation assumption underlying the owner-comp add-back — must be validated.  
        6.6 Real Estate and Related-Party Lease (MEDIUM)  
      The Portland HQ (8.5 acres, purpose-built environmental operations center) is owned personally by Mr. Oakes and leased to CES at $1.4M/yr through 2029, versus an estimated $0.6M market rate. The $0.8M EBITDA normalization is defensible only if the buyer will in fact pay market rent going forward. The buyer must either (a) acquire the property from Mr. Oakes at independently appraised fair market value (adding to uses of funds but eliminating rent and the related-party conflict), or (b) execute a new arm’s-length lease at market rate (preserving the normalization but leaving a related-party landlord in place for the lease term). The CIM notes industrial yard space in Portland leases at $8–$12/SF and that         “    specialized environmental staging facilities    ”         can command $1.0M–$1.2M/yr — which, if accurate, suggests the $0.6M         “    market    ”         estimate may itself be low and that the property’s specialized improvements carry premium value. The real-estate resolution is both a valuation and a transaction-structure issue and should be addressed explicitly in the IOI.  
        6.7 Adjusted EBITDA Adjustment Defensibility (MEDIUM)  
      Beyond the regulatory and owner-comp items already discussed, the phantom equity plan warrants attention. The $0.3M annual non-cash add-back is appropriate for EBITDA, but             all outstanding phantom units settle in cash at closing    . The total cash settlement liability is not quantified in the CIM and must be estimated for sources & uses; based on the annual expense run-rate it could be a low-single-digit-million obligation. This is a use of funds at closing, not an EBITDA item, and reduces net equity proceeds to the seller.  
        6.8 Fund Fit (CONFIRM)  
      The sector memo characterizes CES as fitting         “    squarely within    ”         Fund III’s parameters. Fund III ($425M, closed 2023) is described as targeting platforms in the $30M–$100M enterprise value range. CES’s implied enterprise value of $137M–$171M (CIM) — and our recommended $145M–$165M IOI range — is             above         that stated ceiling. The deal team should confirm Fund III’s actual EV/equity-check limits, concentration limits, and whether a platform of this size requires LPAC approval or a fund-limit exception. At a ~$150M EV with ~40% leverage, the equity check is ~$90M (~21% of fund), which is a meaningful but supportable platform concentration if within mandate.

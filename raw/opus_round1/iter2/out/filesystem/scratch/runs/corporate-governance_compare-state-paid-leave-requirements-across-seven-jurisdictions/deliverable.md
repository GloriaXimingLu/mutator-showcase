                I.  Executive Summary  
            This memorandum sets forth Hartwell & Cabot LLP’s (“H&C” or the “Firm”) gap analysis and risk assessment of Vantage Logistics Group, Inc.’s (“Vantage” or the “Company”) paid leave policies and practices across seven jurisdictions—New Jersey, New York, and Connecticut (current operations) and Massachusetts, Colorado, Oregon, and Washington (planned expansion). It accompanies the Multi-State Compliance Matrix (Deliverable 1) and should be read together with that workbook, which contains the dimension-by-dimension comparison and color-coded gap indicators underlying the findings below.  
            Our review identifies material compliance deficiencies in every jurisdiction. The Company’s Employee Handbook (Rev. January 15, 2022) was drafted primarily for New Jersey and New York and has not been substantively updated in over three years; it does not address the requirements of any of the four expansion states and is non-compliant in several respects even in the current three-state footprint. The most serious findings are structural rather than incremental: (i) the Company’s self-funded paid family leave benefit is below the statutory benefit level in all seven states and no private-plan application has been filed in any jurisdiction; (ii) the Company provides no employer-funded paid medical leave for an employee’s own serious health condition, and HR staff have been directing employees to an employee-paid voluntary short-term disability plan instead of the state programs those employees are funding through payroll withholdings; (iii) the Company has not registered with the paid leave insurance program in any of the four expansion states, with Massachusetts and Colorado on or past the critical path; and (iv) the paid sick leave policy is non-compliant on accrual rate, caps, carryover, covered uses, and family-member definition in most or all states—deficiencies that are the subject of the pending New Jersey Department of Labor complaint (Case No. NJ-ESL-2025-04117, Carla Muñoz v. Vantage Logistics Group, Inc.).

                II.  Scope, Methodology, and Limitations  
            Scope. This engagement encompasses a compliance review of Vantage’s paid leave policies and practices across the seven jurisdictions identified in the engagement letter, covering paid sick leave, paid family leave, paid medical leave, employer obligations (registration, contributions, withholding, recordkeeping, posting), and PTO/vacation payout on separation. The review addresses both current-state gaps (NJ, NY, CT) and prospective gaps for the four expansion states (MA, CO, OR, WA).  
            Methodology. We compared the Company’s written policies (Employee Handbook, Rev. January 15, 2022, Section 6: Leave Policies) and documented practices against the statutes, regulations, and agency guidance of each jurisdiction. We also reviewed the PinnacleHR Solutions, Inc. payroll withholding summary (data as of March 31, 2025), the HR expansion plan memorandum (March 28, 2025), the internal HR email thread (March 10–17, 2025), the NJ DOL complaint correspondence (Case No. NJ-ESL-2025-04117), and a representative sample of the Company’s leave-tracking spreadsheets. Where practice diverges from written policy, we have assessed the practice as well.  
         New Jersey FLI/TDI benefit parameters (duration, maximum weekly benefit) are not detailed in the PinnacleHR withholding report, which addresses NJ contribution rates; those parameters are drawn from the applicable statutes (N.J.S.A. 43:21-25 et seq.) per the engagement's legal-research scope and should be confirmed against current NJDOL guidance.  
            Limitations. Our analysis is based on the laws, regulations, and agency guidance in effect as of the date of this memorandum and on the accuracy and completeness of the information and representations provided by the Company. State paid leave law is a rapidly evolving area; legislative or regulatory changes may occur after delivery. This memorandum (i) is outside scope with respect to representation in the pending NJ DOL complaint, which continues under the separate engagement dated February 15, 2025; (ii) does not constitute a federal FMLA compliance review; (iii) does not address local or municipal paid leave ordinances; and (iv) does not include drafting of the revised Handbook, which would be a separate engagement. This memorandum is a compliance diagnostic and does not constitute legal advice as to any individual employee claim.  
                III.  Current-State Gap Analysis (New Jersey, New York, Connecticut)  
             A.  Paid Sick Leave — Cross-Cutting Deficiencies (NJ, NY, CT)  
            The Company’s paid sick leave policy (Handbook §6.2) is non-compliant in all three current states on multiple dimensions. These deficiencies are not state-specific drafting errors; they reflect a policy designed to a single, simplified standard that falls below the requirements of every jurisdiction in which the Company operates.  
              Accrual rate.           The policy accrues sick leave at one hour per 40 hours worked. New Jersey and New York require one hour per 30 hours worked; Connecticut requires one hour per 40 hours (compliant on rate only). The under-accrual is systemic and quantifiable; the leave-tracking sample confirms it (e.g., the complainant in the Muñoz matter accrued 13.0 hours on 520 hours worked, a 1/40 rate).  
              Annual cap.           The policy caps full-time employees at 40 hours and part-time employees at 24 hours per year. New Jersey, New York, and Connecticut each require 40 hours (New York requires 56 hours for employers with 100 or more employees, a threshold Vantage exceeds with 680 New York employees) and none permits a reduced part-time cap. The part-time cap directly underpays part-time employees and is confirmed in the tracking sample.  
              Waiting period.           The policy imposes a uniform 120-day waiting period before accrued sick leave may be used. New Jersey permits a 120-day waiting period (compliant). New York permits no waiting period—employees may use leave as it accrues—so the 120-day rule is non-compliant there. Connecticut’s eligibility is hours-based rather than a flat calendar waiting period, making the 120-day rule at risk. The tracking sample shows employees denied use during the waiting period in both New York and New Jersey.  
              Carryover.           The policy provides no carryover of unused sick leave (balances reset to zero on January 1) and no year-end payout. All three states require carryover (New Jersey permits carryover of up to 40 hours or, alternatively, year-end payout plus front-loading). The tracking sample confirms year-end forfeitures (e.g., 12 hours and 8 hours forfeited for New Jersey employees).  
              Covered uses.           The policy limits permitted uses to the employee’s own illness, injury, or medical appointment. All three states additionally require leave for care of a family member, absences related to domestic or sexual violence (“safe leave”), public-health-emergency and school/place-of-care closures, and (in New Jersey) school-related meetings and functions. The omission of these uses is a central allegation in the Muñoz complaint.  
            Bereavement (as a sick leave use).        Bereavement is not a required paid sick leave use in any of the seven jurisdictions, so the policy's omission of bereavement from the sick leave policy is not a sick-leave compliance gap.  
              Family-member definition.           The policy defines “family member” as spouse, child, or parent only. Each state’s definition is materially broader. New Jersey’s definition (N.J.S.A. 34:11D-1) expressly includes a child of a domestic partner, domestic and civil-union partners, grandparents, grandchildren, siblings, any blood relative, and any individual whose close association with the employee is the equivalent of a family relationship. The narrow definition is the direct cause of the denial at issue in the Muñoz complaint and is non-compliant in all three states.  
              Documentation threshold.           The policy requires a doctor’s note after two consecutive scheduled workdays. Each state permits documentation to be required only after three or more consecutive days (or, in New York, on reasonable suspicion of abuse). The two-day threshold is more stringent than the statutes permit and creates a denial risk if applied rigidly.  
              Posting and notice.           The Handbook is silent on the state-specific workplace postings and individual hire notices that each state requires. We could not confirm that the required postings are displayed at the Company’s facilities. Posting violations are independently citable.  
             B.  New Jersey — Specific Considerations  
            The New Jersey deficiencies are compounded by the pending NJ DOL complaint (Case No. NJ-ESL-2025-04117). The complaint alleges that the Company denied Carla Muñoz, a warehouse associate at the Edison facility, the right to use accrued earned sick leave to care for her domestic partner’s child—a covered family member under N.J.S.A. 34:11D-1—and that the policy’s enumeration of covered uses omits categories required by N.J.S.A. 34:11D-3. The Company’s February 21, 2025 response acknowledged that the policy language “raises legitimate questions” and committed to a policy review by July 1, 2025. The NJ DOL has reserved the right to conduct a broader investigation of the Company’s sick leave practices, policies, and records. Because the deficiencies identified in the complaint (family-member definition and covered uses) are present in the policy as applied to all New Jersey employees, the exposure is not limited to the individual complainant; a broader investigation could surface the accrual-rate, cap, and carryover deficiencies as well. Penalties are available under N.J.S.A. 34:11D-7.  
             C.  New York — Specific Considerations  
            New York presents the most acute paid sick leave exposure among the current states because the Company exceeds the 100-employee threshold, triggering the 56-hour annual entitlement (versus the policy’s 40-hour cap), and because New York permits no waiting period before use of accrued leave (versus the policy’s 120-day waiting period). The tracking sample shows New York employees denied use during the waiting period and balances forfeited at year-end. The combination of a sub-statutory cap, an impermissible waiting period, and no carryover produces a cumulative shortfall for New York employees that is readily quantifiable from payroll and time records.  
             D.  Connecticut — Specific Considerations  
            Connecticut is the only current state in which the Company’s sick leave accrual rate (1/40) matches the statute. However, the part-time cap (24 hours) is non-compliant (Connecticut applies 40 hours regardless of full-time/part-time status), there is no carryover, and the covered-uses and family-member definitions are narrower than required. Connecticut is also the focal point of the paid medical leave gap discussed in Section V.B below: at least four Bridgeport employees have inquired about paid leave for their own serious health conditions and were directed to the employee-paid voluntary short-term disability plan rather than the Connecticut Paid Leave program they are funding through payroll withholdings.  
                IV.  Prospective Gap Analysis (Massachusetts, Colorado, Oregon, Washington)  
            The four expansion states each impose paid sick leave and paid family/medical leave requirements that the current Handbook does not address. In addition, the Company has not registered with the paid leave insurance program in any of the four states, and no payroll withholding has been configured. The following summarizes the principal prospective gaps; the Compliance Matrix contains the dimension-by-dimension comparison.  
             A.  Massachusetts (facility opens August 1, 2025)  
            Massachusetts is on the critical path. The Massachusetts Paid Family and Medical Leave (MA PFML) program requires employer registration and contribution remittance from the first day any covered employee works in the state. PinnacleHR has advised that it cannot configure withholding until it receives the Company’s registration confirmation, and that its configuration lead time is approximately six to eight weeks. To have withholding operational by the August 1, 2025 opening, registration with the Massachusetts Department of Family and Medical Leave (DFML) should be completed no later than approximately June 1, 2025. As of the date of this memorandum, the Company has not registered.  
            Separately, the paid sick leave policy is non-compliant in Massachusetts: the accrual rate (1/40 versus the required 1/30), the part-time cap (24 hours versus 40 hours), the waiting period (120 days versus the 90-day statutory threshold), the absence of carryover, and the narrow covered-uses and family-member definitions all require correction before the opening. Massachusetts also requires payout of accrued unused vacation upon separation under the Wage Act, M.G.L. c. 149 § 148; the Company’s no-payout policy is non-compliant and exposes the Company to treble damages and attorney’s fees. The 5-day PTO carryover cap is high-risk in Massachusetts for the same reason. The revised Handbook (target July 1, 2025) must address all of these items before the first Massachusetts employee is hired.  
             B.  Colorado (facility opens November 1, 2025; 3 remote hires April 2025)  
            Colorado is the most urgent registration item. Three Colorado-based salaried employees are being hired in April 2025 to work remotely in advance of the Aurora facility opening. If the Colorado FAMLI registration obligation is triggered by the first date a Colorado-based employee performs work—a position the Colorado Division of Family and Medical Leave Insurance generally takes—then the Company’s registration may already be overdue, or will be within days. Even under the more favorable interpretation that registration is tied to the November 1, 2025 facility opening, the six-to-eight-week PinnacleHR lead time requires registration no later than approximately mid-August 2025. We recommend immediate registration and a conservative reading of the trigger date.  
            Colorado is also the only expansion state in which the Company cannot self-fund in lieu of state program participation: the FAMLI private-plan opt-out is limited to local-government employers. The Company must register with and participate in CO FAMLI. The paid sick leave policy is non-compliant in Colorado on the accrual rate (1/40 versus 1/30), the cap (40/24 hours versus 48 hours), the waiting period (120 days versus immediate use—Colorado permits no waiting period), the absence of carryover, and the narrow covered-uses and family-member definitions. Colorado also requires supplemental public-health-emergency leave of up to 80 hours under the Healthy Families and Workplaces Act. Finally, Colorado requires payout of all accrued unused vacation upon separation (C.R.S. § 8-4-101 et seq.); the no-payout policy and the 5-day carryover cap are both non-compliant. A Colorado candidate has already raised the payout issue with the Company’s recruiter.  
             C.  Oregon (facility opens February 1, 2026)  
            Oregon provides more runway but remains on the critical path. Registration with Paid Leave Oregon (administered by the Oregon Employment Department) should be completed no later than approximately December 1, 2025 to allow PinnacleHR configuration before the February 1, 2026 opening. Paid Leave Oregon covers family leave, medical leave (the employee’s own serious health condition), and safe leave (domestic violence, sexual assault, stalking, and harassment); the Company’s self-funded plan covers none of the medical or safe-leave components and is below the statutory benefit on rate, cap, and duration. A private-plan option is available in Oregon but only if the private plan exceeds Paid Leave Oregon benefits. The paid sick leave policy is non-compliant in Oregon on the accrual rate, the part-time cap, the waiting period (120 days versus 90 days), the absence of carryover, and the covered-uses and family-member definitions. Oregon also requires payout of accrued unused vacation upon separation (ORS 652.140), with penalty wages of up to 30 days available under ORS 652.150 for late or withheld payment; the no-payout policy and the 5-day carryover cap are non-compliant.  
             D.  Washington (facility opens May 1, 2026)  
            Washington is the furthest out but requires the same advance planning. Registration with the Washington Paid Family and Medical Leave program (administered by the Washington Employment Security Department) should be completed no later than approximately March 1, 2026. Washington PFML covers family leave, medical leave (the employee’s own serious health condition), and qualifying military exigency; the Company’s self-funded plan omits the medical and military-exigency components and is below the statutory benefit on rate, cap, and duration. A voluntary-plan option is available but only if the voluntary plan meets or exceeds Washington PFML benefits. The paid sick leave policy is non-compliant in Washington on the accrual cap (Washington prohibits accrual caps, though a use cap may be permissible), the waiting period (120 days versus 90 days), the absence of carryover (Washington requires full year-to-year carryover), and the covered-uses and family-member definitions. The accrual rate (1/40) does match Washington’s requirement. Washington, like New Jersey and New York, does not require payout of unused vacation if a clear written forfeit policy is in place, so the no-payout policy is permissible there subject to consistent application.

            The self-funded plan is below the statutory benefit on the replacement rate, the weekly cap, and the duration in every state. In Colorado, a private-plan substitute is not available to private employers at all. In the remaining six states, a private plan is permitted only if it meets or exceeds (in Oregon, exceeds) the state program in all respects; the current plan fails that test in every state, and no application has been filed. The practical consequence is that employees in New Jersey, New York, and Connecticut are paying state program contributions through payroll withholdings while receiving an inferior self-funded benefit—a dual-coverage structure that creates both overpayment/underpayment confusion and a basis for employee complaints. The Muñoz complaint demonstrates that the Company’s employees are willing to escalate to state agencies when they perceive a gap between what they are paying into and what they are receiving.  
            Recommended structural options. The Company should select one of the following approaches, in consultation with H&C, before the July 1, 2025 handbook revision:  
             Option 1 —            participate in the state-administered program in each jurisdiction and treat the self-funded benefit as a supplemental, top-up benefit (or discontinue it to avoid employee confusion and dual-coverage issues);  
             Option 2 —            file private-plan (or voluntary-plan) approvals in the six states that permit them (NJ, NY, CT, MA, OR, WA), with enhanced benefits that meet or exceed each state’s statutory minimum, and participate in CO FAMLI (no opt-out available); or  
             Option 3 —            discontinue the self-funded plan and rely entirely on the state programs, redirecting employees to file claims through the state programs (with the Company administering job protection and any supplemental top-up).  
            H&C’s preliminary recommendation is Option 1 or Option 3. A private-plan approval (Option 2) is administratively burdensome across six states, requires the Company to assume the insurance risk, and must be re-approved as benefits change; it is generally attractive only where an employer can offer benefits meaningfully above the state floor at lower cost, which is not the case here. Whichever option is selected, the Company must register with and begin remitting contributions to the four expansion-state programs regardless, because registration is a prerequisite to either participation or a private-plan filing.  
             B.  Paid Medical Leave and the Voluntary STD Misdirection  
            The Company provides no employer-funded paid medical leave for an employee’s own serious health condition beyond accrued sick leave (capped at 40 hours) and PTO (Handbook §6.4). The voluntary short-term disability plan through Keystone Benefit Administrators is employee-paid and optional; fewer than 30% of Connecticut hourly employees are enrolled. Every state in which the Company operates or will operate provides a state-mandated benefit for the employee’s own serious health condition: New Jersey TDI (up to 26 weeks), New York DBL (up to 26 weeks), Connecticut Paid Leave (up to 12 weeks), and the MA PFML, CO FAMLI, OR PLO, and WA PFML medical-leave components.  
            The more serious concern is operational. The internal email thread (March 10–17, 2025) establishes that HR staff in Connecticut and New Jersey have been directing employees who inquire about paid leave for their own conditions to the employee-paid voluntary STD plan rather than to the state programs (NJ TDI, NY DBL, CT PL) that those employees are funding through payroll withholdings. At least seven such inquiries have been identified across the current footprint (four in Bridgeport, two in Edison, one in Queens). This pattern of misdirection creates two distinct exposures: (i) employees may have retroactive claims for state benefits they were discouraged from filing; and (ii) the pattern itself is evidence of a systemic administration failure that a state agency investigating a complaint (such as the Muñoz matter) could expand to examine. The General Counsel’s March 17, 2025 interim guidance—directing HR staff not to turn away employees who ask about paid medical leave for their own conditions without checking whether a state program applies, and to escalate uncertain cases—is an appropriate stopgap but must be formalized in the revised Handbook and reinforced with training.  
                VI.  Operational Risk Assessment: Manual Leave Tracking  
            The Company tracks sick leave accruals, usage, and balances through manual Microsoft Excel workbooks maintained by two HR coordinators, one workbook per state. Paid family leave is tracked through a separate paper-based process; PTO is tracked within WorkBridge Pro but is not connected to the sick leave spreadsheets. The spreadsheets are not integrated with WorkBridge Pro (HRIS) or ClockSync Enterprise (time and attendance). This approach has been adequate for three states but presents material compliance and recordkeeping risk as the Company scales to seven states and approximately 2,560 employees.  
            Specific deficiencies identified in the HR expansion plan memorandum and confirmed by the tracking sample include:  
              No state-specific rule differentiation.            The spreadsheets apply a single company-wide policy and do not differentiate state-specific accrual rates, caps, waiting periods, or carryover rules. As documented above, the states differ significantly on each of these dimensions.  
              No automated alerts.            There are no automated notifications for accrual caps, waiting-period eligibility, or carryover deadlines. Coordinators must manually review each employee record. At 2,560 employees this is not sustainable.  
              No audit trail.            Changes to the spreadsheets are not logged. There is no record of original entries, correction dates, or reasons for adjustments. In an audit or complaint proceeding, the Company could not demonstrate how a particular balance was determined.  
              No system integration.            Hours-worked data is manually extracted from ClockSync and re-entered; demographic data is manually cross-referenced against WorkBridge Pro. This creates transcription-error risk and data inconsistency between systems.  
              Limited scope.            The spreadsheets track only earned sick leave. Paid family leave, PTO, and state program claims are tracked elsewhere or not at all. There is no single view of an employee’s total leave entitlements and usage.  
            Recent errors. Internal audits have identified a part-time New Jersey employee whose sick leave balance was incorrectly capped at 24 hours due to application of the wrong accrual rate; a full-time New York employee who was told he could not use accrued sick leave until 120 days after hire (a waiting period whose legality HR could not confirm); and negative balances in the tracking sample (a New Jersey employee at –2.5 hours and a New York employee at –0.75 hours), indicating data-entry or calculation errors. Several states impose specific recordkeeping requirements regarding leave accruals and usage; the manual system’s lack of an audit trail and state-specific logic creates a recordkeeping deficiency independent of the substantive policy gaps.  
            Recommendation. We concur with the Director of Human Resources’ recommendation that the Company invest in a dedicated leave management solution that integrates with WorkBridge Pro and ClockSync Enterprise and can be configured with state-specific rules for all seven jurisdictions. The estimated cost ($40,000–$75,000) falls within the $175,000 compliance remediation budget but would consume a material portion of it; the Company should confirm budget allocation priorities with Finance. The system should be selected by July 1, 2025 and live by Q1 2026 to support the Oregon and Washington openings. In the interim, the manual process must be supplemented with documented state-specific calculation rules, a change log, and a reconciliation procedure against WorkBridge Pro and ClockSync data.

                IX.  NJ DOL Complaint — Resolution Strategy Framework  
            Representation in the pending NJ DOL complaint (Case No. NJ-ESL-2025-04117, Carla Muñoz v. Vantage Logistics Group, Inc.) is outside the scope of this engagement and continues under the separate engagement dated February 15, 2025. To the extent the compliance review findings inform the Company’s response posture in that proceeding, we offer the following framework for the Company’s consideration with the litigation team:  
              Remediation as good faith.            The Company’s February 21, 2025 response acknowledged that the policy language “raises legitimate questions” and committed to a policy review by July 1, 2025. The compliance review confirms that the family-member definition and covered-uses deficiencies identified in the complaint are present in the policy as applied to all New Jersey employees. A revised policy adopted before the July 1, 2025 target will support the Company’s good-faith remediation posture.  
              Individual make-whole.            The complaint seeks payment for the unpaid time off or restoration of PTO hours used by Ms. Muñoz. Making the complainant whole for the specific incident is a low-cost, high-value step that demonstrates good faith and may support a cooperative resolution with the Department.  
              Anticipate expanded scope.            The Department has reserved the right to conduct a broader investigation. Because the accrual-rate, cap, and carryover deficiencies affect all New Jersey employees, the Company should be prepared for the possibility that the investigation expands beyond the individual complainant. Completing the policy revision and supervisor training before any expanded inquiry materially improves the Company’s position.  
              Cooperative resolution.            The Company should request that the Department allow a reasonable period—through the July 1, 2025 handbook revision—for completion of the review, policy update, and training before any enforcement action, consistent with the position taken in the February 21 response.  
              Record preservation.            The Company should preserve all leave records, payroll records, and communications relevant to the complaint and to the broader sick leave practices, given the Department’s reserved right to investigate and the recordkeeping deficiencies identified in Section VI.

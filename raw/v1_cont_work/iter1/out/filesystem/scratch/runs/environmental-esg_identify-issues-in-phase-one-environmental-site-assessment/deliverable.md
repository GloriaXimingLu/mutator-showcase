       1.0 INTRODUCTION AND SCOPE  
      This memorandum summarizes the environmental issues identified during our review of the Phase I Environmental Site Assessments (Phase I ESAs) and supporting environmental documents for two proposed industrial-property acquisitions by Cornerstone Industrial Partners LLC (    “    Cornerstone    ”         or the         “    Buyer    ”    ). The document set provided for review contains Phase I ESAs and supporting materials for             two separate properties    , each under contract to a different seller:  
              Property A — 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive, Toledo, Ohio         (the         “    Toledo Property    ”    ), to be acquired from Breckenridge Manufacturing Co. (    “    Breckenridge    ”    ) for $8,750,000, with acquisition financing of $6,125,000 from Great Lakes Capital Bank (the         “    Lender    ”    ).  
              Property B — 2850 Millrace Road, Dayton, Ohio         (the         “    Dayton Property    ”    ), to be acquired from Valtec Manufacturing Holdings Inc. (    “    Valtec    ”    ) for $4,200,000.  
      Each issue below is assigned a             severity rating         under the framework set forth in Section 3.0. Issues are enumerated individually; conditions that the record shows were appropriately classified or addressed are also noted (rated Low) so that the scope of the review is complete.  
      This memorandum is based solely on the documents listed in Section 2.0. No independent site reconnaissance, sampling, or regulatory file review was performed.

        3.0 SEVERITY RATING FRAMEWORK  
                         Rating  
          Definition  
                Critical  
          Fundamental deficiency or condition that could jeopardize the transaction, invalidate the All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI) / bona fide prospective purchaser (BFPP) defense, or represent a major unaddressed environmental liability. Requires resolution before proceeding.  
                High  
          Significant environmental liability, contractual exposure, or due-diligence gap requiring action (further investigation, contractual negotiation, or satisfaction of a condition) before or as a condition of closing.  
                Medium  
          Notable concern requiring further investigation, documentation, or monitoring. Not necessarily deal-breaking, but must be resolved or knowingly accepted.

          Minor issue, documentation gap, watch item, or a condition that the record shows was appropriately classified or addressed. Noted for completeness.

           4.3 Detailed Issues — Toledo Property  
          T-1. Off-site PCE groundwater plume (Lake Erie Dry Cleaning) not classified as a REC — CRITICAL.             Sources:         Greenfield Phase I ESA §§ 5.1.1, 9.1, App. A; Lender letter § 2; Ohio EPA response § 4; EDR summary (Adjacent Property A1).  
      The former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility at 4900 Lakeshore Industrial Drive (immediately north, hydraulically upgradient, ~120 feet from the property boundary) has a confirmed tetrachloroethylene (PCE) groundwater plume. Monitoring well MW-7, located ~120 feet north of the property boundary, reported PCE at 87 µg/L — more than 17 times the Ohio VAP groundwater standard of 5 µg/L. The plume migrates south-southeast, directly toward the Toledo Property. Ohio EPA confirms the Toledo Property lies within a designated Groundwater Management Zone (GMZ) for this plume, that             no investigation of PCE beneath the Toledo Property has been conducted    , and that the 2018 VAP Covenant Not to Sue applies             only         to the dry-cleaner parcel and does not address off-site migration.  
      Despite this, Greenfield’s findings (§ 9.1) list only two RECs (TCE and chromium) and do             not         classify the migrating PCE plume as a REC. The Lender’s requirements letter (§ 2) expressly requires the Phase I ESA to         “    evaluate off-site sources of contamination that may be migrating onto or otherwise affecting the subject Property    ”         and states that         “    any such conditions must be identified as recognized environmental conditions.    ”         The Phase I ESA therefore fails to satisfy an explicit lender condition and, in our view, under-classifies a condition that meets the ASTM E1527-21 REC definition (likely presence of a hazardous substance due to a release, with a documented plume migrating toward the property). This is the most significant deficiency in the Toledo due-diligence file.             Recommendation:         Obtain a REC classification or supplemental opinion; commission off-site/on-site groundwater investigation for PCE; satisfy the lender’s off-site plume evaluation requirement before relying on the Phase I ESA.  
          T-2. Seller representations (§§ 4.12, 4.13) appear inaccurate — HIGH.             Sources:         Purchase Agreement §§ 4.12, 4.13, Schedule 4.13; Ohio EPA response § 2; Greenfield ESA §§ 5.1.2, 6.2.3, 7.1; Toledo Fire Dept. record (1988).  
      Multiple seller representations appear contradicted by the record:  
              § 4.12(a)         (    “    no Release of any Hazardous Substances at, on, under, or from the Property    ”    ; Schedule 4.12(a):         “    None    ”    ) — contradicted by the documented 1988 degreasing-solvent spill, the abandoned leaking waste-TCE drum with stained soil, the 2003 inspection finding rusted/leaking drums with chromium waste and ground staining, and the floor staining/sump sheen.  
              § 4.12(b)         (    “    material compliance with all applicable Environmental Laws    ”    ) — contradicted by the 2003 Ohio EPA Notice of Violation (NOV) and consent order for improper hazardous-waste storage.  
              § 4.12(c)         (    “    has not received any Environmental Notice    ”    ; Schedule 4.12(c):         “    None    ”    ) — contradicted by the 2003 NOV/consent order, which is an         “    Environmental Notice    ”         as defined in § 1.1.  
              § 4.12(e)         (    “    no environmental liens, deed restrictions, engineering controls, institutional controls, activity and use limitations, or environmental covenants    ”    ) — questionable because the property lies within an Ohio EPA-designated GMZ, which may constitute an institutional control/activity-and-use limitation restricting groundwater use and well installation.  
              § 4.13(a) / Schedule 4.13         (    “    true, correct, and complete list of all Underground Storage Tanks    ”    ) — Schedule 4.13 lists only the active 10,000-gallon diesel UST and omits the 2,000-gallon waste-solvent UST (shown on the 1987 Sanborn map) and the 500-gallon heating-oil UST (shown on the 1948/1955 Sanborn maps). The representation that         “    no other Underground Storage Tanks are or have been located on the Property    ”         appears inaccurate.  
              § 4.13(b)         (    “    no Releases from any Underground Storage Tank    ”    ) — questionable given the unregistered, un-closed waste-solvent and heating-oil USTs of unknown status.  
      These inaccuracies are significant because the indemnification regime (Article VIII) turns on breach of representations, and the fraud/intentional-misrepresentation exception (§ 8.4(d)) pierces the basket, cap, and survival period.             Recommendation:         Demand correction of Schedules 4.12 and 4.13 and written disclosure of the 1988 spill, 2003 NOV, GMZ, and additional USTs; assess fraud-exception viability with counsel.  
          T-3. REC-1: Former TCE degreasing operations and abandoned waste-TCE drum — HIGH.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA §§ 5.3, 6.3.1, 9.1; Toledo Fire Dept. record (1988).  
      Trichloroethylene (TCE) was used for vapor degreasing in the northwest corner of the main building from 1961 to ~1992 (30+ years). A PID reading of 42.3 ppm was recorded at a floor-slab crack (background <0.5 ppm), indicating residual VOC vapors beneath the slab. No closure, decommissioning, or confirmation-sampling documentation exists for the degreasing area. An abandoned 55-gallon drum labeled         “    WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE    ”         sits behind the maintenance building, approximately half full, with a rusted bottom and evidence of past leakage; stained soil (~3 ft × 3 ft) is present beneath it. A 1988 Toledo Fire Department record documents a degreasing-solvent spill from an overturned drum, cleaned up by employees with no follow-up inspection. Greenfield recommends a Phase II ESA (soil borings, groundwater monitoring wells, VOC analysis). This is a confirmed REC requiring subsurface characterization.             Recommendation:         Proceed with Phase II; include soil-vapor/vapor-intrusion assessment.  
          T-4. REC-2: Former hexavalent chromium electroplating operations — HIGH.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA §§ 5.3, 6.3.2, 9.1; Ohio EPA response §§ 1–2.  
      Hexavalent chromium and nickel electroplating operated in the south wing from 1965 to 2004 (~39 years). The former plating room exhibits blue-green staining on floors and walls (chromium residues), concrete pitting/etching, and an in-ground sump containing ~4 inches of liquid with an iridescent sheen. Hazardous-waste disposal documentation exists only for 2004 (six manifests, 14,200 lbs, RCRA codes D007 and F006); no manifests exist for 1965–2003. A 2003 Ohio EPA inspection documented improper hazardous-waste storage (see T-12). Greenfield recommends a Phase II ESA (sump-liquid characterization, soil borings through the slab, groundwater wells, metals including hexavalent chromium). This is a confirmed REC.             Recommendation:         Proceed with Phase II; characterize sump liquid as a waste for disposal planning.  
          T-5. 2,000-gallon waste-solvent UST — unregistered, no closure records, status unknown — HIGH.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA § 6.2.2; Ohio EPA response § 3; EDR summary (Sanborn table).  
      The 1987 Sanborn map depicts a 2,000-gallon waste-solvent UST on the east side of the property. Ohio EPA/BUSTR confirm             no registration, installation, closure, or removal records         exist for this tank. No surface evidence (fill/vent pipes) was observed, but the tank’s disposition is unknown and it may remain in place. A waste-solvent UST (likely containing chlorinated solvents) with no registration and no closure is a significant potential release source. Greenfield discusses the tank but does             not         classify it as a REC. Ohio EPA recommends geophysical survey, interviews, and fire-department permit review.             Recommendation:         Geophysical survey to locate/confirm the tank; if present, closure assessment with soil/groundwater sampling for VOCs.  
          T-6. Former outdoor drum-storage area — documented leaking drums and stained soil — HIGH.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA §§ 6.2.4, 7.1; Ohio EPA response § 2; EDR summary (aerial photos).  
      A ~40 ft × 60 ft curbed area on the east side shows deteriorating curbing and stressed vegetation; 1990 and 2000 aerial photographs show drum/container storage there. The 2003 Ohio EPA inspection (Attachment A) documented         “    multiple rusted and visibly leaking 55-gallon drums    ”         in an outdoor east-side storage area containing electroplating sludge and spent chromium-bearing wastewater, with         “    staining on the ground surface beneath and around the drums.    ”         The seller acknowledged,         “    We used to keep drums out there before the EPA came around.    ”         This area coincides with the 2,000-gallon waste-solvent UST vicinity (T-5). Greenfield does not separately classify this area as a REC. Given the documented leaking drums of chromium waste and stained soil, this warrants REC-level investigation.             Recommendation:         Include this area in the Phase II scope (soil/groundwater for metals and VOCs).  
          T-7. 39 years of missing hazardous-waste manifests (1965–2003) — HIGH.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA § 8.0 (Data Gap 1), § 9.1; Ohio EPA response § 1.  
      Ohio EPA confirms that the only hazardous-waste manifests on file are for 2004; no manifests, biennial reports, or annual reports exist for 1965–2003, despite ~39 years of electroplating operations generating D007 (chromium) and F006 (electroplating sludge) wastes. The absence of disposal documentation for nearly four decades prevents any assessment of whether wastes were properly managed and raises the possibility of undocumented on-site disposal or releases. This data gap directly supports the REC-2 concern.             Recommendation:         Address in Phase II; consider whether any on-site burial/disposal occurred.  
          T-8. Lender environmental requirements not yet satisfied; funding at risk — HIGH.             Sources:         Lender letter §§ 2, 3.B, 3.C, 5, 6; Greenfield ESA § 9.  
      The Lender classified the property in its         “    Elevated Environmental Risk    ”         category (Lender letter § 1), triggering enhanced due diligence. Because the Phase I ESA identifies RECs, the Lender will             not         fund on the Phase I alone. The following lender requirements are not yet satisfied:  
              Phase II ESA (§ 3.B.1):         ASTM E1903-19, with best-case, most-likely, and worst-case remediation cost estimates (each identifying methodology, applicable cleanup standards, and time to completion), prepared by a licensed professional engineer.  
              Pollution Legal Liability insurance (§ 3.B.2):         carrier rated A- (Excellent) or better by AM Best; limits ≥ the greater of the worst-case cost estimate or $5,000,000; term ≥ 10 years from closing; Bank named additional insured and loss payee. The Lender states environmental insurance is             non-negotiable         where RECs are identified and will not rely on seller indemnity alone.  
              Remediation-cost escrow (§ 3.B.3):         if the most-likely estimate exceeds $250,000, an Environmental Escrow at Great Lakes Capital Bank equal to 125% of the most-likely estimate, drawable only with the Bank’s prior written approval.  
              Remediation plan (§ 3.B.4):         if estimated costs exceed $500,000 (most-likely or worst-case), a plan (licensed EP; submitted to/approved by Ohio EPA if applicable) demonstrating remediation within a commercially reasonable timeframe             not to exceed 36 months         from closing.  
              Vapor-intrusion assessment (§ 3.B.5):         required because VOCs are present; if risks warrant mitigation, a vapor-mitigation plan for occupied/occupiable structures, implemented at or before closing.  
              Phase I threshold requirements (§ 2):         the Phase I must name the Bank as an authorized reliance party (satisfied — the Greenfield report does so), conform to E1527-21 (satisfied), be no older than 180 days from site reconnaissance at closing (site reconnaissance Oct. 2, 2024 → valid through ~March 31, 2025; the Lender’s stated cutoff for a Dec. 15, 2024 closing is a reconnaissance no earlier than June 18, 2024 — satisfied), and the consulting firm must carry ≥ $2,000,000 professional liability (errors and omissions) insurance (Greenfield reports $5,000,000 — satisfied).  
              Unacceptable-risk triggers (§ 3.C):         the Lender may decline to fund if (a) the worst-case estimate exceeds 50% of the appraised value; (b) the property is listed/proposed for the National Priorities List (NPL); (c) the property is subject to an active RCRA corrective-action order or similar enforcement that could result in a lien senior to the mortgage; or (d) unresolved data gaps prevent adequate risk characterization. The unresolved PCE-plume classification (T-1) and the missing manifests/consent-order documentation (T-7, T-12) are data-gap risks under (d).  
              Delivery deadlines (§ 5):         Phase I in final executed form by November 15, 2024; Phase II + PLL binder + escrow evidence by November 30, 2024.  
              Post-closing covenants (§ 6):         the loan documents will require Borrower to (a) notify the Bank within 5 business days of any release/threatened release; (b) maintain environmental insurance (including PLL) for the full loan term; (c) comply with all environmental laws/permits; (d) not install, use, operate, or close any UST without the Bank’s prior written consent; and (e) provide annual environmental compliance certifications.  
          Recommendation:         Immediately commission the Phase II and initiate PLL procurement; confirm escrow and remediation-plan triggers once cost estimates are available; budget for the post-closing covenant obligations.  
          T-9. Indemnification cap ($1.75M) and 3-year survival likely insufficient — HIGH.             Sources:         Purchase Agreement §§ 8.2, 8.4; Lender letter § 3.B.2.  
      Seller’s aggregate indemnification liability is capped at $1,750,000 (20% of the purchase price) and survives only three (3) years after closing (§§ 8.4(b), (c)). Pre-Closing Environmental Liabilities are expressly subject to this cap and survival period. Remediation of chlorinated-solvent (TCE/PCE) and hexavalent-chromium contamination, plus potential off-site plume contribution, can readily exceed $1.75M and may not be discovered within three years. The Lender’s letter (§ 3.B.2) expressly warns that seller indemnification is         “    typically subject to caps, baskets, deductibles, and survival periods that may be insufficient to cover full remediation costs    ”         and will not rely on seller indemnity alone. The basket ($87,500) does not apply to environmental claims (§ 8.4(a)), which is favorable, but the cap and survival are the binding constraints. Two mitigating provisions exist but have limits: (i) the             fraud/intentional-misrepresentation exception         (§ 8.4(d)) is uncapped and non-expiring (no basket, cap, or survival period), but requires proof of intent — potentially relevant given the apparent representation inaccuracies (T-2); and (ii) the             insurance-proceeds offset and mitigation duty         (§§ 8.4(e), (f)) reduce seller indemnity by insurance proceeds actually received (net of collection costs) and require Buyer to use commercially reasonable mitigation efforts (though Buyer need not litigate against insurers).             Recommendation:         Negotiate a higher/dedicated environmental cap and longer survival; rely primarily on PLL insurance and escrow rather than seller indemnity; preserve the fraud-exception argument for the representation inaccuracies.  
          T-10. Environmental Due Diligence Period / termination right on a tight deadline — HIGH.             Sources:         Purchase Agreement §§ 1.1, 6.3; Greenfield ESA (date).  
      The Environmental Due Diligence Period ends at 5:00 p.m. ET on October 31, 2024. The Phase I ESA is dated October 25, 2024 — only six days before the deadline. Under § 6.3(b), Buyer may terminate and recover the $437,500 earnest money (5% of the purchase price) if the Phase I identifies RECs that         “    could result in Remediation Costs exceeding $500,000,    ”         by written notice on or before October 31, 2024. Given the TCE, chromium, and (unaddressed) PCE conditions, remediation costs plausibly exceed $500,000. If Buyer does not timely terminate, the right is automatically waived (§ 6.3(c)) — though indemnification rights survive.             Recommendation:         Evaluate the termination right immediately; if proceeding, document the waiver decision and ensure indemnification/insurance are in place.  
          T-11. Vapor intrusion risk (TCE and PCE) — HIGH.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA § 6.3.1 (PID 42.3 ppm); Ohio EPA response § 4 (PCE plume, dry-cleaner vapor-mitigation requirement); Lender letter § 3.B.5.  
      TCE vapors are indicated beneath the former degreasing area (PID 42.3 ppm). The upgradient PCE plume is a further vapor-intrusion source; the adjacent dry-cleaner parcel itself requires a sub-slab vapor-mitigation system. The Lender requires a vapor-intrusion assessment (Ohio EPA/U.S. EPA guidance) and, if warranted, a vapor-mitigation plan for occupied/occupiable structures, implemented at or before closing.             Recommendation:         Include soil-vapor/sub-slab sampling in the Phase II; plan for vapor mitigation in any occupied redevelopment.  
          T-12. 2003 Ohio EPA NOV and consent order — documentation unresolved — MEDIUM.             Sources:         Ohio EPA response § 2; Greenfield ESA §§ 5.1.2, 7.1, 9.1.  
      The July 17, 2003 Compliance Evaluation Inspection documented three violations: (1) improper storage — rusted/leaking drums of electroplating sludge and spent chromium wastewater with ground staining; (2) failure to label containers with accumulation-start dates; (3) failure to maintain hazardous-waste determination records. A NOV (File No. NW-2003-0471) and a Director’s Final Findings and Orders (consent order) followed, requiring a facility-wide waste audit, proper disposal, a waste-management plan, and a compliance certification within 120 days. The consent order file is archived and unavailable;             no compliance certification was produced    . The seller characterized the matter as         “    resolved years ago    ”         but could not produce the consent order.             Recommendation:         Obtain the consent order and compliance certification from Ohio EPA Central Office (30–45 day retrieval) before closing; confirm the waste audit was completed.  
          T-13. 1988 chemical spill — undocumented cleanup; seller denied knowledge — MEDIUM.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA §§ 5.1.2, 7.1, 7.2; Toledo Fire Dept. record (Mar. 14, 1988).  
      A March 14, 1988 Toledo Fire Department incident report documents a         “    chemical spill    ”         involving an         “    unknown quantity of degreasing solvent    ”         from an overturned drum, cleaned up by Breckenridge employees with no follow-up inspection. The spill does not appear in ERNS. When confronted with the record, the seller stated he was         “    not aware of any spills    ”         and called it         “    a minor incident.    ”         The undocumented nature of the cleanup and the seller’s denial are credibility and liability concerns.             Recommendation:         Include the spill location in Phase II scope; weigh seller credibility in reliance on representations.  
          T-14. Uncharacterized monitoring well MW-1 (northeast boundary) — MEDIUM.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA §§ 6.2.5, 7.1.  
      A flush-mounted monitoring well labeled         “    MW-1    ”         was observed near the northeast property boundary with a damaged cover and accumulated debris. No installation logs, construction details, or analytical data were located, and the seller disclaimed any knowledge of its origin or purpose. Given its location near the upgradient PCE-plume source, MW-1 may relate to the dry-cleaner investigation or to on-site contamination; its uncharacterized status is a data gap.             Recommendation:         Research well registration/permits; gauge and sample MW-1 if accessible; incorporate into the Phase II groundwater network.  
          T-15. 500-gallon Halstead farmhouse heating-oil UST — no closure records — MEDIUM.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA §§ 5.2.1, 6.2.2, 9.3; Ohio EPA response § 3; EDR summary (Sanborn table).  
      The 1948 and 1955 Sanborn maps show a 500-gallon heating-oil UST serving the Halstead farmhouse (northwest portion), demolished in 1961. BUSTR has no registration, closure, or removal records for this tank. No surface evidence was observed, but the tank’s status is unknown and it may remain in place beneath fill/pavement. Greenfield classifies this as an HREC on the assumption the tank was removed with the farmhouse, but that assumption is unconfirmed.             Recommendation:         Geophysical survey at the former farmhouse location; if present, closure assessment.  
          T-16. Property within Ohio EPA Groundwater Management Zone (GMZ) — MEDIUM.             Sources:         Ohio EPA response § 4; Greenfield ESA § 5.1.2; Purchase Agreement § 4.12(e).  
      Ohio EPA confirms the Toledo Property is within a GMZ established for the Lake Erie Dry Cleaning PCE plume. The GMZ may restrict new groundwater withdrawals, new well installations, and certain land uses. This may constitute an institutional control/activity-and-use limitation not reflected in the title or in the seller’s § 4.12(e) representation.             Recommendation:         Confirm GMZ restrictions with Ohio EPA Division of Drinking and Ground Waters; assess impact on redevelopment; verify title/recorded-instrument status.  
          T-17. Seller credibility and cooperation deficiencies — MEDIUM.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA § 7.1.  
      The seller (a) declined to produce corporate records for 1961–1990; (b) denied knowledge of the documented 1988 spill; (c) could not produce the 2003 consent order; (d) disclaimed knowledge of monitoring well MW-1; and (e) could not detail the former drum-storage area. These deficiencies undermine reliance on the seller’s representations and on the completeness of the seller’s document production.             Recommendation:         Reduce reliance on seller knowledge qualifiers; obtain independent records (Ohio EPA, fire department, BUSTR); consider contractual knowledge-qualifier narrowing.  
          T-18. Corporate records (1961–1990) not made available — MEDIUM.             Sources:         Greenfield ESA § 8.0 (Data Gap 2), § 7.1.  
      Operating, environmental, waste-disposal, and compliance records for 1961–1990 were not produced; the seller stated they are         “    in storage    ”         and not readily accessible. This limits characterization of historical chemical use, waste management, and potential releases during the early decades of industrial use (including the TCE-degreasing era).           D-1. Phase I ESA prepared to superseded ASTM E1527-13 standard (AAI/BFPP risk) — CRITICAL.             Sources:         Terravista ESA §§ 1.0, 2.1, 10.1 (ASTM E1527-13); § 8.6 (references E1527-21).  
      The Terravista Phase I ESA states it was         “    prepared in conformance with ASTM E1527-13    ”         and the Environmental Professional’s declaration (§ 10.1) likewise cites E1527-13. ASTM E1527-13 is the             prior, superseded         edition of the Phase I standard; the current edition is ASTM E1527-21. The U.S. EPA All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI) rule at 40 C.F.R. Part 312 has been updated to reference the current (E1527-21) standard. A Phase I ESA performed to the superseded E1527-13 standard may             not         satisfy current AAI requirements, which would jeopardize the BFPP (and innocent-landowner/contiguous-property-owner) CERCLA defenses that Cornerstone expressly intends to obtain (Terravista ESA § 2.1). The report also contains an internal inconsistency: § 8.6 references         “    the ASTM E1527-21 Phase I ESA standard    ”         while the body and certification cite E1527-13.             Recommendation:         Confirm with environmental counsel whether E1527-13 satisfies AAI as of the report date; if not, commission an update or new Phase I ESA to ASTM E1527-21 before closing.

Estimated depth to groundwater is 15–25 ft bgs, and the site is             not         within a federally designated sole-source aquifer area. Recommendation:         Factor the sensitive aquifer setting into Phase II design (include groundwater) and remediation-cost planning; confirm no on-site potable wells.

          X-1. Parallel chlorinated-solvent and chromium risks across both properties — HIGH.         Both properties share strikingly similar environmental profiles: decades of metal stamping, electroplating with hexavalent chromium/nickel, and solvent use (Toledo: confirmed TCE degreasing; Dayton: F001/F002 halogenated-solvent waste codes and the Central Valley Chemical chlorinated-solvent history). Cornerstone should expect comparable remediation cost profiles and should standardize its due-diligence and risk-allocation approach across both transactions.

The Toledo transaction has a detailed purchase agreement (with representations, indemnification, caps, survival, and a termination right) and a lender requirements letter; the Dayton transaction has neither in the document set. Cornerstone should ensure the Dayton transaction obtains contractual protections at least as robust as Toledo’s, and should be aware that the Toledo indemnification cap/survival are themselves likely insufficient (T-9).

The Toledo Phase I ESA was prepared to the current ASTM E1527-21 standard; the Dayton Phase I ESA was prepared to the superseded ASTM E1527-13 standard (D-1). Cornerstone should require a consistent, current-standard Phase I ESA for both properties to preserve AAI/BFPP defenses.

The state-of-formation and contact discrepancies between the two deal files (D-21) should be reconciled to ensure the correct entity is the ASTM         “    User    ”         and contract         “    Buyer    ”         for AAI purposes on each property.

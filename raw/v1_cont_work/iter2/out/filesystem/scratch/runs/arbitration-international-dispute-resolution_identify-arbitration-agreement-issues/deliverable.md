        V. CRITICAL Issues  
       C-1. SA §14.11 absolute consequential-damages bar in arbitration neuters the SA §11.2 indemnification carve-out  
          Severity:         CRITICAL  
          Source passages.         - SA §14.11:         “    The arbitral tribunal shall not have the authority to award punitive, exemplary, or consequential damages.    ”         (No carve-out stated.)         - SA §11.2:         “    EXCEPT FOR INDEMNIFICATION OBLIGATIONS UNDER ARTICLE 10, NEITHER PARTY SHALL BE LIABLE TO THE OTHER PARTY FOR ANY INDIRECT, INCIDENTAL, SPECIAL, CONSEQUENTIAL, OR PUNITIVE DAMAGES …    ”         - SA §11.1 carves out from the liability cap         “    (a) indemnification obligations under Article 10, (b) breaches of confidentiality obligations under Article 12, (c) claims arising from intellectual property infringement under Section 9.2(e), and (d) Buyer’s payment obligations.    ”  
          Analysis.         SA §11.2 expressly preserves consequential damages             for indemnification claims         — a deliberate carve-out. But because Article 14 mandates arbitration for         “    any dispute … arising out of or relating to this Agreement    ”         (§14.1), and §14.11 strips the tribunal of authority to award consequential damages             without any carve-out    , the §11.2 indemnification carve-out is unenforceable in the only forum available. The liability article and the dispute-resolution article are in direct conflict, and the dispute-resolution article prevails as a practical matter because it governs the tribunal’s powers. The same conflict reaches the §11.1 cap carve-outs for confidentiality and IP-infringement claims to the extent those claims seek consequential damages.  
          Recommendation.         Add an express carve-out to SA §14.11 mirroring SA §11.2:         “    The foregoing limitation shall not apply to (i) indemnification obligations under Article 10, (ii) breaches of Article 12 (Confidentiality), or (iii) claims under Section 9.2(e).    ”         Alternatively, delete §14.11 and rely on §11.2 (which already contains the negotiated carve-outs). At minimum, the two provisions must be reconciled so the indemnification carve-out survives in arbitration.  
        C-2. QA §6.6 expressly subordinates Greenleaf’s consequential-damages/indemnification rights to SA Article 14; recall-related consequential damages are barred; conflicts with QA §7.2’s broad-remedy grant  
          Severity:         CRITICAL  
          Source passages.         - QA §6.6:         “    Nothing in this Section 6 shall limit or modify Greenleaf’s rights to consequential damages, indemnification, or other remedies as provided in the Supply Agreement,             except to the extent expressly limited by the dispute resolution provisions of Article 14 of the Supply Agreement    .    ”         - QA §7.2:         “    The arbitral tribunal shall have the authority to award any remedy or relief available under applicable law, including injunctive relief, specific performance, and damages.    ”         - SA §14.11:         “    The arbitral tribunal shall not have the authority to award punitive, exemplary, or consequential damages.    ”  
          Analysis.         QA §6.6 is an express cross-reference that imports the SA Article 14 damages limitations into the recall regime. Because SA §14.11 bars consequential damages, QA §6.6 operates to bar Greenleaf’s consequential-damages recovery for recalls — the precise scenario the internal correspondence identifies as the most likely and most damaging dispute (contaminated/off-spec extract triggering an FDA recall). At the same time, QA §7.2 grants the SIAC tribunal authority to award         “    any remedy or relief available under applicable law, including … damages    ”         with no consequential-damages bar. The QA Letter is therefore internally inconsistent: §7.2 grants broad remedial authority while §6.6 narrows it by reference to SA §14.11. Which controls is ambiguous, and the ambiguity cuts against Greenleaf because the SA’s integration clause (§15.8) makes the SA control on conflict.  
          Recommendation.         (i) Delete the         “    except to the extent expressly limited by the dispute resolution provisions of Article 14 of the Supply Agreement    ”         qualifier in QA §6.6, or replace it with an express carve-out preserving recall-related consequential damages. (ii) Add a carve-out to SA §14.11 for recall-related costs (see C-1). (iii) Conform QA §7.2 to whatever damages regime is ultimately negotiated so the QA Letter is internally consistent.  
        C-3. Consequential-damages waiver + exclusivity + 13-year horizon = amplified, unmitigated exposure; Greenleaf’s stated walk-away position  
          Severity:         CRITICAL  
          Source passages.         - SA §14.11 (quoted in C-1).         - SA §2.1:         “    Supplier hereby grants to Buyer the exclusive right to purchase the Products within the Exclusivity Territory. Supplier shall not, directly or indirectly, sell, distribute, market, license, or otherwise make available any Products to any third party within the Exclusivity Territory during the Term.    ”         - SA §13.2:         “    the maximum duration of this Agreement (absent earlier termination) shall not exceed thirteen (13) years from the Effective Date (the seven (7)-year Initial Term plus two (2) Renewal Terms of three (3) years each).    ”         - SA §4.2: total Minimum Annual Commitment over the Initial Term is         “    $178,000,000, calculated as ($22,000,000 × 3) + ($28,000,000 × 4) = $66,000,000 + $112,000,000.    ”         - J. Park (email, June 30, 2025):         “    Because this is an exclusive supply agreement, Greenleaf has no backup supplier. If Pacifica’s extracts are defective, Greenleaf can’t simply switch — it has no alternative source. The consequential damages from a supply disruption are therefore amplified. The damages waiver, combined with the exclusivity commitment, puts us in a box.    ”         - J. Park:         “    a supplier’s defective ingredient triggered an FDA Class II recall. The direct damages — cost of the ingredient, shipping, disposal — came to approximately $1.8M. The consequential damages … exceeded $14M. If we had been subject to a consequential damages waiver in that supplier agreement, we would have recovered less than 12 cents on the dollar.    ”         - D. Heller (email, July 1, 2025):         “    this relationship could extend to 13 years, through July 31, 2038 … By Year 10, we’ll have built our entire curcumin and ashwagandha product lines around their extracts. A consequential damages waiver at that point is essentially giving Pacifica a free pass to disrupt our business … I’d rather walk away from the deal than sign a blanket consequential damages waiver on a $178M+ exclusive supply agreement. Make sure Marcus understands that this is a hill we’re willing to die on.    ”  
          Analysis.         The arithmetic confirms the Ridgeway precedent: recovery under a waiver = direct damages only = $1.8M; total loss = $1.8M + $14M = $15.8M; recovery ratio = $1.8M ÷ $15.8M = 0.114, i.e.         approximately 11.4 cents on the dollar         — consistent with Park’s         “    less than 12 cents on the dollar.    ”         The exclusivity term (SA §2.1) means Greenleaf contractually has no alternative source, so a supply failure converts what would otherwise be mitigable lost profits into unrecoverable consequential loss. The 13-year maximum duration (SA §13.2) and the $178M initial-term minimum (SA §4.2) — which the correspondence states will be         “    well north of $178M    ”         and         “    potentially $250M+ with renewals    ”         — deepen the embedded dependence over time. Greenleaf’s CEO has stated this is a walk-away issue.  
          Recommendation.         Negotiate one of the following, in descending order of preference: (a) delete SA §14.11’s consequential-damages bar entirely and rely on the negotiated §11.2 regime (which already carves out indemnification); (b) restructure as a cap rather than a waiver; or (c) at minimum, carve out from the waiver (i) recall-related costs, (ii) lost profits directly attributable to supply failure/exclusivity breach, and (iii) third-party claims against Greenleaf arising from defective ingredients — the carve-outs Park specifically recommended. Greenleaf has expressly stated it is         “    fine waiving    ”         punitive damages, so a punitive-only waiver is an acceptable landing zone. This issue should be Priority 1 in the Whitfield & Crane review (per the Matsuda ranking).  
        C-4. Conflicting consolidation & governing law — IPLA §9.3 pulls SA-related claims into LCIA/English law while SA §14.1/§15.11 send SA disputes to ICC/New York law  
          Severity:         CRITICAL  
          Source passages.         - IPLA §9.3:         “    claims arising under this Agreement that also relate to obligations under the Supply Agreement shall be heard jointly in a single arbitration proceeding under this Section 9.    ”         (Section 9 = LCIA, London seat, English law per §9.1/§9.2.)         - SA §14.1:         “    Any dispute, controversy, or claim arising out of or relating to this Agreement shall be finally resolved by arbitration administered by the International Chamber of Commerce under its Rules of Arbitration.    ”         - SA §14.4:         “    The arbitrator(s) shall apply the substantive laws of the State of New York, without regard to its conflict of laws principles.    ”         - SA §15.11:         “    This Agreement shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of New York, without regard to its conflict of laws principles.    ”         - IPLA §9.2:         “    The governing law of this Agreement shall be the laws of England and Wales.    ”  
          Analysis.         IPLA §9.3 purports to channel any IPLA claim that         “    also relates to obligations under the Supply Agreement    ”         into a single LCIA arbitration conducted under English law. But the SA’s own clause (§14.1) sends SA disputes to the ICC, and SA §15.11/§14.4 require New York substantive law. The same Supply Agreement obligation could therefore be governed and adjudicated under two different regimes depending on whether it is brought standalone (ICC, New York law) or alongside an IPLA claim (LCIA, English law). This is a direct, substantive conflict — not merely a procedural choice — because the governing law differs (New York vs. England & Wales) and the administering institution differs (ICC vs. LCIA). It is also one-directional: there is no reciprocal provision by which the SA pulls IPLA claims into the ICC, and no provision at all for consolidating QA Letter claims.  
          Recommendation.         Either (a) adopt a single institution/seat/governing law for all three documents (preferred — see H-1/H-2); or (b) if a multi-institution structure is retained, replace IPLA §9.3 with a mutual, symmetrical consolidation/joinder protocol that designates a single lead forum and a single governing law for consolidated disputes, agreed in writing by both parties, and extend it to cover QA Letter claims. As drafted, §9.3 should not be accepted.  
        C-5. Conflicting document hierarchy — SA §15.8 vs. QA §8.1 vs. IPLA §10.3 on which dispute-resolution mechanism controls quality disputes  
          Severity:         CRITICAL  
          Source passages.         - SA §15.8:         “    In the event of a conflict between the terms of this Agreement and the terms of the QA Side Letter or the IPLA, this Agreement shall control.    ”         - QA §8.1:         “    In the event of any conflict or inconsistency between the terms of this Side Letter and the terms of the Supply Agreement, the Supply Agreement shall control;             provided, however    , that with respect to the subject matters specifically addressed in this Side Letter — including testing protocols, rejection and return procedures, recall cost allocation, and             the dispute resolution mechanism set forth in Section 7         — this Side Letter shall control.    ”         - IPLA §10.3:         “    In the event of any conflict between the terms of this Agreement and the terms of the Supply Agreement, the Supply Agreement shall control,             except with respect to matters specifically and exclusively addressed by this Agreement    .    ”  
          Analysis.         The three integration clauses point in different directions for the same subject matter. A quality dispute (e.g., a Nonconformity escalating to a recall) is         “    specifically addressed    ”         by QA §7 (SIAC expert determination/arbitration)             and         falls within SA §14.1’s broad         “    any dispute … arising out of or relating to this Agreement    ”         (ICC). Under SA §15.8 the SA (ICC) controls; under QA §8.1 the QA §7 (SIAC) controls for quality dispute resolution; under IPLA §10.3 the SA controls except for matters         “    exclusively addressed    ”         by the IPLA. The result is that a party can plausibly invoke two different institutions (ICC vs. SIAC) and two different governing laws (New York vs. Singapore) for the same quality dispute, with no clear winner. This is a classic pathological-clause configuration that invites jurisdictional challenge — the precise risk Pacifica experienced in 2023 (see H-9).  
          Recommendation.         Reconcile the three integration clauses into a single, explicit hierarchy that names, for each category of dispute, the controlling forum and governing law. At minimum, QA §8.1’s carve-out for         “    the dispute resolution mechanism set forth in Section 7    ”         must be either (i) confirmed and mirrored in SA §15.8, or (ii) removed so the SA’s ICC regime governs all disputes. The current state — SA says SA controls, QA says QA §7 controls for quality DR — is unworkable.  
        C-6. SA §14.6 appeal-on-law modeled on English Arbitration Act 1996 s.69, but the seat is Singapore — likely ineffective; pathological-clause risk  
          Severity:         CRITICAL  
          Source passages.         - SA §14.6:         “    The arbitral award shall be final and binding, and judgment upon the award may be entered in any court of competent jurisdiction. Notwithstanding the foregoing, either party may appeal the award on questions of law to the High Court of Singapore.    ”         - SA §14.2:         “    The seat of the arbitration shall be Singapore.    ”         - Harmon & Grey memo:         “    This provision was modeled on the approach under the English Arbitration Act 1996, specifically Section 69, which permits parties to agree to appeal arbitral awards on questions of law to the courts … Section 14.6 provides that any appeal on a question of law arising out of an award shall be brought before the High Court of Singapore, sitting in its capacity as the supervisory court at the juridical seat.    ”         - Harmon & Grey memo (on Pacifica’s 2023 experience):         “    the losing party challenged enforcement in the Korean courts, contending that the arbitration clause was         ‘    pathological    ’         due to certain alleged ambiguities in the clause’s drafting … delayed recovery by approximately fourteen months.    ”  
          Analysis.         The drafters expressly modeled §14.6 on English Arbitration Act 1996 s.69, which permits an appeal on a question of law to the English courts — but only because English curial law provides that mechanism. The seat here is Singapore (§14.2). Singapore’s default curial regime for international arbitrations is the UNCITRAL Model Law (via the International Arbitration Act, Cap. 143A), under which recourse against an award is limited to the grounds in Model Law Article 34 (setting aside) and Article 36 (refusal of enforcement) — procedural/limited grounds, not a general appeal on questions of law. There is no Singapore equivalent of s.69 in the default international regime. An agreed appeal         “    on questions of law to the High Court of Singapore    ”         is therefore of doubtful effectiveness and creates exactly the kind of clause ambiguity that invites post-award challenge — the         “    pathological    ”         clause problem Pacifica suffered in 2023. The provision also undermines the finality it purports to secure (the memo says it         “    was drafted to ensure the finality of arbitral awards    ”    ). Compounding the problem (see M-9), the appeal would require the Singapore High Court to decide questions of             New York         law (§14.4).  
          Recommendation.         Delete the appeal-on-law sentence in SA §14.6 and rely on the Model Law’s limited recourse grounds, which is the Singapore-seated norm and maximizes finality and enforceability. If an appeal mechanism is genuinely desired, seat the arbitration in London (where s.69 actually applies) or opt expressly into a Singapore domestic-regime appeal route with specialist counsel confirmation that it is available — but the cleaner fix is removal. This issue directly conflicts with the memo’s stated objective of avoiding pathological clauses.  
      
         VI. HIGH Issues  
       H-1. Three different arbitral institutions (ICC / SIAC / LCIA) across the three agreements  
          Severity:         HIGH  
          Source passages.         - SA §14.1: ICC. QA §7.1/§7.2: SIAC. IPLA §9.1: LCIA.         - J. Park (email, June 30, 2025):         “    I’ve noticed that the three transaction documents appear to designate three different arbitral institutions and three different governing laws … In my experience, when you have fragmented dispute resolution across related agreements, you end up with parallel proceedings, inconsistent results, and a lot of wasted legal spend. We should ask W&C to scrub the entire package for consistency.    ”         - A. Matsuda (email, July 1, 2025):         “    Cross-document consistency: Ask W&C to identify all conflicts and fragmentation across the SA, QA Side Letter, and IPLA — institutions, governing law, and procedural mechanisms.    ”         - Harmon & Grey memo:         “    We recognize that the parties may wish to discuss whether to harmonize certain procedural aspects of the dispute resolution mechanisms … However, we are of the view that the current structure … is workable and does not require fundamental restructuring.    ”  
          Analysis.         Three institutions mean three sets of rules, three secretariats, three fee schedules, and three procedural cultures for a single integrated transaction. The Harmon & Grey memo concedes the parties         “    may wish to discuss whether to harmonize    ”         — an implicit acknowledgment that the structure is fragmented — but asserts it is         “    workable.    ”         The internal correspondence disagrees and flags parallel proceedings, inconsistent results, and wasted spend. Because the agreements are cross-referenced and incorporated (SA §3.3 incorporates the QA Letter; SA §8.2 incorporates the IPLA; QA §8.1 makes the QA Letter part of the SA), a single factual dispute (e.g., a defective batch that is also a warranty breach and an IP/regulatory-data issue) can plausibly be filed in two or three institutions at once.  
          Recommendation.         Consolidate to a single institution for all three documents. Given Pacifica’s preference for a Singapore-connected forum and the New York Convention enforceability of Singapore-seated awards, SIAC (Singapore) for all three is the most defensible single-institution outcome; ICC is an acceptable alternative. If three institutions are retained, add an express, symmetrical consolidation protocol and a clear subject-matter allocation table so each dispute type has one and only one designated forum.  
        H-2. Three different governing laws (New York / Singapore / England & Wales)  
          Severity:         HIGH  
          Source passages.         - SA §14.4 and §15.11: New York. QA §7.3: Singapore. IPLA §9.2: England & Wales.         - J. Park (email, June 30, 2025):         “    the three transaction documents appear to designate … three different governing laws … intentional or a drafting oversight by Harmon & Grey?    ”  
          Analysis.         Three governing laws for an integrated transaction create the risk that the same conduct is characterized differently depending on the agreement under which it is pleaded (e.g., a warranty breach may sound in UCC terms under the SA, in Singapore contract/Sale of Goods terms under the QA Letter, and in English law terms under the IPLA). This drives inconsistent outcomes and complicates any consolidated proceeding. It also interacts with C-4: the IPLA’s English-law consolidation would displace the SA’s New York law for the very SA obligations being consolidated.  
          Recommendation.         Adopt a single governing law across all three documents. New York law is the SA’s choice and is well understood for UCC sale-of-goods and commercial matters; Singapore law is defensible given Pacifica’s domicile and the QA Letter’s manufacturing focus. The key is consistency. If a single law is not achievable, at minimum align the IPLA’s governing law with the SA’s (both commercial/IP licensing work well under either New York or English law) and document the rationale.  
        H-3. No joinder mechanism for non-signatory co-manufacturers in any of the three clauses  
          Severity:         HIGH  
          Source passages.         - A. Matsuda (email, June 30, 2025):         “    none of the three arbitration clauses I’ve reviewed appear to contain any mechanism for joinder of non-signatories or third parties. The co-manufacturers are not parties to the SA, the QA Side Letter, or the IPLA … Under the ICC Rules, joinder of a non-signatory requires the non-signatory’s consent, which our co-manufacturers would have no incentive to give.    ”         - SA §15.9:         “    Nothing in this Agreement … is intended to or shall confer upon any Person other than the Parties and their respective successors and permitted assigns any legal or equitable right, benefit, remedy, or claim …    ”         (No third-party beneficiaries.)         - IPLA §10.8: same no-third-party-beneficiaries formulation.  
          Analysis.         Greenleaf’s manufacturing model relies on U.S. co-manufacturers that blend, encapsulate, and package finished supplements using Pacifica’s extracts. A quality failure will typically implicate both Pacifica (defective ingredient) and the co-man (failure to detect or contribution to the defect). None of the three arbitration clauses permits joinder of these non-signatories, and the ICC/SIAC/LCIA rules each require the non-signatory’s consent to joinder — consent the co-mans have no incentive to give. The no-third-party-beneficiaries clauses reinforce the bar. The practical consequence is detailed in H-4.  
          Recommendation.           Source passages.         - QA §7.1:         “    The Expert’s determination shall be final and binding on the Parties, subject to Section 7.2.    ”         - QA §7.2:         “    If the dispute involves an amount in excess of Five Million United States Dollars ($5,000,000), or if the expert determination rendered pursuant to Section 7.1 is challenged by either Party, the dispute shall be escalated to binding arbitration …    ”         - Harmon & Grey memo:         “    Expert determination offers a streamlined, cost-effective resolution pathway appropriate for the majority of quality-related disagreements likely to arise in the ordinary course of performance.    ”

The         “    final and binding    ”         language in §7.1 is hollowed out by §7.2, which permits             either         party to escalate to full arbitration simply by         “    challenging    ”         the expert determination — with no standard for what constitutes a permissible challenge and no consequence for challenging in bad faith.           Severity:         HIGH  
          Source passages.         - SA §14.5:         “    No discovery shall be permitted in the arbitration. Each party shall have the right to present documentary evidence and witness testimony at the hearing.    ”         - A. Matsuda (email, July 1, 2025):         “    In a quality dispute, we’ll need access to Pacifica’s manufacturing records, batch testing data, and Certificates of Analysis.           Analysis.         A blanket         “    no discovery    ”         bar is broader than the IBA Rules the memo cites. The IBA Rules on the Taking of Evidence (Article 3) in fact permit limited, targeted document production via a Redfern-schedule request keyed to specific issues — they constrain, but do not prohibit, document production. SA §14.5 goes further and bars all compelled production. In a contamination/adulteration dispute, Greenleaf’s case will depend on Pacifica’s manufacturing records, batch testing data, and Certificates of Analysis — documents that sit with Pacifica. Although the QA Letter grants audit rights (§4.4) and contemplates exchange of samples/data (§3.4), those pre-dispute mechanisms do not guarantee production of the full documentary record needed to prove a claim in arbitration, and §14.5 would bar the tribunal from ordering it. The memo’s own rationale — protecting Pacifica’s trade secrets from         “    fishing expeditions    ”         — is achievable through confidentiality orders and targeted production without a blanket bar.  
          Recommendation.         Replace the blanket bar with a targeted, IBA-consistent document-production regime: permit document production limited to specific, identified documents relevant to material issues in dispute, governed by a Redfern schedule, with a confidentiality/attorney-eyes-only overlay for trade secrets. Preserve the right to present documentary evidence and witness testimony. At minimum, carve out production of manufacturing records, batch records, COAs, and testing data for any batch that is the subject of the dispute.  
        H-8. SA §14.9 one-year limitation period — enforceability doubt under New York UCC; aggressive for a 7-year supply relationship with latent defects; conflicts with SA §7.3 latent-defect window  
          Severity:         HIGH  
          Source passages.         - SA §14.9:         “    The limitation period for commencing arbitration shall be one (1) year from the date the claiming party knew or should have known of the facts giving rise to the claim.    ”         - A. Matsuda (email, July 1, 2025):         “    The SA imposes a one-year limitations period for all claims. I have questions about whether that’s enforceable under New York law for a UCC-governed sale of goods, and frankly it seems very aggressive for a 7-year supply relationship where latent defects in botanical extracts may not be discovered for years after delivery.    ”         - SA §7.3:         “    Claims for latent defects may be raised by Buyer in writing to Supplier within twelve (12) months of the date of delivery of the applicable Products.    ”         - QA §5.1: latent defects         “    within thirty (30) calendar days of discovery for latent defects that are not reasonably discoverable through standard incoming verification testing.    ”         - SA §13.1/§13.2: Initial Term seven (7) years (August 1, 2025 – July 31, 2032), extendable to thirteen (13) years.  
          Analysis.         Three problems. (1)             Enforceability.         Under UCC §2-725 (as adopted in New York), the default limitations period for sale-of-goods warranty claims is four years; parties may reduce it by agreement but not below one year. A one-year period sits at the statutory floor and is the most aggressive reduction permitted — and, because §2-725(2) generally accrues a breach-of-warranty claim at tender of delivery (not at discovery), a         “    one year from knowledge    ”         trigger may be inconsistent with UCC accrual rules, creating enforceability doubt. (2)             Latent defects.         Botanical-extract defects (e.g., contamination, adulteration, stability failures) may not manifest until well after delivery — the QA Letter itself recognizes latent defects         “    not reasonably discoverable through standard incoming verification testing.    ”         A one-year-from-knowledge clock is short relative to the seven-to-thirteen-year relationship. (3)             Internal conflict.         SA §7.3 measures latent-defect claims from the             date of delivery         (12 months), while §14.9 measures arbitration from the             date of knowledge         (1 year). For a latent defect discovered, e.g., 13 months after delivery, §7.3’s delivery-based window may have closed while §14.9’s knowledge-based clock may still be running — or vice versa — producing contradictory results depending on which provision is applied.  
          Recommendation.         Lengthen the limitation period to at least the UCC default (four years) or, better, to a period tied to the relationship (e.g., the longer of three years from discovery or the Term plus a survival tail). Reconcile §14.9 with §7.3 so both run from the same trigger (discovery) for latent defects, and confirm the period is enforceable under New York UCC §2-725. Matsuda has flagged this for Whitfield & Crane analysis; it should be a signing condition.

To the extent New York law does not address an issue, the arbitrator(s) shall apply general principles of international commercial law.    ”  
          Analysis.         For a sale-of-goods contract, New York law (UCC Article 2) comprehensively addresses the commercial issues most likely to arise. The fallback to         “    general principles of international commercial law    ”         is vague (commonly read as the UNIDROIT Principles, but not specified) and could be invoked to avoid or supplement UCC provisions in ways the parties did not intend, introducing unpredictability.  
          Recommendation.         Either delete the fallback (relying on New York UCC alone) or specify the fallback source (e.g., UNIDROIT Principles of International Commercial Contracts) and limit it to issues genuinely not addressed by New York law.  
        L-2. Harmon & Grey memo mischaracterizes the IPLA as a         “    cross-licensing    ”         — it is a one-way license  
          Severity:         LOW  
          Source passages.         - Harmon & Grey memo:         “    The IPLA, which governs the cross-licensing of certain intellectual property rights, regulatory data packages, and proprietary formulations …    ”         - IPLA §2.1:         “    Pacifica hereby grants to Greenleaf a limited, non-exclusive, non-transferable, non-sublicensable, royalty-free license to use the Licensed IP solely for the Authorized Purpose …    ”  
          Analysis.         The IPLA is a one-way license from Pacifica to Greenleaf, not a cross-license. The mischaracterization in the rationale memo is minor but suggests the drafter’s overview may not precisely track the operative grant, which is worth noting given that the same memo drives the dispute-resolution rationale.  
          Recommendation.         Correct the characterization in any revised rationale memo; no operative-text change required, but confirm the consolidation rationale (C-4) is not premised on a cross-license assumption.  
        L-3. IPLA §2.2(b) restricts sharing Licensed IP with co-manufacturers without Pacifica’s consent — compounds co-man proof problems  
          Severity:         LOW  
          Source passages.         - IPLA §2.2(b): Greenleaf shall not         “    disclose, distribute, or make available the Licensed IP to any third party, including without limitation Greenleaf’s co-manufacturers, contract laboratories, or Affiliates, without Pacifica’s prior written consent, except as strictly necessary for FDA submissions …    ”         - H-3/H-4 joinder and parallel-proceedings analysis.  
          Analysis.         The IPLA restricts Greenleaf’s ability to share Licensed IP (extraction process data, analytical methods, batch records) with its co-manufacturers without Pacifica’s consent. In a co-man dispute, this restriction could impede Greenleaf’s ability to share the very process data needed to prove (or disprove) the co-man’s contribution to a defect, compounding the joinder/parallel-proceedings problem in H-3/H-4.  
          Recommendation.         Add a carve-out to IPLA §2.2(b) permitting disclosure to co-manufacturers (and their counsel) to the extent reasonably necessary to investigate or defend a quality claim, subject to written confidentiality obligations. Coordinate with the H-3 joinder fix.  
        L-4.         “    No third-party beneficiaries    ”         clauses (SA §15.9; IPLA §10.8) reinforce the joinder bar  
          Severity:         LOW  
          Source passages.         - SA §15.9 and IPLA §10.8: no third-party beneficiaries formulations (quoted in H-3).         - H-3 joinder analysis.  
          Analysis.         The no-third-party-beneficiaries clauses reinforce that co-manufacturers (non-parties) derive no rights under the agreements and cannot invoke the arbitration clauses — a secondary support for the H-3 joinder gap rather than an independent defect.  
          Recommendation.         Address through the H-3 joinder/consent mechanism (which would operate by separate joinder agreement, not by third-party-beneficiary status). No standalone change required, but ensure the joinder mechanism is drafted so it does not rely on third-party-beneficiary theory.  
      
         L-5. SA Article 14 is silent on the language of arbitration, while IPLA §9.1 and QA §7.2 both specify English  
      Severity: LOW  
      Source passages. - SA Article 14 (§§14.1–14.11): no provision specifies the language of the arbitration. - IPLA §9.1: “The language of the arbitration shall be English.” - QA §7.2: “The language of the arbitration shall be English.”  
      Analysis. The IPLA and QA Letter each expressly designate English as the arbitration language; the SA — the core commercial agreement and the one most likely to host the largest disputes — does not. Under the ICC Rules the tribunal may determine the language in the absence of party agreement, defaulting to the language of the contract (English), so the practical risk is low. But the omission is an inconsistency across the suite and leaves a procedural variable open that the other two agreements close.  
      Recommendation. Add a sentence to SA §14.1 (or a new §14.12) designating English as the language of the arbitration, conforming to IPLA §9.1 and QA §7.2.

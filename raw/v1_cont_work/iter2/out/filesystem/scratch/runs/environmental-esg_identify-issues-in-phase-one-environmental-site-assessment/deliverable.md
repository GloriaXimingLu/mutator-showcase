        IV. ISSUES LEDGER  
       A. CRITICAL ISSUES  
       Issue 1 — Off-site PCE groundwater plume NOT classified as a REC; direct nonconformance with the Lender’s express requirement  
          Severity: CRITICAL             Sources:         Lender letter §2; Ohio EPA letter §4; Phase I ESA §5.1.1 and §9.0 (Findings); EDR summary, Adjacent Property A1.  
      The Lender’s letter expressly requires that the Phase I ESA evaluate off-site contamination migrating toward the Property             and classify any such condition as a REC    :  
      “    The Bank specifically requires that the Phase I ESA evaluate off-site sources of contamination that may be migrating onto or otherwise affecting the subject Property, including but not limited to contaminated groundwater plumes from adjacent or nearby properties…             any such conditions must be identified as recognized environmental conditions.    ”         (Lender letter §2)  
      The record documents a confirmed tetrachloroethylene (PCE) plume migrating toward the Property from the former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility immediately to the north (hydraulically upgradient):  
      “    the Subject Property at 4820 Lakeshore Industrial Drive is located within a designated             Groundwater Management Zone         (    ‘    GMZ    ’    ) established by Ohio EPA in connection with a confirmed release of             tetrachloroethylene    … from the former Lake Erie Dry Cleaning facility… The maximum PCE concentration detected during the 2014 investigation was             87 µg/L    … at monitoring well             MW-7    , which is located approximately             120 feet north         of the northern property boundary of the Subject Property… the applicable generic numerical groundwater quality standard for PCE under Ohio’s Voluntary Action Program is             5 µg/L    … The detected concentration of 87 µg/L therefore exceeds the applicable standard by a factor of approximately             17.4    .    ”         (Ohio EPA letter §4)  
      “        no investigation of PCE concentrations in groundwater beneath the Subject Property has been conducted    , and the lateral and vertical extent of PCE plume migration onto or beneath the Subject Property has not been delineated.    ”         (Ohio EPA letter §4)  
      “    A             covenant not to sue         was issued under… the Ohio VAP… for the Lake Erie Dry Cleaning property… the VAP covenant applies             exclusively         to the Lake Erie Dry Cleaning parcel and does not address, release, or otherwise cover off-site migration of the PCE groundwater plume onto adjacent properties, including the Subject Property.    ”         (Ohio EPA letter §4)  
          Analysis.         The Phase I ESA discusses the dry-cleaner plume in its records review (§5.1.1) but             does not carry it into the Findings/Conclusions (§9.0)         as a REC, CREC, or HREC. The Findings identify only REC-1 (TCE), REC-2 (chromium), HREC-1 (farmhouse UST), and one de minimis condition. The off-site PCE plume — at 17.4× the standard, migrating toward the Property, with the Property inside a designated GMZ and no delineation beneath the Property — is omitted from the findings entirely. This is a direct nonconformance with the Lender’s express requirement that off-site migrating contamination         “    must be identified as recognized environmental conditions.    ”         Under Lender §3.B, the Lender will not fund on a Phase I alone once RECs are identified; the failure to classify this plume as a REC both understates the environmental risk and fails the Lender’s specific deliverable requirement. The Lender’s environmental counsel is directed to         “    independently review the Phase I ESA for AAI compliance    ”         (Lender letter §4).  
          Recommendation.         Require Greenfield to issue a supplement classifying the off-site PCE plume as a REC (or, at minimum, a CREC/vapor condition) and to add off-site groundwater and vapor investigation to the Phase II scope. Confirm with the Lender that the supplement cures the nonconformance before relying on the report.  
      
        Issue 2 — Two historical USTs omitted from Schedule 4.13; potential breach of §4.13(a) and (b)  
          Severity: CRITICAL             Sources:         APA Schedule 4.13; APA §4.13(a)–(b); Phase I ESA §5.2.1, §6.2.2; Ohio EPA letter §3; EDR summary (Sanborn table).  
      Schedule 4.13 lists only one UST (the active 10,000-gallon diesel UST, Facility ID 48-22-10-0087) and affirmatively represents completeness:  
      “        No other Underground Storage Tanks are or have been located on the Property.    ”         (Schedule 4.13)  
      Section 4.13(a) represents that Schedule 4.13         “    sets forth a true, correct, and complete list of all Underground Storage Tanks that are or have been located on the Property,    ”         and §4.13(b) represents that         “    there have been no Releases from any Underground Storage Tank located on the Property.    ”  
      The record documents             two additional historical USTs         that are not on Schedule 4.13 and for which no registration or closure records exist:  
      “    A notation indicates a             ‘        2,000-gal. waste solvent tank (underground)        ’         on the east side of the property, in an area between the main building and the eastern property boundary.    ”         (Phase I ESA §5.2.1, 1987 Sanborn)  
      “    A notation on the map indicates a             ‘        500-gal. fuel oil tank (underground)        ’         serving the farmhouse.    ”         (Phase I ESA §5.2.1, 1948/1955 Sanborn)  
      “    BUSTR’s database contains             no records of any other registered underground storage tanks         at the Subject Property. Specifically, no registration filings, installation permits, closure notifications, tank removal reports, site assessment results, or any other documentation exists in BUSTR’s records for either of the following: A             2,000-gallon waste solvent underground storage tank    …; or A             500-gallon heating oil underground storage tank    …    ”         (Ohio EPA letter §3)  
      “    UNKNOWN — No registration, no closure records.             Tank may remain in place.    ”         (EDR summary, Sanborn table, 2,000-gal waste solvent UST)  
      “    UNKNOWN — Pre-1961 farmhouse demolished. No records of UST removal.             Tank may remain in place beneath current pavement/fill.    ”         (EDR summary, Sanborn table, 500-gal heating oil UST)  
          Analysis.         Schedule 4.13 is incomplete and contains an affirmative misstatement (    “    No other Underground Storage Tanks are or have been located on the Property    ”    ). Two historical USTs — a 2,000-gallon waste-solvent UST and a 500-gallon heating-oil UST — are documented on Sanborn maps, are absent from the BUSTR database, and have no closure/removal records; both         “    may remain in place.    ”         A 2,000-gallon waste-solvent UST with no closure records, located in the same east-side area where stressed vegetation and historical drum storage were observed (see Issue 10), is a material omission. This is a potential breach of §4.13(a) (incomplete list) and §4.13(b) (no-release representation cannot be supported for tanks whose status is         “    unknown    ”    ). The         “    to Seller’s knowledge    ”         qualifier in §4.13(b) is of limited value where the tanks’ very existence is documented on Sanborn maps in the Seller’s possession.  
          Recommendation.         Demand that Seller supplement Schedule 4.13 to disclose both historical USTs and produce any closure/removal documentation; if none exists, require a geophysical survey to locate/confirm the tanks as a condition of closing, and seek a specific indemnity for these tanks outside the Cap (or treat as a §4.13 rep breach).  
      
        Issue 3 — 2003 Ohio EPA Notice of Violation not disclosed; potential breach of §4.12(c)  
          Severity: CRITICAL             Sources:         APA §4.12(c) and Schedule 4.12(c); Ohio EPA letter §2; Phase I ESA §5.1.2, §7.1.  
      Section 4.12(c) represents:  
      “    Except as set forth on Schedule 4.12(c), Seller has not received any             Environmental Notice         from any Governmental Authority regarding the Property, and there are no pending or, to Seller’s knowledge, threatened environmental enforcement actions, investigations, claims, or proceedings relating to the Property. There are no outstanding consent orders, consent decrees, administrative orders, settlement agreements, or court orders to which Seller is a party or by which the Property is bound…    ”  
      Schedule 4.12(c) states:             “        None.        ”  
      The record establishes that Seller received an Environmental Notice — a Notice of Violation — addressed personally to the Seller’s principal:  
      “    Ohio EPA’s Northwest District Office conducted a Compliance Evaluation Inspection (    ‘    CEI    ’    )… on July 17, 2003… As a result of the violations documented during the July 17, 2003 CEI, Ohio EPA issued a             Notice of Violation (        ‘        NOV        ’        ), File No. NW-2003-0471, on August 12, 2003. The NOV was addressed to Breckenridge Manufacturing Co. and its principal, Gerald Breckenridge III    , as the owner and operator of the facility.    ”         (Ohio EPA letter §2)  
      “    The NOV was subsequently resolved through a             Director’s Final Findings and Orders (consent order)    , which required Breckenridge Manufacturing Co. to undertake the following corrective actions: (a) Conduct a facility-wide waste audit… (b) Properly characterize and arrange for lawful disposal of all accumulated hazardous waste… (c) Develop and implement a written hazardous waste management plan… (d) Submit a written compliance certification…    ”         (Ohio EPA letter §2)  
          Analysis.         The 2003 NOV is an         “    Environmental Notice    ”         within the APA’s definition, and the resulting Director’s Final Findings and Orders is an         “    outstanding consent order    ”         within the meaning of §4.12(c). Both were received by Seller (and addressed personally to Gerald Breckenridge III). Schedule 4.12(c) discloses         “    None.    ”         This is a direct breach of §4.12(c). The         “    to Seller’s knowledge    ”         qualifier does not protect Seller here because the NOV was addressed to Breckenridge III personally, and Breckenridge III acknowledged the 2003 inspection in his interview (Phase I §7.1:         “    Mr. Breckenridge acknowledged that an Ohio EPA compliance inspection occurred in 2003 and that a Notice of Violation was issued    ”    ). This is also a potential fraud/intentional-misrepresentation trigger that would pierce the Basket, Cap, and Survival Period under §8.4(d).  
          Recommendation.         Treat as a §4.12(c) breach; require Seller to disclose the NOV and consent order on Schedule 4.12(c) and produce the consent order and compliance certification; evaluate whether the knowing non-disclosure supports a fraud claim outside the §8.4 limitations.  
      
        Issue 4 — Lender Phase II and conditional requirements triggered; the Loan cannot close on the Phase I alone  
          Severity: CRITICAL             Sources:         Lender letter §3.B; Phase I ESA §9.1 (REC-1, REC-2).  
      The Phase I ESA identifies two RECs (TCE and hexavalent chromium — see Issues 5 and 6). The Lender’s letter makes the consequences automatic:  
      “    If the Phase I ESA identifies one or more RECs,             the Bank will not fund the Loan based solely on the Phase I ESA    . In such event, the Borrower must satisfy             all         of the following additional requirements before the Bank will proceed to closing: 1. Phase II Environmental Site Assessment… 2. Environmental Insurance… 3. Remediation Cost Reserve / Escrow… 4. Remediation Plan… 5. Vapor Intrusion Assessment.    ”         (Lender letter §3.B)  
          Analysis.         Because two RECs are identified,             all five         conditional requirements are triggered simultaneously. The transaction cannot close unless and until the Borrower delivers: (i) a Phase II ESA (ASTM E1903-19) with best-/most-likely-/worst-case remediation cost estimates; (ii) a PLL policy (≥ $5,000,000 / 10-year term, Bank as additional insured and loss payee); (iii) an Environmental Escrow (125% of most-likely cost if > $250,000); (iv) a remediation plan (if costs > $500,000), approved by Ohio EPA where applicable, with completion within 36 months; and (v) a vapor intrusion assessment (VOCs are present — TCE). Each of these is detailed at Issues 11–14 below. The Lender also reserves the right to decline outright under §3.C if, e.g., the worst-case cost exceeds 50% of appraised value or unresolved data gaps prevent adequate characterization (see Issue 19).  
          Recommendation.         Immediately commission the Phase II ESA scoped to all RECs             and         the off-site PCE plume (Issue 1); obtain PLL quotes; model the escrow and remediation-plan triggers; and confirm the November 30, 2024 delivery deadline is achievable (Issue 17).  
      
         B. HIGH-SEVERITY ISSUES  
       Issue 5 — REC-1: Former TCE vapor-degreasing operations and abandoned waste-TCE drum  
          Severity: HIGH             Sources:         Phase I ESA §1.0, §6.2.3, §6.3.1, §9.1.  
      “    Trichloroethylene was used as a degreasing solvent in the northwest portion of the main manufacturing building from             1961 through approximately 1992    . A photoionization detector (PID) reading of             42.3 ppm         was recorded at a crack in the floor slab of the former degreasing area, indicating residual volatile organic compound vapors.             No closure documentation was located         for the degreasing area following the cessation of TCE use. An abandoned 55-gallon drum labeled             ‘        WASTE TRICHLOROETHYLENE        ’         was observed behind the maintenance building, approximately half full, with a rusted bottom and             evidence of past leakage onto the surrounding soil    . A 1988 chemical spill involving degreasing solvent was documented in Toledo Fire Department records.    ”         (Phase I ESA §1.0)  
      “    Background PID readings in other areas of the main building were generally less than 0.5 ppm.    ”         (Phase I ESA §6.3.1)  
          Analysis.         TCE is a carcinogenic chlorinated solvent; 30+ years of vapor-degreasing use, an elevated PID reading (42.3 ppm vs. <0.5 ppm background — 42.3 ÷ 0.5 ≈             84× background    ), undocumented closure, a leaking waste-TCE drum with stained soil, and a documented 1988 spill collectively establish a release to soil and likely groundwater. This is the most significant on-site REC and the principal driver of the Lender’s vapor-intrusion and PLL requirements.  
      
        Issue 6 — REC-2: Former hexavalent-chromium and nickel electroplating operations  
          Severity: HIGH             Sources:         Phase I ESA §1.0, §6.3.2, §9.1; Ohio EPA letter §1.  
      “    Electroplating operations involving hexavalent chromium and nickel compounds were conducted from             1965 to 2004         in the south wing of the main manufacturing building. Visible blue-green staining on concrete floors and walls is consistent with chromium residues. An active in-ground sump in the former electroplating room contains approximately four inches of liquid with a             visible iridescent sheen    . Hazardous waste disposal documentation was available only for the final year of operations (2004): six manifests documenting the disposal of             14,200 pounds         of waste under RCRA codes             D007 (chromium) and F006 (electroplating sludge)    .             No manifests or disposal records were produced for any of the preceding 39 years of operation (1965–2003).    ”         (Phase I ESA §1.0)  
          Analysis.         Hexavalent chromium is highly toxic and mobile. 39 years of operations, visible chromium staining, a sump containing liquid with sheen, and a near-total absence of disposal documentation (see Issue 9) establish a potential release of chromium-bearing waste to the subsurface. The 2003 Ohio EPA inspection corroborates a release in the outdoor storage area (Issue 10).

        Issue 9 — 39-year gap in hazardous-waste disposal documentation (1965–2003)  
          Severity: HIGH             Sources:         Ohio EPA letter §1; Phase I ESA §1.0, §8.0 (Data Gap No. 1); EDR summary (RCRA-SQG listing).  
      “    Ohio EPA has no hazardous waste manifests, biennial reports, or annual hazardous waste generation reports on file for this facility for any reporting year other than 2004. This is notable in light of Ohio EPA inspection records, which indicate that Breckenridge Manufacturing Co. conducted electroplating operations at the Subject Property from approximately 1965 through 2004 —             a period of approximately 39 years for which no hazardous waste generation or shipment documentation exists in Ohio EPA’s files.    ”         (Ohio EPA letter §1)  
      “    Only six hazardous waste manifests from 2004, documenting the disposal of 14,200 pounds of waste (RCRA codes D007 and F006), were produced by the property owner.    ”         (Phase I ESA §8.0)  
          Analysis.         Of ~40 years of electroplating operations (1965–2004), only the final year (2004) is documented (6 manifests, 14,200 lbs). The remaining             39 years (1965–2003)         have no manifests, biennial reports, or annual reports on file with Ohio EPA. This is both a significant AAI data gap and a potential off-site disposal liability exposure (unknown disposition of chromium-bearing and solvent wastes over four decades). It also corroborates the §4.12(b) compliance concern (Issue 8) and the §4.12(d) document-delivery concern (Issue 22).  
      
        Issue 10 — East-side outdoor drum-storage area with stressed vegetation not classified as a REC; corroborated by 2003 inspection

      “    Objects consistent with drums or containers are visible in this area, arranged in an irregular pattern over an area of approximately 40 feet by 60 feet.    ”         (Phase I ESA §5.2.2, 1990 aerial)  
      “    Inspectors observed multiple rusted and visibly leaking 55-gallon drums in an             outdoor storage area located on the east side of the Subject Property    . The drums were found to contain electroplating sludge and spent chromium-bearing wastewater…             staining on the ground surface beneath and around the drums was observed    .    ”         (Ohio EPA letter §2, 2003 inspection)  
      “    ‘    We used to keep drums out there before the EPA came around.    ’    ”         (Phase I ESA §7.1, Breckenridge III)  
          Analysis.         The 2003 Ohio EPA inspection directly observed             leaking drums of chromium-bearing waste with ground staining         in an outdoor east-side storage area — direct evidence of a release to soil. The same east-side area shows stressed vegetation today and corresponds to the 1990/2000 aerial drum-storage area and to the 1987 Sanborn         “    2,000-gal. waste solvent tank    ”         location (Issue 2). The Phase I ESA describes these observations in §6.2.4 but             does not classify the area as a REC         in §9.0. This is an under-classification: the 2003 inspection evidence (leaking chromium drums + ground staining) is independent, documented evidence of a release that warrants REC classification and Phase II soil sampling in the east-side area. The recommended Phase II scope (Phase I §9.6) does not include this area (Issue 19).

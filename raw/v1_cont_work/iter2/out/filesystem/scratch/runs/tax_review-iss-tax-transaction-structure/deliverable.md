        I. Executive Summary  
      This report identifies issues, inconsistencies, and omissions in the Tax Structure Memorandum by cross-referencing it against the supporting deal documents: the Thornfield Accounting Group Tax Due Diligence Report (    “    TDDR    ”    ), the Agreement and Plan of Merger excerpts dated January 28, 2025 (the         “    Purchase Agreement    ”         or         “    PA    ”    ), the Beacon Insights, LLC Amended and Restated Operating Agreement (the         “    Beacon OA    ”    ), the Thornfield Asset Valuation Summary / preliminary purchase price allocation (the         “    AVS    ”    ), the Prism capitalization table (the         “    Cap Table    ”    ), and the Linden Rock Advisory QSBS email thread (the         “    QSBS Email    ”    ).  
      The review identified             32 distinct issues    , ranked by severity:             3 Critical, 5 High, 18 Moderate, and 6 Low    .  
          Most significant findings:  
             The Aggregate Deemed Sale Price (ADSP) is overstated by $48 million.         The Memo computes ADSP as enterprise value ($425,000,000) plus funded debt ($48,000,000) = $473,000,000. This double-counts the funded debt, which is already embedded in enterprise value. The AVS expressly flags this as a         “    SIGNIFICANT DISCREPANCY    ”         and computes the correct ADSP as $425,000,000 (amount realized on the stock of $370,000,000 + target liabilities of $48,000,000 + $7,000,000). The error inflates the Class VII goodwill residual by $48,000,000, the total deemed sale gain, the per-shareholder gain allocations, and the buyer’s amortization benefit estimate.  
             The Memo’s S corporation eligibility conclusion directly contradicts the diligence record.         The Memo states         “    we are not aware of any ineligible shareholders    ”         and         “    All shareholders of Prism are U.S. individuals.    ”         The TDDR flags as a             Critical         finding that eligibility could not be confirmed for restricted stock holders, and the Cap Table marks three holders as         “    Unconfirmed.    ”         Validity of the S election is a threshold requirement for the Section 338(h)(10) election and is a closing condition under PA Section 8.2(c).  
             The QSBS / Section 1202 analysis is entirely omitted.         Calloway expressly committed in a November 20, 2024 email to include a QSBS section in the Memo; Linden Rock formally requested it and followed up on January 6, 2025 noting no analysis had been received; and the TDDR flags it as an unresolved finding. The Memo contains no QSBS analysis whatsoever, even though the Section 338(h)(10) election may foreclose the exclusion for shareholders whose shares were issued during the C corporation period — a potentially seven-figure-per-shareholder issue.  
      Additional high-severity items include: a $28,500,000 understatement in the Class V allocation (developed technology valued at $19,000,000 in the Memo versus $41,800,000 in the AVS, plus an omitted $5,700,000 government software license line); an escrow tax-treatment recommendation that contradicts the executed Purchase Agreement; an aggregate adjusted tax basis figure ($42,800,000) that does not reconcile to the AVS asset-by-asset total ($64,300,000); an unaddressed risk that the non-transferable North Carolina data processing license may be deemed terminated in the Section 338(h)(10) deemed sale (threatening ~$19,000,000 in annual revenue); and an unanalyzed Beacon right of first refusal / automatic IP license termination triggered by the change of control.  
      The detailed findings follow, each grounded in quoted source passages with supporting arithmetic.

        III. Severity Definitions  
              Critical:         An error or omission that, if uncorrected, could invalidate the transaction structure, produce a materially incorrect tax result, or expose the parties to significant tax liability or lost benefit. Must be resolved before closing.  
              High:         A material inconsistency or omission that produces an incorrect quantitative result or contradicts an executed agreement, requiring correction and likely affecting deal terms or filings.  
              Moderate:         A gap, inconsistency, or unsupported position that should be addressed but is not, by itself, deal-breaking; may affect accuracy of filings or allocations.  
              Low:         A citation, dating, or minor data-quality discrepancy that should be corrected for accuracy and professionalism.  
      
        IV. Critical Issues  
       C-1. ADSP double-counts funded debt — overstated by $48,000,000  
          Severity: Critical  
          The Memo’s calculation (Section IV.B):         The Memo computes the ADSP as enterprise value plus funded debt:  
      “    The ADSP is calculated as the enterprise value of Prism ($425,000,000) plus the assumed liabilities of the Target ($48,000,000 in funded debt at closing): …             ADSP $473,000,000    .    ”  
      The same Section II.C of the Memo derives equity value as enterprise value             minus         funded debt and seller transaction expenses:  
      “    Enterprise Value $425,000,000 / Less: Funded Debt at Closing ($48,000,000) / Less: Seller Transaction Expenses ($7,000,000) /             Equity Value $370,000,000    .    ”  
          The AVS expressly flags the error (Allocation Summary tab):  
      “    SIGNIFICANT DISCREPANCY. The memorandum computes ADSP as $425M enterprise value + $48M liabilities = $473M. This double-counts the $48M funded debt, which was already subtracted to derive equity value ($425M − $48M − $7M = $370M) and is then added back again. Under Treas. Reg. 1.338-4, ADSP should be computed starting from the amount realized on the stock ($370M) plus target liabilities. Thornfield’s computation: $370M + $48M + $7M = $425M. This $48M ADSP overstatement directly inflates the Class VII goodwill residual in the memo.    ”  
          AVS Note 1:         “    The memorandum’s computation starts from enterprise value ($425M) and adds $48M liabilities again, yielding an incorrect ADSP of $473M. This double-counts the funded debt because enterprise value already includes the debt component (EV = Equity + Net Debt).    ”  
          Arithmetic:         - Memo ADSP: $425,000,000 (EV) + $48,000,000 (debt) =             $473,000,000         - Correct ADSP (per AVS): $370,000,000 (amount realized on stock) + $48,000,000 (debt) + $7,000,000 (seller transaction expenses) =             $425,000,000         - Overstatement: $473,000,000 − $425,000,000 =             $48,000,000  
          Impact / cascading effects:         - Class VII goodwill residual is overstated by $48,000,000 (Memo $272,800,000 vs. AVS $196,300,000; see H-1 for the additional $28,500,000 Class V component).         - Total deemed sale gain is overstated (see H-3).         - Per-shareholder gain allocations are overstated proportionally.         - Buyer’s annual goodwill amortization deduction is overstated: $272,800,000 ÷ 15 = $18,186,667/yr (Memo) vs. $196,300,000 ÷ 15 = $13,086,667/yr (corrected) — a $5,100,000/yr reduction (see M-11).  
          Recommendation:         Recompute the ADSP from the amount realized on the stock ($370,000,000) plus target liabilities, reconcile to the AVS, and revise the entire purchase price allocation, gain computation, and buyer benefit analysis before filing Form 8883.  
      
        C-2. S corporation shareholder-eligibility conclusion contradicts the diligence record  
          Severity: Critical  
          The Memo’s conclusion (Section III.A):  
      “    We have reviewed the S election file and the annual shareholder records provided to us. No events causing a termination of the S election have been identified.             All shareholders of Prism are U.S. individuals, and we are not aware of any ineligible shareholders.    ”  
          The TDDR flags this as a Critical finding (Section III.B and Finding No. 1):  
      “    Thornfield was unable to confirm S corporation eligibility for 2 of the 8 restricted stock holders. One holder appears to be a Canadian national on an H-1B visa whose substantial presence test compliance has not been verified for all years; another holder’s shares may have been transferred to a family LLC, which would be an ineligible shareholder.             This is a critical gap because the validity of the S election is a threshold requirement for the proposed Section 338(h)(10) election.    ”  
      “    If the S election is found to have been inadvertently terminated in any prior year, Prism would have been a C corporation for that year and all subsequent years, with cascading consequences including entity-level federal and state income tax liability for all open years, potential penalties and interest, and the unavailability of the Section 338(h)(10) election.    ”  
          The Cap Table marks three restricted stock holders as                 “        Unconfirmed        ”        :         -             Lin Wei Zhang         —         “    H-1B visa holder — permanent residency application pending. Citizenship: People’s Republic of China. If classified as nonresident alien at time of grant or currently, S corp eligibility may be jeopardized.    ”         -             Yusuf Al-Rashidi         —         “    Dual citizen (U.S./Jordan) — documentation of U.S. citizenship not on file.    ”         -             Nina Petrova         —         “    Born in Bulgaria. Green card holder (lawful permanent resident). … Documentation not on file.    ”  
          The PA both relies on and represents eligibility:         - PA Section 2.5:         “    The Company represents that each holder of restricted stock is an individual who is a citizen or resident of the United States and that a timely and valid election under Section 83(b) of the Code was filed with respect to each outstanding restricted stock grant.    ”         - PA Section 8.2(c) (closing condition):         “    The Company shall have maintained its status as a validly electing S corporation under Section 1361 of the Code continuously through the Closing Date.    ”         - PA Section 8.2(d) (closing condition):         “    All Shareholders shall have executed IRS Form 8023 and all other 338(h)(10) Forms necessary to effectuate the Section 338(h)(10) Election.    ”  
          Inconsistency:         The Memo’s blanket statement that all shareholders are U.S. individuals and that no ineligible shareholders exist is directly contradicted by the TDDR (which the Memo states it reviewed) and the Cap Table. The Memo does not acknowledge, qualify, or address the eligibility concern anywhere — not in Section III.A, not in the open-items list (Section IX.C), and not in the Conclusions (Section X, which states unconditionally that         “    No Built-In Gains Tax    ”         will apply and that the structure is appropriate). The PA’s representation in Section 2.5 that each restricted stock holder         “    is a citizen or resident of the United States    ”         is not supported by the diligence record.  
          Note on a secondary discrepancy:         The TDDR describes the eligibility concerns as (a) a         “    Canadian national on an H-1B visa    ”         and (b) a possible         “    transfer to a family LLC,    ”         while the Cap Table identifies three unconfirmed holders (an H-1B holder described as a PRC citizen, a dual U.S./Jordan citizen, and a Bulgarian-born green-card holder). The two diligence documents do not fully reconcile on the number or identity of the at-risk holders, which itself warrants resolution.  
          Recommendation:         Obtain definitive immigration/residency documentation and entity-classification documentation for every shareholder (including every restricted stock holder) for every year since January 1, 2017, before closing. The Memo should be revised to disclose the open eligibility issue and to condition its conclusions on resolution. The PA Section 2.5 representation should not be relied upon until the underlying documentation is produced.  
      
        C-3. QSBS / Section 1202 analysis is entirely omitted  
          Severity: Critical  
          The Memo contains no Section 1202 / QSBS analysis in any section.         This is a complete omission of a topic that was (i) expressly committed to by counsel, (ii) formally requested by the shareholders’ advisor, and (iii) flagged by the TDDR.  
          Calloway’s express commitment (QSBS Email, November 20, 2024):  
      “    We intend to include a QSBS analysis as part of the transaction structure memorandum we are preparing for Ridgeline. … My goal is to have the QSBS section drafted and incorporated into the memorandum well before the purchase agreement is executed, so that the shareholders have the information they need to make an informed decision.    ”  
          Linden Rock’s formal request and follow-up (QSBS Email, initial request and January 6, 2025):  
      “    we are requesting that Calloway, Stern & Whitaker provide a written analysis of QSBS eligibility — ideally as a dedicated section in the transaction structure memorandum your team is preparing, or alternatively as a standalone memo.    ”  
      “    I have not yet received any written analysis or update on the QSBS question since your November 20 email … we need a formal QSBS analysis — either as a dedicated section in the transaction structure memorandum or as a standalone memo — before the purchase agreement is finalized.    ”  
          The TDDR flags it as an unresolved finding (Section VIII.B and Finding No. 4):  
      “    QSBS (Section 1202) analysis requested by shareholders but not yet performed; interaction with Section 338(h)(10) deemed asset sale is unresolved.    ”  
      “    Critical Question — Interaction with Section 338(h)(10). The proposed Section 338(h)(10) election would treat the transaction as a deemed asset sale by the S corporation, followed by a deemed liquidation — not as a sale of stock by individual shareholders. … If the transaction is recharacterized as a deemed asset sale rather than a stock sale for federal tax purposes, the QSBS exclusion may not be available because the shareholders are not treated as selling their stock.    ”  
          Materiality:         The Cap Table identifies multiple shareholders who acquired shares during the C corporation period (June 14 – December 31, 2016) and who therefore may hold stock that satisfies the Section 1202(c)(1) C-corporation-issuance requirement (subject to the other Section 1202 requirements). The         “    other shareholders    ”         group receives $74,000,000 in aggregate cash consideration. A 100% federal QSBS exclusion, if available, would be a seven-figure benefit for several of these individuals. The Section 338(h)(10) election — which all shareholders must consent to as a closing condition (PA Section 8.2(d)) — may eliminate that benefit, as Calloway himself preliminarily observed.  
          Additional omission — escrow component:         The January 6, 2025 email specifically asks that the QSBS analysis         “    address the escrow component as well    ”         (whether the exclusion applies to gain attributable to the $22,000,000 escrow that may not be released for 18 months). The Memo does not address this.  
          Recommendation:         Prepare and incorporate a dedicated QSBS analysis addressing (a) which shares were issued during the C corporation period and satisfy the Section 1202 requirements, (b) whether the Section 338(h)(10) deemed asset sale / deemed liquidation forecloses the exclusion, (c) whether an alternative structure (e.g., a stock sale without the Section 338(h)(10) election) would preserve the exclusion, and (d) the escrow component. This should be completed and delivered to affected shareholders before they are required to consent to the Section 338(h)(10) election.  
      
         V. High-Severity Issues  
       H-1. Class V allocation understated by $28,500,000 (developed technology and omitted government software licenses)  
          Severity: High  
          The Memo’s Class V (Section IV.C) = $68,500,000    , comprised of: Real Property $14,200,000; FF&E $6,800,000; Software / Developed Technology $19,000,000; Beacon 80.5% interest $28,500,000.  
          The AVS Asset Detail tab values the Class V assets differently:         - A-006         “    Developed technology / proprietary software platform    ”         — Preliminary FMV             $41,800,000         (Memo: $19,000,000)         - A-007         “    Software licenses — government-sector platforms    ”         — Preliminary FMV             $5,700,000         (not separately listed in the Memo)         - A-008         “    NC data processing license    ”         — $0 (not separately listed in the Memo)  
          The AVS Allocation Summary flags the variance:  
      “    Class V … Thornfield Preliminary FMV $97,000,000 … Memo Allocation $68,500,000 … Variance $28,500,000 … 41.6% … SIGNIFICANT DISCREPANCY.    ”  
          Reconciliation of the $28,500,000 variance:         The AVS Note 2 attributes the variance to the Beacon interest based on a January 28, 2025 draft of the Memo. The final February 10, 2025 Memo             does         include the Beacon interest ($28,500,000). The remaining $28,500,000 variance is therefore attributable to (i) developed technology valued at $19,000,000 in the Memo versus $41,800,000 in the AVS (a $22,800,000 understatement), and (ii) the Memo’s omission of the $5,700,000 government-sector software license line (A-007). Together: $22,800,000 + $5,700,000 =             $28,500,000    .  
          Arithmetic:         - AVS Class V: $14,200,000 + $6,800,000 + $41,800,000 + $5,700,000 + $0 + $28,500,000 =             $97,000,000         - Memo Class V: $14,200,000 + $6,800,000 + $19,000,000 + $28,500,000 =             $68,500,000         - Variance: $97,000,000 − $68,500,000 =             $28,500,000  
          Impact:         The $28,500,000 understatement in Class V, combined with the $48,000,000 ADSP overstatement (C-1), produces a $76,500,000 overstatement of the Class VII goodwill residual:         - Memo Class VII: $473,000,000 − $200,200,000 = $272,800,000         - AVS Class VII: $425,000,000 − $228,700,000 = $196,300,000         - Variance: $272,800,000 − $196,300,000 =             $76,500,000         (= $48,000,000 ADSP overstatement + $28,500,000 Class V understatement)  
      This misallocation affects (a) the character of gain to selling shareholders (Class V developed technology is amortizable under Section 167 over ~5 years; goodwill under Section 197 over 15 years), (b) the buyer’s depreciation/amortization profile, and (c) the Form 8883 allocation that both parties must follow.  
          Recommendation:         Revise the Class V allocation to reflect the AVS values for developed technology ($41,800,000) and government-sector software licenses ($5,700,000), and separately identify the NC data processing license at $0.  
      
        H-2. Escrow installment-sale recommendation contradicts the executed Purchase Agreement  
          Severity: High  
          The Memo (Section V.C) recommends deferral:  
      “    Under Section 453, the selling shareholders may elect installment sale treatment with respect to the escrow amount, deferring recognition of a proportionate share of gain until the escrow is released. … the escrow constitutes a contingent payment within the meaning of the installment sale provisions.    ”  
          The PA (Section 7.4,                 “        Tax Treatment        ”        ) specifies current inclusion:  
      “    The parties intend that the Escrow Amount be reported as an amount realized by the Shareholders in the taxable year in which the Closing occurs, consistent with the treatment of the Escrow Amount as a portion of the Cash Consideration deposited with a third-party agent for the benefit of the Shareholders. Notwithstanding the foregoing, each Shareholder shall be solely responsible for determining the proper treatment of the Escrow Amount on such Shareholder’s own Tax returns…    ”  
          Inconsistency:         The Memo advises shareholders that they         “    may elect installment sale treatment    ”         to defer gain on the $22,000,000 escrow until the September 15, 2026 release. The executed PA states the opposite — that the escrow is to be         “    reported as an amount realized … in the taxable year in which the Closing occurs.    ”         The Memo’s characterization of the escrow as         “    contingent consideration    ”         is also in tension with the PA’s treatment of it as         “    a portion of the Cash Consideration deposited with a third-party agent for the benefit of the Shareholders    ”         (with interest accruing to the shareholders).  
          Additional omission:         The January 6, 2025 QSBS Email asks counsel to address         “    the timing of gain recognition on the escrow portion    ”         in the QSBS context. The Memo does not address the escrow timing question in any QSBS analysis (because no QSBS analysis exists — see C-3).  
          Recommendation:         Reconcile the Memo’s escrow tax-treatment discussion with PA Section 7.4. If the parties intend current inclusion (as the PA provides), the Memo’s installment-sale recommendation should be withdrawn or heavily qualified. The Memo should also address the escrow timing question in the QSBS analysis.

        H-4. Non-transferable North Carolina data processing license — deemed-termination risk unaddressed  
          Severity: High  
          The AVS (A-008) raises a material risk:  
      “    IMPORTANT: NC DIT data processing license is non-transferable and non-assignable per license terms. In a deemed asset sale under §338(h)(10), this license cannot be transferred and may need to be re-obtained by the surviving entity. Thornfield has assigned $0 FMV for PPA purposes.             Prism Data Services government-sector revenue (~$19M annually) partially depends on this license.         Legal counsel should confirm whether the license survives the reverse triangular merger or is deemed terminated. If terminated, value associated with the government business line should be reassigned to goodwill or reduced.    ”  
          The PA (Section 4.9(i)) confirms non-transferability:  
      “    Prism Data Services, LLC holds the NC Data Processing License (License No. DIT-2019-04821), which, pursuant to its terms, is non-transferable and non-assignable.    ”  
          The Memo dismisses the issue (Sections VI.A and IX.A):  
      “    we expect that the existing license should remain valid post-closing, subject to confirmation with the North Carolina Department of Information Technology.    ”  
      “    certain government contracts and licenses held by Prism Data Services may require notification to or consent from the applicable government authority in connection with the change of control. This is a commercial matter to be addressed by deal counsel in the pre-closing period.    ”  
          Inconsistency / omission:         The Memo treats the license as a         “    commercial matter    ”         and expresses an expectation that it         “    should remain valid,    ”         but does not analyze the specific risk that the Section 338(h)(10)             deemed asset sale         could be treated as a deemed transfer/termination of a non-transferable license held by a disregarded entity — the precise risk the AVS flags. Approximately $19,000,000 in annual government-sector revenue depends on this license. The license is also not separately identified in the Memo’s purchase price allocation (the AVS assigns it $0 and notes it should be separately identified).  
          Recommendation:         Obtain a definitive legal opinion or confirmation from the North Carolina Department of Information Technology on whether the license survives the reverse triangular merger / Section 338(h)(10) deemed sale. If the license is deemed terminated, reassess the government-sector business valuation and the PPA. Add the license (at $0) as a separately identified line in the allocation.  
      
        H-5. Beacon right of first refusal and automatic IP license termination — unanalyzed  
          Severity: High  
          The Beacon OA contains a right of first refusal triggered by a change of control of Prism (Section 8.03):  
      “    Section 8.03(a)(ii): the right of first refusal … shall be triggered upon … a Change of Control of any Member that is an entity.    ”  
      “    Change of Control    ”         (Article I) includes         “    a merger, consolidation, or other business combination transaction involving such Member as a result of which the equity holders of such Member immediately prior to such transaction hold less than fifty percent (50%) of the voting equity interests of the surviving entity    ”         or         “    a sale, transfer, or other disposition of all or substantially all of the assets of such Member.    ”         The Section 338(h)(10) deemed asset sale of Prism’s assets (including its Beacon interest) would constitute a Change of Control of Prism, a Member that is an entity. Under Section 8.03(c), the non-transferring members (Delano 12.0%, Avesta 7.5%) would have 45 calendar days to elect to purchase Prism’s 80.5% interest at Fair Market Value (determined under Section 8.04).  
          The Beacon OA also provides for automatic termination of the IP License (Section 9.02):  
      “    The IP License Agreement shall terminate automatically upon a Transfer of Prism’s Membership Interest in the Company (including a deemed Transfer resulting from a Change of Control of Prism), unless the parties agree otherwise in writing.    ”  
      Beacon’s business relies on IP licensed from Prism (the TDDR notes Beacon         “    relies on IP licensed from Prism    ”         and generates ~$11,000,000 in annual revenue using the licensed IP).  
          The PA recognizes Beacon consent as a closing condition (Section 8.2(g)):  
      “    All consents, waivers, and approvals of third parties required to be obtained in connection with the transactions contemplated hereby shall have been obtained, including any required consents under the operating agreement of Beacon Insights, LLC.    ”  
          The Memo mentions the ROFR only in passing (Sections III.C and VIII):  
      “    The Beacon operating agreement contains customary transfer restrictions, including a right of first refusal on transfers of membership interests. We understand that the parties and their counsel are addressing these transfer provisions in the context of the overall transaction documentation and pre-closing conditions.    ”  
          Omission:         The Memo does not analyze (a) whether the Section 338(h)(10) deemed sale / Change of Control of Prism triggers the ROFR, (b) the 45-day exercise period and Fair Market Value mechanics (Section 8.04), (c) the risk that Delano and Avesta could exercise the ROFR and purchase Prism’s 80.5% Beacon interest (FMV $28,500,000), removing Beacon from the acquired asset base, or (d) the automatic termination of the IP License Agreement upon the deemed Transfer / Change of Control of Prism and the consequences for Beacon’s ongoing operations.  
          Recommendation:         Analyze the ROFR trigger and the automatic IP license termination, obtain the required Beacon consents/waivers as a closing condition, and address whether the IP License Agreement must be reaffirmed or replaced post-closing.  
      
         VI. Moderate-Severity Issues  
       M-1. Section 482 transfer-pricing exposure (~$6,300,000) not addressed  
          Severity: Moderate

        IX. Conclusion  
      The Tax Structure Memorandum contains a sound high-level description of the reverse triangular merger and Section 338(h)(10) mechanics, and several of its conclusions (e.g., expiration of the Section 1374 built-in-gains recognition period on December 31, 2021; the NUBIG of $46,500,000; the Form 8023 filing deadline of December 15, 2025; the Beacon outside basis of $6,400,000 and FMV of $28,500,000) are consistent across the Memo, the TDDR, the PA, and the AVS.  
      However, the Memo contains three Critical defects that should be resolved before closing: (1) a $48,000,000 overstatement of the ADSP that propagates through the entire purchase price allocation, gain computation, and buyer benefit analysis; (2) an S corporation eligibility conclusion that directly contradicts the diligence record on a threshold requirement for the election; and (3) the complete omission of the QSBS analysis that counsel committed to provide. Five High-severity items — the Class V undervaluation, the escrow tax-treatment contradiction, the unreconciled aggregate basis, the NC license termination risk, and the unanalyzed Beacon ROFR/IP license termination — require correction before the Memo can be relied upon for filing or for shareholder decision-making. The eighteen Moderate and six Low items should be addressed to bring the Memo into full conformity with the supporting deal documents.  
      This report is based solely on the documents listed in Section II and does not constitute a tax opinion.

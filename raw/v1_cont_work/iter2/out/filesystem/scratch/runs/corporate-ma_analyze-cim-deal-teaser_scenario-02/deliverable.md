       I. Executive Summary and Recommendation  
      This memorandum presents the deal team’s buy-side analysis of Cascade Environmental Solutions, Inc. (    “    CES    ”         or the         “    Company    ”    ) in advance of the July 14, 2025 Indication of Interest (    “    IOI    ”    ) deadline. It is prepared using the sector-benchmark framework set out in Sarah Lindgren’s June 12, 2025 sector memo and identifies the financial inconsistencies and risk factors surfaced by cross-referencing the Confidential Information Memorandum (    “    CIM    ”    ), the Customer Summary and Backlog Report (    “    Backlog Report    ”    ), the Financial Exhibits workbook, and the Greenleaf process letter.  
          Recommendation: Proceed to a non-binding IOI, but at a valuation meaningfully below the sell-side guide and subject to explicit diligence conditions.         CES is a genuine, well-positioned Pacific Northwest environmental services platform with a real growth vector in PFAS remediation, and it fits Thornfield’s sector focus. However, the marketing materials contain a pattern of internal inconsistencies and aggressive adjustments that, taken together, support a buy-side Adjusted EBITDA and enterprise value below the sell-side guide:  
              Sell-side guide:         FY2024 Adjusted EBITDA of             $17.1M         (19.6% margin) → EV             $136.8M–$171.0M         at 8.0x–10.0x; FY2025E Adjusted EBITDA of             $19.7M         → EV             $157.6M–$197.0M    .  
              Buy-side base case:         FY2024 Adjusted EBITDA of             ~$16.0M–$16.4M         after correcting the lease and regulatory add-backs → EV             ~$128M–$164M         at 8.0x–10.0x; equity value             ~$113M–$149M         after ~$15.0M net debt.  
              Forward case is further at risk         because ~64% of projected incremental revenue depends on a two-year-old PFAS practice growing 125.8% in FY2025E — roughly three times the 30–50% company-level rate the sector memo identifies as realistic.

        B. ISSUE — Related-party lease add-back ($0.8M) is contradicted by the CIM’s own market-rent disclosure  
      The CIM normalizes the HQ lease to             $600K/year         market rent, adding back             $800K         (actual $1.4M − $600K). (CIM, Sections VI.C and VIII.A.) However,             CIM Section IV.C         states:         “    Industrial yard properties in the Portland metropolitan area typically lease in the range of $8–$12 per square foot, with             specialized environmental staging facilities commanding rates of $1.0 million to $1.2 million annually         for properties of comparable acreage and infrastructure.    ”  
      The CES headquarters is, by the CIM’s own description (Section VIII.A), a         “    purpose-built environmental services operations center    ”         on ~8.5 acres with         “    specialized environmental staging infrastructure including secondary containment areas, roll-off container staging, and soil stockpile areas with stormwater controls    ”         — i.e., a             specialized environmental staging facility    , the very category Section IV.C prices at $1.0M–$1.2M. The $600K normalization therefore appears to reference generic industrial yard space, not the specialized facility CES actually occupies.  
          Impact:         If market rent is $1.0M, the defensible add-back is $1.4M − $1.0M =             $400K         (overstated by $400K). If market rent is $1.2M, the defensible add-back is             $200K         (overstated by $600K). At 8.0x–10.0x, the $400K–$600K overstatement translates to             $3.2M–$6.0M of enterprise value    . This is the single largest defensible adjustment correction and is grounded entirely in the CIM’s own disclosures.  
        C. ISSUE — Regulatory/legal add-back ($1.2M) is likely overstated as non-recurring  
      The CIM adds back the full             $1.2M         for the August 14, 2024 Oregon DEQ enforcement action ($875K settlement + $325K legal fees) related to improper storage of contaminated soils at the Portland facility. (CIM, Section VI.C and Appendix D.)  
      The sector memo concludes regulatory/compliance costs are             recurring         in this sector at             $200K–$500K/year         and that         “    it is inappropriate to treat all regulatory costs as         ‘    one-time    ’         add-backs.    ”         (Sector memo, Section 5.2.) Thornfield’s portfolio experience is directly on point: GreenWorks’         “    $380K         ‘    one-time    ’         settlement    ”         was followed by ~$275K/year of recurring regulatory cost, and the QoE added back only the excess over a $250K run-rate; Allied Waste averaged ~$310K/year recurring with one $840K spike year. (Sector memo, Sections 5.2 and 6.)  
      Two facts in the record reinforce the recurring nature here: (i)             CIM Appendix B shows                 “        One-time legal/regulatory costs        ”                 of $0.2M in FY2023         as well as $1.2M in FY2024 — i.e., regulatory costs appear in two consecutive years, indicating a baseline; and (ii) the sector memo notes Oregon DEQ         “    has been particularly active in enforcement over the past several years.    ”         (Sector memo, Section 5.1.)  
          Impact:         Per the sector memo, if a $300K run-rate is appropriate, the defensible add-back is ~$900K ($1.2M − $300K), overstated by $300K; if a $500K run-rate is appropriate, the defensible add-back is ~$700K, overstated by $500K. At 8.0x–10.0x, the $300K–$500K overstatement translates to             $2.4M–$5.0M of enterprise value    . The deal team should request CES’s complete 10-year regulatory enforcement history to determine whether the DEQ matter is isolated or systemic.  
        D. ISSUE — Owner compensation normalization ($1.9M) requires verification and the projections appear to over-state the benefit  
      The CIM adds back             $1.9M    , equal to Mr. Oakes’s $2.4M total compensation (including         “    personal expenses allocated to the Company    ”    ) less an assumed             $500K         replacement-CEO cost. (CIM, Sections VI.C and VII.C.) Three concerns:  
              “        Personal expenses allocated to the Company        ”         are bundled into the $2.4M. The QoE must verify these are genuinely personal and non-recurring, and quantify them separately.  
              The $500K replacement-CEO assumption is at the low end and is not consistent across history.         The historical add-backs in Appendix B imply a higher replacement cost: FY2021 $2.1M − $1.4M = $0.7M; FY2022 $2.2M − $1.5M = $0.7M; FY2023 $2.3M − $1.7M = $0.6M; FY2024 $2.4M − $1.9M = $0.5M. The implied replacement cost declines from $700K to $500K, so the FY2024 $500K is the most aggressive year. If a $700K replacement is more appropriate, the add-back shrinks by $200K (≈$1.6M–$2.0M of EV).  
              The projections bake in $0 CEO cost, not the $500K normalized cost.         The Financial Exhibits income statement shows         “    Owner Compensation (R. Oakes)    ”         of             $0 (        “        —        ”        ) for FY2025E–FY2027E    . This also ignores any transition/advisory payments to Mr. Oakes during his 18–24 month transition period, during which the buyer may pay both a new CEO and Mr. Oakes.

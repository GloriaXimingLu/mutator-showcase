## V. Detailed Gap Analysis

### A. Education (J.B.1) — No Gap

The position requires a Master's degree in Computer Science, Machine Learning, or a closely related field. The NC State transcript confirms the Beneficiary was awarded a **Master of Science in Computer Science on May 12, 2018** (cumulative GPA 3.74/4.00, 45 credit hours), with a thesis titled "Attention-Based Transformer Models for Clinical Text Classification." This directly satisfies J.B.1. The Beneficiary's prior credential — a Bachelor of Technology in Electronics and Communication Engineering from MIT-Pune (June 2013) — was evaluated by IAE (a NACES-member agency) as equivalent to a U.S. bachelor's degree. Because the qualifying degree is the U.S. M.S., no foreign-credential evaluation of the master's was required, and the IAE evaluation of the B.Tech serves to document the prior degree. **No gap.**

### B. Experience (J.B.3) — No Gap as to Quantity and Progression

The position requires 5 years of progressive post-master's experience in machine learning engineering or a closely related occupation.

- The M.S. was conferred on **May 12, 2018**. The Beneficiary commenced employment at DataBridge Solutions LLC on **June 4, 2018** as a Data Scientist I and was promoted to Data Scientist II effective **January 4, 2021**. As of the March 15, 2025 filing date, this yields approximately **6 years and 9 months** of post-master's experience — exceeding the 5-year requirement. The ETA-9089 characterizes this as "approximately 7 years" (K.2), which is a reasonable approximation.
- The Data Scientist I → Data Scientist II promotion evidences **progressive** experience (increasing responsibility and complexity), satisfying the "progressive" element.
- The DataBridge duties (deep learning, NLP, Spark MLlib, SageMaker deployment) are squarely within machine learning engineering / data science, a closely related occupation. The position itself is classified under SOC 15-2051 (Data Scientists).
- The two earlier experience entries — Evalpoint Technologies (Software Engineer, July 2013 – July 2016) and the NC State Research Assistantship (August 2016 – May 2018) — are **pre-master's** and therefore do **not** count toward the 5-year post-master's requirement. This is consistent with the application, which attributes the qualifying post-master's experience solely to DataBridge (K.2). The qualifying experience is clean and was not gained with the petitioning employer (K.5 — No), so 20 C.F.R. § 656.17(i) (substantially comparable experience with the petitioning employer) is not implicated.

**No gap as to the experience requirement's quantity or progression.** (Related documentation and skill-matching issues are addressed in Sections V.C–V.F below.)

### E. Certification Requirement (J.B.5) — CRITICAL GAP

Section J.B.5 requires the "AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification or equivalent." This is the most significant issue in the file.

**What the Beneficiary holds (per the certifications compilation):**

1. **AWS Certified Cloud Practitioner** — Validation No. AWS-CCP-2022-0418-MEHROTRA; issued January 18, 2022; **expired January 18, 2025**; status "EXPIRED." The compilation expressly describes this as a **foundational-level** certification that "does not demonstrate specialization in any specific AWS service domain, including machine learning."
2. **TensorFlow Developer Certificate** — Validation No. TF-DEV-2023-0310-AM; issued March 10, 2023; no expiration; status "ACTIVE." The compilation notes this is a **framework-specific** certification issued by **Google**, "not affiliated with Amazon Web Services (AWS)."

**The compilation further states:** "Counsel specifically inquired whether the beneficiary holds the AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification or any other AWS specialty-level certification; the beneficiary confirmed she does not hold any such certification."

**Analysis:**

- The Beneficiary **does not hold** the AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification.
- The **AWS Cloud Practitioner is not equivalent**: it is an entry-level/foundational credential, not a specialty-level credential, and it does not test machine-learning-specific AWS competencies. Critically, it **expired on January 18, 2025 — before the March 15, 2025 PERM filing date**. As of the priority date, the Beneficiary held no active AWS certification of any kind.
- The **TensorFlow Developer Certificate is not equivalent**: it is issued by Google (not AWS), is framework-specific (TensorFlow model development), and does not test the AWS ML service competencies (SageMaker, model deployment/monitoring on AWS, etc.) that the AWS ML – Specialty certification validates. While it is a professional-level credential and was active at the priority date, it is not an AWS certification and is not a functional substitute for the AWS ML – Specialty credential.
- Accordingly, **neither documented certification satisfies the J.B.5 certification requirement**, and the "or equivalent" language does not rescue the gap on the present record.

**Attestation discrepancy.** Section K.3 attests that the Beneficiary "possess[es] the special skills and other requirements stated in Section J.B.5" (Yes), and K.4 attests she "is qualified for the job opportunity" (Yes). As to the certification element of J.B.5, these attestations are **not supported** by the documentary credentials. This is a material gap between the application's attestations and the supporting evidence.

**Implications:**

- **I-140 adjudication.** USCIS evaluates whether the Beneficiary possessed the minimum qualifications stated in the certified PERM **as of the priority date** (March 15, 2025). Qualifications acquired after the priority date generally cannot be used to satisfy the minimum requirements. As of that date, the Beneficiary did not hold the AWS ML – Specialty certification or an active AWS certification; the only arguably "equivalent" credential held at the priority date is the TensorFlow Developer Certificate, which is a weak substitute. This creates a real risk that USCIS will find the Beneficiary did not meet the minimum requirements as stated, supporting an I-140 denial.
- **Audit / revocation exposure.** DOL may audit a certified application and may revoke a certification granted in error or based on fraud or willful misrepresentation. An attestation that the Beneficiary possesses a required certification she does not hold is a vulnerability, particularly if the certification requirement is later scrutinized.
- **Duty of candor.** Counsel and the Employer should reconcile the K.3/K.4 attestations with the credentials before relying on the certification in the I-140 filing.

### F. Supervisory Duty — Moderate Gap

J.B.5 states the position "Will supervise a team of 3–5 ML engineers." The DataBridge experience letter and the resume establish that the Beneficiary, as Data Scientist II, "**Led a team of 2 junior data scientists**," providing mentorship and code reviews.

Two concerns arise:

1. **Headcount and title mismatch.** The documented supervisory experience is over **2** junior **data scientists**, whereas the position duty references **3–5** **ML engineers**. The Beneficiary's prior supervisory experience is narrower in scope than the duty describes.
2. **Defensibility of a fixed headcount.** A requirement (or duty) framed around a specific headcount (3–5) is susceptible to challenge as unduly restrictive and potentially tailored to the alien, unless it is normal for the occupation or supported by business necessity. See Section VI below.

This is a **moderate gap**. Because the statement appears in J.B.5 as a forward-looking job duty ("Will supervise …") rather than as an express prior-experience prerequisite, the Beneficiary's documented supervisory experience demonstrates supervisory *capability*, which partially mitigates the gap. Nonetheless, the headcount discrepancy and the specificity of the duty warrant attention (see Recommendations R-4 and R-6).

## VI. PERM Defensibility Considerations — The Certification Requirement

The certification gap is compounded by several PERM-defensibility concerns specific to the J.B.5 certification requirement itself. These bear on both the audit risk and the remediation strategy.

1. **"Or equivalent" language.** The requirement is phrased as "AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification **or equivalent** required." DOL scrutinizes "or equivalent" phrasing for special requirements because it can vest the Employer with subjective discretion to determine equivalency, undermining the standard that the job opportunity be clearly open to U.S. workers. The phrase is disfavored and, if audited, may draw a request for the Employer's objective criteria for "equivalence."

2. **Normal for the occupation / business necessity.** Under 20 C.F.R. § 656.17(h), a job requirement that is not normal to the occupation is permissible only if the Employer demonstrates business necessity (i.e., the requirement bears a reasonable relationship to the occupation and is essential to perform the job duties in a reasonable manner). The AWS ML – Specialty certification is not universally held by, or required of, Data Scientists (SOC 15-2051); it is more commonly associated with ML/cloud-engineering roles. If DOL or USCIS views the certification as not normal to the occupation, the Employer must be prepared to articulate business necessity. The current record contains no business-necessity justification for the certification.

3. **Application to U.S. applicants.** A special requirement must have been applied equally to all applicants, including U.S. workers. The recruitment report (§G.3) states that three applicants were found unqualified — two for lacking the required educational credentials and one for lacking the required minimum experience. The report is **silent as to whether any applicant was evaluated against, or rejected for lacking, the AWS ML – Specialty certification**. If a U.S. applicant who otherwise met the education and experience requirements was not rejected for lacking the certification, the Employer's consistent application of the requirement is open to question — an audit vulnerability.

4. **Priority-date constraint on curing the gap.** Because USCIS assesses the Beneficiary's qualifications as of the priority date (March 15, 2025), the Beneficiary **obtaining the AWS ML – Specialty certification now would not, by itself, cure the gap** for the existing certified PERM (she did not hold it at the priority date). The only credential held at the priority date that could support an "equivalent" theory is the TensorFlow Developer Certificate (active since March 10, 2023) — a weak basis, as analyzed in Section V.E. This constraint heavily informs the remediation options in Section VII.

## VII. Remedial Recommendations

Recommendations are prioritized by severity. "Critical" items should be resolved before the Form I-140 is filed.

### Critical

**R-1. Resolve the certification gap (J.B.5) — choose a defensible path.** This is the central issue and cannot be left unaddressed. The Employer and counsel should select one of the following strategies:

- **Option A — Defend on "equivalent" theory (preserves priority date; higher risk).** Argue that the TensorFlow Developer Certificate (a professional-level, active credential held at the priority date) is the "equivalent" of the AWS ML – Specialty certification insofar as both validate production machine-learning/deep-learning model development competency. **Mitigations required:** (i) obtain a detailed expert letter (e.g., from a qualified ML practitioner or the IAE/another evaluator) opining that the TensorFlow Developer Certificate is substantively equivalent to the AWS ML – Specialty certification for the duties of this position; (ii) prepare a business-necessity memorandum justifying the certification requirement under § 656.17(h); and (iii) be prepared to explain the "or equivalent" standard with objective criteria. **Caveat:** This is a contested theory. USCIS may reject it because the TensorFlow credential is Google-issued, framework-specific, and does not test AWS ML services. This option preserves the March 15, 2025 priority date but carries denial risk.

- **Option B — Refile the PERM without the certification requirement (resets priority date; lower risk).** If the AWS ML – Specialty certification is not genuinely essential to performing the duties (and the special skills (a)–(e) already capture the substantive competencies), remove the certification requirement and refile. This eliminates the beneficiary-qualification gap, the "or equivalent" problem, and the business-necessity burden, but requires new recruitment and sacrifices the current priority date. Given the Beneficiary's STEM OPT expiration (September 15, 2025) and the pending H-1B, counsel should weigh the priority-date cost against the denial risk of Option A.

- **Option C — Refile after the Beneficiary obtains the AWS ML – Specialty certification.** Have the Beneficiary sit for and pass the AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty examination, then refile the PERM with the certification requirement intact (ideally dropping "or equivalent" or defining equivalence objectively). The Beneficiary would then hold the required certification as of the new priority date. This is the cleanest substantive outcome but is the slowest and also resets the priority date.

- **In all cases**, the Beneficiary should be encouraged to **obtain the AWS ML – Specialty certification promptly**. Even under Option A, holding the certification strengthens the overall profile and supports the H-1B/future filings; under Options B/C it is either unnecessary or prerequisite. Note the AWS Cloud Practitioner has expired and should not be relied upon.

**R-2. Reconcile the FEIN.** Confirm the Employer's correct FEIN with Prismex's payroll/finance records (Cornerpoint Payroll Services) and determine whether the error is on the ETA-9089 (84-3291756) or the PWD (84-3291047). Ensure the correct FEIN is used consistently on the I-140, I-129, and all future filings. If the PERM contains the incorrect FEIN, document the correct FEIN in the audit file and be prepared to explain the discrepancy; if the PWD contains the incorrect FEIN, consider whether a corrected PWD is warranted (though the PWD has expired or will expire November 7, 2025 and the PERM is already certified).

### Moderate

**R-3. Obtain an NC State Research Assistant verification letter.** Have the Beneficiary contact the NC State Department of Computer Science administrator or the university HR office (as the HR email already contemplates) to obtain a letter confirming the Research Assistant appointment, dates (August 15, 2016 – May 15, 2018), part-time hours (20/week), supervisor (Professor Helen Tsai), and duties (NLP research; transformer-based text classification in PyTorch; clinical dataset preprocessing; co-authored publications). The transcript notation is useful corroboration but should be supplemented with a letter for audit defensibility. Although the RA experience does not count toward the post-master's requirement, it is listed on the ETA-9089 and should be independently verifiable.

**R-4. Clarify and, if possible, broaden the supervisory record.** Obtain a supplemental statement from Dr. Okonkwo (DataBridge) clarifying the full scope of the Beneficiary's supervisory responsibilities — e.g., whether she also oversaw contractors, interns, or matrixed/rotating team members that would bring the supervised headcount closer to 3–5, and whether her reports' work was ML-engineering in nature. If the supervised headcount was genuinely only 2, consider whether the "3–5 ML engineers" duty should be softened (see R-6).

**R-5. Strengthen the R-proficiency record.** Obtain a supplemental DataBridge letter or a project addendum detailing the Beneficiary's substantive use of R — specific statistical models, packages (e.g., ggplot2, caret, tidyverse), and deliverables produced in R — to elevate the showing from "some use" to "proficiency." The academic coursework (ST 558, A-) should be cited in the I-140 support letter as corroboration. If R usage was genuinely limited, consider whether "proficiency in R" should be re-examined as a requirement (see R-6).

### Minor

**R-6. Re-examine potentially restrictive special requirements.** Before the I-140, evaluate whether (i) the AWS ML – Specialty certification, (ii) the "3–5 ML engineers" headcount, and (iii) "proficiency in R" are each normal to the occupation and supported by business necessity. For any that are not essential, the cleanest long-term fix is a refile without them (Option B). If the case proceeds on the existing certified PERM (Option A), prepare a business-necessity memorandum for each contested requirement and ensure the recruitment file reflects that each requirement was applied to all U.S. applicants.

**R-7. Reconcile the "Dr." honorific.** Use "Ananya Mehrotra" (or "Ms.") consistently in the I-140 and H-1B filings unless and until a doctoral credential is documented. Correct the "also known as: Dr." entry on any future filings.

**R-8. Confirm contact information.** Reconcile the Employer telephone number of record (555-0174 vs. 555-0184) and use the correct number consistently.

**R-9. Assemble the audit file now.** Even though the PERM is certified, DOL may audit. Ensure the audit file contains: the recruitment documentation (Broadleaf Staffing Advisors file), the SWA job order and print advertisements, the recruitment report, all experience letters (including the NC State RA letter once obtained), the transcript, the IAE evaluation, the certifications (with the AWS ML – Specialty once obtained, if applicable), the PWD, and the business-necessity memoranda for any contested special requirements.

## VIII. Conclusion and Recommended Next Steps

The Beneficiary is well-credentialed for the substantive technical core of the Senior Machine Learning Engineer position: the M.S. in Computer Science satisfies the education requirement, and approximately 6.75 years of progressive post-master's experience at DataBridge satisfy the 5-year experience requirement, with special skills (a)–(d) fully documented. The case's principal vulnerability is the **AWS Certified Machine Learning – Specialty certification (or equivalent)** required by J.B.5, which the Beneficiary does not hold and which is not satisfied by her expired AWS Cloud Practitioner or her Google-issued TensorFlow Developer Certificate. This gap is not supported by the K.3/K.4 attestations and creates material risk at I-140 adjudication and in any audit, compounded by the "or equivalent" phrasing, an unaddressed business-necessity question, and a recruitment report silent on the certification's application to U.S. applicants.

**Recommended immediate actions:**

1. Decide between Option A (defend on "equivalent" theory), Option B (refile without the certification), and Option C (refile after the Beneficiary earns the AWS ML – Specialty certification), weighing priority-date preservation against denial risk and the September 15, 2025 STEM OPT deadline.
2. Reconcile the FEIN (R-2) and obtain the NC State RA verification letter (R-3).
3. Supplement the DataBridge letter to strengthen the R-proficiency (R-5) and supervisory-scope (R-4) records.
4. Prepare business-necessity memoranda for any contested special requirements retained (R-6) and assemble the complete audit file (R-9).

We are available to discuss the strategic choice among Options A–C and to assist with the supplemental documentation and any refiled application.

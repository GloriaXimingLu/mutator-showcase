       I. Executive Summary  
      This memorandum summarizes our review of the governance documents of Terravox Systems, Inc. (    “    Terravox    ”         or the         “    Company    ”    ) in connection with the proposed acquisition of 100% of the Company’s outstanding equity by Hargrove Capital Partners III, L.P. (    “    Hargrove    ”         or         “    Buyer    ”    ), structured as a reverse triangular merger at an enterprise value of $485 million (the         “    Transaction    ”    ). We reviewed the Company’s Third Amended and Restated Certificate of Incorporation (the         “    Restated Certificate    ”    ), Amended and Restated Bylaws (the         “    Bylaws    ”    ), Stockholders’ Agreement, Voting Agreement, Registration Rights Agreement (the         “    RRA    ”    ), and Board minutes for the period January 2023 through September 2024 (the         “    Board Minutes    ”    ).  
          The central finding of this diligence is that Stonemill Capital Partners Fund VI, L.P. (        “        Stonemill        ”        ) holds a hard, charter-level blocking right over the Transaction.         Stonemill is the sole holder of the Company’s Series C Preferred Stock (7,800,000 shares, representing 100% of the outstanding Series C). Under Section 4.3.6(b)(i) of the Restated Certificate, the Company may not         “    effect any Liquidation Event    ”         — a defined term that expressly captures the reverse triangular merger — without the prior written consent or affirmative vote of the holders of at least 66⅔% of the outstanding Series C Preferred Stock, voting as a separate class. Because Stonemill holds 100% of the Series C, this separate-class consent cannot be obtained without Stonemill’s affirmative cooperation. This was acknowledged on the record by the Company’s own counsel and by Julian Trask (Stonemill’s board designee) at the September 25, 2024 Board meeting.  
      A secondary, related finding is that the drag-along provisions in the Stockholders’ Agreement provide a             colorable but legally contested         theory for compelling Stonemill’s vote. The drag-along can likely be             triggered         without Stonemill’s participation (two of its three prongs are clearly satisfiable without Stonemill, and the third is likely satisfiable under the natural reading of the applicable defined term). Once triggered, the drag-along purports to compel Stonemill to vote its Series C shares in favor of the Transaction, which would nominally satisfy the 66⅔% Series C threshold.             However, whether a stockholders’ agreement drag-along can override or satisfy a charter-level separate-class protective provision is a contested question of Delaware law    , the outcome of which is uncertain and would likely require litigation to resolve. Accordingly, the drag-along is best understood as a meaningful negotiating lever, not a clean path to closing over Stonemill’s objection.

        B. Board approvals (DGCL § 251(b))  
      Under Section 251(b) of the Delaware General Corporation Law (the         “    DGCL    ”    ), the board of directors of each corporation party to a merger must adopt a resolution approving the agreement of merger. Accordingly:  
              Terravox Board approval         of the definitive merger agreement is required. The Bylaws (Section 3.7) require a quorum of a majority of the directors then in office (4 of 7) and action by a majority of the directors present at a meeting at which a quorum is present. A 6-1 approval (as has occurred at the July 10 and September 25, 2024 meetings on related matters) is sufficient. Mr. Trask’s dissent will be recorded but does not prevent Board approval.  
              Hargrove and Merger Sub Board approvals         are required and will be obtained as a matter of course.  
          Status: Not yet obtained (the September 25, 2024 resolution authorized negotiation of definitive documentation only).  
        C. Stockholder approval — DGCL § 251(c)  
      Under DGCL § 251(c), adoption of a merger agreement requires the approval of         “    a majority of the outstanding stock of the corporation entitled to vote thereon,    ”         unless the charter requires a greater vote. The Restated Certificate does not impose a supermajority stockholder vote for mergers generally; the Common Stock and Preferred Stock vote together as a single class on an as-converted basis (Restated Certificate §§ 4.2.1, 4.3.5). The Bylaws (Section 2.6) and Restated Certificate (Article VI, § 6.2) permit stockholder action by written consent in lieu of a meeting, which the Company intends to use.  
      The DGCL § 251(c) vote requires a majority of the outstanding shares entitled to vote, calculated on an as-converted basis. The Founders (7,300,000 shares) together with Ridgeline (3,500,000) and Northbridge (5,200,000) hold 16,000,000 shares on an as-converted basis. Whether this clears the majority threshold depends on the denominator (i.e., whether unexercised options and unsettled RSUs are counted). Under the natural reading —         “    outstanding stock entitled to vote    ”         excludes unexercised options and unsettled RSUs, which are not outstanding shares — the denominator is issued Common plus Preferred-as-converted, and the 16,000,000 held by the Founders, Ridgeline, and Northbridge would exceed a majority. Even under a fully-diluted denominator (33,900,000; majority = 16,950,000), the Founders, Ridgeline, and Northbridge (16,000,000) fall short by approximately 950,000 shares, which could be supplied by additional common stockholders.             We recommend confirming the exact issued-share count and lining up additional common-holder support to ensure the § 251(c) vote is secured.  
          Status: Not yet obtained; likely satisfiable without Stonemill, subject to confirming the issued-share denominator and securing additional common-holder votes if needed.

The margin is thin (450,000 shares), so the full support of both Ridgeline and Northbridge is essential.  
          Status: Not yet obtained; satisfiable without Stonemill (requires Ridgeline + Northbridge).

        F. Charter and bylaw amendments (if required)  
      A reverse triangular merger in which Terravox survives as a subsidiary generally does not require an amendment to Terravox’s charter. If, however, the Transaction structure or the merger agreement requires any charter amendment, Section 8.1 of the Restated Certificate requires (i) Board approval, (ii) a majority of the outstanding capital stock entitled to vote, and (iii) the separate Preferred consents under Section 4.3.6 — including, for any amendment adversely and disproportionately affecting the Series C, the 66⅔% Series C separate-class consent under Section 4.3.6(b)(ii). Bylaw amendments by stockholders require a 66⅔% vote of the outstanding voting power (Restated Certificate § 8.2; Bylaws § 9.2), though the Board may amend the Bylaws by majority vote (Bylaws § 9.1). We do not currently anticipate that charter or bylaw amendments will be required, but this should be confirmed once the merger agreement structure is finalized.

      The Stockholders’ Agreement contains drag-along rights that, if the applicable thresholds are met, purport to compel all stockholder parties to vote in favor of a Sale of the Company. The drag-along is analyzed in detail in Area 2. For purposes of the consent checklist, the drag-along is a mechanism for             compelling         votes, not a freestanding consent; its ability to satisfy the Series C separate-class protective consent is the central legal question of this diligence.  
        H. DGCL § 251(f) — parent-subsidiary merger (not applicable)

         IV. Area 2 — Drag-Along Analysis  
       A. The three-pronged trigger  
      Section 3.1(a) of the Stockholders’ Agreement provides that if a         “    Sale of the Company    ”         is approved by each of the following — (i) holders of at least a majority of the outstanding Common Stock (as-converted), (ii) holders of at least a majority of the outstanding Preferred Stock (as-converted, voting as a single class), and (iii) at least two of the three Investor Stockholders — then each         “    Restricted Stockholder    ”         (including any that did not vote in favor) must vote all of its shares in favor of the Sale of the Company, refrain from exercising appraisal rights, take all actions necessary to consummate the sale, and not impede the transaction (the         “    Drag-Along Approval    ”    ). Section 3.3 grants an irrevocable proxy to the holders constituting the Drag-Along Approval. All three prongs must be satisfied; fewer than all three does not trigger the drag-along (Section 3.1(b)).  
      Stonemill is a party to the Stockholders’ Agreement as an         “    Investor Stockholder    ”         and therefore a         “    Restricted Stockholder.    ”         Accordingly, if a Drag-Along Approval is obtained, the drag-along purports to bind Stonemill.  
        B. Can the drag-along be triggered without Stonemill?  
      We analyze each prong:  
          Prong (iii) — at least two of three Investor Stockholders.         The three Investor Stockholders are Ridgeline, Northbridge, and Stonemill. Ridgeline and Northbridge together satisfy this prong without Stonemill.             Satisfied without Stonemill.  
          Prong (ii) — majority of Preferred (as-converted, single class).         This is the same threshold as the all-Preferred protective consent under Section 4.3.6(a): more than 8,250,000 shares. Ridgeline (3,500,000) and Northbridge (5,200,000) together hold 8,700,000 shares, exceeding the threshold.             Satisfied without Stonemill         (margin of 450,000 shares; full support of both Ridgeline and Northbridge required).  
          Prong (i) — majority of Common (as-converted).         This is the binding constraint. The Founders (7,300,000), Ridgeline (3,500,000), and Northbridge (5,200,000) together hold 16,000,000 shares on an as-converted basis. The threshold is a majority of the         “    outstanding shares of Common Stock (calculated on an as-converted to Common Stock basis, treating each share of Preferred Stock as having been converted).    ”  
          Under the natural reading,         “    outstanding shares of Common Stock    ”         refers to issued and outstanding shares (excluding unexercised options and unsettled RSUs, which are not outstanding shares), plus Preferred-as-converted. On that reading, the denominator is issued Common plus 16,500,000 (Preferred-as-converted), and the 16,000,000 held by the Founders, Ridgeline, and Northbridge would exceed a majority.             Likely satisfied without Stonemill         under this reading.  
          Under a fully-diluted reading (denominator of 33,900,000; majority = 16,950,000), the Founders, Ridgeline, and Northbridge (16,000,000) fall short by approximately 950,000 shares and would require additional common-holder support to trigger.  
          Conclusion on trigger:         The drag-along can likely be triggered without Stonemill. Prongs (ii) and (iii) are clearly satisfied without Stonemill. Prong (i) is likely satisfied under the natural (issued-shares) reading but is the tightest prong; we recommend (a) confirming the exact count of issued Common Stock (excluding unexercised options/RSUs), and (b) identifying and pre-confirming additional common stockholders willing to vote in favor, to ensure prong (i) is met under either reading. Notably, the drag-along cannot be used to compel the very votes needed to trigger it (that would be circular); the triggering majority must be assembled from willing voters first.  
        C. Can the drag-along override the charter-level Series C protective provision? — CRITICAL LEGAL QUESTION  
      This is the decisive question. Even if the drag-along is triggered, the Transaction remains blocked unless the Series C separate-class consent under Section 4.3.6(b)(i) is satisfied. The drag-along purports to compel Stonemill to         “    vote … all Shares held by such Restricted Stockholder in favor of such Sale of the Company and in favor of any other matter required to be approved in connection with such Sale of the Company    ”         (Section 3.1(a)(A)). Because Stonemill holds 100% of the Series C, a compelled affirmative vote by Stonemill would nominally constitute 100% (≥ 66⅔%) of the Series C separate-class vote — i.e., it would facially satisfy the protective provision. The question is whether that compelled vote counts.  
          Arguments that the drag-along can satisfy the protective provision (Buyer’s position):  
          Stonemill contractually agreed to the drag-along, including the irrevocable proxy in Section 3.3, when it executed the Stockholders’ Agreement. A stockholder may waive its own protective-protection rights by contract.  
          DGCL § 217(c) provides that written voting agreements are         “    specifically enforceable    ”         and         “    shall not be invalid on the ground that [they are] inconsistent with the voting rights of the stockholders.    ”         Delaware courts have enforced drag-along provisions (including via specific performance and irrevocable proxies).  
          The drag-along expressly requires voting in favor of         “    any other matter required to be approved in connection with such Sale of the Company,    ”         which can be read to include the Series C separate-class protective consent.  
          Because Stonemill is the sole Series C holder, its compelled vote is the class vote; there is no separate class of other Series C holders whose rights would be impaired by Stonemill’s waiver.  
          Arguments that the drag-along cannot override the protective provision (Stonemill’s likely position):  
          The Restated Certificate is the Company’s supreme governing document; a stockholders’ agreement cannot override the charter. The protective provision is a class right embedded in the charter and adopted with the consent of all stockholders.  
          The Stockholders’ Agreement contains no express waiver of the charter protective provisions and no express conflict provision subordinating the charter protective provisions to the drag-along. (Notably, the companion Voting Agreement             does         state in Section 8.3 that         “    in the event of any conflict between the terms of this Agreement and the Restated Certificate, the terms of the Restated Certificate shall control    ”         — a strong indication that the parties understood the charter to control over the stockholder agreements. The Stockholders’ Agreement lacks an equivalent provision, but the Voting Agreement’s conflict clause supports the inference that the charter is supreme across the suite of governance documents.)  
          The protective provision requires the         “    prior written consent or affirmative vote    ”         of 66⅔% of Series C. A vote compelled under threat of breach and extracted via specific performance is arguably not a voluntary         “    consent,    ”         and a court may be reluctant to construe the drag-along as an implied waiver of a charter class right absent express language.  
          Specific performance overriding a charter protective right is an extraordinary remedy that a court may decline, particularly where the result is to extinguish a class protection that the charter deliberately embedded.  
          Assessment.         This is a genuinely contested question of Delaware law on which reasonable minds differ, and the outcome would likely turn on the specific facts and the court’s view of the parties’ intent. The drag-along theory is             colorable and provides real negotiating leverage    , but it is             not a clean or certain path to closing    . A sophisticated buyer seeking closing certainty would not rely on it as the sole means of closing over Stonemill’s objection. The practical consequences of this uncertainty are significant: (a) Hargrove’s merger agreement would need a closing condition requiring the requisite consents, and if the Series C consent is not delivered, Hargrove faces closing risk; (b) Stonemill would likely seek to enjoin or challenge the Transaction; and (c) the resulting litigation would consume time and create uncertainty that could jeopardize the Q1 2025 target closing.  
          Recommendation.         Do not treat the drag-along as a substitute for Stonemill’s consent. Instead, use it as a negotiating lever — demonstrating to Stonemill that its blocking right is not airtight and that litigation carries risk for Stonemill too — while pursuing Stonemill’s affirmative consent as the primary path. We recommend a formal Delaware-law opinion on the drag-along/protective-provision interaction to inform the negotiation and the merger agreement’s closing conditions.  
        D. Drag-along terms affecting consideration  
      Section 3.2(a) provides that each Restricted Stockholder receives the same form and amount of consideration per share (as-converted),         “    provided, however, that the foregoing shall not restrict the application of liquidation preferences as set forth in the Restated Certificate, and consideration shall be distributed in accordance with the liquidation waterfall provisions of the Restated Certificate as if the Sale of the Company were a Liquidation Event.    ”         This means the drag-along does             not         override the liquidation waterfall — consideration flows through the charter waterfall (Series C first, then Series B, then Series A, then Common, with preferred electing conversion where favorable). This is consistent with the economic analysis above (all preferred converts). Section 3.2(d) provides that no stockholder can be required to provide indemnification in excess of the consideration received, and no stockholder can be required to enter non-compete/non-solicit covenants — terms that should be reflected in the merger agreement’s stockholder representative and indemnity provisions.  
      
         V. Area 3 — Impediments to Closing a Reverse Triangular Merger  
       A. The reverse triangular structure is clearly captured by all defined terms  
      A reverse triangular merger — in which Merger Sub merges into Terravox, Terravox survives, and Terravox’s stockholders receive cash — is expressly captured by the charter’s         “    Liquidation Event    ”         definition. Section 4.3.2(d)(i) defines a Liquidation Event to include         “    any merger, consolidation, or other business combination of the Corporation with or into another entity (        whether or not the Corporation is the surviving entity    ), other than a merger … in which the stockholders of the Corporation immediately prior to such transaction continue to hold at least fifty percent (50%) of the voting power of the surviving or resulting entity.    ”         The parenthetical         “    (whether or not the Corporation is the surviving entity)    ”         directly addresses the reverse triangular case. Because Terravox’s pre-merger stockholders will hold 0% of the voting power of the surviving entity (they receive cash and are not Hargrove stockholders), the Transaction is a Liquidation Event. The same conclusion follows under the         “    Sale of the Company    ”         definitions in the Stockholders’ Agreement (Section 1.1(i)) and the Voting Agreement (Section 1(a)), each of which captures a merger in which the Company’s stockholders do not retain a majority of the voting power of the surviving entity.  
          We find no structural ambiguity in the defined terms as applied to the reverse triangular merger.         The charter drafters anticipated this structure.  
        B. Structural considerations and impediments  
             Consent requirements are the principal impediment, not the structure.         The reverse triangular merger requires the same stockholder and protective-provision consents as a forward merger. The Series C separate-class consent (Area 1.E) is the binding constraint.  
             Surviving-entity status preserves contracts and licenses — but exposes change-of-control triggers.         Because Terravox survives as a subsidiary, its existing contracts (including the Clearfield term loan, the Permian Basin Energy Co. master services agreement, and any SensorLink obligations) remain in place unless they contain change-of-control provisions. The Clearfield term loan agreement (not yet produced) very likely contains a change-of-control covenant that could accelerate or default the loan; this must be reviewed. The SensorLink LOI exclusivity (Area 8.C) is a direct potential conflict.  
             20-day Liquidation Event notice.         Section 4.3.2(d) requires the Company to provide written notice of any proposed Liquidation Event to each Preferred holder not less than 20 days prior to the anticipated closing date, setting forth material terms, the anticipated closing date, and a good-faith estimate of per-share amounts distributable to each series. This notice obligation must be satisfied and should be calendared in the signing-to-closing timeline.  
             No appraisal rights elimination.         The drag-along (Section 3.1(a)(B)) requires stockholders to refrain from exercising appraisal rights, but appraisal rights under DGCL § 262 are statutory and cannot be eliminated by contract for cash-out mergers; the drag-along operates as a contractual waiver/forebearance, enforceable by specific performance, but does not alter the statutory availability of appraisal. This is a minor point but worth noting for the merger agreement.  
      
         VI. Area 4 — Transfer Restrictions  
       A. The merger does not trigger ROFR or co-sale  
      Section 2.1 of the Stockholders’ Agreement prohibits any         “    Restricted Stockholder    ”         from transferring shares except (a) in compliance with Article 2, (b) pursuant to a         “    Permitted Transfer    ”         (Section 2.7), or (c)         “    in connection with a Sale of the Company consummated in accordance with Article 3.    ”         The merger is a         “    Sale of the Company    ”         (Section 1.1) and will be consummated in accordance with Article 3 (the drag-along article). Accordingly, the transfer of shares in the merger is             expressly carved out         of the transfer restrictions.  
              Right of first refusal (Section 2.2):         Triggered only when a stockholder receives a bona fide written offer from a third party to purchase its shares and desires to accept. The merger is not such a voluntary sale by an individual stockholder; it is a company-wide Sale of the Company.             Not triggered.  
              Co-sale right (Section 2.3):         Applies only when the Company and other Restricted Stockholders decline to purchase all shares subject to a ROFR notice. Because the merger is not a ROFR-triggering event, co-sale does not apply.             Not triggered.

On that construction, the RRA terminates on the merger, and registration rights do not survive closing.  
          However, the term’s absence from the RRA’s own definitions creates a risk         that a court could find the RRA does not automatically terminate on the merger, particularly if Stonemill (or another Holder) argued that         “    Deemed Liquidation Event    ”         should be narrowly construed or that the parties failed to incorporate the charter definition. If registration rights survive the Transaction — even unintentionally — Hargrove/the surviving entity could inherit demand registration obligations (two demand registrations available after November 3, 2024, per Section 2.1(a)), piggyback rights (Section 2.2), and unlimited S-3 registration rights (Section 2.3), as well as the associated indemnification obligations (Section 7) and the Company’s obligation to bear Registration Expenses (Section 6). This would be an undesirable post-closing liability.  
        C. Demand registration timing note  
      Section 2.1(a) makes demand registration rights available         “    at any time after November 3, 2024 (three (3) years from the date of this Agreement).    ”         As of the date of this memorandum (October 25, 2024), demand rights are not yet available, but they will become available on November 3, 2024 — before the anticipated Q1 2025 closing. This means that, if the RRA does not clearly terminate on the merger, a Holder (e.g., Stonemill) could exercise demand registration rights against the surviving entity post-closing. This heightens the importance of ensuring clean termination.  
        D. Recommendation  
              Address expressly in the merger agreement.         Include a specific provision terminating the RRA at closing and obtaining each Holder’s written waiver/release of registration rights, rather than relying solely on the         “    Deemed Liquidation Event    ”         construction. Obtain signed terminations or joinders from each Investor Stockholder (Ridgeline, Northbridge, Stonemill) at or prior to closing.  
              If Stonemill withholds cooperation    , the RRA termination could become another leverage point — Stonemill could refuse to sign a termination, leaving the definitional ambiguity unresolved. The merger agreement should provide for termination by operation of the Transaction itself (e.g., a covenant that the Company terminates the RRA effective at closing) to the extent permissible, and Hargrove should condition closing on satisfactory resolution of registration rights.  
              Confirm the construction         with a Delaware-law opinion on whether         “    Deemed Liquidation Event    ”         incorporates the charter’s         “    Liquidation Event    ”         definition.  
      Severity:             Significant         (post-closing liability risk if not addressed).

         XIV. Conclusion  
      The governance documents reveal a Company with a well-structured but investor-protective governance framework typical of a Series C venture-backed company. The Transaction is clearly captured by all operative change-of-control definitions, and the transfer restrictions, board designation rights, and observer rights terminate cleanly at closing. The principal obstacle is             Stonemill’s charter-level Series C separate-class blocking right    , which cannot be satisfied without Stonemill’s affirmative consent. The drag-along provides a colorable but contested theory to compel Stonemill’s vote, but it does not provide closing certainty. Two additional issues — the             SensorLink LOI exclusivity         (potentially live and conflicting) and the             potential protective-provision violation in the Clearfield term loan         — require prompt attention. The             Special Committee’s composition         and the             narrow sale process         create fiduciary-duty vulnerabilities that Stonemill can exploit. We recommend prioritizing (1) immediate resolution of the SensorLink exclusivity, (2) a negotiation strategy centered on Stonemill’s consent with the drag-along as a fallback lever, (3) completion of the equity-award analysis upon receipt of the Plan and award agreements, and (4) shoring up the sale-process procedural protections.  
      We are available to discuss any of the foregoing and to refine the analysis as additional documents are received.

       1. Executive Summary and Recommendation  
      This memorandum presents the deal team’s buy-side analysis of Cascade Environmental Solutions, Inc. (    “    CES    ”         or the         “    Company    ”    ), a Pacific Northwest environmental services platform being sold by Greenleaf Advisory Group, LLC (    “    Greenleaf    ”    ) on behalf of founder/CEO Randall Oakes. The analysis is based on the Confidential Information Memorandum (    “    CIM    ”    ) dated June 9, 2025, the accompanying Financial Exhibits, the Customer Summary and Backlog Report, the Process Letter, and the Thornfield sector screening memo dated June 12, 2025. It is prepared ahead of the             July 14, 2025 Indication of Interest (        “        IOI        ”        ) deadline    .  
          Recommendation: Proceed to IOI at a disciplined valuation, with contingent structure.         CES is a genuine, well-positioned platform that fits Fund III’s environmental services thesis and benefits from real regulatory tailwinds (notably PFAS). However, the sell-side materials contain a pattern of financial inconsistencies and aggressive assumptions that, taken together, meaningfully reduce the headline Adjusted EBITDA on which the asking price is built. We recommend submitting an IOI at an             enterprise value of approximately $130–145 million         (≈8.0x–9.0x our buy-side LTM Adjusted EBITDA of ~$15.7M), with             up to $15–20 million of contingent consideration         tied to PFAS revenue milestones and renewal of the largest customer contract, plus an option to acquire the headquarters real estate. This structure bridges toward the seller’s implied range while protecting the Fund against the two largest value risks (PFAS execution and customer concentration).

        4. Investment Thesis  
      The following attributes support proceeding, and are consistent with the Thornfield sector memo’s positive assessment:  
              Genuine platform with durable moats.         16-year operating history, integrated three-segment offering, four-state permit footprint, and licensed Professional Engineer capacity that take years to replicate. The integrated model drives cross-selling and share-of-wallet.  
              Real growth, even discounting PFAS.         12.5% revenue CAGR (FY21–24); ex-PFAS, ~6% CAGR — within the 5–10% sector range. Reported EBITDA margin of 13.9% is within the 10–15% sector norm, indicating a reasonably well-run operation.  
              PFAS regulatory tailwinds are real.         EPA finalized drinking water MCLs for six PFAS compounds in April 2024; Oregon and Washington are advancing state-level rules. PFAS is widely viewed as a multi-decade remediation challenge. CES is an early mover (launched 2022) with specialized sampling/treatment equipment and a growing track record.  
              Revenue visibility and stickiness.         $42.6M contracted backlog + $31.4M pipeline = $74.0M opportunity set; ~90% historical MSA renewal rate; no top-10 customer lost in five years; ~60% of revenue under MSAs.  
              Fragmented market with M&A optionality.         Management has identified 5–7 tuck-in targets; CES can serve as a consolidation platform, consistent with Fund III’s build-up approach.  
              Strong safety record.         EMR of 0.87 is a meaningful qualifier for access to industrial/refining facilities.  
              Strategic fit.         Aligns with Fund III’s environmental/industrial services focus; Thornfield’s GreenWorks and Allied Waste experience provides directly relevant operating benchmarks and pattern recognition.

             (a) The bridge does not foot — $0.3M overstatement.         Reported EBITDA ($12.1M) plus the six stated adjustments ($1.9 + $0.8 + $1.2 + $0.4 + $0.3 + $0.1 = $4.7M) equals             $16.8M    , not the $17.1M presented. We verified that FY2021 ($9.7M), FY2022 ($11.1M), and FY2023 ($13.4M) each reconcile exactly; only FY2024 is overstated. Because the entire 8.0x–10.0x valuation is applied to $17.1M, this $0.3M error inflates the implied EV by $2.4M–$3.0M on its own.  
          (b) Related-party lease normalization is overstated by ~$0.4–0.6M.         CES leases its 8.5-acre, purpose-built HQ from Mr. Oakes at $1.4M/year. The $0.8M add-back assumes market rent of $0.6M, described in Section VIII.A as         “    comparable industrial yard space.    ”         However, Section IV.C of the same CIM states that             “        specialized environmental staging facilities commanding rates of $1.0 million to $1.2 million annually for properties of comparable acreage and infrastructure.        ”         The CES HQ is precisely such a specialized facility (permitted wash rack, secondary containment, roll-off/soil stockpile staging, 12,000 SF maintenance bays). The correct market comparable is therefore $1.0M–$1.2M, implying above-market rent of only $0.2M–$0.4M — not $0.8M. This also has a forward-looking implication: a buyer that continues leasing should expect to pay closer to $1.0M–$1.2M,             higher         than the $0.6M normalized into Adjusted EBITDA.  
          (c) The $1.2M DEQ/legal add-back is partially recurring and may be double-counted.         On August 14, 2024, CES settled an Oregon DEQ enforcement action for improper storage of contaminated soils at its Portland facility ($875K settlement + $325K legal). Two concerns: (i) Per the Thornfield sector memo and Thornfield’s own portfolio data (GreenWorks ~$275K/yr; Allied ~$310K/yr recurring), a $0.2M–$0.5M annual regulatory run-rate is inherent to this business; only the excess over run-rate is a defensible add-back (e.g., ~$0.7M–$0.9M, not $1.2M). (ii) The Financial Exhibits classify the $875K settlement as         “    Other Income/(Expense)    ”             below         the operating income line — meaning it may already be excluded from Reported EBITDA, in which case adding it back double-counts. If the below-the-line classification is correct, only the $325K legal fees (in operating expenses) are an EBITDA add-back at all. This single item is the largest swing factor in the bridge and must be resolved in QoE. We also note the reputational dimension: a compliance failure at an environmental services company’s             own         facility warrants a full 10-year enforcement history review.  
          (d) Owner compensation and projection inconsistency.         The $1.9M add-back ($2.4M actual less $0.5M replacement CEO) is defensible in principle. However, the management projections in the Financial Exhibits set owner compensation to             $0         in FY2025E–FY2027E — i.e., they assume             no         replacement CEO cost at all, which is more aggressive than the $0.5M normalization used for the LTM bridge. A platform of this size will require a real CEO; budgeting $0 understates projected operating expenses and overstates projected Adjusted EBITDA. We would carry at least $0.5M–$0.7M of replacement CEO cost in the forward model.  
          Combined effect:         Buy-side LTM Adjusted EBITDA of ~$15.5–16.0M (midpoint ~$15.7M, ~18.0% margin) versus sell-side $17.1M. The Adjusted EBITDA CAGR of         “    20.8%    ”         cited in the CIM is also affected: using a corrected FY2024 figure, the CAGR is closer to ~18%.

        11. Recommendation and Next Steps  
          Recommendation: Proceed to IOI.         CES is an attractive, on-thesis environmental services platform with genuine competitive moats and real (if PFAS-concentrated) growth. However, the sell-side materials exhibit a consistent pattern — an EBITDA bridge that does not foot, an overstated lease add-back, a partially recurring/possibly double-counted regulatory add-back, understated maintenance capex, an aggressive PFAS ramp, and unreconciled financial statements — that collectively reduce the earnings base from $17.1M to ~$15.7M and argue against paying the top of the comp range.

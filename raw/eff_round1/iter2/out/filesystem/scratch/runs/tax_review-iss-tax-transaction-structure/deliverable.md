        I. Executive Summary  
      This report reviews the Tax Structure Memorandum prepared by Calloway, Stern & Whitaker LLP (    “    CSW    ”    ) against the supporting deal documents made available for the proposed acquisition of Prism Health Analytics, Inc. (    “    Prism    ”    ) by Ridgeline Capital Partners Fund III, LP (    “    Ridgeline    ”         or         “    Buyer    ”    ).           Documents reviewed.              The three most serious findings are:  
             The Aggregate Deemed Sale Price (ADSP) is overstated by $48 million.         The Memo computes ADSP as Enterprise Value ($425M) plus funded debt ($48M) = $473M. This double-counts the funded debt, because Enterprise Value already embeds the debt (Equity Value $370M = EV $425M − debt $48M − seller expenses $7M). The Thornfield Asset Valuation expressly flags this as a         “    SIGNIFICANT DISCREPANCY.    ”         The correct ADSP is approximately $425M (amount realized on the stock $370M + target liabilities $48M + $7M). The $48M overstatement flows directly into an overstated Class VII goodwill residual and an overstated deemed-sale gain.  
             The Memo affirmatively states that there are no ineligible S corporation shareholders, directly contradicting the Thornfield DDR’s #1 Critical finding and the Cap Table.         The Memo asserts that         “    All shareholders of Prism are U.S. individuals, and we are not aware of any ineligible shareholders.    ”         The Thornfield DDR identifies two restricted-stock holders whose eligibility is unconfirmed (an H-1B visa holder and a possible transfer to a family LLC), and the Cap Table flags three holders as         “    Unconfirmed.    ”         Valid S corporation status is a threshold requirement for the Section 338(h)(10) election; if the S election was inadvertently terminated, the election is unavailable and cascading C corporation tax applies for all open years.  
             The aggregate adjusted tax basis used to compute the deemed-sale gain ($42.8M) does not reconcile to the per-asset tax bases in the Asset Valuation (which sum to ~$64.3M).         The unreconciled $21.5M gap, combined with the ADSP overstatement, means the Memo’s deemed-sale gain of $430.2M may be overstated by approximately $69.5M, and every per-shareholder gain figure in the Memo is correspondingly overstated.  
          Other headline concerns.         The Memo entirely omits the Section 1202 QSBS analysis that CSW expressly committed to provide in the QSBS Email Thread — an omission that is material to the selling shareholders, several of whom hold shares issued during Prism’s 2016 C corporation period and face seven-figure after-tax swings. The Memo’s treatment of the $22M escrow (recommending installment-sale deferral) directly contradicts the Merger Agreement, which specifies that the escrow be reported as amount realized in the year of closing. The Memo also understates the Beacon Insights, LLC (    “    Beacon    ”    ) complications — the operating-agreement right of first refusal triggered by Prism’s change of control, the automatic termination of the intercompany IP license on a deemed transfer, the Section 751         “    hot asset    ”         character issue, and the Section 754 election decision — any of which could affect the structure or the character of gain.  
          Bottom line.         The Memo should not be relied upon in its current form for the Section 338(h)(10) election, the Form 8883 allocation, or shareholder tax modeling. The ADSP and purchase price allocation must be corrected, the S corporation eligibility question must be resolved as a condition to closing, the aggregate basis figure must be reconciled, and the QSBS analysis must be completed and circulated to the selling shareholders before they are asked to consent to the election.

             Methodology and severity definitions.  
              Critical         — An error or omission that, if uncorrected, could (a) invalidate or jeopardize the intended tax structure (e.g., the Section 338(h)(10) election), (b) produce a materially incorrect tax result that would be reported on a filed return (e.g., Form 8883), or (c) expose the parties to significant, quantifiable tax liability or to a misstatement relied upon by third parties.  
              High         — A material gap or contradiction that significantly affects shareholder after-tax economics, that contradicts a negotiated contractual position, or that requires resolution before closing but that, standing alone, does not invalidate the structure.  
              Moderate         — An issue that should be addressed and documented but that is unlikely, by itself, to derail the transaction or to produce a large quantifiable exposure.  
              Low         — A refinement, verification, or housekeeping item.

        III. Severity-Ranked Findings  
       A. Critical Findings  
       C-1. ADSP Overstated by $48 Million — Double-Counting of Funded Debt  
          Memo position (Section IV.B).         The Memo computes the Aggregate Deemed Sale Price as:  
      Enterprise Value $425,000,000 + Assumed Liabilities (Funded Debt) $48,000,000 =             ADSP $473,000,000  
          Supporting-document position.         The Thornfield Asset Valuation (Allocation Summary tab) expressly flags this as a         “    SIGNIFICANT DISCREPANCY    ”         and a double-count. Thornfield computes ADSP as the amount realized on the stock ($370M = $333M cash + $37M rollover equity) plus the liabilities of old target ($48M funded debt + $7M seller transaction expenses) =             $425,000,000    . The Asset Valuation explains:         “    The memorandum computes ADSP as $425M enterprise value + $48M liabilities = $473M. This double-counts the $48M funded debt, which was already subtracted to derive equity value ($425M − $48M − $7M = $370M) and is then added back again.    ”  
          Nature of the issue.         Enterprise Value, by definition, already includes the debt component (EV = Equity Value + Net Debt). The Memo itself derives Equity Value as EV ($425M) − funded debt ($48M) − seller transaction expenses ($7M) = $370M. Starting the ADSP computation from Enterprise Value and then adding the funded debt back therefore counts the debt twice. Under Treas. Reg. 1.338-4, the ADSP is the grossed-up amount realized on the stock plus the liabilities of old target; the correct starting point is the amount realized on the stock ($370M), not Enterprise Value. The correct ADSP is approximately $425M (or $418M if seller transaction expenses are not treated as target liabilities). The Memo’s $473M is overstated by at least $48M.  
          Cascading impact.         Because the residual method allocates any excess to Class VII goodwill, the $48M overstatement inflates the Class VII goodwill residual. The Memo reports Class VII goodwill of $272.8M; on a corrected ADSP of $425M (and using the Memo’s own Classes I–VI subtotal of $200.2M), goodwill would be approximately $224.8M — an overstatement of $48M. The overstated goodwill also overstates Buyer’s Section 197 amortization deductions (and correspondingly understates the faster-amortizing Class V/VI assets), and it overstates the deemed-sale gain by $48M (see C-3). The ADSP and allocation will be reported on Form 8883 and agreed between Buyer and the Shareholder Representative under Merger Agreement Section 7.3(b); an incorrect figure would be filed inconsistently by the parties and is vulnerable to IRS challenge.  
          Recommended action.         Recompute the ADSP from the amount realized on the stock ($370M) plus target liabilities before the Form 8883 allocation is finalized. Reconcile with Thornfield. Re-issue the Memo with corrected ADSP, allocation, and all downstream gain figures.  
        C-2. S Corporation Shareholder Eligibility Risk Not Disclosed — Contradicts Due Diligence and Cap Table  
          Memo position (Section III.A).         The Memo states:         “    No events causing a termination of the S election have been identified. All shareholders of Prism are U.S. individuals, and we are not aware of any ineligible shareholders.    ”         Section II.A similarly represents that all restricted-stock grants were made to eligible holders and that the 20%         “    other shareholders    ”         block is comprised of individuals.  
          Supporting-document position.  
              Thornfield DDR (Section III.B; Finding #1, rated                 “        Critical        ”        ).         Thornfield was         “    unable to confirm S corporation eligibility for 2 of the 8 restricted stock holders    ”    : (a) one holder who is a foreign national on an H-1B visa whose substantial-presence-test compliance has not been verified for all years; and (b) one holder whose shares may have been transferred to a         “    Family Holdings, LLC,    ”         which would be an ineligible shareholder. Thornfield warns that if the S election was inadvertently terminated,         “    Prism would have been a C corporation for that year and all subsequent years, with cascading consequences including entity-level federal and state income tax liability for all open years, potential penalties and interest, and the unavailability of the Section 338(h)(10) election.    ”  
              Cap Table.         Three restricted-stock holders are flagged         “    Unconfirmed    ”         for S corporation eligibility: Lin Wei Zhang (H-1B visa holder, PRC citizen, permanent-residency application pending); Yusuf Al-Rashidi (dual U.S./Jordan citizen, U.S. citizenship documentation not on file); and Nina Petrova (Bulgarian-born, green-card/lawful-permanent-resident status not verified).  
              Merger Agreement (Sections 4.9(a) and 2.5).         The Company represents that         “    each shareholder is an eligible S corporation shareholder    ”         and that         “    each holder of restricted stock is an individual who is a citizen or resident of the United States.    ”         These representations are not supported by the due-diligence record.  
          Nature of the issue.         The Memo’s blanket assertion that all shareholders are U.S. individuals and that no ineligible shareholders exist is contradicted by the very due-diligence report the Memo cites as a basis for its analysis (the Memo’s preamble references         “    the quality of earnings and tax due diligence report prepared by Thornfield Accounting Group    ”    ). Valid S corporation status is a threshold requirement for the Section 338(h)(10) election: the election is available only where the target is an S corporation, and the entire tax structure — including the conclusion that no Section 1374 built-in-gains tax applies and that gain passes through to shareholders without entity-level federal tax — depends on continuous, valid S status since January 1, 2017. The Memo’s failure to even acknowledge the eligibility risk, let alone condition its conclusions on resolution of that risk, is a critical omission.  
          Cross-document inconsistencies (noted for resolution).         (i) The Thornfield DDR describes the H-1B holder as a         “    Canadian national,    ”         while the Cap Table identifies Lin Wei Zhang as a citizen of the People’s Republic of China — the two accounts do not match. (ii) The Thornfield DDR’s         “    transfer to a family LLC    ”         concern is not reflected anywhere in the Cap Table, which shows all eight restricted-stock holders as individuals. Either the Cap Table is incomplete or the alleged transfer did not occur; the discrepancy must be resolved.  
          Recommended action.         Before closing, obtain definitive documentation of the eligibility of every shareholder — and in particular every restricted-stock holder — for every year since January 1, 2017, with priority on (a) immigration/residency documentation sufficient to confirm substantial-presence-test or lawful-permanent-resident status for the H-1B holder, (b) U.S. citizenship documentation for the dual-citizen holder, and (c) confirmation of whether any shares were transferred to a family LLC. The Memo should be revised to disclose the open eligibility question and to state that its conclusions are contingent on confirmation of valid S status. The accuracy of the Merger Agreement representations in Sections 4.9(a) and 2.5 should be confirmed against the resolved record.  
        C-3. Aggregate Adjusted Tax Basis Does Not Reconcile to Asset Valuation — Deemed-Sale Gain Potentially Overstated by ~$69.5M  
          Memo position (Sections III.B and V.A).         The Memo states that Prism’s         “    current aggregate adjusted tax basis in its assets (as of the most recent available date) is approximately $42,800,000    ”         and computes the total deemed-sale gain as ADSP ($473M) − aggregate basis ($42.8M) =             $430,200,000    . All per-shareholder gain figures are derived from this number (Chandra 52% = $223,704,000; Stowe 28% = $120,456,000; Other 20% = $86,040,000).  
          Supporting-document position.         The Asset Valuation lists a per-asset         “    Adjusted Tax Basis    ”         for each of Prism’s assets. Those per-asset figures sum to approximately             $64,300,000         (cash $12.4M; commercial AR $18.7M; government AR $6.4M; real property $9.1M; FF&E $3.2M; developed technology $4.8M; government software licenses $1.2M; NC data processing license $0; Beacon interest $6.4M; proprietary data sets $2.1M; customer relationships, non-competes, workforce, and goodwill all $0).  
          Nature of the issue.         The Memo’s $42.8M aggregate basis does not reconcile to the ~$64.3M sum of the per-asset tax bases in the Asset Valuation — an unreconciled gap of approximately $21.5M. The Memo provides no reconciliation and does not explain which assets are included or excluded. If the per-asset figures are correct, the deemed-sale gain is materially lower than reported. Combining the corrected ADSP (C-1, ~$425M) with the Asset Valuation basis (~$64.3M) yields a deemed-sale gain of approximately             $360.7M    , versus the Memo’s $430.2M — an overstatement of approximately             $69.5M    . The corresponding corrected per-shareholder gains would be approximately Chandra $187.6M, Stowe $101.0M, and Other $72.1M (versus the Memo’s $223.7M, $120.5M, and $86.0M, respectively).  
          Recommended action.         Reconcile the aggregate adjusted tax basis to the per-asset schedule and confirm whether the Asset Valuation column represents tax basis (as opposed to book carrying value). Re-state the deemed-sale gain and all per-shareholder gain figures once the ADSP (C-1) and basis figures are settled. Until reconciled, the per-shareholder gain figures in the Memo should not be shared with shareholders for tax modeling.  
      
         B. High-Severity Findings  
       H-1. QSBS / Section 1202 Analysis Entirely Omitted Despite Express Commitment  
          Memo position.         The Memo contains no Section 1202 / QSBS analysis. The term         “    QSBS    ”         and the citation         “    Section 1202    ”         do not appear in the body, executive summary, or conclusions of the Memo.  
          Supporting-document position.  
              QSBS Email Thread.         On behalf of several angel-investor shareholders, Linden Rock Advisory formally requested a written QSBS analysis in November 2024, identifying four specific questions (C-to-S conversion effect; interaction with the Section 338(h)(10) deemed asset sale; the $50M qualified-small-business test; and the position of the two California-resident shareholders). CSW responded on November 20, 2024, acknowledging that         “    the interaction between Section 1202 and the proposed Section 338(h)(10) election    ”         is         “    the issue I expect will require the most attention,    ”         and committing:         “    We intend to include a QSBS analysis as part of the transaction structure memorandum we are preparing for Ridgeline… My goal is to have the QSBS section drafted and incorporated into the memorandum well before the purchase agreement is executed.    ”         Linden Rock followed up on January 6, 2025, stating that no analysis had been received and reiterating that it was needed         “    before the purchase agreement is finalized.    ”         The Merger Agreement was signed January 28, 2025; the Memo is dated February 10, 2025 — and still contains no QSBS analysis.  
              Cap Table.         At least eleven shareholders acquired shares during Prism’s 2016 C corporation period (Chandra, Stowe, Greenfield, Muñoz, Yoon, Fielding, Whitford, Okoye, Donnelly, Srinivasan, and Hawkins’ 2016 common tranche), and Prism’s gross assets at formation were well below the $50M Section 1202(d) ceiling. These shares are, on their face, candidates for QSBS treatment.  
              Thornfield DDR (Section VIII.B; Finding #4,                 “        Moderate        ”        ).         Thornfield flags that         “    the interaction of the QSBS rules with S corporation status and the Section 338(h)(10) deemed sale framework has not been analyzed    ”         and that         “    if the Section 338(h)(10) election forecloses QSBS treatment, this may be a material consideration in the election decision for affected shareholders.    ”  
          Nature of the issue.         This is a material omission. Section 1202 by its terms applies to gain on the         “    sale or exchange    ”         of qualified small business stock. The Section 338(h)(10) election recharacterizes the transaction as a deemed asset sale by the S corporation followed by a deemed liquidation — i.e., the shareholders are treated as receiving deemed liquidating distributions, not as selling their stock. CSW’s own November 20 email acknowledged a         “    real question    ”         whether the deemed-asset-sale treatment is         “    in tension with the statutory language of Section 1202.    ”         The potential loss of a 100% federal exclusion is, for several shareholders, a seven-figure swing in after-tax proceeds. The Memo’s silence is inconsistent with CSW’s express commitment and leaves the selling shareholders without the analysis they need to make an informed consent decision. The Memo also does not address California’s non-conformity to the Section 1202 exclusion (raised in the QSBS Email Thread), which affects the two California-resident shareholders.  
          Recommended action.         Prepare and circulate a definitive QSBS analysis addressing (i) whether shares issued during the 2016 C corporation period retained QSBS character through the S corporation years, (ii) whether the Section 338(h)(10) deemed asset sale forecloses the Section 1202 exclusion, (iii) the $50M aggregate-gross-assets qualification, (iv) the escrow component, and (v) the California non-conformance issue. The analysis should be provided to the selling shareholders before they execute the Form 8023 consent, and the Memo should be supplemented with a QSBS section or a companion memorandum.  
        H-2. Class V Software / Developed Technology Understated by $28.5 Million  
          Memo position (Section IV.C).         The Memo’s Class V allocation is $68,500,000, comprised of real property $14.2M, FF&E $6.8M,         “    Software / Developed Technology    ”         $19.0M, and the Beacon 80.5% interest $28.5M.  
          Supporting-document position.         The Asset Valuation identifies three Class V software/technology assets: developed technology / proprietary software platform (A-006) at $41,800,000 (relief-from-royalty method); government-sector software licenses (A-007) at $5,700,000 (income approach); and the NC data processing license (A-008) at $0 (non-transferable). The combined Class V software/technology value is therefore $47.5M, not $19.0M. Thornfield’s total Class V is $97.0M (real property $14.2M + FF&E $6.8M + developed technology $41.8M + government software licenses $5.7M + NC license $0 + Beacon $28.5M).  
          Nature of the issue.         The Memo’s         “    Software / Developed Technology    ”         line of $19.0M understates the Asset Valuation’s $47.5M by $28.5M. The entire Class V variance between the Memo ($68.5M) and Thornfield ($97.0M) is attributable to this single line — the Beacon interest is, in fact, included in both (contrary to Thornfield’s own variance note, which incorrectly states that the Memo         “    excludes the Beacon interest entirely    ”    ). The understatement matters because Class V developed technology is amortizable under Section 167 (the Asset Valuation indicates a 5-year useful life), whereas the residual goodwill into which the $28.5M is effectively pushed is amortizable over 15 years under Section 197. The misallocation therefore both overstates goodwill (by $28.5M, on top of the $48M ADSP error — a combined $76.5M goodwill overstatement) and slows Buyer’s amortization recovery. The Asset Valuation’s own reconciliation note contains an internal error (asserting the Beacon interest is excluded when it is not); that error should not be allowed to obscure the real, $28.5M software-line discrepancy.  
          Recommended action.         Replace the $19.0M         “    Software / Developed Technology    ”         line with the Asset Valuation’s itemized software/technology assets ($41.8M developed technology + $5.7M government software licenses + $0 NC license = $47.5M). Re-state Class V ($97.0M) and Class VII goodwill accordingly. Confirm the Section 167 vs. Section 197 amortization characterization for the developed-technology step-up.  
        H-3. Beacon Insights — Section 751         “    Hot Assets    ”         and Section 754 Election Omitted  
          Memo position (Section VIII).         The Memo states that the gain on the deemed disposition of Prism’s 80.5% Beacon interest         “    is expected to be long-term capital gain, given that Prism has held its membership interest in Beacon for more than one year.    ”         The Memo does not address Section 751, does not perform a look-through analysis of Beacon’s underlying assets, and does not address a Section 754 election.  
          Supporting-document position.  
              Thornfield DDR (Section VI.B).         Beacon’s underlying assets include accounts receivable of approximately $2.1M and accrued-but-unbilled revenue of approximately $1.8M as of December 31, 2023. Thornfield expressly notes that         “    the nature of the accounts receivable and unbilled revenue items should be analyzed under Section 751 for         ‘    hot asset    ’         characterization in connection with any transfer or deemed transfer of Prism’s partnership interest.    ”  
              Asset Valuation (Note 2).         “    The amortization and depreciation treatment of the step-up allocated to this interest will depend on the nature of Beacon’s underlying assets and whether a §754 election is made by Beacon. Buyer’s counsel should determine whether a §754 election is advisable and, if so, prepare the §743(b) basis adjustment computations for Beacon.    ”  
              Beacon OA (Section 11.05).         “    As of the date of this Agreement, the Company has not made an election under Section 754 of the Code.    ”  
          Nature of the issue.         Two distinct issues are omitted. First, under Section 751, a partnership interest’s         “    hot assets    ”         (unrealized receivables and substantially appreciated inventory items) produce ordinary income — not capital gain — on the sale or exchange of the interest. Beacon holds receivables and unbilled revenue that are likely hot assets; to that extent, the Memo’s blanket         “    long-term capital gain    ”         characterization is likely incorrect, and a portion of the $22.1M deemed-disposition gain should be ordinary income passing through to Prism’s shareholders. Second, because the Beacon interest is treated as a single Class V asset in the deemed asset sale, the step-up in Buyer’s basis inside Beacon depends on whether Beacon makes a Section 754 election (producing a Section 743(b) special basis adjustment). No Section 754 election is currently in effect. Without it, Buyer may not obtain the inside basis step-up that the Memo’s amortization analysis implicitly assumes. Both the character of the shareholders’ gain and the magnitude of Buyer’s future Beacon-level deductions are at stake.  
          Recommended action.         (i) Perform a Section 751 hot-asset analysis on Beacon’s receivables and unbilled revenue and re-characterize the corresponding portion of the deemed-disposition gain as ordinary income. (ii) Analyze whether a Beacon Section 754 election is advisable and, if so, prepare the Section 743(b) basis adjustment computations. (iii) Revise the Memo’s gain-character and Buyer-basis discussions accordingly.  
        H-4. Escrow Tax Treatment — Memo Contradicts the Merger Agreement  
          Memo position (Section V.C).         The Memo states that the $22M escrow         “    constitutes contingent consideration    ”         and that the selling shareholders         “    may elect installment sale treatment with respect to the escrow amount, deferring recognition of a proportionate share of gain until the escrow is released    ”         (i.e., until September 15, 2026).  
          Supporting-document position.         Merger Agreement Section 7.4 (    “    Tax Treatment    ”    ) provides:         “    The parties intend that the Escrow Amount be reported as an amount realized by the Shareholders in the taxable year in which the Closing occurs, consistent with the treatment of the Escrow Amount as a portion of the Cash Consideration deposited with a third-party agent for the benefit of the Shareholders.    ”         The Agreement further provides that each shareholder is         “    solely responsible for determining the proper treatment of the Escrow Amount on such Shareholder’s own Tax returns.    ”  
          Nature of the issue.         The Memo’s recommendation that shareholders defer escrow gain via Section 453 installment treatment directly contradicts the negotiated position in the Merger Agreement, which specifies current-year recognition of the escrow as amount realized at closing. A shareholder who follows the Memo’s advice would take a return position inconsistent with the Agreement’s stated intent and with the position the parties expect to report. The Memo also does not grapple with the fact that, under a Section 338(h)(10) election, the deemed asset sale and deemed liquidation occur at the close of the acquisition date — the escrow is part of the consideration for the deemed sale/liquidation, and the availability of installment treatment in a deemed-liquidation context is more constrained than the Memo’s casual treatment suggests. Finally, the Memo does not address the tax treatment of the escrow earnings, which Merger Agreement Section 7.4 allocates to the shareholders.  
          Recommended action.         Revise Section V.C to align with Merger Agreement Section 7.4 (current-year recognition as amount realized), or, if deferral is genuinely available and desired, flag the inconsistency with the Agreement and coordinate an amendment to Section 7.4 before shareholders rely on the Memo. Address the tax treatment of escrow earnings.  
      
         C. Moderate-Severity Findings  
       M-1. Beacon ROFR, Change-of-Control, and IP-License Auto-Termination Inadequately Addressed  
          Memo position (Section VIII).         The Memo notes that         “    the Beacon operating agreement contains customary transfer restrictions, including a right of first refusal    ”         and that         “    the parties and their counsel are addressing these transfer provisions in the context of the overall transaction documentation and pre-closing conditions.    ”         The deemed-transfer and IP-license-termination consequences are not analyzed.  
          Supporting-document position.  
              Beacon OA Section 8.03.         A right of first refusal is triggered not only on a transfer of a membership interest but also on a         “    Change of Control of any Member that is an entity.    ”         The merger constitutes a Change of Control of Prism (an entity member). Prism must deliver a ROFR Notice within 10 business days; the non-transferring members (Marcus Delano, 12.0%, and Shirin Avesta, 7.5%) then have 45 days to elect to purchase all of Prism’s 80.5% interest at Fair Market Value (determined under Section 8.04, without discount for lack of control or marketability).  
              Beacon OA Section 9.02.         The IP License Agreement         “    shall terminate automatically upon a Transfer of Prism’s Membership Interest in the Company (including a deemed Transfer resulting from a Change of Control of Prism), unless the parties agree otherwise in writing.    ”         Beacon’s business relies on IP licensed from Prism.  
              Merger Agreement Section 8.2(g).         “    Any required consents… under the operating agreement of Beacon Insights, LLC    ”         is a closing condition — but a ROFR is a purchase right, not a consent, and its exercise could result in Delano and Avesta acquiring Prism’s interest rather than the interest transferring to Buyer.  
          Nature of the issue.         The Memo materially understates the Beacon complications. (i) If the ROFR is exercised, Prism would sell its 80.5% Beacon interest to Delano and Avesta at Fair Market Value — the interest would not transfer to Buyer, and Prism would recognize sale gain (separate from the 338(h)(10) deemed sale), disrupting the structure and the allocation. (ii) The IP License Agreement auto-terminates on a         “    deemed Transfer resulting from a Change of Control of Prism    ”         unless the parties agree otherwise in writing; if it terminates, Beacon’s service-delivery platform (and ~$11M of annual revenue) is impaired, which in turn affects the $28.5M Beacon valuation and the overall enterprise value. (iii) The Memo does not address whether the Section 338(h)(10) deemed asset sale itself constitutes a         “    deemed Transfer    ”         that triggers both the ROFR and the IP-license termination. The Merger Agreement’s listing of         “    consents    ”         as a closing condition does not capture the ROFR purchase right.  
          Recommended action.         Obtain ROFR waivers or compliant ROFR Notices from Delano and Avesta as a condition to closing; analyze the tax consequences if the ROFR is exercised; negotiate a written agreement to preserve the IP License Agreement (or a replacement license) post-closing; and analyze whether the 338(h)(10) deemed sale triggers the         “    deemed Transfer    ”         provisions.         M-4. Suspended R&D Credits ($3.8M) — Inadequate Treatment  
          Memo position (Section III.D).         The Memo notes the $3.8M in unused federal R&D credits and states that they         “    will be addressed in the post-closing tax planning.    ”

Thornfield DDR (Sections IV.B and IX.A; Finding #2,         “    Moderate    ”    ) provides a detailed analysis concluding that the credits are         “    at significant risk of permanent expiration in connection with this transaction    ”         because (i) no Section 1374 built-in-gains tax applies to offset them, (ii) the post-termination transition period mechanism for suspended credits (as opposed to deduction carryforwards) is not clearly established, and (iii) whether the deemed liquidation triggers a final C corporation return that could use the credits is an open question of law. Thornfield recommends that tax counsel analyze whether any structuring alternative could preserve the credits.  
          Nature of the issue.         The Memo’s one-line deferral to         “    post-closing tax planning    ”         is inadequate for a $3.8M attribute that may be permanently lost upon the deemed liquidation. The interaction of Sections 1371(b), 1371(e), 1374, and 338(h)(10) is precisely the kind of analysis the Memo should perform, and the risk of permanent expiration should be disclosed to the parties before the election is made.

Analyze whether any portion of the $3.8M can be salvaged (including via the post-termination transition period or a final-return mechanism) and whether any structuring modification would preserve value.         M-5. Non-Compete Agreements — Compensation vs. Purchase Price Not Analyzed  
          Memo position (Section IV.C).         The Memo allocates $15,000,000 to non-compete agreements (Chandra $10.5M; Stowe $4.5M) as Class VI Section 197 intangibles, amortizable by Buyer over 15 years, and treats the corresponding gain as part of the deemed-sale gain passing through to shareholders.  
          Supporting-document position.         Asset Valuation (A-013) values the non-competes at $15M and notes they arise from         “    Dr. Naveen Chandra: 4-year employment agreement with non-compete. Rachel Stowe: 2-year consulting agreement with non-compete.    ”         The Merger Agreement (Sections 8.2(e) and 8.2(f)) makes the Chandra employment agreement and the Stowe consulting agreement closing conditions.  
          Nature of the issue.         The non-compete covenants are being entered into at closing (as part of Chandra’s new employment agreement and Stowe’s new consulting agreement), not pre-existing assets of old Prism being sold. Payments for covenants not to compete entered into in connection with an acquisition are a classic tax-character issue: if the payments are in substance additional compensation to the executives, they are ordinary income to Chandra and Stowe (and deductible by Buyer as compensation, subject to Section 162(a)(1) reasonableness) rather than capital gain to the shareholders and a Section 197 intangible amortizable by Buyer. The Memo treats the $15M as a purchased intangible without analyzing the compensation alternative. The character (capital vs. ordinary) and the deductibility/amortization profile both turn on this analysis.  
          Recommended action.         Analyze whether the $15M non-compete value represents purchased intangibles (capital gain to shareholders; Section 197 amortization to Buyer) or compensation (ordinary income to Chandra/Stowe; Section 162 deduction to Buyer). Document the conclusion and align the PPA and the executive agreements accordingly.  
        M-6. Post-Closing Check-the-Box Recommendation — Taxable-Liquidation Consequences Not Analyzed  
          Memo position (Section VII.B).         The Memo recommends that         “    Buyer consider converting Prism from a C corporation to a disregarded entity or partnership for federal income tax purposes following the closing, by making a check-the-box election under Treas. Reg. 1.301-7701-3,    ”         so that Prism’s income and stepped-up amortization deductions flow through to Fund III.  
          Nature of the issue.         A check-the-box election to change a corporation’s classification to a disregarded entity or partnership is treated as a deemed liquidation of the corporation (under the entity-classification regulations), which can trigger corporate-level gain under Sections 331 and 336 on the distribution of appreciated assets — including the very assets whose basis was just stepped up in the 338(h)(10) deemed sale. The Memo makes the recommendation without analyzing this tax cost, which could be substantial and could undermine the intended flow-through benefit. The recommendation also interacts with Dr. Chandra’s rollover into         “    Parent (or a designated acquisition vehicle)    ”         (Merger Agreement Section 2.4(b)–(e)); if the acquisition vehicle is a partnership, Section 721 contribution mechanics may be relevant (see L-7).  
          Recommended action.         Before any post-closing conversion, model the deemed-liquidation tax cost of the check-the-box election and compare it to the flow-through benefit. Revise the Memo to present the recommendation as conditional on that modeling rather than as an unqualified suggestion.

Ensure that the consent-collection process covers all 19 shareholders (or whatever the reconciled number is). Correct the Memo’s Section II.A count and residency breakdown.  
        M-8. AAA / AE&P / PTI Characterization of the Deemed Liquidation Omitted  
          Memo position (Sections V and VIII).         The Memo treats the deemed-sale gain as passing through to shareholders in proportion to stock ownership and does not address the Accumulated Adjustments Account (AAA), Accumulated Earnings and Profits (AE&P), or Previously Taxed Income (PTI) in characterizing the deemed liquidating distributions.  
          Supporting-document position.         Thornfield DDR (Section IX.C) reports, as of December 31, 2023: AAA $64,200,000; OAA $0; PTI $792,000; AE&P $792,000. Thornfield notes that in the deemed liquidation, distributions are characterized first against AAA (tax-free to the extent of stock basis), then against AE&P (taxable as a dividend), then as return of capital, then as capital gain.  
          Nature of the issue.         The deemed liquidation following the 338(h)(10) deemed sale requires characterization of the deemed liquidating distributions against AAA, AE&P, and stock basis. The $792,000 of AE&P could produce dividend income (taxable at ordinary dividend rates) to the extent not absorbed by AAA, and the AAA balance affects the timing and character of the shareholders’ recognition. The Memo’s per-shareholder gain figures, while approximately capturing the pass-through deemed-sale gain, do not reflect this characterization layer. This is a technical gap in the deemed-liquidation analysis.  
          Recommended action.         Add a deemed-liquidation characterization analysis (AAA → AE&P → return of capital → capital gain) to the Memo, updated for the final-year income allocation’s effect on AAA, and confirm the dividend potential from the $792,000 AE&P.

Recommended action:         confirm the California treatment of the deemed-sale gain at the entity vs. shareholder level and refine the California tax estimate.

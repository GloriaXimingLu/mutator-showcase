               This memorandum accompanies the draft Motion to Dismiss and Proposed Order transmitted herewith. It flags threshold issues that the team must resolve before filing. The most significant—a likely defect in the Court's subject-matter jurisdiction—is addressed first and requires a strategic decision before the January 15, 2024 dispositive-motion deadline.  
              1. Subject-Matter Jurisdiction — A Likely Complete-Diversity Defect (CRITICAL)  
               Our own Notice of Removal asserts diversity jurisdiction on the theory that Arcadia is a citizen of Texas only, based solely on the domicile of its Managing Member, Dr. Okonkwo. That analysis is almost certainly wrong and, if so, the Court lacks subject-matter jurisdiction over the entire action.  
               An LLC's citizenship is determined by the citizenship of all of its members. Harvey v. Grey Wolf Drilling Co., 542 F.3d 1077, 1080 (5th Cir. 2008). A limited partnership's citizenship is determined by the citizenship of all of its partners, general and limited. Carden v. Arkoma Assocs., 494 U.S. 185, 195-96 (1990). Applying these rules to the Operating Agreement produced in initial disclosures (Exhibit 7):  
               (a) Dr. Rachel Okonkwo — Texas citizen (FAC ¶ 7; Operating Agreement, Ex. A, Note 1).  
               (b) Martin Schreiber — Texas citizen (FAC ¶ 7; Operating Agreement, Ex. A, Note 2).  
               (c) Apex Medical Ventures, LP — a Delaware limited partnership (FAC ¶ 7; Operating Agreement, Ex. A, Note 3). Apex's partners are: (i) Apex Medical Ventures GP, Inc., a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Wilmington, Delaware — a citizen of Delaware; and (ii) the Okonkwo Family Trust — a citizen of Texas (the trustee and beneficiary, Dr. Okonkwo, is domiciled in Texas). Apex is therefore a citizen of Delaware and Texas.  
               Accordingly, Arcadia is a citizen of Texas and Delaware. Meridian is a citizen of Delaware (incorporation) and Texas (nerve center in Austin). Hertz Corp. v. Friend, 559 U.S. 77, 92-93 (2010). Both parties share citizenship in Delaware and Texas. Complete diversity does not exist. Strawbridge v. Curtiss, 7 U.S. (3 Cranch) 267 (1806). The amount in controversy is satisfied, but the diversity requirement is not.  
               Subject-matter jurisdiction cannot be waived and may be raised by the Court sua sponte at any time. Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U.S. 500, 514 (2006). Indeed, Judge Alvarez's Standing Order and the Federal Rules require the Court to assure itself of jurisdiction. This creates real risk: the Court could raise the defect on its own initiative—potentially after we have briefed and argued the motion to dismiss—and remand the case to state court. Arcadia could also raise it (it filed in state court originally and may prefer to return).  
               Two additional notes. First, there is a discrepancy in the record: the FAC (¶ 8) describes the Okonkwo Family Trust as "irrevocable," while the Operating Agreement (Ex. A, Note 3(b)) describes it as a "revocable inter vivos trust." The distinction does not change the outcome here—under either characterization the trust is a Texas citizen through Dr. Okonkwo—but it does show the FAC's citizenship allegations are not reliable, and we should not rely on them. Second, the diversity-destroying citizenship is Apex Medical Ventures GP, Inc. (Delaware); there is no way around it on the present record.  
               Recommendation.            This is a threshold strategic decision that must be made before filing. Because diversity is absent and there is no federal question, the case cannot remain in federal court on the current jurisdictional basis; it will ultimately be remanded. The question is whether Meridian prefers federal or state court and who raises the defect. Federal court has favored Meridian so far—Judge Alvarez's rigorous pleading standards, the enforced jury-trial waiver (§ 12.9, bench trial), and the Austin forum. But we cannot manufacture jurisdiction. Options: (i) file the motion to dismiss by January 15 to preserve the arguments and let the jurisdictional issue surface on its own (risk: sua sponte remand after we invest in briefing); (ii) proactively move to remand (controls timing and narrative, but concedes the federal forum); or (iii) file the motion and simultaneously alert the Court to the defect. My recommendation is option (i) for now—file the motion to preserve our arguments (which are equally usable in state court), while we obtain client input on whether to raise remand proactively. Note that on remand the § 12.8 forum-selection clause (Austin, Travis County) and the § 12.9 jury waiver remain enforceable in state court, so Meridian retains meaningful protections either way. We should confirm the Apex partnership agreement to lock down the analysis before any jurisdictional filing.  
              2. Post-Answer Motion — Rule 12(b)(6) vs. Rule 12(c)  
               There is a minor tension between the Court's Standing Order and the Scheduling Order. Standing Order § 3.4 provides that a post-answer motion to dismiss is treated as a Rule 12(b)(6) motion (not Rule 12(c)) where the movant preserved the defense in its answer. The Scheduling Order states the Court "will treat any such motion as a Rule 12(c) motion ... evaluated under the same standard as a Rule 12(b)(6) motion." The standard is identical in either case, so the distinction is largely cosmetic. We preserved the Rule 12(b)(6) defense in the Answer (First Affirmative Defense; Part III) and met the Standing Order's conditions, so I have titled and framed the motion as a Rule 12(b)(6) motion. If the Court prefers, it can treat it as Rule 12(c) without affecting the analysis. The deadline is January 15, 2024; the page limit is 25 pages (the draft is within limit).  
              3. Choice of Law — Delaware vs. Texas for the Tort Claims  
               Section 12.7 selects Delaware law for "this Agreement." The breach-of-contract claim (Count I) is governed by Delaware law. The scope of § 12.7 as to the tort claims (Counts II and III) is debatable: a narrow reading covers only claims "under" the agreement, while a broad reading covers all claims "arising out of or relating to" the contractual relationship. Under Texas's most-significant-relationship test, the tort claims likely are governed by Texas law (the misrepresentations were made and relied upon in Texas, and the injury was suffered in Texas). The motion therefore argues the tort claims fail under both Delaware law (broader economic loss doctrine, anti-reliance doctrine, strict integration-clause enforcement) and Texas law (duty-focused economic loss rule, puffery, no justifiable reliance). This belt-and-suspenders approach protects us regardless of how the Court resolves the choice-of-law question. The DTPA (Count V) and unjust enrichment (Count IV) are governed by Texas law regardless.  
              4. DTPA § 17.49(f) — The $25 Million Asset-Threshold Dispute  
               Our strongest DTPA argument is the § 17.49(f) exemption: total consideration of $17.05 million exceeds $500,000, and Arcadia is an LLC, not an individual. Both elements are established on the face of the FAC, and Arcadia concedes the consideration element. The motion leads with this ground.  
               However, Arcadia's July 25, 2023 letter asserts that § 17.49(f) is conjunctive—requiring both the >$500,000 consideration and the consumer's assets of $25 million or more—and claims Arcadia's total assets were $23.8 million as of December 31, 2022, below the $25 million threshold. If the Court adopts Arcadia's reading and credits the $23.8 million figure, the exemption would not apply and Count V could survive the motion on this ground alone (the asset question is factual and not resolved by the FAC). Our research memo reads § 17.49(f) as requiring only >$500,000 consideration plus a non-individual consumer, with no asset prong; on that reading the exemption is dispositive on the pleadings. The motion adopts the latter construction and notes that Arcadia's asset figure appears only in pre-suit correspondence, not in the FAC.  
               Recommendation.            Confirm the precise current text of § 17.49(f) and any companion asset-based exemption before the response brief is due. If an asset prong is implicated, consider whether Arcadia's audited financials can be judicially noticed or whether we should develop the asset issue through a targeted record. Importantly, Count V has independent, non-exemption grounds for dismissal (puffery, Rule 9(b), and lack of producing cause), so even if the exemption argument fails, the DTPA claim should not survive.  
              5. DTPA Pre-Suit Notice — The 60-Day Waiting Period Was Not Satisfied  
               Section 17.505(a) requires a consumer to give written notice "at least 60 days before filing" a DTPA action. Arcadia's pre-suit demand is dated June 15, 2023 (our July 10, 2023 response references a June 26, 2023 letter; either date controls). The original petition was filed August 7, 2023—only 53 days after June 15 (or 42 days after June 26). Under either date, Arcadia filed before the 60-day period expired. This is a failure of a statutory condition precedent. The motion does not lean heavily on this point because the likely remedy for noncompliance is abatement of the DTPA claim rather than dismissal, but it is an additional threshold defect worth preserving. We should be prepared to press it in the reply or at oral argument if the Court reaches the DTPA merits.  
              6. Jury-Trial Waiver (§ 12.9)  
               The Scheduling Order recites that the parties waived their right to a jury trial pursuant to § 12.9 and that the case will be tried to the bench. The FAC nonetheless demands a jury (FAC ¶ 122), and our Answer preserved a jury demand "to the extent that the Court determines the jury trial waiver ... is not enforceable." This is not directly relevant to the motion to dismiss, but it bears on the overall case strategy and on the forum decision in Item 1 (the bench trial is a meaningful advantage in a technical commercial dispute). No action is needed for the motion, but we should be ready to enforce the waiver if the case proceeds.  
              7. Count I (Breach of Contract) — Dismissal vs. Damages Limitation  
               Full dismissal of the breach-of-contract claim is the most aggressive posture. The deemed-acceptance provision (§ 5.3), the expired limited warranty (§ 9.1), and the exclusive-remedy framework (§§ 8.3, 9.1(b)) support dismissal, and the motion so argues. But courts are sometimes reluctant to dismiss a breach-of-contract claim entirely at the pleading stage where, as here, the plaintiff alleges non-delivery of conforming software. The motion therefore argues in the alternative that, if Count I survives, Arcadia's damages must be limited to the § 8.1 liability cap and the exclusive remedies, with all consequential damages (lost profits, operating costs, reputational harm) barred by § 8.2. This alternative alone strips roughly $30 million from Arcadia's demand. We should be prepared for the Court to grant the alternative (damages limitation) on Count I while dismissing the tort and statutory counts outright.  
              8. Documents the Court May Consider  
               The motion relies on the MSLSA, SOW-1, and the four Change Orders. Under Fifth Circuit law, the Court may consider documents "referred to in the plaintiff's complaint and central to the plaintiff's claim" without converting the motion into one for summary judgment. Lormand, 565 F.3d at 255-57. The FAC repeatedly references these documents (FAC ¶¶ 33-35, 46-47, 69, 99-100) and they are central to every count. I have not physically attached them to the motion to avoid bulk, but we can attach them as an appendix if the Court prefers. The Schreiber due-diligence memo, the negotiation emails, and the Okonkwo email are not attached to or referenced in the FAC, so they are outside the pleadings and should not be offered on this motion (they will be powerful at summary judgment). The motion references them only as background where they are reflected in the Answer's averments.

               Standing Order § 3.1 requires a certificate of conference, filed as a separate document concurrently with the motion. I have included a certificate in the draft motion reflecting a January 8, 2024 telephone conference with Breckenridge. Please confirm the actual conference date and substance before filing, and ensure the certificate is docketed as a separate entry (not merely embedded in the motion).  
              10. Bottom Line  
               The motion presents strong, largely dispositive grounds for dismissing all five counts: the § 17.49(f) DTPA exemption (Count V); the express-contract bar on unjust enrichment (Count IV); Rule 9(b) failure, the economic loss doctrine, puffery, the integration/anti-reliance doctrine, and the absence of justifiable reliance (Counts II and III); and deemed acceptance, warranty expiration, exclusive remedies, and the consequential-damages waiver and liability cap (Count I). The single most important threshold issue is the likely diversity defect, which could moot the motion and require remand. I recommend we (a) finalize and file the motion by January 15 to preserve our arguments, (b) obtain a client decision on whether to raise the jurisdictional defect proactively, and (c) confirm the § 17.49(f) statutory text before the response is due.               I. INTRODUCTION  
               This is a breach-of-contract case dressed up as fraud. Plaintiff Arcadia Health Systems, LLC ("Arcadia") is a sophisticated commercial enterprise with approximately $62 million in annual revenue that negotiated and executed a comprehensive $14.7 million Master Software License and Services Agreement (the "MSLSA") with Defendant Meridian Cloud Solutions, Inc. ("Meridian") after months of due diligence, a two-week proof-of-concept trial, and arm's-length negotiations conducted through experienced counsel on both sides. Having received the software it licensed and accepted it under the contract's deemed-acceptance provision, Arcadia now seeks to escape the bargain it struck by recasting ordinary commercial disappointment as fraud, negligent misrepresentation, unjust enrichment, and statutory consumer-protection violations—and by demanding $47.3 million in damages that the MSLSA expressly excludes.  
               The First Amended Complaint (Dkt. 12) (the "FAC") fails as a matter of law for multiple independent reasons. Count V (DTPA) is barred by the statutory exemption in Texas Business & Commerce Code § 17.49(f), which categorically excludes transactions involving more than $500,000 in total consideration where the consumer is not an individual. Count IV (unjust enrichment) is unavailable because an express, enforceable contract governs the same subject matter. Counts II and III (fraud and negligent misrepresentation) are barred by the economic loss doctrine, the MSLSA's integration clause and anti-reliance provisions, the puffery doctrine, the absence of justifiable reliance, and Arcadia's failure to satisfy Rule 9(b). Count I (breach of contract) cannot survive the MSLSA's deemed-acceptance provision, the expired limited warranty, and the exclusive-remedy framework—and, in any event, the damages Arcadia seeks are contractually barred.  
               Meridian preserved each of these defenses in its Answer (Dkt. 16) and respectfully requests that the Court dismiss the FAC in its entirety with prejudice pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6).  
              II. STATEMENT OF FACTS  
              A. The Parties Are Sophisticated Commercial Entities That Negotiated a Comprehensive Agreement Through Counsel.  
               Arcadia is a Texas limited liability company with approximately $62 million in annual revenue that operates electronic health record integration and analytics platforms for 23 hospitals and over 140 clinics. FAC ¶¶ 2, 6. Meridian is a publicly traded Delaware corporation with approximately $380 million in annual revenue. FAC ¶ 9. On March 15, 2022, after a multi-month sales and evaluation process, the parties executed the MSLSA and Statement of Work #1 ("SOW-1") for the deployment of Meridian's NexusCore Healthcare Analytics Module. FAC ¶¶ 33, 35. The MSLSA is a comprehensive, 12-article agreement that the parties negotiated at arm's length through experienced counsel—Arcadia through Wexford Hale LLP and Meridian through Stonebridge & Calloway LLP. FAC ¶ 32.  
               The negotiation record is unequivocal. Arcadia's counsel proposed, and the parties discussed, the very provisions that Arcadia now seeks to evade. Arcadia specifically sought to delete the consequential-damages waiver and to add a "fraud carve-out" to the integration clause, and Meridian declined both. Arcadia then expressly accepted the consequential-damages waiver "as drafted" and accepted the integration clause "without a fraud carve-out," telling Meridian that Arcadia was "entering this agreement with eyes wide open regarding the risk allocation framework" and that "[t]he consequential damages waiver, the aggregate liability cap, the warranty limitations, and the deemed acceptance mechanism were all carefully considered by Arcadia's leadership and legal team." Arcadia's counsel further memorialized that Arcadia "understands that in the event of a breach by Meridian, its recovery would be limited to direct damages not exceeding the fees paid or payable during the 12-month period preceding the event giving rise to liability, and that claims for lost profits, business interruption, reputational harm, and similar consequential damages would be waived."  
              B. Arcadia Conducted Extensive Independent Due Diligence Before Contracting.  
               Arcadia's CTO, Martin Schreiber, conducted a thorough independent technical and operational due-diligence review before the MSLSA was executed. He performed reference calls with two existing Meridian healthcare clients—San Marcos Regional Health and Coastal Bend Medical Group—and ran a two-week proof-of-concept trial in Arcadia's own San Antonio test environment. In his January 19, 2022 internal memorandum, Schreiber concluded that "NexusCore appears well-suited to our needs" and that "[i]ntegration complexity is manageable but will require a skilled SI partner." He specifically identified multi-EHR integration complexity and data-migration scale as the principal risks, recommended that Arcadia "engage an experienced, independent systems integration firm to manage the data migration workstream," and recommended that Arcadia "engage outside counsel" to negotiate limitation-of-liability, warranty, and acceptance-testing provisions. Both reference clients cautioned that data migration was the most challenging aspect of their deployments and that integration timelines in multi-EHR environments would be longer.  
               Arcadia's counsel expressly relied on this diligence in withdrawing the fraud-carve-out request, telling Meridian that "Martin Schreiber's independent due diligence—including reference calls ... as well as the 2-week proof-of-concept trial—provided Arcadia with an independent basis for its evaluation of NexusCore apart from any representations made during the sales process."  
              C. The MSLSA Allocates Risk and Provides Exclusive Remedies.  
               The MSLSA reflects a deliberate allocation of risk between sophisticated parties. Section 8.1 caps each party's aggregate liability at the total fees paid or payable during the twelve months preceding the event giving rise to liability. Section 8.2 excludes all consequential, incidental, special, punitive, and exemplary damages—including lost profits, goodwill, and reputational harm—"regardless of the form of action, whether in contract, tort (including negligence), strict liability, or otherwise." Section 8.3 designates the MSLSA's remedies as Arcadia's "sole and exclusive remedies." Section 8.5 confirms that these limitations "represent a reasonable allocation of risk" and "form an essential basis of the bargain," and that they apply "even if any limited or exclusive remedy ... is found to have failed of its essential purpose."  
               Section 9.1 provides a limited 90-day warranty that the software will "perform substantially in accordance with the Documentation," with repair or replacement as the sole remedy and a pro-rata refund as the backstop if Meridian cannot cure within 60 days. Section 9.4 disclaims all other warranties, including implied warranties of merchantability and fitness for a particular purpose, and states that the software is provided "AS IS" and "AS AVAILABLE." Section 12.1 is a comprehensive integration clause providing that the MSLSA "constitutes the entire agreement" and "supersedes all prior and contemporaneous agreements, proposals, representations, and understandings, whether oral or written," including "marketing materials, product descriptions, brochures, presentations, and demonstrations," and that neither party relied on any extra-contractual statement. Section 12.7 designates Delaware law. Section 12.3 bars oral modifications.  
              D. Arcadia Was Responsible for Data Migration; Its Own Delays and Its Integrator's Errors Caused the Implementation Difficulties.  
               SOW-1 Section 3.2 and Exhibit C, Section C.2 expressly provide that Arcadia "shall be solely responsible for all data migration activities, including extraction, transformation, and loading of legacy data," and that Meridian's role was limited to advisory consulting with "no responsibility for the accuracy, completeness, or integrity of migrated data." Arcadia independently retained Linden Park Consulting, LLC ("Linden Park") to perform the data migration. The 137-day delay in achieving Go-Live (FAC ¶ 45) was attributable to Arcadia's own failures and those of its integrator: (1) a 45-day gap in Arcadia's designation of a Project Manager after its project manager departed on April 28, 2022; (2) Arcadia's 75-day delay in delivering complete API specifications (not delivered until July 20, 2022, against a May 6, 2022 milestone); and (3) critical errors in Linden Park's data-migration scripts. Section 3.5 of the MSLSA provides for day-for-day schedule adjustments for Licensee-caused delays. The four Change Orders Arcadia executed (CO-001 through CO-004, totaling $2.35 million) were mutually signed under Section 5.4; CO-004 ($900,000) specifically remediated errors in Linden Park's data migration.  
              E. Arcadia Deemed-Accepted the Software and First Complained After the Acceptance and Warranty Windows Closed.  
               NexusCore achieved Go-Live on January 15, 2023. FAC ¶ 45. Under Section 5.3, Arcadia had 30 days to conduct acceptance testing and deliver written notice of any material nonconformity; the acceptance window closed on February 14, 2023. Arcadia delivered no such written notice. Acceptance was therefore deemed granted as of February 14, 2023. Arcadia's first written communication about performance—Dr. Okonkwo's March 8, 2023 email to Meridian's Vice President of Customer Success—was sent 22 days after the acceptance window closed. That email, which referenced only "intermittent latency issues" and "occasional report generation errors," thanked Meridian for getting Arcadia "through Go-Live," stated that "[n]either of these issues has caused any patient-safety concerns," and asked how to "get tickets opened." It was a routine support request, not a contractual Nonconformity Notice. The 90-day limited warranty under Section 9.1 expired on April 15, 2023. Of the 47 support tickets Arcadia submitted between March and July 2023, 31 were resolved within applicable SLA timeframes, 12 traced to data-quality issues caused by the Linden Park migration, and 4 involved software defects that Meridian patched through updates released on May 2 and June 19, 2023.  
              III. LEGAL STANDARD  
               To survive a motion to dismiss, a complaint must contain sufficient factual matter to "state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face." Bell Atl. Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544, 570 (2007); Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662, 678 (2009). "Threadbare recitals of the elements of a cause of action, supported by mere conclusory statements, do not suffice." Iqbal, 556 U.S. at 678. The Court must "accept all well-pleaded facts as true, viewing them in the light most favorable to the plaintiff," but need not credit legal conclusions couched as factual allegations. Lormand v. US Unwired, Inc., 565 F.3d 228, 255-57 (5th Cir. 2009).  
               Although Meridian has answered, the Court's Standing Order § 3.4 permits a post-answer motion to dismiss where the movant preserved the Rule 12(b)(6) defense in its answer, and the Court applies the same standard as a pre-answer motion. Meridian preserved its Rule 12(b)(6) defense in its Answer (Dkt. 16) (First Affirmative Defense; Part III (Affirmative Defenses)), and the specific grounds preserved there and asserted here are: (1) failure to state a claim upon which relief may be granted; (2) the economic loss doctrine; (3) the DTPA statutory exemption under § 17.49(f); (4) the MSLSA's integration clause and anti-reliance provisions and the absence of justifiable reliance; (5) the limitation-of-liability and consequential-damages waiver (§§ 8.1, 8.2); (6) the deemed-acceptance provision (§ 5.3); (7) the limited warranty and exclusive-remedy framework (§§ 9.1, 8.3); (8) the puffery doctrine; (9) failure to satisfy Rule 9(b); (10) the bar on unjust enrichment where an express contract governs; (11) the absence of an independent duty supporting negligent misrepresentation; and (12) the absence of producing cause under the DTPA. No party will be prejudiced by the Court's consideration of this motion as a Rule 12(b)(6) motion, and the motion is filed within the dispositive-motion deadline of January 15, 2024. To the extent the Court treats this motion as one for judgment on the pleadings under Rule 12(c), the standard is identical. The Court may consider the MSLSA, SOW-1, and the Change Orders without converting this motion into one for summary judgment, because those documents are referenced in the FAC and are central to Arcadia's claims. Lormand, 565 F.3d at 255-57; see also FAC ¶¶ 33-35, 46-47, 69, 99-100.  
               Claims sounding in fraud must also satisfy the heightened pleading requirements of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 9(b), which requires a plaintiff to "specify the statements contended to be fraudulent, identify the speaker, state when and where the statements were made, and explain why the statements were fraudulent." Benchmark Elecs., Inc. v. J.M. Huber Corp., 343 F.3d 719, 724 (5th Cir. 2003). Rule 9(b) applies to all claims whose gravamen is fraudulent or deceptive conduct, including negligent misrepresentation and DTPA claims premised on the same alleged misrepresentations. Id.  
               For the convenience of the Court, and consistent with Standing Order § 1.4, Meridian notes the binding or persuasive status of the authorities cited. Decisions of the United States Supreme Court and the Fifth Circuit are binding on the federal procedural and pleading standards governing this motion. Because Section 12.7 of the MSLSA selects Delaware law, decisions of the Delaware Supreme Court and the Delaware Court of Chancery are binding on the contract claim (Count I) and on the tort claims (Counts II and III) to the extent Delaware law governs them. Decisions of the Texas Supreme Court and Texas Courts of Appeals are binding on the Texas-law claims—the DTPA (Count V) and unjust enrichment (Count IV)—and are cited as persuasive authority in the alternative Texas-law analysis of the tort claims. Out-of-circuit federal authority (e.g., the Third Circuit) is cited only as persuasive support. Where an opinion is unpublished, that status is indicated.  
              IV. ARGUMENT  
              A. Count V (DTPA) Must Be Dismissed Because the Transaction Is Statutorily Exempt Under § 17.49(f).  
               Count V must be dismissed because the transaction falls squarely within the statutory exemption in Texas Business & Commerce Code § 17.49(f), which provides that the DTPA "does not apply to a cause of action arising from a transaction, a project, or a set of transactions relating to the same project, involving total consideration by the consumer of more than $500,000, unless the consumer is an individual." Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 17.49(f). Both requirements are satisfied on the face of the FAC and are beyond genuine dispute.  
               First, the total consideration vastly exceeds $500,000. The MSLSA alone had a total contract value of $14,700,000. FAC ¶ 34. The four Change Orders added $2,350,000, for total consideration of $17,050,000. FAC ¶¶ 46-47. The § 17.49(f) exemption is determined by the total consideration involved in the transaction, not the amount of damages claimed, and is measured at the time of the transaction. See PPG Indus., Inc. v. JMB/Houston Ctrs. Partners Ltd., 146 S.W.3d 79, 89 (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 2004, no pet.). Arcadia itself concedes that the total consideration "exceeds $500,000."  
               Second, Arcadia is not an individual. Arcadia is a Texas limited liability company. FAC ¶ 6. The § 17.49(f) exemption was enacted precisely to prevent sophisticated commercial parties engaged in high-value transactions from wielding the DTPA—with its treble-damages provision—as a weapon in ordinary commercial disputes. See PPG, 146 S.W.3d at 89. A $17 million enterprise software transaction between two commercial entities, each represented by counsel, is the paradigm case for the exemption. The FAC alleges no facts that could bring Arcadia within the "individual" exception. Count V is therefore barred as a matter of law and must be dismissed.  
               Even if the DTPA were applicable, Count V fails on the merits. The alleged representations—that NexusCore delivers "industry-leading performance," that clients "typically see 30-40% improvement in reporting efficiency," and that the platform would "integrate seamlessly with any EHR platform," FAC ¶¶ 23, 25, 79—are non-actionable puffery. See Pizza Hut, Inc. v. Papa John's Int'l, Inc., 227 F.3d 489, 497 (5th Cir. 2000) (general claims of product superiority are puffery). Meridian's December 9, 2021 written proposal expressly disclaimed that "[e]stimated timelines and performance metrics are provided for planning purposes only and do not constitute guarantees." And Arcadia cannot establish "producing cause," because the implementation difficulties flowed from Arcadia's own delays and Linden Park's data-migration errors, not from any pre-sale statement. See Melody Home Mfg. Co. v. Barnes, 741 S.W.2d 349, 351-52 (Tex. 1987). Count V also fails Rule 9(b) for the reasons discussed in Section IV.C.  
              B. Count IV (Unjust Enrichment) Must Be Dismissed Because an Express Contract Governs the Same Subject Matter.  
               Count IV fails as a matter of law because the parties' relationship is governed by an express, valid, and enforceable written contract. Under Texas law, "recovery under quantum meruit [or unjust enrichment] is not available when there is a valid, express contract covering the subject matter of the dispute." Fortune Prod. Co. v. Conoco, Inc., 52 S.W.3d 671, 684 (Tex. 2000); see also Excess Underwriters at Lloyd's v. Frank's Casing Crew & Rental Tools, Inc., 246 S.W.3d 42, 59-60 (Tex. 2008) (the "same subject matter" test is applied broadly). The MSLSA indisputably governs the entire scope of the parties' software-licensing and implementation relationship—the very subject matter of Count IV. FAC ¶¶ 99-103.  
               Critically, Arcadia does not allege that the MSLSA is void or unenforceable. To the contrary, Arcadia's Count I (breach of contract) affirmatively relies on the existence and validity of the MSLSA. FAC ¶ 69. Arcadia thus simultaneously sues on the contract and seeks equitable relief that is available only in the absence of a contract—a position the Texas Supreme Court has held irreconcilable. Fortune Prod., 52 S.W.3d at 684. The fact that the MSLSA's limitation-of-liability and consequential-damages provisions may limit Arcadia's contractual recovery does not create a "gap" that unjust enrichment can fill. Excess Underwriters, 246 S.W.3d at 59 ("equity follows the law"; where parties have allocated risk through contract, equity will not rewrite the bargain). Count IV must be dismissed.  
              C. Count II (Fraud) Must Be Dismissed for Failure to Plead Fraud with Particularity, the Economic Loss Doctrine, Puffery, and the Absence of Justifiable Reliance.  
               Count II fails for four independent reasons.  
               1. The FAC does not satisfy Rule 9(b). Rule 9(b) requires Arcadia to plead the "who, what, when, where, and why" of the alleged fraud. Benchmark Elecs., 343 F.3d at 724. Although the FAC identifies James Poletti and approximate dates, it does not plead why the statements were false when made. Arcadia's theory is entirely retrospective: because the implementation was delayed and performance issues emerged post-Go-Live, Meridian must have known its pre-sale representations were false. That post-hoc reasoning is insufficient. See Flaherty & Crumrine Preferred Income Fund, Inc. v. TXU Corp., 565 F.3d 200, 207-08 (5th Cir. 2009) (post-hoc product failure does not support an inference of scienter absent internal documents or contemporaneous admissions). The FAC alleges no internal Meridian documents, no contemporaneous admissions, and no specific facts showing that Poletti knew any statement was false when made. The FAC also "lumps" together multiple pre-sale communications and characterizes them collectively as fraudulent, failing to analyze each statement individually. Dorsey v. Portfolio Equities, Inc., 540 F.3d 333, 339-40 (5th Cir. 2008).  
               2. The economic loss doctrine bars the fraud claim. The MSLSA's Section 12.7 selects Delaware law, under which the economic loss doctrine bars tort claims that seek purely economic losses arising from a contractual relationship absent an independent duty. Brasby v. Morris Dynamics, Inc., 947 A.2d 1042, 1049-50 (Del. 2008). The doctrine applies with particular force where, as here, sophisticated parties represented by counsel executed a comprehensive agreement containing an integration clause and warranty disclaimers. Kuhn Constr., Inc. v. Diamond State Port Corp., 990 A.2d 393, 401-02 (Del. 2010). All of Arcadia's damages are economic, and the alleged misrepresentations concern the quality and performance of NexusCore—the subject of the MSLSA. Even under Texas's more limited economic loss rule, the claim fails because Arcadia's losses are purely economic, relate to the subject matter of the MSLSA, and Meridian owes no duty independent of the contract. Chapman Custom Homes, Inc. v. Dallas Plumbing Co., 445 S.W.3d 716, 718-19 (Tex. 2014); Sharyland Water Supply Corp. v. City of Alton, 354 S.W.3d 407, 415-16 (Tex. 2011). Arcadia's fraud claim is, at core, a claim that Meridian did not deliver software that performed as promised—the essence of breach of contract.  
               3. The alleged statements are non-actionable puffery. "Industry-leading performance for healthcare analytics" and "30-40% improvement in reporting efficiency" are vague, subjective claims of product superiority and forward-looking projections on which no sophisticated buyer could reasonably rely. Pizza Hut, 227 F.3d at 496-97; Castrol Inc. v. Pennzoil Co., 987 F.2d 939, 945 (3d Cir. 1993). Meridian further denies that Poletti stated NexusCore "will integrate seamlessly with any EHR platform"; his contemporaneous notes reflect that he stated the platform "is designed to integrate with major EHR platforms through our standard API framework, subject to proper configuration." In any event, the December 9, 2021 written proposal expressly disclaimed that estimated timelines and performance metrics "do not constitute guarantees."  
               4. Arcadia cannot establish justifiable reliance. Arcadia is a sophisticated commercial entity that conducted extensive independent due diligence—including reference calls and a two-week proof-of-concept trial—and whose CTO acknowledged in writing that integration "will require a skilled SI partner." A sophisticated buyer who conducts its own investigation and forms its own conclusions cannot claim it was defrauded by a seller's general promotional statements. Presidio Enters., Inc. v. Warner Bros. Distrib. Corp., 784 F.2d 674, 679-80 (5th Cir. 1986). Moreover, under Delaware's anti-reliance doctrine, a party who negotiates a comprehensive agreement with an integration clause cannot later assert fraud based on the very extra-contractual representations the clause was designed to supersede. Eagle Indus., Inc. v. DeVilbiss Health Care, Inc., 702 A.2d 1228, 1232 (Del. 1997); SIGA Techs., Inc. v. PharmAthene, Inc., 67 A.3d 330, 344 (Del. 2013). Arcadia's counsel expressly accepted the integration clause "without a fraud carve-out" and represented that Arcadia proceeded "on the basis of the express contractual terms rather than pre-contractual representations." Count II must be dismissed.  
              D. Count III (Negligent Misrepresentation) Must Be Dismissed Because Meridian Owes No Independent Duty and the Damages Are Contractual.  
               Count III fails for the same reasons as Count II and for the additional reason that Arcadia cannot identify any duty independent of the MSLSA. Texas recognizes negligent misrepresentation under Restatement (Second) of Torts § 552, but liability is constrained by the independent-duty requirement. McCamish, Martin, Brown & Loeffler v. F.E. Appling Interests, 991 S.W.2d 787, 791-92 (Tex. 1999). Meridian's relationship with Arcadia is purely contractual; there is no fiduciary or professional relationship giving rise to a heightened duty, and the MSLSA's warranty provisions (Sections 9.1, 9.4) define the scope of Meridian's obligations regarding software performance. Arcadia cannot circumvent those provisions through a tort claim.  
               Arcadia's damages theory also confirms that Count III is a contract claim in tort's clothing. Arcadia seeks the $14.7 million contract value, $18.4 million in lost profits, and $6.7 million in increased operating costs—all benefit-of-the-bargain and consequential damages recoverable only in contract. Federal Land Bank Ass'n of Tyler v. Sloane, 825 S.W.2d 439, 442-43 (Tex. 1992) (benefit-of-the-bargain losses are recoverable only in contract; where the plaintiff's true complaint is that the other party did not deliver what was contractually promised, the claim sounds in contract). The economic loss doctrine bars the claim for the reasons stated in Section IV.C.2, and Rule 9(b) applies because the claim sounds in fraud. Benchmark Elecs., 343 F.3d at 724. Count III must be dismissed.  
              E. Count I (Breach of Contract) Must Be Dismissed, or in the Alternative Limited to the Contractual Remedies.  
               Count I cannot survive the MSLSA's acceptance, warranty, and remedy provisions. First, Arcadia deemed-accepted the software. Section 5.3 provides that if the licensee fails to deliver written notice of material nonconformity within the 30-day acceptance window, acceptance is "deemed" granted. NexusCore went live on January 15, 2023; the window closed on February 14, 2023; and Arcadia delivered no written Nonconformity Notice. Dr. Okonkwo's March 8, 2023 email came 22 days late and was a routine support request, not the contractual notice Section 5.3 requires. Deemed-acceptance provisions are enforceable bright-line rules, and oral or informal communications do not satisfy a written-notice requirement. Simulados, Inc. v. Canton Health Mgmt. Co., 2019 WL 4573218, at *5-6 (W.D. Tex. Sept. 20, 2019) (unpublished); Precision Healthcare Sols. v. Nextera Data Sys., 458 F. Supp. 3d 544, 551-52 (N.D. Tex. 2020) (deemed acceptance applies to latent as well as patent defects). Once acceptance was deemed granted, Arcadia's remedies for any defect were channeled through the warranty provisions, not through rejection or a theory of fundamental breach.  
               Second, the limited warranty has expired. Section 9.1's 90-day warranty ran from Go-Live and expired on April 15, 2023. The sole remedy for a warranty breach is repair or replacement, or a pro-rata refund if Meridian cannot cure within 60 days. Arcadia did not deliver a warranty claim during the warranty period, and Meridian nonetheless patched the four identified software defects. Third, the MSLSA's remedies are exclusive. Section 8.3 designates the MSLSA's remedies as Arcadia's "sole and exclusive remedies," and Section 9.1(b) makes repair, replacement, or pro-rata refund the exclusive remedy for nonconformance. See Kana Software, Inc. v. Sealand Tech., Inc., 178 A.3d 1045, 1058-60 (Del. Ch. 2017) (where the contract provides a specific remedy for nonconformance, that remedy is exclusive and precludes other damage theories).  
               Fourth, and in any event, the damages Arcadia seeks are contractually barred. Section 8.2 excludes all consequential, incidental, special, punitive, and exemplary damages—including lost profits, goodwill, and reputational harm—"regardless of the form of action." The $18.4 million in lost profits, $6.7 million in increased operating costs, and $5.0 million in reputational harm are consequential damages that are categorically excluded. Section 8.1 caps aggregate liability at the fees paid or payable in the twelve months preceding the event. Limitation-of-liability and consequential-damages provisions in negotiated commercial software agreements between sophisticated parties are enforceable, and a consequential-damages waiver survives even if the limited remedy fails of its essential purpose. Dresser-Rand Co. v. Virtual Automation Inc., 361 F.3d 831, 838-40 (5th Cir. 2004); see also MSLSA § 8.5. Arcadia's counsel expressly acknowledged Arcadia's understanding of these very limitations. At most, Arcadia's recoverable damages are a fraction of its $47.3 million demand. Count I should be dismissed; alternatively, Arcadia's damages must be limited to the contractual cap and the exclusive remedies.

        I. Executive Summary  
      The MKPoly joint venture combines two complementary contributions: Meridian’s proprietary coating formulations and intellectual property (23 items independently appraised at $28.6 million) together with its North American commercial platform, and Kastor’s capital (€38 million / $41.8 million), manufacturing process expertise (9 contributed IP items), raw-material supply chain, and global distribution infrastructure. The parties have agreed on a 55/45 equity split (Meridian/Kastor) reflecting the strategic value of Meridian’s contributed IP and operating role, against total contributions of $42.9 million (Meridian) and $43.8 million (Kastor) and aggregate capitalization of $86.7 million. The near-parity of the dollar contributions, alongside the majority equity position held by Meridian, is the central structural fact that the five contested provisions must reconcile.  
      The five contested provisions, and the proposed compromise landing point for each, are summarized below. The detailed clause language, position summaries, and rationale follow in Sections II through VI. A summary concession matrix appears in Section VII.  
             Management Committee Composition and Governance (Section 5.01).         A five-member Management Committee with three Meridian appointees and two Kastor appointees, reflecting the 55/45 equity split; simple-majority voting for ordinary matters; supermajority voting (four of five, requiring at least one Kastor-appointed Manager) on a defined list of Reserved Matters limited to genuinely existential decisions; and a tiered deadlock mechanism under which the CEO casts the deciding vote on operational matters while Reserved-Matter deadlocks escalate to principals, then mediation, then the buy-sell mechanism.  
             Capital Calls and Dilution (Section 4.03).         A 60-day cure period (75 days inclusive of notice), penalty interest accruing during the cure period, a foreign-exchange accommodation for Kastor’s euro-denominated treasury, a dilution remedy capped at 15% per annum with a forced buy-sell backstop once cumulative dilution reaches 15% in any 24-month period, and an optional bridge-loan alternative on above-market terms that converts to dilution on default.  
             Non-Competition (Section 11.01).         An 18-month post-exit restricted period; a product scope covering the JV’s actual product lines (Schedule 2.04) plus substantially similar or directly competitive products; a geographic scope covering the United States and Canada plus any other jurisdiction where the Company generated more than $500,000 in revenue during the 24 months preceding exit; and supporting enforceability provisions (severability/reformation, governing law, and forum selection).  
             Exit Valuation Methodology (Sections 10.03–10.04).         A 7.0× trailing twelve-month adjusted EBITDA multiple; a single jointly-selected independent Big Four (or other nationally recognized) valuation firm — not the Company’s auditor — with an American Arbitration Association fallback if the parties cannot agree within 30 days; an EBITDA-only methodology (no discounted cash flow as the primary method); and four trigger events (change of control, material breach, deadlock, and involuntary transfer).  
             IP License-Back and Co-Developed IP (Section 12.05).         Reversion of contributed IP to the contributing party (agreed); a symmetric, non-sublicensable cross-license of contributed IP for each party’s internal operations; a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to co-developed IP for both parties, with reciprocal territorial sublicensing (Meridian in the Americas; Kastor outside North America) subject to a hard exclusion of Restricted Competitors (Schedule 12.05) and a dynamic revenue-based negative covenant; and a symmetric treatment of improvements to contributed IP under which         “    primarily derivative    ”         improvements revert to the contributing party with a territorial license to the other.  
      The proposed compromises are designed so that each party secures meaningful protection on the matters most important to it while accommodating the other party’s legitimate concerns. On governance, Meridian retains a board majority and operational tie-breaking authority, while Kastor secures supermajority veto rights on existential decisions and a structured deadlock-to-exit path on those same decisions. On capital calls, Meridian retains a meaningful dilution remedy and funding discipline, while Kastor secures a workable cure period, a dilution cap, and a loan alternative that protect against disproportionate consequences from temporary liquidity constraints. On the non-compete, Meridian secures an enforceable, substantive restriction tied to the JV’s actual business, while Kastor secures a duration and scope that respect its diversified global business. On exit valuation, Kastor secures an independent appraiser and a market-centered multiple, while Meridian secures a defined, EBITDA-based methodology that provides certainty and avoids projection-based disputes. On IP, Meridian secures absolute protection against its contributed formulations reaching North American competitors, while Kastor secures a broad, sublicensable co-developed IP license-back outside North America and a workable framework for improvements.

          Section 5.01(d) — Deadlock.         In the event the Management Committee is unable to reach the required vote on any matter after two (2) consecutive meetings (each held at least ten (10) business days apart) at which such matter is considered (a         “    Deadlock    ”    ), the following procedures shall apply:  
              Operational Matters.         For any matter that is not a Reserved Matter (an         “    Operational Matter    ”    ), the Chief Executive Officer shall cast the deciding vote to resolve the Deadlock. The CEO’s casting vote shall be exercisable only after the two-meeting Deadlock threshold has been met and shall not apply to any Reserved Matter.  
              Reserved Matters — Executive Escalation.         For any Reserved Matter, the matter shall first be referred to the chief executive officer of Meridian and the chief strategy officer of Kastor (or their respective designees) for good-faith negotiation. Such senior officers shall meet within fifteen (15) days after referral and shall use commercially reasonable efforts to resolve the Deadlock within thirty (30) days after the initial referral.  
              Reserved Matters — Mediation.         If the Deadlock on a Reserved Matter is not resolved through executive escalation within the thirty (30)-day period, the Members shall submit the matter to non-binding mediation administered by the American Arbitration Association, conducted by a single mediator mutually selected by the Members (or, failing agreement within ten (10) days, appointed by the AAA). The mediation shall be completed within sixty (60) days following appointment of the mediator. The costs of mediation shall be shared equally by the Members.  
              Reserved Matters — Buy-Sell.         If the Deadlock on a Reserved Matter remains unresolved after completion of the mediation process, either Member may initiate the buy-sell procedures set forth in Sections 10.02 through 10.04 by delivering written notice to the other Member.  
          Section 5.02 — Reserved Matters (conforming amendment).         The Reserved Matters shall be limited to the following actions, each requiring Supermajority approval under Section 5.01(c):

          any merger, consolidation, reorganization, or business combination involving the Company, or any transaction constituting a change of control of the Company;

          any material change to the Business Purpose of the Company as set forth in Section 2.04 and Schedule 2.04;  
          the incurrence by the Company of indebtedness for borrowed money in excess of Ten Million Dollars ($10,000,000) in the aggregate at any time outstanding;  
          any single capital expenditure, or series of related capital expenditures within any twelve (12)-month period, in excess of Five Million Dollars ($5,000,000) not included in the approved annual operating budget; and  
          the appointment or removal of the Chief Executive Officer.  
      Ordinary-course operating decisions — including the approval of the annual operating budget, hiring and termination of personnel below the C-suite level, entry into customer or supplier contracts in the ordinary course, pricing decisions, and product-development priorities — shall not constitute Reserved Matters and shall be decided by simple majority vote (with the CEO’s casting vote available on Operational Matter deadlocks as provided in Section 5.01(d)(i)).  
          Section 6.01(a)(i) — Chief Executive Officer (conforming amendment).         The Chief Executive Officer shall be nominated by Meridian and appointed subject to confirmation by the Management Committee at the Supermajority threshold set forth in Section 5.01(c). The CEO shall have general responsibility for the day-to-day management and operations of the Company, subject to the oversight and direction of the Management Committee. The Chief Financial Officer shall be appointed by the Management Committee upon the recommendation of the CEO (simple majority). The Chief Technology Officer shall be jointly approved by both Members, with each Member having a veto right over the appointment.

           C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Board composition.         A five-member Management Committee with three Meridian appointees and two Kastor appointees directly reflects the parties’ agreed 55/45 equity split and is the standard mechanism for aligning governance with majority ownership in specialty-chemicals joint ventures. In each of the three comparable transactions reviewed, the majority equity holder retained governance control — either through a 3-to-2 board (in the 60/40 transaction) or through a 3-to-3 board with a casting vote and sole officer-appointment authority (in the 55/45 transaction). At the same time, the proposal acknowledges the near-parity of the parties’ dollar contributions ($42.9 million versus $43.8 million) by pairing the 3-to-2 composition with meaningful protective rights for Kastor, rather than treating the board majority as a license for unilateral action. Kastor has signaled that board composition numbers are an area of flexibility provided that supermajority protections on Reserved Matters are preserved; this proposal honors that signal.  
          Supermajority on Reserved Matters.         The Supermajority threshold (four of five, requiring at least one Kastor Manager) gives Kastor an effective veto on the decisions that most directly affect the integrity of its investment — amendments to the governing documents, dissolution, sale of substantially all assets, change-of-control transactions, admission of new members, related-party transactions, changes to the business purpose, material indebtedness, major off-budget capital expenditures, and appointment or removal of the CEO. These are precisely the categories in which a minority partner’s protective interest is legitimate, and the proposal concedes them in full. The list is deliberately confined to genuinely existential and structural decisions. Ordinary operating decisions — annual budget line-items, sub-C-suite hiring, ordinary-course contracts, pricing, and product-development priorities — remain subject to simple-majority voting, because subjecting routine management to minority consent would convert the majority equity position into minority effective control and would impede the efficient operation of the Company. This distinction between protective rights and affirmative management control is well established in Delaware LLC practice and is the standard framework for majority-minority ventures.  
          Tiered deadlock mechanism.         The proposed deadlock mechanism reconciles the parties’ competing concerns. Meridian requires the ability to break deadlocks on operational matters so that the Company is never paralyzed by an unresolved operating disagreement; the CEO’s casting vote on Operational Matters — exercisable only after two successive meetings have failed to produce a majority — preserves that ability. Kastor has objected, with force, that a CEO casting vote on Reserved Matters would render the Supermajority protection illusory, because the CEO will be a Meridian appointee. The proposal accepts that objection in full: the CEO’s casting vote does not extend to any Reserved Matter. Instead, Reserved-Matter deadlocks follow a structured escalation path — principals’ negotiation, then non-binding mediation, then the buy-sell mechanism — that preserves both parties’ rights while ensuring that an unresolvable Reserved-Matter deadlock has a definitive terminal step rather than indefinite paralysis. This structure also mitigates the risk, which Meridian has flagged, that a forced-buyout trigger available on all deadlocks could incentivize the manufacture of deadlocks to force an exit at a favorable valuation: the buyout trigger is confined to Reserved Matters that have survived two prior escalation steps.  
          CEO appointment.         Designating the CEO’s appointment and removal as a Reserved Matter — with Meridian retaining the nomination right and the Management Committee confirming at the Supermajority threshold — gives Kastor a meaningful voice over who leads the Company (a matter of legitimate concern to a 45% partner that has committed $43.8 million), while preserving Meridian’s ability to put forward its preferred candidate. The CEO’s day-to-day management authority, exercised within the approved budget and subject to the Management Committee’s oversight, remains intact.

         III. Provision 2 — Capital Calls and Dilution (Section 4.03)  
       A. Proposed Compromise Clause Language  
      The following language is proposed to replace Section 4.03(b), (c), and (d) of the JVA.  
          Section 4.03(b) — Obligation to Fund; Notice.         Each Member shall fund its proportionate share of a Capital Call within sixty (60) calendar days after receipt of the Capital Call notice (the         “    Cure Period    ”    ). The Management Committee shall deliver the Capital Call notice not less than fifteen (15) business days prior to the funding deadline specified therein, which notice shall state (i) the aggregate amount, (ii) each Member’s proportionate share, (iii) the purpose, and (iv) the funding deadline. The period from delivery of the Capital Call notice through expiration of the Cure Period shall not be less than seventy-five (75) calendar days in the aggregate.  
          Section 4.03(c) — Penalty Interest; Foreign-Exchange Accommodation.  
              Penalty Interest.         During the Cure Period, interest shall accrue automatically on any unfunded portion of a Member’s proportionate share of a Capital Call at a per annum rate equal to the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (SOFR) plus four hundred (400) basis points (the         “    Penalty Rate    ”    ). Accrued Penalty Interest shall be payable to the Company for the account of the Contributing Member upon funding or upon any Dilution Event, as applicable.  
              Foreign-Exchange Accommodation.         To the extent Kastor’s funding obligations are funded from euro-denominated accounts, Kastor may fund in euros at the European Central Bank reference rate quoted on the date of the Capital Call notice, with conversion to U.S. dollars handled by the Company’s treasury at the Company’s cost. This accommodation is intended to accommodate Kastor’s multi-currency treasury operations and is available to either Member funding from a non-USD-denominated account.  
          Section 4.03(d) — Remedies Upon Failure to Fund.         If a Member (the         “    Defaulting Member    ”    ) fails to fund its proportionate share of a Capital Call within the Cure Period, the non-defaulting Member (the         “    Contributing Member    ”    ) may, at its sole election, exercise either of the following remedies:  
              Dilution.         The Defaulting Member’s Percentage Interest shall be reduced on a pro-rata basis to reflect the relative Capital Contributions of the Members (a         “    Dilution Event    ”    ),             provided that         the aggregate dilution of the Defaulting Member’s Percentage Interest shall not exceed fifteen percent (15%) per annum of its then-current Percentage Interest (the         “    Annual Dilution Cap    ”    ). Any dilution that would exceed the Annual Dilution Cap in a given Fiscal Year shall be carried forward and applied in succeeding Fiscal Years until fully applied.             Forced Buy-Sell Backstop:         If a Member’s cumulative dilution reaches fifteen percent (15%) of its then-current Percentage Interest in any twenty-four (24)-month period, the Contributing Member may, at its election, initiate the buy-sell procedures set forth in Sections 10.02 through 10.04 to acquire the Defaulting Member’s entire membership interest at the Appraised Fair Market Value.  
              Bridge Loan Alternative.         The Contributing Member may, at its election, advance the Defaulting Member’s unfunded share as a bridge loan to the Company on behalf of the Defaulting Member. The bridge loan shall (A) bear interest at SOFR plus five hundred (500) basis points per annum, compounding quarterly; (B) be secured by a first-priority security interest in the Defaulting Member’s entire membership interest in the Company, perfected by the filing of UCC financing statements; (C) be repayable in full (principal plus accrued interest) within twelve (12) months of disbursement; (D) automatically convert to a Dilution Event (calculated as provided in clause (i) above) if not repaid in full within such twelve-month period; and (E) be available to any Member no more than once in any twenty-four (24)-month period. If the Contributing Member elects the bridge loan and the Defaulting Member repays in full within the twelve-month period, no Dilution Event shall occur with respect to such Capital Call.  
          Section 4.03(e) — Suspension of Voting Rights (conforming).         During any period in which a Member is a Defaulting Member, such Member’s voting rights on the Management Committee shall be suspended with respect to matters requiring or relating to additional capital expenditure by the Company, without affecting such Member’s rights with respect to Reserved Matters that do not relate to capital expenditures.

           C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Cure period and FX accommodation.         Meridian’s opening 30-day cure period and Kastor’s opening 90-day cure period reflect legitimate but different concerns: Meridian requires funding certainty for the Company’s operations, while Kastor’s multi-currency, multi-jurisdictional treasury operations require time to arrange U.S.-dollar funding and comply with cross-border transfer requirements. A 60-day cure period, preceded by a 15-day notice period (75 days in aggregate), provides a sophisticated multinational treasury ample time to arrange funding while preserving meaningful urgency. The foreign-exchange accommodation — under which Kastor may fund in euros at a locked reference rate with conversion handled by the Company at the Company’s cost — directly addresses the operational constraint Kastor has cited as the principal justification for a 90-day cure, and in doing so supports a cure period shorter than Kastor’s opening position. Penalty interest accruing during the cure period at SOFR plus 400 basis points compensates the Company and the Contributing Member for the time value of delayed capital and creates a financial incentive to fund promptly, addressing Meridian’s concern that an extended cure period should not be costless.  
          Dilution cap and forced buy-sell backstop.         Meridian’s opening position of uncapped, immediate dilution reflects the principle that a partner which does not fund the venture’s growth should not retain an equity position disproportionate to its contribution. Kastor’s opening position of a 10% annual cap reflects the principle that a temporary liquidity disruption should not permanently destroy a partner’s equity stake. The proposed 15% annual cap is the midpoint between these positions and is supported by market precedent: the most directly comparable 55/45 transaction employed a 15% annual dilution cap. Critically, the cap is paired with a forced buy-sell backstop — once a Member’s cumulative dilution reaches 15% in any 24-month period, the Contributing Member may compel a buy-sell of the diluted Member’s interest at fair market value. This structure converts the cap from a shield behind which a non-funding partner can indefinitely free-ride into a bridge to an orderly exit. It protects Kastor against disproportionate single-event dilution (the cap) while ensuring that persistent non-funding has definitive consequences (the backstop), which is Meridian’s core concern. The cap and backstop apply symmetrically to both Members.  
          Bridge loan alternative.         Meridian’s opening position offered no loan alternative; Kastor’s opening position proposed a market-rate loan (SOFR plus 250 basis points) that would preserve the non-funding Member’s equity indefinitely. A market-rate loan, however, would be cheaper than the expected return on Company equity and would therefore rationally be chosen in lieu of funding in every case — eliminating the economic incentive to fund and enabling a non-funding partner to retain its equity position, governance rights, and share of growth while paying a below-equity-return cost. The proposed bridge loan is structured as a short-term accommodation, not an escape hatch: the rate (SOFR plus 500 basis points) is above market; repayment is mandatory in full within 12 months; the loan is secured by the borrowing Member’s entire JV interest; and any default converts the loan automatically to dilution. A rational party acting in good faith will fund rather than borrow on these terms, but a party experiencing a genuine short-term liquidity constraint has a workable alternative that keeps the Company fully funded without immediate dilution. The single-use-per-24-months limitation prevents serial use of the loan to defer capital commitments indefinitely.

           C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Duration.         An 18-month restricted period sits at the center of the safe harbor recognized by the courts of the two jurisdictions most likely to govern MKPoly — Delaware and Texas — both of which enforce post-exit non-competition covenants of one to two years in the commercial and sale-of-business context. Eighteen months is also the median duration across the three comparable specialty-chemicals joint ventures reviewed (12, 18, and 24 months). It is a meaningful concession from Meridian’s opening two-year position and a meaningful extension from Kastor’s opening one-year position, and it provides adequate time for the competitive value of confidential information and customer relationships acquired during the JV term to dissipate following a member’s exit. Because MKPoly is a Delaware LLC with its operational headquarters in Houston, Texas, the covenant is treated as ancillary to the disposition of a membership interest (not as an employment restraint), which receives the more deferential sale-of-business standard of review in both jurisdictions.  
          Product scope.         Meridian’s opening scope of         “    all specialty polymer coatings    ”         risks overbreadth: it would capture product categories the JV may never enter, and courts in both Delaware and Texas are reluctant to enforce restraints extending beyond the business actually conducted. Kastor’s opening scope of         “    specific JV product lines only    ”         risks gamesmanship: a departing member could compete with functionally identical products that fall outside a fixed product list by technical distinctions in formulation, branding, or application. The proposed formulation — the JV Product Lines as defined in Schedule 2.04, plus products substantially similar to or directly competitive with those lines — tracks the JV’s actual business (satisfying the         “    no broader than necessary    ”         standard) while providing a protective penumbra that prevents circumvention through trivial product differentiation. This         “    substantially similar or directly competitive    ”         formulation is a standard commercial non-compete construction upheld in both Delaware and Texas. The scope is tied to Schedule 2.04 (Business Purpose), giving both parties a durable, defined reference and accommodating Kastor’s legitimate interest in continuing to operate its pre-existing, non-competing product lines (preserved by the exception in Section 11.01(d)(ii)).  
          Geographic scope.         Meridian’s opening scope of         “    all of North America    ”         and Kastor’s opening scope of         “    territories with active sales at exit    ”         represent the two poles of the enforceability analysis. A fixed North American territory provides certainty but may extend to areas where the JV has no protectable interest; a pure active-sales formulation introduces definitional ambiguity (what constitutes an         “    active sale    ”         in a         “    territory    ”    ?) and could exclude jurisdictions where the JV is actively developing market entry. The proposed hybrid — the United States and Canada, plus any other jurisdiction where the Company generated more than $500,000 in revenue during the 24 months preceding exit — is anchored to the Company’s actual commercial footprint (satisfying the nexus requirement in all surveyed jurisdictions, including California’s narrow LLC exception under Business and Professions Code § 16602.5), eliminates jurisdictions of only incidental sales, and uses a defined lookback period for certainty. If MKPoly achieves its projected sales distribution, the practical coverage of this formulation will be essentially coextensive with Meridian’s North America proposal, but with a defensible analytical foundation. The de minimis revenue threshold and 24-month lookback also address the concern, flagged in the enforceability analysis, that a pure active-sales test could be manipulated.  
          Enforceability provisions.         The severability and reformation clause, the Delaware governing-law designation, and the forum-selection clause (Delaware Court of Chancery, with Harris County, Texas as an alternative) channel enforcement to jurisdictions where the covenant is most likely to be enforced as drafted and where reformation is available. These provisions protect both parties: they give the restraint a reliable enforcement framework while ensuring that any overbreadth is cured by reformation rather than wholesale invalidation.

           C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Multiple.         A 7.0× trailing twelve-month EBITDA multiple is near the median (7.1×) of the Frazier Whitmore comparable-transactions dataset (range 6.2× to 8.1×) and falls within the range both parties have indicated is acceptable — at the upper end of Meridian’s flexibility and within Kastor’s stated 6.5×–7.5× band. It represents a meaningful movement from Meridian’s opening 6.5× position (a 7.7% increase) toward the market center, supported by independent data rather than by negotiation pressure alone. A fixed contractual multiple provides certainty and predictability at exit, eliminates the most significant source of valuation dispute (the appropriate capitalization rate), and allows the exit transaction to be completed in weeks rather than months. Because the multiple is fixed and the methodology is EBITDA-based, the Independent Appraiser’s role is confined to determining the adjusted EBITDA figure and the net-debt and working-capital adjustments — objective, verifiable determinations drawn from audited financial statements — rather than selecting the multiple itself. This division preserves appraiser independence on the financial determination while providing both parties with certainty on the capitalization rate.  
          Appraiser independence.         Kastor has identified appraiser independence as a top-priority concern, and the concern is legitimate: an entity that serves as the Company’s auditor maintains an ongoing commercial relationship with the Company and a primary reporting relationship to a Meridian-influenced management structure, which creates an inherent conflict when that entity is also asked to set the price at which one member buys out the other. The proposal concedes this point in full. The Independent Appraiser is a single, jointly-selected Big Four (or equivalent) firm with no pre-existing engagement with the Company or either Member, and is expressly not the Company’s auditor. The selection mechanism is deliberately simpler than the three-appraiser baseball-arbitration process Kastor initially proposed: the parties attempt mutual agreement on a single firm, with an AAA appointment as a fallback if agreement is not reached within 30 days. A single-firm mechanism is faster, less expensive, and less prone to procedural dispute than a three-appraiser process, while fully satisfying the independence requirement. The Frazier Whitmore comparable-transactions data — which Kastor has acknowledged is well-researched and useful — is preserved as a non-binding reference input to the Independent Appraiser, so the analytical work product is not lost even though the firm itself is replaced as appraiser. The optional baseball-arbitration alternative is retained by mutual consent for parties who prefer that structure in a particular exit scenario.  
          Methodology.         Both parties agree that trailing twelve-month EBITDA is the appropriate foundational metric for a specialty-chemicals exit valuation: it captures actual operating performance, is verifiable against audited financial statements, and is not susceptible to the projection-based manipulation that makes discounted cash flow unreliable in a disputed exit. The proposal adopts EBITDA as the sole primary methodology and excludes DCF and comparable-company analysis as primary methods — a constraint that narrows the range of possible outcomes and reduces dispute risk. This is consistent with market practice: the comparable transactions reviewed employed EBITDA-multiple methodologies as the primary valuation framework.  
          Trigger events.         The proposal adopts all four trigger events Kastor has sought — change of control, material breach, deadlock, and involuntary transfer — in addition to the two Meridian originally proposed. Adding deadlock as a trigger is consistent with the tiered deadlock mechanism in Section 5.01(d): a Reserved-Matter deadlock that survives escalation and mediation has a definitive terminal step. Adding involuntary transfer protects both parties against the prospect of being forced into a joint venture with an unknown successor (such as a bankruptcy trustee or creditor) following a compulsory disposition of a member’s interest. The intra-group reorganization carve-out ensures that ordinary corporate housekeeping — a transfer to a wholly-owned affiliate or a parent-company merger that does not change beneficial ownership — does not inadvertently trigger a buyout, protecting both Members’ flexibility to manage their own corporate structures.

           C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Reversion and cross-license of contributed IP.         Both parties agree that contributed IP reverts to the contributing party upon exit or dissolution; this is retained without change. The principal point of divergence on contributed IP concerns whether the non-contributing party receives any license to use it post-exit. Meridian’s opening position would grant no license to Kastor of Meridian’s contributed IP while granting Meridian a license to Kastor’s; Kastor’s opening position would grant a symmetric, non-sublicensable license to both parties. The proposal adopts the symmetric structure — each party grants the other a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to use the other’s contributed IP in its own internal operations — because symmetric treatment of the parties’ contributed IP is commercially fair and easy to administer, and because the non-sublicensable restriction is the mechanism that protects each party’s core competitive interest. The absolute prohibition on sublicensing the other party’s contributed IP — to any person, in any territory, under any circumstances — ensures that neither party’s contributed formulations or process technology can reach a third party, and in particular ensures that Meridian’s proprietary coating formulations (the 23 items appraised at $28.6 million, including the formulations embedded in the JV’s product lines) cannot be placed in the hands of Meridian’s North American competitors through Kastor. This is the protective mechanism that matters; the internal-use license is bounded by it.  
          Co-developed IP license and sublicensing.         Co-developed IP — the intellectual property created by or on behalf of the Company during its operations, funded by both parties’ capital contributions — is the asset in which both parties have the strongest shared claim, because both parties’ contributions (Meridian’s formulations and Kastor’s cash and process expertise) directly fund its creation. Kastor has identified a broad co-developed IP license-back with sublicensing rights as a top priority, and the proposal concedes a broad license: each party receives a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual, irrevocable license to co-developed IP for its own internal use worldwide, and each party may sublicense co-developed IP within its respective territory (Meridian in the Americas; Kastor outside North America). This gives Kastor the meaningful commercial flexibility it requires to monetize co-developed IP through its European and Asian manufacturing and distribution partners — the core of Kastor’s global business model — while preserving Meridian’s North American competitive position through the territorial division.  
          Sublicensing restrictions.         The territorial division alone does not fully address Meridian’s concern that co-developed IP — which incorporates elements of Meridian’s contributed formulations — could reach North American competitors through a Kastor sublicense to a partner that operates in North America. The proposal addresses this concern through three overlapping, reciprocal mechanisms. First, neither party may sublicense co-developed IP to any entity on Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors), regardless of territory — a named-entity exclusion that is reciprocal (binding Meridian equally) and global. Second, a revenue-based negative covenant prohibits sublicensing to any entity that derives more than 25% of its consolidated annual revenue from specialty polymer coatings in North America — a dynamic anti-circumvention mechanism that captures new entrants, restructured entities, and foreign companies with material North American coatings operations that are not on the named list. Third, an annual mutual-update mechanism keeps Schedule 12.05 current, and an audit right allows verification of compliance. Together, these mechanisms give Kastor broad sublicensing freedom outside North America (its stated interest) while ensuring that co-developed IP cannot reach Meridian’s North American competitors through any pathway — direct, indirect, or contingent. The reciprocity of the restrictions (Meridian is equally bound not to sublicense to Kastor’s listed competitors) addresses Kastor’s interest in equal treatment.  
          Improvements to contributed IP.         Both parties have flagged the         “    improvements gap    ”         — the treatment of JV-funded improvements to contributed IP, particularly Meridian’s $28.6 million contributed portfolio, which will almost certainly be the subject of JV-funded R&D during the venture’s life. The proposal adopts a         “    primarily derivative    ”         classification test: an improvement that builds primarily upon one party’s contributed IP is treated as an improvement to that party’s contributed IP (and reverts to the contributing party upon exit, with the other party receiving a territorial license subject to the same sublicensing restrictions), while improvements that build equally on both parties’ IP or are not primarily derivative of either are treated as co-developed IP. This is the commercially logical outcome: a party that contributes IP should not find, upon exit, that an improved version of its own contributed IP is available only to the other party. At the same time, the other party — whose capital funded the improvement — receives a meaningful territorial license to use the improvement, subject to the sublicensing restrictions that protect the contributing party’s competitive position. The structure is symmetric, applying identically to improvements to Meridian’s contributed formulations and to Kastor’s contributed process technology.

      Read across the five provisions, the package achieves a balanced exchange. Each party secures its principal protective objectives while accommodating the other’s. Meridian retains a board majority and operational tie-breaking authority, an enforceable non-compete tied to the JV’s actual business, a defined EBITDA-based exit methodology, absolute protection against its contributed formulations reaching North American competitors, and reversion of improvements to its contributed IP. Kastor secures supermajority veto rights on existential decisions, a structured deadlock-to-exit path on those decisions, an independent exit appraiser at a market-centered multiple, a workable capital-call framework with a dilution cap and loan alternative, a broad and sublicensable co-developed IP license-back outside North America, and symmetric treatment of contributed IP and improvements. The concessions on each side are calibrated so that the package as a whole reflects the near-parity of the parties’ economic contributions alongside Meridian’s majority equity position and the strategic centrality of its contributed intellectual property.

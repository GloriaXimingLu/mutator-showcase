               III. Threshold Issue #1 — Subject-Matter Jurisdiction: Likely No Complete Diversity (URGENT)  
              This is the most important issue in the file. Our own Notice of Removal asserted diversity jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1332(a), but the citizenship analysis in the Notice is incomplete and, on the current record, complete diversity does not appear to exist. Subject-matter jurisdiction cannot be waived and may be raised at any time by the Court sua sponte or by a party. Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U.S. 500, 514 (2006); Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(h)(3).  
               A. The citizenship analysis.  
              Meridian is a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Austin, Texas. It is therefore a citizen of both Delaware and Texas. 28 U.S.C. § 1332(c)(1); Hertz Corp. v. Friend, 559 U.S. 77, 92–93 (2010). The Notice of Removal admits as much. (Notice of Removal ¶¶ 9–11.)  
              Arcadia is a Texas limited liability company, whose citizenship is determined by the citizenship of all of its members. Harvey v. Grey Wolf Drilling Co., 542 F.3d 1077, 1080 (5th Cir. 2008). The Amended and Restated Operating Agreement (produced in initial disclosures, Exhibit A) identifies three members: (1) Dr. Rachel Okonkwo, an individual domiciled in Texas (55%); (2) Martin Schreiber, an individual domiciled in Texas (15%); and (3) Apex Medical Ventures, LP, a Delaware limited partnership (30%). A limited partnership’s citizenship is determined by the citizenship of all of its partners. Carden v. Arkoma Assocs., 494 U.S. 185, 195–96 (1990). Apex’s partners are: (a) Apex Medical Ventures GP, Inc., a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Wilmington, Delaware—a citizen of Delaware; and (b) the Okonkwo Family Trust, whose trustee and primary beneficiary is Dr. Okonkwo, domiciled in Texas—a citizen of Texas.  
              On that record, Arcadia is a citizen of Texas (through Okonkwo, Schreiber, and the Trust) and of Delaware (through Apex Medical Ventures GP, Inc.). Meridian is a citizen of Texas and Delaware. The parties therefore share citizenship in both Texas and Delaware. Complete diversity does not exist. Strawbridge v. Curtiss, 7 U.S. (3 Cranch) 267 (1806).  
               B. The error in the Notice of Removal.  
              The Notice of Removal alleged only that “Plaintiff Arcadia is a citizen of the State of Texas” (Notice ¶¶ 12–14), based solely on Dr. Okonkwo’s domicile, and concluded that “Defendant’s Delaware citizenship is not shared by Plaintiff” (Notice ¶ 15). That analysis is wrong on two independent grounds: (1) it ignored Schreiber and Apex as members and the Trust as a partner of Apex, through which Arcadia is also a citizen of Delaware (via Apex GP, Inc.); and (2) it ignored that Meridian itself is a citizen of Texas (principal place of business in Austin), which is shared by Arcadia (via Okonkwo, Schreiber, and the Trust). Either defect alone destroys complete diversity.  
               C. A minor discrepancy to note.  
              The FAC (¶ 8) describes the Okonkwo Family Trust as “irrevocable,” while the Operating Agreement (Exhibit A, Note 3) describes it as a “revocable inter vivos trust” under Texas law. This discrepancy does not affect the analysis: under either characterization, the trust’s citizenship follows its trustee/beneficiary (Okonkwo, a Texas citizen), so the trust is a Texas citizen either way. We should nonetheless obtain the trust instrument and the Apex partnership agreement to confirm there are no additional partners or co-trustees that could change the analysis.  
               D. Consequences.  
              If the defect is confirmed, the Court lacks subject-matter jurisdiction and the case must be remanded to the 73rd District Court of Bexar County, Texas (where it was originally filed). A remand for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction may be ordered at any time—unlike procedural removal defects, which must be raised within 30 days under 28 U.S.C. § 1447(c). The MSLSA’s forum-selection clause (§ 12.8) would then likely require transfer of the state-court action to a court in Austin, Travis County, Texas. The 12(b)(6) motion could be mooted if the Court remands before ruling on it.  
               E. Strategic complications.  
              Meridian removed this case and invoked federal jurisdiction. There is authority that a removing defendant may be judicially estopped from later moving to remand on the basis of a diversity defect it created and invoked (the “blowing hot and cold” doctrine). That doctrine, however, does not bind the Court—which has an independent obligation to confirm jurisdiction—nor does it bind the plaintiff, who may move to remand at any time on a jurisdictional ground. So even if we are estopped from moving to remand, the case could still be remanded on the Court’s own motion or on Arcadia’s motion.  
               F. Federal vs. state court.  
              Federal court (Judge Alvarez) appears more favorable to us: rigorous Twombly/Iqbal pleading standards (which favor our 12(b)(6) motion) and a bench trial (the § 12.9 jury waiver is being enforced per the Scheduling Order). State court (Bexar County, likely transferred to Travis County under the forum-selection clause) may apply a more plaintiff-friendly pleading standard and, if the jury waiver is found unenforceable under Texas law, a jury trial. But we cannot manufacture jurisdiction: if there is no diversity, we cannot remain in federal court regardless of preference.  
               G. Recommendation.  
              (1) File the 12(b)(6) motion by January 15 to preserve our strongest merits arguments, which are strong independent of forum. (2) Immediately obtain the Apex partnership agreement and the trust instrument to confirm the citizenship analysis. (3) Convene a team meeting to decide how to handle the jurisdictional issue. The realistic options are: (a) do nothing and let the Court or Arcadia raise it (risk: an inopportune remand after we have invested in briefing); (b) file a separate notice/motion under Rule 12(h)(3) alerting the Court to the apparent defect (transparent, but inconsistent with our removal and likely to hasten remand); or (c) if we conclude state court is preferable, take a transparent approach. This is a strategic call for the lead partner and client. My preliminary view is to confirm the analysis, file the 12(b)(6) motion to preserve our arguments, and let the jurisdictional question resolve on its own timeline unless we conclude state court is preferable.  
               IV. Threshold Issue #2 — 12(b)(6) vs. 12(c) Characterization  
              The Standing Order (§ 3.4) provides that a preserved post-answer motion will be treated as a Rule 12(b)(6) motion, not a Rule 12(c) motion. The Scheduling Order, by contrast, states that the Court “will treat any such motion as a Rule 12(c) motion for judgment on the pleadings, evaluated under the same standard as a Rule 12(b)(6) motion.” The two orders are in tension. Because the governing standard is identical (Twombly/Iqbal), there is no practical difference. The draft motion is captioned under Rule 12(b)(6) (consistent with the Standing Order and our preserved defense) and pleads Rule 12(c) in the alternative. We preserved the defense in the Answer (First Affirmative Defense) and by letter of November 8, 2023, satisfying the Standing Order’s post-answer conditions. Note also a page-limit discrepancy: the Standing Order allows a 15-page reply, while the Scheduling Order caps the reply at 10 pages. We should use the 10-page limit to be safe.  
               V. Threshold Issue #3 — Choice of Law  
              Section 12.7 selects Delaware law to govern “this Agreement,” without regard to conflict-of-law principles. The scope of that clause as to non-contractual claims is debatable. Count I (breach of contract) is plainly governed by Delaware law, under which the contractual limitations are strictly enforced. The tort counts (II and III) are likely governed by Texas law under the most-significant-relationship test (the representations were made and received in Texas, both parties operate in Texas, and the injury was suffered in Texas). The draft motion briefs the tort counts under both Delaware and Texas law and demonstrates that they fail under either. The DTPA count is a question of Texas statute regardless of § 12.7. We should be prepared to address choice of law if the Court sets a hearing.  
               VI. Threshold Issue #4 — Documents Outside the Complaint  
              Many of our strongest arguments depend on the MSLSA, SOW-1, and the four Change Orders—the deemed-acceptance provision, the limited warranty, the consequential-damages waiver, the liability cap, the integration clause, and the data-migration allocation. These documents are referenced throughout the FAC and central to every claim, so the Court may consider them without converting the motion to summary judgment. Lormand, 565 F.3d at 255–57; Standing Order § 3.4. The draft motion invokes this doctrine. Before filing, we must attach the MSLSA, SOW-1, and CO-001–CO-004 as exhibits, and we must be careful not to rely on matters genuinely outside the pleadings (e.g., Poletti’s contemporaneous notes, the Schreiber due-diligence memo, the support-ticket logs), which would risk conversion. Those items are for summary judgment, not this motion. The motion instead relies on the FAC’s own allegations (e.g., ¶¶ 27, 31, 32, 45, 50, 53) plus the contract.  
               VII. Threshold Issue #5 — Jury Trial Waiver  
              Section 12.9 of the MSLSA contains a mutual jury-trial waiver, and the Scheduling Order reflects that the case will be tried to the bench. Arcadia nevertheless demanded a jury in the FAC, and our Answer demanded a jury “to the extent the Court determines the jury trial waiver … is not enforceable.” Texas law scrutinizes jury waivers (requiring knowing, voluntary, and conspicuous assent), so enforceability may be contested. This does not affect the 12(b)(6) motion, but it bears on trial posture and on the forum analysis in Part III.F. Flag for later.  
               VIII. Threshold Issue #6 — DTPA Pre-Suit Demand  
              The DTPA requires a 60-day pre-suit demand letter under Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 17.505. The document set includes Arcadia’s DTPA pre-suit demand letter and our response, so it appears a demand was made. Because the § 17.49(f) exemption is dispositive, the pre-suit-demand issue is moot for the motion. We should nonetheless verify compliance (timing and content) in case the § 17.49(f) argument is somehow overcome, and we may add a § 17.505 argument as a further alternative ground in the reply if warranted.  
               IX. Threshold Issue #7 — Leave-to-Amend Risk  
              Courts frequently grant leave to amend after dismissing fraud claims for Rule 9(b) inadequacy. We have requested dismissal with prejudice, which is supported by grounds that cannot be cured by amendment: the § 17.49(f) exemption (Count V), the express-contract bar on unjust enrichment (Count IV), the deemed-acceptance and warranty-expiration provisions (Count I), and—as to the tort counts—the economic loss rule, the puffery doctrine, the integration clause, and the absence of justifiable reliance. These are legal bars, not pleading deficiencies. Even so, the Court may grant leave to amend on the Rule 9(b) grounds for Counts II and III. We should be prepared to oppose leave by emphasizing that the FAC already reflects Arcadia’s best shot (it is an amended complaint) and that the defects are substantive, not curable. If leave is granted, we should move to strike any repleaded tort claims that depend on extra-contractual representations barred by the integration clause.  
               X. Threshold Issue #8 — Statute of Limitations (weak)  
              Texas fraud carries a two-year limitations period subject to the discovery rule and fraudulent concealment. The pre-sale representations were made in October 2021–March 2022, and suit was filed in August 2023—likely timely. I do not recommend a limitations argument in the motion. The Answer preserved it generally; we can revisit if discovery reveals a later-discovered theory.  
               XI. Threshold Issue #9 — Damages-Cap Computation (§ 8.1)  
              Section 8.1 caps aggregate liability at the fees paid or payable during the 12 months preceding the “event giving rise to” liability. The identity of that “event” is ambiguous (the pre-sale representations, the Go-Live date, or the first post-Go-Live complaint), and the cap amount depends on Meridian’s billing records for the relevant period. This affects the alternative damages-limitation argument for Count I. We should pull the billing records and compute the cap under each candidate “event” so we are ready for the reply and for any damages briefing.  
               XII. Threshold Issue #10 — Counterclaims / Third-Party Practice  
              We have not asserted counterclaims or a third-party claim against Linden Park Consulting, LLC (Arcadia’s systems integrator, whose data-migration errors caused much of the harm Arcadia blames on us). The Scheduling Order contemplates third-party discovery as to Linden Park. Asserting a third-party claim now would complicate the 12(b)(6) motion and the remand analysis, and the deadline for joinder/amendment (December 15, 2023) has passed, so leave would be required. I recommend deferring any such claims until after the motion is resolved, and revisiting if the case survives dismissal.

               I. INTRODUCTION  
              This is a case in which a sophisticated commercial party—represented by experienced counsel and armed with the results of its own due diligence—is attempting to use fraud, tort, and consumer-protection theories to escape the comprehensive written contract it negotiated and signed. Arcadia is a $62-million healthcare IT company that investigated Meridian’s product, received express disclaimers, and—through its own counsel—bargained over (and accepted) the very risk-allocation provisions it now seeks to avoid. (FAC ¶¶ 31–32.) The FAC repackages a disappointed commercial expectation as five separate causes of action. Each fails as a matter of law.  
              The parties’ relationship is governed by a fully integrated, Delaware-law contract that contains a comprehensive integration clause, an “as is” warranty disclaimer, a limited 90-day warranty with an exclusive remedy, a deemed-acceptance provision, a consequential-damages waiver, and an aggregate-liability cap. Arcadia was represented by Wexford Hale LLP throughout negotiations and expressly negotiated these provisions. (FAC ¶ 32.) The FAC nevertheless asks the Court to ignore that bargain and to permit Arcadia to recover $47.3 million in tort and statutory damages that the contract forecloses. The Court should decline the invitation.  
               II. RELEVANT FACTS  
              The Court may consider the MSLSA, Statement of Work #1 (“SOW-1”), and the four Change Orders on this Motion because they are referenced throughout the FAC and are central to every claim. See Lormand v. US Unwired, Inc., 565 F.3d 228, 255–57 (5th Cir. 2009); Standing Order § 3.4. The following facts are drawn from the FAC and these documents.

Arcadia is a regional healthcare IT provider with approximately $62 million in annual revenue. (FAC ¶ 6.)               Sophisticated, counseled negotiation. Arcadia engaged outside counsel to negotiate the MSLSA and “negotiate more favorable terms, including enhanced limitation of liability provisions and expanded warranty protections.” (FAC ¶ 32.) The risk-allocation provisions were thus the subject of deliberate, informed negotiation—not boilerplate that escaped notice.  
              The integration clause and warranty disclaimer. Section 12.1 provides that the MSLSA “constitutes the entire agreement between the Parties … and supersedes all prior and contemporaneous agreements, proposals, representations, and understandings, whether oral or written,” and that “[e]ach Party acknowledges that, in entering into this Agreement, it has not relied upon any statement, representation, warranty, or agreement of the other Party except as expressly set forth in this Agreement.” Section 9.4 disclaims all warranties other than the limited warranty in Section 9.1 and states that the software is provided “as is” and that Arcadia “has relied on its own due diligence and evaluation in selecting the Licensed Software.”

               III. LEGAL STANDARD  
              To survive a Rule 12(b)(6) motion, a complaint must contain sufficient factual matter to “state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face.” Bell Atl. Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544, 570 (2007); Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662, 678 (2009). The Court accepts all well-pleaded factual allegations as true, but “[t]hreadbare recitals of the elements of a cause of action, supported by mere conclusory statements, do not suffice.” Iqbal, 556 U.S. at 678. “Determining whether a complaint states a plausible claim … calls for the court to draw on its judicial experience and common sense,” and to consider whether an “obvious alternative explanation” accounts for the defendant’s conduct. Id. at 679, 682.  
              The Court may consider documents “referred to in the plaintiff’s complaint and central to the plaintiff’s claim” without converting the motion into one for summary judgment. Lormand, 565 F.3d at 255–57; see also Standing Order § 3.4. The MSLSA, SOW-1, and Change Orders satisfy that test. Claims sounding in fraud must additionally satisfy the particularity requirements of Rule 9(b). Benchmark Elecs., Inc. v. J.M. Huber Corp., 343 F.3d 719, 724 (5th Cir. 2003).  
               IV. ARGUMENT  
               A. Count V (DTPA) Is Barred by the Statutory Exemption in § 17.49(f).  
              The DTPA claim fails as a matter of law because the transaction falls within the statutory exemption in Texas Business & Commerce Code § 17.49(f). That section provides that the DTPA “does not apply to a cause of action arising from a transaction, a project, or a set of transactions relating to the same project, involving total consideration by the consumer of more than $500,000,” unless the consumer is an individual. Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 17.49(f). Both conditions are met here.  
              First, Arcadia is a Texas limited liability company—not an individual. (FAC ¶ 6.) Second, the total consideration for this transaction vastly exceeds $500,000: the MSLSA alone had a contract value of $14,700,000 (FAC ¶ 34), and the four Change Orders added another $2,350,000 (FAC ¶ 46), for total consideration of $17,050,000—more than 34 times the statutory threshold. The exemption is determined by the total consideration involved in the transaction, not by the amount of damages claimed. PPG Indus., Inc. v. JMB/Houston Ctrs. Partners Ltd., 146 S.W.3d 79, 89 (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 2004, no pet.). The § 17.49(f) exemption exists precisely to keep sophisticated commercial parties in high-value transactions from wielding the DTPA’s treble-damages remedy in ordinary commercial disputes. Id.  
              The FAC does not—and cannot—allege facts bringing this transaction within the DTPA. Arcadia is not an individual, and the consideration exceeds $500,000 by orders of magnitude. Count V must be dismissed. Even if the exemption did not apply, the claim would still fail: the alleged representations are non-actionable puffery (see Section IV.C.2); Arcadia cannot establish producing cause given its own conduct and the data-migration errors of its chosen systems integrator, see Melody Home Mfg. Co. v. Barnes, 741 S.W.2d 349, 351–52 (Tex. 1987); and the DTPA deceptive-act allegations sound in fraud and must satisfy Rule 9(b), Benchmark, 343 F.3d at 724.  
               B. Count IV (Unjust Enrichment) Fails Because an Express Contract Governs the Same Subject Matter.  
              Unjust enrichment is unavailable where a valid express contract covers the subject matter of the dispute. Fortune Prod. Co. v. Conoco, Inc., 52 S.W.3d 671, 684 (Tex. 2000); Excess Underwriters at Lloyd’s v. Frank’s Casing Crew & Rental Tools, Inc., 246 S.W.3d 42, 59 (Tex. 2008). The MSLSA is a comprehensive written agreement governing the parties’ entire software-licensing and implementation relationship—the very subject matter of this dispute. Arcadia does not contend that the MSLSA is void or unenforceable; to the contrary, Count I is premised on the existence and validity of the MSLSA. (FAC ¶ 69.) Arcadia thus simultaneously sues on the contract and seeks quasi-contractual recovery for the same subject matter—claims that are irreconcilably inconsistent. Fortune Prod., 52 S.W.3d at 684.  
              Arcadia’s only basis for unjust enrichment is that the MSLSA’s limitation-of-liability and warranty provisions prevent full recovery under the contract. (See FAC ¶ 99 (unjust enrichment pleaded “in the alternative … to the extent the MSLSA or any provision thereof is found to be unenforceable”).) But the Texas Supreme Court has squarely rejected the argument that contractual limitations on recovery create a “gap” that unjust enrichment may fill. Excess Underwriters, 246 S.W.3d at 59–60. “Equity follows the law”: where sophisticated parties have allocated risk through a negotiated contract, equity will not rewrite the bargain. Id. Count IV must be dismissed.  
               C. Count II (Fraud) Fails Under Rule 9(b), the Economic Loss Rule, the Puffery Doctrine, and the Integration Clause.  
               1. The FAC does not satisfy Rule 9(b).  
              Rule 9(b) requires a fraud plaintiff to “specify the statements contended to be fraudulent, identify the speaker, state when and where the statements were made, and explain why the statements were fraudulent.” Benchmark, 343 F.3d at 724. The FAC identifies the speaker (Poletti) and the dates of the presentations (FAC ¶¶ 22, 24), but it does not allege facts showing why the statements were false when made. Its theory is entirely retrospective: because NexusCore allegedly performed poorly after Go-Live in January 2023, the pre-sale statements made in October–November 2021 must have been false. (FAC ¶ 80.) Post-hoc product failure does not establish that a pre-sale representation was false when made. Flaherty & Crumrine Preferred Income Fund, Inc. v. TXU Corp., 565 F.3d 200, 207–08 (5th Cir. 2009).  
              The FAC’s scienter allegations are likewise deficient. Paragraphs 56–60 allege Meridian’s knowledge and concealment “on information and belief,” without any factual basis—precisely the type of conclusory averments that Rule 9(b) and Iqbal forbid. Flaherty, 565 F.3d at 207; Iqbal, 556 U.S. at 678. The FAC also “lumps” numerous statements together without distinguishing opinion, puffery, and actionable fact. Dorsey v. Portfolio Equities, Inc., 540 F.3d 333, 339–40 (5th Cir. 2008). The fraud claim does not satisfy Rule 9(b).  
               2. The alleged statements are non-actionable puffery.  
              The core representations—“industry-leading performance for healthcare analytics,” “30-40% improvement in reporting efficiency,” “seamless” EHR integration, and a “proven” and “purpose-built” platform—are general claims of product superiority and forward-looking projections. (FAC ¶¶ 23, 25, 28, 79.) Such statements are non-actionable puffery. Pizza Hut, Inc. v. Papa John’s Int’l, Inc., 227 F.3d 489, 496–97 (5th Cir. 2000); Castrol Inc. v. Pennzoil Co., 987 F.2d 939, 945 (3d Cir. 1993). Forward-looking statements about expected performance, made in a sales context, are puffery upon which a sophisticated buyer cannot reasonably rely. Presidio Enters., Inc. v. Warner Bros. Distrib. Corp., 784 F.2d 674, 679–80 (5th Cir. 1986). The December 9, 2021 written proposal—acknowledged in the FAC—expressly disclaimed that performance metrics “are provided for planning purposes only and do not constitute guarantees.” (FAC ¶ 27.)  
               3. The economic loss rule bars the fraud claim.  
              Whether governed by Delaware or Texas law, the fraud claim is barred because it arises from the contractual relationship and seeks only economic losses. Under Delaware law, the economic loss doctrine bars tort claims that seek economic losses arising from a contractual relationship absent an independent duty. Brasby v. Morris Dynamics, Inc., 947 A.2d 1042, 1049–50 (Del. 2008); Kuhn Constr., Inc. v. Diamond State Port Corp., 990 A.2d 393, 401–02 (Del. 2010). Under Texas law, the same result obtains where the losses are purely economic, relate to the subject matter of the contract, and the defendant owes no independent duty. Chapman Custom Homes, Inc. v. Dallas Plumbing Co., 445 S.W.3d 716, 718–19 (Tex. 2014); Sharyland Water Supply Corp. v. City of Alton, 354 S.W.3d 407, 415–16 (Tex. 2011). Arcadia’s alleged misrepresentations all concern the quality and performance of NexusCore—the subject of the MSLSA—and every dollar of damages sought is economic. No independent duty exists.  
               4. The integration clause and anti-reliance doctrine bar reliance on extra-contractual representations.  
              Section 12.1 is a comprehensive integration clause that supersedes “all prior and contemporaneous agreements, proposals, representations, and understandings, whether oral or written,” and provides that each party “has not relied upon any statement, representation, warranty, or agreement of the other Party except as expressly set forth in this Agreement.” Under Delaware law, a party who negotiates such a clause—represented by counsel—cannot later assert fraud based on the very extra-contractual representations the clause was designed to supersede. Eagle Indus., Inc. v. DeVilbiss Health Care, Inc., 702 A.2d 1228, 1232 (Del. 1997); SIGA Techs., Inc. v. PharmAthene, Inc., 67 A.3d 330, 344 (Del. 2013); Kuhn Constr., 990 A.2d at 401.  
               5. Reliance was not justifiable.  
              Even under Texas law, justifiable reliance is required. Hollcroft-Storms v. Pac. Mut. Life Ins. Co., 51 S.W.3d 573, 577 (Tex. 2001). The FAC’s own allegations defeat justifiable reliance: Arcadia conducted independent due diligence, including reference calls with existing Meridian clients (FAC ¶ 31); received express disclaimers in the written proposal (FAC ¶ 27); and was represented by experienced counsel who negotiated the MSLSA’s risk-allocation provisions, including the limitation of liability (FAC ¶ 32). A sophisticated party that investigates, receives disclaimers, and negotiates contractual protections cannot justifiably rely on a salesperson’s promotional puffery. Presidio, 784 F.2d at 680; Eagle Indus., 702 A.2d at 1232. Count II must be dismissed.  
               D. Count III (Negligent Misrepresentation) Fails for Lack of an Independent Duty and Because the Damages Are Contractual.  
               1. No independent duty.  
              Texas recognizes negligent misrepresentation under Restatement (Second) of Torts § 552, but liability requires a duty of care independent of any contractual obligation. McCamish, Martin, Brown & Loeffler v. F.E. Appling Interests, 991 S.W.2d 787, 791–92 (Tex. 1999). Meridian’s relationship with Arcadia is purely contractual. The MSLSA’s express warranty (§ 9.1) and warranty disclaimer (§ 9.4) define the scope of Meridian’s obligations regarding software performance. There is no fiduciary relationship, no professional duty, and no statutory duty independent of the contract. The claim is therefore barred. Id.; see also Sharyland, 354 S.W.3d at 415–16; Chapman, 445 S.W.3d at 718–19.  
               2. The damages are benefit-of-the-bargain, not out-of-pocket.  
              A negligent misrepresentation claim is available only for “out-of-pocket” losses—actual expenditures made in reliance on false information—not for benefit-of-the-bargain damages. Fed. Land Bank Ass’n of Tyler v. Sloane, 825 S.W.2d 439, 442–43 (Tex. 1992). Arcadia seeks the $14.7 million contract value, $18.4 million in lost profits, and $6.7 million in increased operating costs (FAC ¶¶ 62–64)—all benefit-of-the-bargain and consequential damages that sound in contract, not tort. Id. Arcadia’s true complaint is that NexusCore did not perform as contractually warranted—a breach of contract claim, not negligent misrepresentation.  
               3. Rule 9(b), puffery, and the reliance defenses also apply.  
              Because the negligent misrepresentation claim sounds in fraud, it must satisfy Rule 9(b). Benchmark, 343 F.3d at 724. It does not, for the reasons stated in Section IV.C.1. The alleged statements are puffery (Section IV.C.2), and reliance was not justifiable (Section IV.C.5). Count III must be dismissed.  
               E. Count I (Breach of Contract) Fails Under the Deemed-Acceptance, Warranty, and Exclusive-Remedy Provisions; In the Alternative, the Claimed Damages Are Barred by the Contractual Limitations.  
               1. Deemed acceptance bars the defective-software theory.  
              Section 5.3 of the MSLSA gives the licensee 30 days following deployment to conduct acceptance testing and deliver written notice of any Material Nonconformity; failure to do so results in “deemed” acceptance. (MSLSA § 5.3(a), (d).) SOW-1 fixes the Acceptance Testing Period at 30 days following the Go-Live Date. (SOW-1 § 4.2.) NexusCore went live on January 15, 2023 (FAC ¶ 45), making February 14, 2023 the deadline for a Nonconformity Notice. Arcadia did not deliver any written notice of nonconformity until Dr. Okonkwo’s email of March 8, 2023 (FAC ¶ 50)—22 days after the acceptance window closed. Under the deemed-acceptance provision, the software was conclusively accepted as of February 14, 2023.  
              Deemed-acceptance provisions in software contracts are enforceable and provide a bright-line rule. Simulados, Inc. v. Canton Health Mgmt. Co., 2019 WL 4573218, at *5–6 (W.D. Tex. Sept. 20, 2019) (enforcing an identical provision; oral and post-deadline complaints do not satisfy the written-notice requirement); Precision Healthcare Sols. v. Nextera Data Sys., 458 F. Supp. 3d 544, 551–52 (N.D. Tex. 2020) (deemed acceptance applies to latent as well as patent defects). The FAC’s post-Go-Live defect allegations (FAC ¶¶ 49–54) are therefore post-acceptance complaints channeled to the warranty provisions, not a basis for rejecting the software or asserting fundamental breach.  
               2. The limited warranty expired, and its remedy is exclusive.  
              Section 9.1 warrants that the software would “perform substantially in accordance with the Documentation” for 90 days following Go-Live—a period that expired on April 15, 2023. The sole and exclusive remedy for any breach of that warranty is repair or replacement, or, if Meridian cannot cure within 60 days, a pro-rata refund of pre-paid license fees. (MSLSA § 9.1(b).) Section 8.3 designates the MSLSA’s remedies as Arcadia’s “sole and exclusive remedies.” The FAC does not allege that Arcadia invoked the warranty process or that Meridian failed to cure within the contractual period; to the contrary, the FAC acknowledges that Meridian released software patches on May 2 and June 19, 2023. (FAC ¶ 53.) The breach-of-contract claim, to the extent premised on software defects, is limited to the expired warranty’s exclusive remedy.  
               3. The delay theory is attributable to Arcadia under § 3.5.  
              To the extent Count I rests on the 137-day Go-Live delay (FAC ¶¶ 45, 72(b)), Section 3.5 provides that the project schedule “shall be adjusted on a day-for-day basis” for delays caused by the licensee’s failure to fulfill its responsibilities, and that Meridian “shall not be liable for any failure to meet the Target Go-Live Date … to the extent such failure is caused by Licensee’s breach.” The FAC itself acknowledges that the parties executed four Change Orders to address evolving scope (FAC ¶¶ 46–47)—each mutually executed under § 5.4. The FAC’s conclusory attribution of the entire delay to Meridian (FAC ¶ 72) is not plausible. See Iqbal, 556 U.S. at 682.  
               4. In the alternative, the claimed damages are barred by §§ 8.1 and 8.2.  
              Even if any breach-of-contract claim survives, the FAC’s $47.3 million damages demand is barred as a matter of law. Section 8.2 waives all “indirect, incidental, special, consequential, punitive, or exemplary damages, including … damages for loss of profits, goodwill, use, data, or other intangible losses.” The $18.4 million lost-profits claim (FAC ¶ 63), the $6.7 million increased-operating-costs claim (FAC ¶ 64), the $5 million reputational-harm claim (FAC ¶ 65), and any punitive damages are consequential damages expressly waived. Section 8.1 caps each party’s aggregate liability at the fees paid or payable during the 12 months preceding the event giving rise to liability. Limitation-of-liability and consequential-damages-waiver provisions in negotiated commercial software agreements between sophisticated parties are enforceable. Dresser-Rand Co. v. Virtual Automation, Inc., 361 F.3d 831, 838–40 (5th Cir. 2004); Kana Software, Inc. v. Sealand Tech., Inc., 178 A.3d 1045, 1058–60 (Del. Ch. 2017). Arcadia was represented by counsel and unsuccessfully sought to modify these very provisions (FAC ¶ 32)—confirming that the risk allocation was deliberate and informed. Count I must be dismissed, or in the alternative, the FAC’s damages demands must be stricken to the extent they exceed the contractual limitations.  
               V. CONCLUSION  
              For the foregoing reasons, Meridian respectfully requests that the Court grant this Motion and dismiss all counts of the First Amended Complaint with prejudice, and award Meridian such other and further relief, including its reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs, as the Court deems just and proper.

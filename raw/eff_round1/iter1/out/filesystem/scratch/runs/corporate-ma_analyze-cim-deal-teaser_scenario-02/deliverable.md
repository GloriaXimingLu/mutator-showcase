           1.2 Key Findings  
      CES is a genuinely attractive lower-middle-market environmental services platform with a defensible integrated service model, real regulatory tailwinds (notably PFAS), and a credible 16-year operating track record.             The business merits advancing to an IOI.         However, the marketing materials contain a number of material financial inconsistencies and aggressive assumptions that, taken together, meaningfully affect both the earnings base used for valuation and the cash-flow profile a financial sponsor would underwrite. The most important are:  
             The headline Adjusted EBITDA of $17.1M does not foot.         Reported EBITDA of $12.1M plus the six listed adjustments totaling $4.7M equals             $16.8M, not $17.1M         — a $0.3M overstatement that appears in both the CIM and the Financial Exhibits. At an 8.0x–10.0x multiple this is $2.4M–$3.0M of enterprise value.  
             The CIM’s summary financials and the detailed Financial Exhibits do not reconcile         across the income statement (COGS, gross margin, operating income, net income), the balance sheet (cash, PP&E, total assets, debt, equity), and the cash flow statement. The Exhibits’ income statement is also internally inconsistent: reported EBIT of $11.1M plus D&A of $4.2M implies EBITDA of $15.3M, yet EBITDA is stated as $12.1M (a $3.2M unreconciled gap).  
             Stated DSO of 52 days is unsupported.         Accounts receivable of $16.9M against $87.3M of revenue implies             ~71 days    , not 52 — above the 45–55 day sector norm and the 60-day threshold that signals collection risk. The 52-day figure would require AR of only ~$12.4M.  
             Maintenance capex of $3.0M (3.4% of revenue) is well below the 5–7% sector range         ($4.4M–$6.1M) and is presented as             declining         as a percentage of revenue — a pattern consistent with pre-sale capex deferral observed in Thornfield’s GreenWorks and Allied Waste investments. Normalizing to ~$5.5M reduces annual free cash flow by ~$2.5M.  
             PFAS growth projections are aggressive.         FY2025E PFAS revenue of $14.0M implies 125.8% growth for a two-year-old practice, versus the 30–50% company-level rate indicated by sector data and Thornfield’s GreenWorks analog. PFAS is modeled to drive ~64% of incremental revenue growth through FY2026E, yet no contracted PFAS backlog breakout is provided.  
             The $1.2M regulatory add-back is likely overstated.         Sector and portfolio data indicate an ongoing regulatory/compliance run-rate of $200K–$500K; only the excess should be added back, reducing the defensible add-back to ~$0.7M–$0.9M.  
             The largest customer’s MSA expires March 31, 2025 — before the IOI deadline.         Pacific Northwest Refining Co. (PNR) is 21.4% of revenue; renewal status is the single most urgent diligence item.  
             Customer-identity and relationship-tenure data conflict between the CIM and the Backlog Report         for the entire bottom half of the top-10 (customers #6–#10 are different companies in the two documents) and for several start dates in the top 5.  
        1.3 Recommendation  
          Proceed to submit a non-binding IOI by July 14, 2025    , with valuation anchored on a buyer-normalized Adjusted EBITDA of             ~$16.0M–$16.5M         (FY2024) rather than the seller’s $17.1M, and a multiple of             8.0x–9.0x         reflecting the concentration, capex, PFAS-execution, and key-person risks identified herein. This implies an enterprise value indication of approximately             $130M–$148M    , with a path to the upper end contingent on satisfactory resolution of the Phase II diligence items in §7. The IOI should be explicitly conditional on (a) confirmation of the PNR contract renewal, (b) a PFAS contracted-backlog breakout, (c) a fleet age/condition and capex assessment, and (d) a 10-year regulatory enforcement history. Detailed guidance is in §8–9.

        3. Investment Thesis / Attractive Attributes  
              Integrated, regulated platform with real barriers to entry.         Four-state hazardous waste transporter permits, DEQ contractor license, PE-stamped remediation work, and a purpose-built environmental staging yard are not quickly replicated. The integrated remediation/cleaning/emergency-response model drives cross-selling and share-of-wallet.  
              Genuine growth track record.         12.5% revenue CAGR (FY21–FY24) is above the 5–10% Pacific Northwest sector norm, even after discounting the PFAS contribution. Reported EBITDA margin expanded from 12.1% to 13.9%.  
              Regulatory tailwinds.         EPA’s 2024 PFAS drinking water rule and Oregon/Washington state initiatives create a multi-decade demand cycle for investigation and remediation. CES is an early mover with dedicated PFAS sampling/treatment equipment.  
              Revenue visibility and stickiness.         $42.6M contracted backlog; ~90% MSA/service-agreement renewal rate; no top-10 customer lost in five years; high switching costs (safety qualifications, regulatory knowledge, on-site integration).  
              Fragmented market with consolidation runway.         Management has identified 5–7 tuck-in targets; CES can serve as a regional platform, consistent with Fund III’s strategy.  
              Fit with Fund III mandate.         Environmental services is a core Fund III end market, and Thornfield has direct operating experience via GreenWorks Remediation and Allied Waste Systems (Fund II) that is directly applicable.

         6. Key Risks and Diligence Priorities  
       6.1 Customer Concentration and Near-Term Contract Expirations  
      Top-10 concentration is 71.0% (top-5 52.5%), typical for the sector but meaningful.             The most urgent issue is PNR (21.4% of revenue), whose MSA expires March 31, 2025 — before the July 14 IOI deadline.         Renewal status must be confirmed immediately; a lapsed or renegotiated PNR contract is a deal-altering event. Other near-term expirations: Meridian L&P (Dec 31, 2025), and several #6–#10 contracts in 2026. Willamette Steel (5.6%) is on a month-to-month arrangement with no contractual security. Oil & Gas/Refining is 25.7% of revenue, with PNR alone representing ~83% of that sector — concentrating cyclical exposure in a single relationship.  
        6.2 PFAS Execution Risk  
      The growth thesis hinges on a two-year-old practice more than doubling in FY2025E. Key constraints: (i) no contracted PFAS backlog breakout is provided (signed vs. pipeline); (ii) PE headcount fell from three to two in January 2025 and has not been replaced, while PFAS work requires PE oversight — projected growth likely requires hiring 2–3 additional PEs in a tight market with 6–12 month recruitment timelines; (iii) larger national firms are entering PFAS with greater resources. Request the PFAS backlog breakout and management’s PE hiring plan as a first diligence step.  
        6.3 Capex Understatement / Fleet Condition  
      Stated maintenance capex (3.4% of revenue) is 150–200 bps below the sector floor and declining as a percentage of revenue. Combined with $2.5M of unexplained goodwill and a $22M+ fleet at ~4.5 years average age, this raises deferred-maintenance and hidden-acquisition concerns. A detailed fleet age/condition assessment and a 3-year maintenance capex rebuild are essential; the FCF model should be re-run at ~5.5–6.0% maintenance capex.  
        6.4 Regulatory Cost Recurrence and Compliance History  
      The DEQ enforcement action (improper storage of contaminated soils at the Portland facility) is presented as isolated. Request CES’s complete 10-year regulatory enforcement history across all four states. Oregon DEQ enforcement has been increasing; a pattern of prior actions would suggest systemic issues and a higher ongoing run-rate. Normalize the EBITDA bridge to a $300K–$500K regulatory run-rate.  
        6.5 Key-Person and Management Transition  
      Oakes (68% owner, 61) is central to strategy, customer relationships, and the real-estate arrangement. His planned 18–24 month transition creates execution risk; the GreenWorks analog saw a key project manager depart within six months of close. Janet Prewitt (VP Operations, 18 years) is a critical retention target.             Retention/rollover agreements for Prewitt and other key operators should be a condition of the IOI and a closing condition.         Confirm the feasibility and timing of recruiting a replacement CEO at the assumed $500K package.  
        6.6 Real Estate / Related-Party Arrangements  
      The HQ (8.5 acres, purpose-built environmental staging) is owned by Oakes and leased to CES at $1.4M/yr through 2029 — $0.8M above estimated market. The $0.8M EBITDA add-back is only realizable if the buyer secures market-rate occupancy, which requires either (a) purchasing the property from Oakes at fair value (estimated ~$6–8M at a ~7.5–8% cap on $600K market rent — additional cash consideration to the seller), (b) renegotiating the lease with a seller who retains leverage, or (c) relocating (operationally disruptive for a customized facility). The process letter explicitly asks bidders to address this. The IOI should state the buyer’s intended treatment and condition the lease add-back on a satisfactory resolution.  
        6.7 Earnings Quality and Adjustments  
      Collectively, the adjustments move EBITDA from $12.1M (reported, 13.9%) to $17.1M (stated, 19.6%) — a 41% uplift. After the arithmetic correction, regulatory normalization, and conditional treatment of the lease, a buyer-normalized figure is closer to             $16.0M–$16.5M (18.3%–18.9%)    . The gap between reported and adjusted is large enough that the QoE should test every adjustment against supporting documentation.  
        6.8 Sizing vs. Fund III Mandate  
      The CIM’s implied EV of $136.8M–$171.0M (and $157.6M–$197.0M forward) is at/above the upper bound of the         “    $30M–$100M enterprise value    ”         platform target cited in the sector memo, even though that memo states CES         “    fits squarely within these parameters.    ”         The deal team should confirm Fund III’s actual capacity and single-asset exposure limits for a transaction of this size (the $425M fund can likely support it, potentially with a co-invest), and reconcile the mandate language before committing to a process that may anchor on a $170M+ EV.

         8. Transaction Structure and Process Considerations  
              Structure.         Seller expects a stock purchase of 100% equity, cash-free/debt-free, with normalized NWC. Existing Cascadia Commercial Bank debt ($17.3M per CIM; ~$19.1M per Exhibits — reconcile) to be refinanced/repaid at close.  
              Real estate.         State the intended treatment of the Oakes-owned HQ (purchase at fair value vs. market-rate lease) and condition the lease add-back accordingly. Request a property appraisal and the existing lease terms in the VDR.  
              Phantom equity.         Size the aggregate cash settlement liability and confirm treatment (deduction from equity proceeds). Ensure all phantom holders are identified.  
              Seller transition.         Accommodate Oakes’ 18–24 month advisory transition, but tie any earnout or rollover to (i) PNR renewal, (ii) PFAS revenue milestones, and (iii) successful CEO recruitment — not solely to time. Consider a seller note or rollover equity (~5–10%) to align Oakes on post-close performance and the real-estate outcome.  
              Earnout.         Given the PFAS projection risk, an earnout tied to FY2025–FY2026 PFAS revenue (e.g., $0.50–$1.00 per $1.00 of PFAS revenue above an agreed threshold, capped) is a reasonable mechanism to bridge the valuation gap between seller and buyer views.  
              Financing.         Indicate committed Fund III equity and anticipated leverage (sector-typical ~3.0x–4.0x EBITDA, ~$48–66M on the normalized base), with named lenders. Note the term loan matures August 2026, providing a natural refinancing window.  
              Approvals.         Confirm Fund III IC and LPAC approval thresholds and timeline; flag any antitrust/HSR considerations (low risk given size, but confirm).

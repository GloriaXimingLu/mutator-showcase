        II. Guiding Principles  
      The compromise package is constructed around four principles, each of which is reflected in the drafting and rationale that follow.  
          1. Market-anchored.         Every proposed term is supported by the KTM Survey of 42 broadly syndicated 1L/2L intercreditor agreements closed January 2022 through December 2024, all governed by New York law (40 of 42) or Delaware law (2 of 42), with total funded secured debt in excess of $400 million. Where the survey identifies a clear median or majority practice, the compromise tracks it. Where the agents’ positions bracket the market, the compromise lands at the market center. This grounds the package in objective, defensible precedent rather than in either side’s opening posture.  
          2. Within each side’s stated flexibility.         The compromises are calibrated to the flexibility signals communicated by Thomas Buchanan III (Aldgate & Pryor LLP, for the 1L Agent) and Naomi Oduya (Sternfeld Yates & Carroll LLP, for the 2L Agent) in their respective without-prejudice communications, as well as to the formal positions set forth in the agents’ position letters. No proposed term requires either side to cross a stated firm line. Where a side has identified a         “    deal-breaker    ”         or a         “    non-negotiable,    ”         the compromise respects it.  
          3. Economically quantified.         The capital structure is tight — Total LTV of 97.66%, a 2L equity cushion of 25.78% ($165 million), and total leverage of 6.99x — and the economic consequences of each intercreditor term are correspondingly magnified. The rationale for each provision quantifies the dollars at stake so that the agents’ credit committees can evaluate the package on its economics, not only on its drafting.  
          4. Indivisible.         As noted above, the four provisions are offered as a single package. The rationale for each provision explains not only why it is acceptable to each side in isolation, but how it contributes to the overall balance that makes the package acceptable as a whole.

        IV. Provision 1 — Standstill Period (Section 3.01(b))  
       A. Respective Opening Positions  
              1L Agent:         A 270-day standstill, commencing on delivery of a Blockage Notice, with             no early-termination events         under any circumstance. The 1L Agent’s position letter expressly rejects early-termination carve-outs based on (among other things) involuntary bankruptcy filings, third-party foreclosures, collateral-value diminution, or any failure by the 1L to commence enforcement within a specified period.  
              2L Agent:         A 90-day standstill, measured from receipt of the Blockage Notice, with early-termination triggers including (i) cessation of active 1L enforcement activity for a specified period, (ii) disposition of substantially all of the collateral, and (iii) filing of a plan of reorganization that impairs 2L claims without 2L consent.  
        B. Proposed Compromise Language  
      The following is proposed to replace the bracketed text in Section 3.01(b) of the draft ICA:  
      Following the occurrence and during the continuance of a Second Lien Default, the Second Lien Agent shall not exercise any rights or remedies with respect to the Collateral for a period of one hundred eighty (180) days following the date of receipt by the Second Lien Agent of a Blockage Notice specifying the Second Lien Default giving rise thereto (the         “    Standstill Period    ”    ). The Standstill Period shall terminate prior to the expiration of such one hundred eighty (180)-day period upon the occurrence of any of the following early termination events (each, an         “    Early Termination Event    ”    ), each of which shall be objectively verifiable:  
      (A) the First Lien Agent (or any First Lien Lender) shall have credit bid all or any portion of the First Lien Obligations in connection with a sale or other disposition of all or substantially all of the Collateral;  
      (B) the First Lien Agent shall have commenced a foreclosure, levy, or other realization upon, or the liquidation of, all or substantially all of the Collateral;  
      (C) the Borrower or any other loan party shall have consummated a sale, lease, or other disposition of all or substantially all of the Collateral (whether pursuant to Section 363 of the Bankruptcy Code or otherwise) without the consent of the Second Lien Agent; or  
      (D) a plan of reorganization or liquidation shall have been filed in any Insolvency Proceeding that impairs the Second Lien Obligations without the consent of the Second Lien Agent.  
      Upon the occurrence of any Early Termination Event, the Standstill Period shall terminate immediately and the Second Lien Agent may exercise its rights and remedies in accordance with Section 3.03. The First Lien Agent may deliver Blockage Notices from time to time during the term of the Second Lien Credit Agreement; provided that (x) no Blockage Notice shall be delivered while a Standstill Period is then in effect, and (y) the aggregate number of days during which one or more Standstill Periods are in effect shall not exceed three hundred sixty (360) days in any rolling three hundred sixty-five (365)-day period. Each Blockage Notice shall give rise to a separate Standstill Period.  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Duration — 180 days.         A 180-day standstill is the precise median of the KTM Survey (median 180 days; mean 172.9 days; range 90–270 days; 25th percentile 150 days; 75th percentile 202.5 days). It is a meaningful concession by the 2L Agent, which doubles its opening 90-day position, and a meaningful concession by the 1L Agent, which reduces its opening 270-day position by one-third. Critically, 180 days falls squarely within the 1L Agent’s stated flexibility: Mr. Buchanan has advised that the credit committee         “    could live with something in the 150- to 180-day range,    ”         with 150 days as the hard floor. A 180-day period sits at the top of that range and gives the 1L syndicate a full six months — a complete calendar half-year — to pursue a restructuring, an orderly asset sale, or a refinancing without 2L interference. It is also within the 2L Agent’s stated flexibility: Ms. Oduya has advised that Ridgeline         “    can accept up to a 180-day standstill,    ”         provided it is paired with meaningful early-termination triggers. The duration and the triggers are therefore offered together, as the 2L Agent has insisted they must be.  
          Early-termination triggers — narrow and 1L-action-based.         The proposed triggers are drawn from the most common triggers observed in the KTM Survey and from the 2L Agent’s enumerated concerns, but are deliberately limited to events that are (i) objectively verifiable and (ii) based on affirmative actions by the 1L Agent or the Borrower, rather than on the 1L Agent’s inaction. This design respects the 1L Agent’s formal objection (in its position letter) to inaction-based triggers — specifically its rejection of any trigger premised on         “    any failure by the First Lien Agent or the First Lien Secured Parties to commence an Enforcement Action within any specified period    ”         — while delivering the         “    meaningful early termination triggers    ”         that the 2L Agent has identified as a firm condition.  
      The four proposed triggers map directly to market practice:  
              Trigger (A) — 1L credit bid.         Credit bidding by the 1L is the second-most-common trigger in the survey, appearing in 22 of 42 deals. Once the 1L itself credit bids the debt to acquire the collateral, the standstill’s purpose — affording the 1L a protected period to pursue its own remedies without 2L interference — has been fulfilled, and the 2L should be released to protect its residual interest in the sale proceeds.  
              Trigger (B) — 1L foreclosure/liquidation of substantially all collateral.         This is the single most common trigger in the survey, appearing in 29 of 42 deals. It is the paradigm case for early termination: the 1L has itself commenced the realization that the standstill was designed to give it time to pursue.  
              Trigger (C) — sale of substantially all collateral without 2L consent.         This appears in 9 of 42 deals (sale motion without 2L consent) and addresses the 2L Agent’s enumerated concern regarding disposition of substantially all of the collateral. Limiting the trigger to sales             without         2L consent preserves the 2L’s plan-participation rights without impeding consensual sales.  
              Trigger (D) — plan impairing 2L without 2L consent.         This addresses the 2L Agent’s enumerated concern regarding a plan of reorganization that impairs 2L claims without 2L consent, and protects the 2L’s bankruptcy-participation rights.  
      The pairing of a ≥180-day standstill with early-termination triggers is the clear market standard:             25 of the 42 surveyed deals combine a standstill of 180 days or longer with early-termination triggers.         By contrast, only 10 of 42 deals (24%) employ a standstill with no triggers at all — the 1L Agent’s opening formulation — and those tend to be smaller, non-sponsor transactions with thicker equity cushions than Meridian’s. The 1L Agent’s cited precedents (Hartsfield/Consolidated Metals at 270 days with no triggers; Kingsbridge/Atlas Materials at 300 days with no triggers) are real, but they are outliers relative to the broader 42-deal dataset, and both involved either a thicker equity cushion (Hartsfield, ~28%) or a standstill 30 days longer than the 1L Agent is seeking here (Kingsbridge). The compromise’s 180-day-with-triggers formulation is both more representative of current market practice and more defensible to both credit committees.  
          Blockage-notice cap.         Neither side’s bracketed proposal included a cap on the number of Blockage Notices. The proposed cap — no stacking (no notice while a standstill is in effect) and an aggregate limit of 360 standstill days in any rolling 365-day period — directly addresses the 2L Agent’s stated concern that the 1L could         “    run out the clock on Second Lien remedies while collateral value declines    ”         by serially resetting the standstill. With 180-day standstills, the cap permits the 1L up to two full standstill periods per year (360 days), preserving robust protection, while guaranteeing the 2L a window of access and foreclosing the perpetual-blockage scenario. This is a 2L-protective element that balances the 180-day duration (a 1L-protective element) and is an essential component of the package’s balance.  
          What each side gains and concedes.         The 1L Agent gains a standstill twice as long as the 2L’s opening position and within the 1L’s own 150–180 day flexibility range, plus a blockage cap that permits up to 360 days of protection per year. The 1L concedes the inclusion of early-termination triggers (versus its         “    no triggers    ”         opening), but those triggers are limited to 1L affirmative actions and exclude the inaction-based and involuntary-bankruptcy triggers the 1L expressly rejected. The 2L Agent gains meaningful, market-standard early-termination triggers (its firm condition) and a blockage cap preventing perpetual blockage, while conceding a 180-day duration (double its 90-day opening) and accepting triggers narrower than its opening set (no cessation-of-enforcement trigger, no involuntary-petition trigger).

       A. Respective Opening Positions

              1L Agent:         Par — 100% of funded principal plus accrued interest at the             default rate         plus all fees, costs, and expenses;             mandatory         assumption of all unfunded revolver commitments ($125 million); exercisable only upon an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement.  
              2L Agent:         The             lesser of par         (at the non-default contract rate)             or fair market value         determined by an independent dealer poll;             no         obligation to assume unfunded revolver commitments (which would terminate upon purchase); exercisable upon a 1L Event of Default, a 2L Event of Default, or delivery of a 1L acceleration notice.  
        B. Proposed Compromise Language  
      The following is proposed to replace the bracketed text in Section 5.01 of the draft ICA:  
      At any time following (i) the occurrence and during the continuance of an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement or (ii) the delivery by the First Lien Agent of a notice of acceleration of the First Lien Obligations, the Second Lien Agent (on behalf of the Second Lien Lenders) shall have the right (but not the obligation) to purchase all (but not less than all) of the First Lien Obligations (the         “    Second Lien Purchase Right    ”    ) by paying to the First Lien Agent in immediately available funds a purchase price (the         “    Purchase Price    ”    ) equal to the sum of:  
      (i) one hundred percent (100%) of the aggregate outstanding principal amount of all funded First Lien Obligations (including all amounts outstanding under the First Lien Term Loan A and all drawn amounts under the First Lien Revolver);  
      plus (ii) all accrued and unpaid interest thereon, calculated at the non-default contract rate applicable under the First Lien Credit Agreement (i.e., SOFR plus 250 basis points), through and including the date of purchase, calculated on the basis of a 360-day year for the actual number of days elapsed;  
      plus (iii) all fees, costs, and expenses (including reasonable and documented fees and disbursements of counsel), indemnification obligations, breakage costs (including any swap breakage or early termination costs), yield protection amounts, and all other amounts then due and payable or accrued under or in connection with the First Lien Loan Documents, whether or not invoiced and whether fixed or contingent.  
      As a condition to the purchase, the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) may, at its election, either (A) assume all unfunded commitments under the First Lien Revolver by executing and delivering to the First Lien Agent an assumption agreement in form and substance reasonably satisfactory to the First Lien Agent, pursuant to which the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) shall be irrevocably obligated to fund subsequent draws by the Borrower on terms no less favorable to the Borrower than those set forth in the First Lien Credit Agreement; or (B) decline to assume such unfunded commitments, in which case the unfunded commitments under the First Lien Revolver shall terminate concurrently with the consummation of the purchase, and the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) shall, concurrently with the purchase, cash collateralize all then-outstanding letters of credit issued under the First Lien Credit Agreement in an amount equal to one hundred five percent (105%) of the undrawn face amount thereof. The election under this clause shall be made by the Second Lien Agent in the Purchase Notice.  
      The purchase shall be on a par, non-recourse, as-is, where-is basis, without representation or warranty of any kind by the First Lien Agent or any First Lien Lender (other than representations as to the authority of the assigning lender to assign and the enforceability of the transfer).  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Purchase price — par for funded debt (Sub-issue A).         Par for funded debt is the 1L Agent’s stated non-negotiable, and the 2L Agent has acknowledged that         “    par for funded debt may be acceptable.    ”         The compromise adopts par (100% of funded principal) as the purchase-price mechanic, dropping the 2L’s         “    lesser of par or FMV    ”         formulation. This is market-standard:             71% of surveyed deals (30 of 42) use a par mechanic    , and pure FMV deals are rare (10%, and typically only where the 2L is held by a single CLO). The FMV/dealer-poll construct is dispensed with as a concession to the 1L, in reliance on the 1L’s reciprocal concessions on the interest rate and the revolver assumption (see below) — illustrating why the package must remain indivisible.  
          Interest rate — non-default contract rate (Sub-issue B).         The compromise calculates accrued interest at the non-default contract rate (SOFR + 250 bps; 7.83% all-in at current SOFR of 5.33%), rather than the default rate (SOFR + 450 bps; 9.83% all-in). This is within both sides’ flexibility: Mr. Buchanan has advised that the credit committee         “    may be able to accept accrued interest calculated at the non-default contract rate,    ”         and Ms. Oduya has confirmed the 2L is         “    willing to discuss    ”         the non-default rate. It is also the clear market majority:             69% of surveyed deals (29 of 42) exclude default-rate interest from the par calculation and use the non-default contract rate    , while only 31% (13 of 42) include default-rate interest. Notably, two of the three precedents the 1L Agent itself cited (Hartsfield/Consolidated Metals and Briarcliff/Weston Energy Partners) used the non-default rate; only Kingsbridge/Atlas Materials used the default rate. The non-default rate is therefore both the market majority and consistent with the majority of the 1L’s own cited precedents.  
      The economic magnitude of this point is modest but real. The 200 basis-point spread between the default and non-default rates, applied to the $475 million funded facility, produces the following incremental interest over the standstill period:

          Unfunded revolver assumption — optional (Sub-issue C).         The compromise makes assumption of the $125 million in unfunded revolver commitments             optional at the purchaser’s election    , rather than mandatory. This is the 2L Agent’s firm condition: Ms. Oduya has advised that Ridgeline’s fund limited partnership agreement imposes structural constraints that         “    prevent the fund from taking on open-ended revolving credit commitment obligations,    ”         and that mandatory assumption is         “    a deal-breaker given the LPA constraints.    ”         Mr. Buchanan has acknowledged this is         “    a practical consideration, not a positional one    ”         and that the committee         “    may be able to soften this requirement, potentially making assumption of unfunded Revolver commitments optional rather than mandatory.    ”         Optional assumption is also the clear market trend:             62% of surveyed deals (26 of 42) make revolver assumption optional    , against only 24% (10 of 42) that make it mandatory, and the trend is accelerating — optional assumption rose from 50% of 2022 deals to 77% of 2024 deals. The comparable Deal-007 uses optional assumption.  
      The compromise addresses the 1L Agent’s legitimate concern — that permitting the purchaser to acquire funded debt without addressing the unfunded commitments would bifurcate the 1L syndicate and leave the revolver in limbo — through the election-and-termination mechanism. If the purchaser elects to assume, it executes a customary assumption agreement and the facility remains intact for the Borrower. If the purchaser declines (as Ridgeline’s LPA may require), the unfunded commitments             terminate concurrently         with the purchase, and the purchaser             cash-collateralizes outstanding letters of credit at 105%    . There is no limbo: the revolver is either cleanly assumed or cleanly terminated, and LC beneficiaries are protected. This is a materially better outcome for the 1L than the 2L’s opening position (which would have left the revolver commitments to terminate by their own terms without an LC-cash-collateralization backstop), and it respects the 2L’s structural constraint.  
      The economic significance of the optional structure is substantial. At full draw, a mandatory-assumption requirement would force the 2L to deploy approximately $484.3 million to acquire funded debt             plus         commit to fund up to $125 million of future revolver draws — pushing the 2L’s total capital at risk to approximately $609 million (and, with the 2L’s existing $150 million position, to approximately $759 million of total exposure), a figure that approaches and could exceed the Borrower’s $640 million TEV. In a distressed scenario in which TEV has already declined below the appraised value, requiring the 2L to commit capital in excess of enterprise value to acquire the senior position would be, as the 2L Agent has argued, economically irrational and would render the purchase right a dead letter. The optional structure preserves the purchase right as a viable protective mechanism — which is, after all, its purpose in the intercreditor framework.  
          Exercise triggers — 1L Event of Default or 1L acceleration (Sub-issue D).         The compromise permits exercise upon (i) a 1L Event of Default or (ii) delivery of a 1L acceleration notice. This is narrower than the 2L’s opening position (which would have added a 2L Event of Default as a trigger) but broader than the 1L’s opening position (1L Event of Default only) by adding the acceleration trigger. The acceleration trigger is universal in the market —             100% of surveyed deals (42 of 42) permit exercise upon acceleration of the 1L         — and is objectively verifiable. Excluding the 2L Event of Default as a standalone trigger is consistent with market practice: the survey’s exercise triggers are uniformly 1L-centric (acceleration 100%; commencement of enforcement 74%; payment default beyond cure 45%; bankruptcy filing 38%), and a 2L-only default trigger (which would permit the 2L to buy out a performing 1L) is not supported by the data and is outside the 1L’s flexibility. The compromise therefore gives the 2L a modest, market-standard expansion of the trigger set while remaining within the 1L’s flexibility.  
          What each side gains and concedes.         The 1L Agent gains its non-negotiable par purchase price for funded debt, the elimination of the FMV/dealer-poll construct, and a trigger set limited to 1L-centric events. The 1L concedes the non-default interest rate (versus its default-rate opening) and optional revolver assumption (versus mandatory), but obtains the election-and-termination mechanism with LC cash collateralization that resolves its bifurcation concern. The 2L Agent gains the non-default interest rate, optional revolver assumption (resolving its LPA constraint), and the acceleration trigger, while conceding par (versus lesser-of-par-or-FMV) and the 2L-Event-of-Default trigger.  
      
         VI. Provision 3 — Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness (Section 2.03(c))  
       A. Respective Opening Positions  
      The draft identifies three sub-issues: (A) the principal-increase cap; (B) the maturity floor; and (C) the restrictiveness test.  
              1L Agent:         (A) 0% cap (equal or lesser principal only); (B) maturity no earlier than 91 days after the 1L Maturity Date (i.e., September 14, 2030); (C) a blanket         “    no more restrictive in any material respect    ”         test.  
              2L Agent:         (A) up to 120% of original 2L principal ($30 million increase); (B) maturity no earlier than the original 2L Maturity Date (June 15, 2031); (C) no restrictiveness test.  
        B. Proposed Compromise Language

        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Principal-increase cap — 110%, ring-fenced to fees and costs (Sub-issue A).         The compromise permits refinancing indebtedness of up to 110% of the principal being refinanced — a $15 million increase on the current $150 million Second Lien Term Loan B — but             ring-fences the incremental $15 million to refinancing-related fees, costs, premiums, and expenses    , with an express prohibition on use of the excess for general corporate purposes. This is within the 2L Agent’s stated flexibility: Ms. Oduya has advised that Ridgeline         “    can accept a 10% principal increase cap … provided the increase is limited to refinancing-related fees and costs.    ”         It is a movement from the 1L’s 0% position, but one that is well-supported by the market and that does not impair the 1L’s core concern.  
      The market data is decisive.             The median principal-increase cap in the KTM Survey is 10%    , with 52% of deals (22 of 42) falling in the 1–10% range. Critically,             79% of deals that permit any increase (26 of 33) limit the increase to fees, costs, and premiums only         — precisely the structure proposed here. The comparable Deal-007 (a $475M 1L / $150M 2L structure identical to Meridian’s) employs a 10% cap ($15 million) limited to fees and costs. A 0% cap, by contrast, is employed in only 21% of deals (9 of 42) and tends to appear in smaller, non-sponsor transactions with thicker equity cushions.  
      The ring-fence is what makes the 110% cap acceptable to the 1L. The 1L Agent’s objection to any principal increase is rooted in its concern that incremental junior secured debt would compress the 25.78% equity cushion and push the 97.66% Total LTV toward or beyond 100%. That concern is fully addressed by limiting the $15 million to transaction costs: because the excess pays refinancing costs (not the Borrower’s general indebtedness), the Borrower’s net secured leverage is unchanged. The gross refinancing facility is $165 million, but $15 million of that is consumed by OID, call premiums, arranger fees, and transaction expenses, leaving $150 million to refinance the existing Second Lien Term Loan B.             Total LTV remains 97.66%; the equity cushion is not compressed.         By contrast, the 2L’s original 20% proposal ($30 million), if not ring-fenced, could have increased total secured debt to $655 million and pushed Total LTV to approximately 102.3% — the precise outcome the 1L underwrote against. The ring-fenced 110% cap avoids that outcome entirely.  
      The $15 million (10%) figure is also appropriately sized to the costs it is permitted to cover. Customary refinancing costs on a $150 million second lien facility include: a call premium of 102% in year one or 101% in year two (approximately $3.0 million or $1.5 million, respectively, on $150 million); arranger and underwriting fees of 200–350 basis points (approximately $3.0 million to $5.25 million); original issue discount of 97–99 cents on the dollar (approximately $1.5 million to $4.5 million); and legal, advisory, and rating agency fees (approximately $1.0 million to $2.0 million). The aggregate of these customary costs falls in the range of approximately $8.5 million to $15.75 million, confirming that a $15 million (10%) cap is right-sized to cover bona fide refinancing costs without providing surplus capacity for incremental leverage.  
          Maturity floor —                 “        later of        ”                 both tests (Sub-issue B).         The compromise sets the refinancing maturity floor at the             later of         (A) 91 days after the First Lien Maturity Date (September 14, 2030) and (B) the original Second Lien Maturity Date (June 15, 2031). Because June 15, 2031 is approximately nine months (274 days) after September 14, 2030, the effective floor in the current capital structure is June 15, 2031.  
      This formulation satisfies both sides’ positions simultaneously and is the market-standard approach. The 1L Agent’s 91-day test is preserved (and, importantly, recalculates if the 1L Maturity Date is ever extended, so the 1L retains its temporal buffer in all scenarios). The 2L Agent’s original-maturity floor is also preserved. The KTM Survey confirms this is standard:             93% of deals (39 of 42) employ the 91-days-after-1L-maturity test, and 81% (34 of 42) also include the original-2L-maturity floor         — i.e., the         “    both tests    ”         approach is the majority practice. The comparable Deal-007 includes both.  
      A key point for the 1L Agent’s credit committee:             the 2L Agent’s maturity position does not disadvantage the 1L in any scenario.         The original 2L maturity of June 15, 2031 already exceeds the 1L’s 91-day floor by approximately nine months. Requiring the refinancing maturity to be no earlier than June 15, 2031 is therefore             more         restrictive than the 1L’s own floor, not less. The         “    later of    ”         formulation simply codifies this in a way that protects the 1L if the 1L maturity is ever extended (in which case the 91-day test would recalculate and could become the binding floor). There is no principled basis for the 1L to object to a maturity floor that is, in the current structure, more protective of the 1L than the 1L’s own proposal.  
          Restrictiveness test — narrowed test paired with MFN on financial covenants (Sub-issue C).         The compromise replaces the 1L’s blanket         “    no more restrictive in any material respect    ”         test with a             narrowed test         limited to three enumerated categories — (a) financial maintenance covenants, (b) events of default relating to cross-default or cross-acceleration to the First Lien Obligations, and (c) mandatory prepayment and stated-maturity provisions — and pairs it with an             MFN right         limited to financial maintenance covenants. This is precisely the middle ground both agents have signaled: Mr. Buchanan has advised that Cartwright is         “    open to a narrowed restrictiveness test … provided it is combined with a most-favored-nation right for the benefit of the 1L lenders,    ”         and Ms. Oduya has confirmed the 2L is         “    open to discussing whether a narrowed restrictiveness test paired with an MFN right could work.    ”  
      The market strongly supports this pairing.             52% of surveyed deals (22 of 42) use a narrowed restrictiveness test    , against 33% (14 of 42) that use a blanket test and 14% (6 of 42) that use no test.             100% of narrowed-test deals pair the test with an MFN provision    , and             82% of those MFN provisions (18 of 22) are limited to financial covenants only         — exactly the structure proposed here. The trend is decisively toward this formulation: the narrowed-test-plus-MFN combination rose from 38% of 2022 deals to 65% of 2024 deals, and the 10%-cap-for-fees/costs structure is         “    becoming modal.    ”         The comparable Deal-007 uses a narrowed test paired with an MFN on financial covenants.  
      The narrowed scope is calibrated to the 1L Agent’s actual concerns while preserving the 2L’s commercial flexibility. The 1L Agent’s position letter identified three specific risks from unrestricted refinancing terms: (1) a tightening of the Total Leverage financial maintenance covenant (currently 6.5x) that could trigger a 2L default while the Borrower remains in 1L compliance; (2) an expansion of negative covenants that could limit Borrower actions permitted under the 1L Credit Agreement; and (3) reduced cure periods that could accelerate 2L enforcement inconsistently with the standstill. The compromise addresses risk (1) directly through the narrowed test on financial maintenance covenants and the MFN right. It addresses the cross-default dimension of risk (1) through the narrowed test on cross-default/cross-acceleration events of default. It addresses maturity-shortening through the narrowed test on mandatory prepayment and stated-maturity provisions (reinforcing the maturity floor in clause (ii)). Risks (2) and (3) are addressed structurally rather than through the restrictiveness test: the ICA’s standstill (Section 3.01) governs 2L enforcement timing regardless of the 2L cure periods, so changes to 2L cure periods cannot undermine the standstill; and the Borrower’s own commercial judgment (and its need to remain in 1L compliance) constrains the adoption of restrictive negative covenants in any refinancing. Leaving negative covenants and cure periods outside the narrowed test preserves the 2L’s commercial flexibility — which is the 2L’s principal objective on this sub-issue — without creating the cross-default risk the 1L underwrote against.  
      The MFN right is the quid pro quo that makes the narrowed test acceptable to the 1L. Benchmarked against the existing 2L financial maintenance covenant (Total Leverage ≤ 6.5x), the MFN ensures that if a refinancing tightens the leverage covenant below the corresponding 1L threshold, the 1L Lenders automatically receive the benefit of the tighter covenant via a no-cost amendment to the First Lien Credit Agreement. This converts the 1L’s concern about covenant tightening from a risk into a benefit: any tightening inures to the 1L’s advantage. Limiting the MFN to financial maintenance covenants (the 82% market majority) keeps it focused and administrable, and avoids the open-ended         “    all covenants    ”         MFN that only 18% of deals employ.  
          What each side gains and concedes.         The 1L Agent gains a refinancing cap (110%) that is ring-fenced to fees and costs (so no incremental leverage and no equity-cushion compression), a maturity floor that is more protective than its own proposal in the current structure, a narrowed restrictiveness test covering the provisions that generate cross-default risk, and an MFN right that converts covenant-tightening into a 1L benefit. The 1L concedes the 110% cap (versus 0%) and the narrowed (versus blanket) test. The 2L Agent gains a refinancing cap sufficient to cover transaction costs (versus 0%), preservation of the original 2L maturity floor, a narrowed rather than blanket restrictiveness test, and freedom on all non-enumerated commercial terms. The 2L concedes the ring-fence (versus an unrestricted 20% increase) and the MFN right (versus no test).  
      
         VII. Provision 4 — Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds (Section 4.02)  
       A. Respective Opening Positions  
              1L Agent:         A 100% hard sweep of all Net Cash Proceeds from Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events to the First Lien Obligations,             overriding         the reinvestment rights in the First Lien Credit Agreement (including the 365-day reinvestment period and the $5 million individual / $15 million annual aggregate thresholds), with no 2L access until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations.  
              2L Agent:         Application in accordance with the First Lien Credit Agreement’s reinvestment and mandatory prepayment mechanics (365-day reinvestment period; $5M / $15M thresholds), with residual proceeds flowing to the Second Lien Obligations.  
        B. Proposed Compromise Language  
      The following is proposed to replace the bracketed text in Section 4.02 of the draft ICA:  
      (a) Bifurcated Application. All Net Cash Proceeds received by the Borrower or any other loan party in respect of any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event shall be applied as follows:  
      (i) Ordinary-Course Dispositions and Casualty Events. With respect to any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event the Net Cash Proceeds of which are less than $20,000,000 (each, an         “    Ordinary-Course Proceeds Event    ”    ), such Net Cash Proceeds shall be applied in accordance with the reinvestment and mandatory prepayment provisions of the First Lien Credit Agreement, including the Borrower’s right to reinvest such Net Cash Proceeds in replacement assets used or useful in the Borrower’s business during the three hundred sixty-five (365)-day reinvestment period set forth therein and the individual $5,000,000 and annual aggregate $15,000,000 mandatory prepayment thresholds set forth therein.  
      (ii) Large or Extraordinary Dispositions and Casualty Events. With respect to any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event the Net Cash Proceeds of which are equal to or exceed $20,000,000 (each, a         “    Large Proceeds Event    ”    ), one hundred percent (100%) of such Net Cash Proceeds shall be applied to prepay the First Lien Obligations in accordance with the mandatory prepayment provisions of the First Lien Credit Agreement, and the reinvestment rights granted to the Borrower under the First Lien Credit Agreement shall be superseded by this Section 4.02 with respect to such Net Cash Proceeds; provided that no portion of such Net Cash Proceeds shall be available for reinvestment by the Borrower, for prepayment of the Second Lien Obligations, or for distribution to the Borrower, its equity holders, or any other person, in each case until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations shall have occurred.  
      (b) Anti-Evasion. For purposes of this Section 4.02, all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events that are consummated pursuant to a single plan or series of related transactions, or that are consummated with any affiliate of the Borrower, shall be aggregated and treated as a single Asset Disposition or Casualty Event, as applicable, for purposes of determining whether the $20,000,000 threshold in Section 4.02(a) has been exceeded. No transaction shall be structured, and no series of transactions shall be undertaken, with the purpose or effect of evading the application of Section 4.02(a)(ii) by remaining at or below the $20,000,000 threshold.  
      (c) Residual Proceeds to Second Lien. Following the Discharge of First Lien Obligations, any remaining Net Cash Proceeds (whether from Ordinary-Course Proceeds Events or Large Proceeds Events) shall be applied to prepay the Second Lien Obligations in accordance with the mandatory prepayment provisions of the Second Lien Credit Agreement; provided that no such remaining Net Cash Proceeds shall be distributed to the Borrower or any other person until the Second Lien Obligations have been paid in full.  
      (d) Reporting. The Borrower shall deliver to the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent (i) a quarterly certificate of compliance setting forth all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events during the applicable fiscal quarter and the application of Net Cash Proceeds therefrom; (ii) prompt written notice (and in any event within five (5) Business Days) of any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event generating Net Cash Proceeds equal to or exceeding $5,000,000; and (iii) an annual aggregate disposition report setting forth an itemized list of all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events during the applicable fiscal year and the application of Net Cash Proceeds therefrom.  
      (e) Conflicts. In the event of any conflict between this Section 4.02 and the reinvestment or mandatory prepayment provisions of the First Lien Credit Agreement, the provisions of this Section 4.02 shall govern and control as among the First Lien Agent, the First Lien Lenders, the Second Lien Agent, the Second Lien Lenders, and the Borrower (without prejudice to Section 9.10).  
        C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Bifurcated framework — ordinary-course reinvestment plus hard sweep for large sales.         The compromise adopts a bifurcated structure that is the clear market-standard compromise and that falls within both sides’ stated flexibility. For Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events with Net Cash Proceeds below $20 million (ordinary-course), the ICA defers to the First Lien Credit Agreement’s existing reinvestment and mandatory prepayment mechanics — preserving the Borrower’s 365-day reinvestment right and the $5 million individual / $15 million annual aggregate thresholds. For events with Net Cash Proceeds of $20 million or more (large or extraordinary), the ICA imposes a hard 100% sweep to the First Lien Obligations, overriding reinvestment rights, until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations.  
      This bifurcation is precisely the approach both agents have signaled. Mr. Buchanan has advised that         “    a hard sweep of proceeds from large and/or extraordinary asset sales is essential … and is not subject to significant compromise    ”         (a firm requirement), but that         “    reinvestment rights for smaller, ordinary-course asset sales may be acceptable,    ”         and that         “    a workable approach might be for the ICA to defer to the 1L Credit Agreement’s existing reinvestment mechanics for ordinary-course sales below a defined threshold, while imposing a hard 100% sweep to 1L obligations for larger or extraordinary dispositions.    ”         Ms. Oduya has advised that the 2L         “    understands that hard sweeps on large or extraordinary asset sales may be necessary,    ”         and         “    could live with the 1L reinvestment mechanics applying for ordinary-course sales … provided that the residual after 1L application flows to the 2L rather than back to the Borrower.    ”         The compromise implements both descriptions exactly.  
      The market data confirms this is the dominant structure.             100% of surveyed deals sweep proceeds to the 1L         (universal).             81% (34 of 42) preserve a reinvestment right    , and of those,             91% (31 of 34) use a 365-day reinvestment period         tracking the First Lien Credit Agreement.             90% (38 of 42) include a hard sweep for large or extraordinary sales.         The combination — reinvestment for ordinary-course sales plus a hard sweep for large sales — is therefore the majority practice, not a novel construct. The comparable Deal-007 (a $475M 1L / $150M 2L structure) employs precisely this framework: a $20 million ordinary-course threshold with anti-evasion at the same level, a 365-day reinvestment period, a hard sweep for extraordinary sales, and 2L residual access after full 1L paydown.  
          The $20 million ordinary-course threshold.         A $20 million threshold is the             median         ordinary-course threshold in the KTM Survey (mean $19.7 million; range $10 million–$30 million), and             97% of deals that exclude ordinary-course sales include an anti-evasion clause at the same threshold    . The $20 million figure represents approximately 22.4% of LTM EBITDA ($89.4 million), meaning that any single asset sale or casualty event generating proceeds equal to roughly one-fifth of annual cash flow is treated as a large or extraordinary event subject to the hard sweep — a sensible dividing line for a manufacturer whose routine asset turnover (sale of worn-out or surplus equipment, ordinary-course inventory dispositions) will typically fall well below $20 million, while a disposition of a facility, a business line, or a major equipment fleet will exceed it. The $5 million individual and $15 million annual aggregate prepayment triggers in the First Lien Credit Agreement continue to operate for ordinary-course events below $20 million, so the 1L retains the benefit of its negotiated prepayment mechanics for routine sales; the ICA simply does not override those mechanics for sub-threshold events.  
          Anti-evasion language.         Both agents have independently flagged the need for robust anti-evasion protection, and the compromise includes it as an essential element. The anti-evasion clause aggregates all dispositions consummated pursuant to a single plan or series of related transactions, and all dispositions with affiliates, for purposes of the $20 million threshold test, and prohibits structuring to evade the hard sweep. This directly addresses Mr. Buchanan’s stated concern that         “    any threshold distinguishing ordinary-course from extraordinary sales must be carefully drafted to prevent evasion — specifically, to guard against a large disposition being structured as a series of smaller transactions,    ”         and Ms. Oduya’s parallel concern regarding         “    asset-stripping through a series of sub-threshold sales structured to stay below the … ordinary-course threshold.    ”         The 97% market prevalence of anti-evasion clauses at the ordinary-course threshold confirms this is standard and expected.  
          2L residual access after 1L Discharge.         The compromise provides that, following the Discharge of First Lien Obligations, any remaining Net Cash Proceeds flow to the Second Lien Obligations (not back to the Borrower). This is the 2L Agent’s minimum position: Ms. Oduya has characterized 2L residual access as         “    symbolically important even if economically limited,    ”         because the 2L lenders         “    need to demonstrate to their own investors and LPs that the intercreditor structure preserves at least a theoretical waterfall right after full 1L satisfaction.    ”         It is also the market majority:             79% of surveyed deals (33 of 42) provide 2L residual access         after 1L paydown. Critically, it is within the 1L Agent’s flexibility: Mr. Buchanan has advised that         “    the question of residual proceeds flowing to 2L after full 1L satisfaction … is something the syndicate would need to evaluate in context, but candidly it is less of a concern if the hard sweep for significant sales is preserved.    ”         Because the compromise preserves the hard sweep for all sales of $20 million or more, the residual-access provision is economically limited (it operates only after the 1L is paid in full) and does not impair the 1L’s recovery. Directing the residual to the 2L rather than to the Borrower is also more efficient than the 1L’s opening position (which would have left residual proceeds as unrestricted cash at the Borrower level): it deleverages the capital structure for the benefit of the next-priority secured creditors, consistent with the waterfall in Section 4.01.  
          Reporting mechanism.         The compromise adds a reporting framework — a quarterly compliance certificate, prompt event-driven notice for any event generating $5 million or more, and an annual aggregate disposition report — that neither side’s bracketed proposal included but that the drafting note identified as a gap. The reporting framework is market-standard (quarterly certificate 90% of deals; event-driven notice above the individual trigger 79%; annual aggregate report 38%) and is essential to the administrability of the bifurcated structure: without reporting, neither agent could verify compliance with the $20 million threshold, the anti-evasion aggregation, or the reinvestment-period mechanics. The reporting obligation is imposed on the Borrower (which is in the best position to report on its own dispositions) and benefits both agents equally.  
          Conflicts clause.         The compromise includes an express conflicts provision confirming that Section 4.02 governs over inconsistent reinvestment or prepayment provisions of the First Lien Credit Agreement as among the parties to the ICA. This implements the 1L Agent’s objective that the ICA waterfall control as between the lien levels, while preserving the First Lien Credit Agreement’s reinvestment rights as between the 1L Agent and the Borrower for ordinary-course events (where the ICA defers to the First Lien Credit Agreement). The provision operates consistently with, and without prejudice to, the general conflicts rule in Section 9.10 of the draft ICA.  
          What each side gains and concedes.         The 1L Agent gains its firm requirement — a hard 100% sweep for all large or extraordinary sales ($20 million and above), overriding reinvestment rights — plus robust anti-evasion protection, a reporting framework, and a conflicts clause establishing ICA control. The 1L concedes the preservation of reinvestment rights for ordinary-course sales below $20 million (versus its opening override of all reinvestment rights) and 2L residual access after 1L Discharge. The 2L Agent gains preservation of the Borrower’s reinvestment rights for ordinary-course sales, 2L residual access (its minimum position), and a reporting framework that gives it visibility into proceeds application. The 2L concedes the hard sweep for large sales (versus its opening position of following the 1L Credit Agreement mechanics for all sales) and accepts the $20 million threshold (which sweeps large sales that, under the 1L Credit Agreement alone, might have been reinvestable).

      The indivisibility of the package is not merely a negotiating tactic; it reflects the economic logic of the capital structure. The thin 25.78% equity cushion and 97.66% Total LTV mean that the value at stake in each provision is large relative to the cushion, and the provisions interact. A longer standstill (a 1L gain) is tolerable to the 2L only because it is paired with early-termination triggers and a blockage cap (2L gains). A par purchase price (a 1L gain) is tolerable to the 2L only because it is paired with the non-default rate and optional revolver assumption (2L gains). A hard sweep on large sales (a 1L gain) is tolerable to the 2L only because it is paired with ordinary-course reinvestment and residual access (2L gains). A refinancing cap (a 2L gain) is tolerable to the 1L only because it is ring-fenced to fees and costs and paired with an MFN (1L gains). Unbundling any one provision would break these linkages and is likely to cause the side that loses its offsetting concession to retreat from the compromise on the unbundled provision — reopening, rather than closing, the negotiation.  
      For these reasons, KTM recommends that the package be presented to both agents as a single, take-it-or-leave-it integrated proposal, with an express statement that no provision is offered on a standalone basis and that the Borrower is prepared to endorse and advocate for the package as a whole.

           IV. Issues and Risks  
       A. RSU Valuation Error (corrected in redline)  
      The Agreement states that the 5,000 accelerated RSUs are valued at         “    $10.00 per share, based on the most recent independent valuation conducted by Ridgepoint Valuation Services,    ”         yielding $50,000. This is             factually incorrect    . The employment records reflect two 409A valuations: $10.00 per share dated July 1, 2024, and             $12.50 per share dated January 15, 2025    . The January 15, 2025 valuation is the most recent. The Agreement therefore (i) contains a false recital that the $10.00 figure is the         “    most recent    ”         valuation, and (ii) understates the consideration by             $12,500         (5,000 × $2.50).  
      We have corrected the per-share value to $12.50, tied it to the January 15, 2025 valuation, and updated the aggregate to $537,500.             Recommendation:         confirm the corrected figure with Delano’s counsel; if they resist, note that the $10.00 recital is demonstrably inaccurate and exposes the Agreement to a misrepresentation challenge. The Compensation Committee’s discretion to approve accelerated vesting in separation agreements is confirmed in the records, so the acceleration itself is authorized.  
        B. Back-Pay Characterization (decision required — flagged in comment)  
      Section 3(b) pays $125,000         “    as back pay representing lost wages from the Separation Date through the Effective Date    ”         (March 14 – May 5, 2025, approximately 52 days). At Delano’s $218,000 base salary, straight-line wages for that period are approximately             $31,000         (approximately $37,000 including his 20% target bonus). The stated back-pay amount therefore exceeds actual lost wages by roughly $88,000–$94,000.  
      Characterizing the full $125,000 as W-2 wages creates two risks: (i)             payroll-tax exposure    —Greenleaf pays employer FICA (and applicable state payroll taxes) on the full wage amount, and the excess over actual wages is vulnerable to             IRS recharacterization    ; and (ii) internal inconsistency that Delano’s counsel or a tax authority could challenge.             Recommendation:         either (a) reduce the back-pay line to actual lost wages and move the balance into the §3(a) compensatory-damages (1099) bucket—keeping the total payment unchanged while curing the characterization—or (b) obtain a written opinion from tax counsel that the wage treatment is defensible. This is a business decision; we have flagged it in an inline comment rather than unilaterally changing the economics.  
        C. ADEA / OWBPA Release Compliance (corrected in redline)  
      The ADEA release in Section 5 is             likely invalid as drafted         because it fails two mandatory requirements of the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA), 29 U.S.C. § 626(f):  
              Consideration period.         The Agreement allows only             14 days         to consider the release. OWBPA requires             at least 21 days         for an individual (non-group) release. We have revised §5(c) and §16 to 21 days.  
              Revocation period.         The Agreement contains             no revocation right    . OWBPA requires a             7-day revocation period         after execution, during which the release is not effective. We added §5(g) (revocation mechanics), §5(h) (no waiver of post-execution claims), §5(i) (attorney-consultation advisement), and redefined the             Effective Date         in §1(b) as the eighth day after execution (i.e., the day after the revocation period expires without revocation).  
      Without these fixes, Delano could sign, accept the payment, and later assert that his ADEA waiver was not         “    knowing and voluntary,    ”         preserving his age-discrimination claim. The revisions cure this.             Recommendation:         ensure the payment-delivery timing (currently         “    within 14 days of the Effective Date    ”    ) is consistent with the new Effective Date, and do not disburse funds until the revocation period has expired.  
        D. Whistleblower, OSHA, and Agency-Reporting Protections (corrected in redline)  
      This is the area of greatest exposure in the original draft. Delano engaged in protected activity (an internal food-safety complaint and an OSHA complaint), and an OSHA investigation is             open and pending    . The original confidentiality (§6) and non-disparagement (§7) clauses contained             no carve-out for communications with government agencies    , and §15(a)(ii) broadly promised no future filings. These provisions conflict with:  
              OSHA/Food Safety Modernization Act whistleblower protections         (21 U.S.C. § 2220), which protect reporting of food-safety violations and restrict retaliatory confidentiality agreements;  
          the             Defend Trade Secrets Act         (18 U.S.C. § 1833(b)), which requires a specific immunity notice in any agreement that governs trade secrets; and  
          general principles preserving the right to report violations to, and cooperate with, government agencies and to receive whistleblower awards.  
      An overbroad confidentiality clause can itself constitute an adverse action and create             independent liability    . We added: (i) a government-agency exception (§6(v)) and a general agency-reporting carve-out; (ii) the             DTSA immunity notice    ; (iii) a government-agency carve-out to non-disparagement (§7); and (iv) a carve-out in §15(a)(ii) expressly preserving Delano’s right to participate in the Oregon OSHA investigation.             Important:         the release of Delano’s             retaliation         claim does             not         terminate the underlying OSHA investigation, which proceeds independently; Greenleaf remains exposed on the underlying food-safety matter regardless of this settlement.  
        E. Non-Competition Enforceability (partially corrected; further decision recommended)  
      Section 9 imposes an 18-month non-compete across Oregon, Washington, Idaho, and California. Two problems:  
              California.         California broadly voids post-employment non-competes (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600). Including California renders the provision unenforceable as to California and risks California-law entanglement. We             removed California         (and Idaho), narrowing the scope to Oregon and Washington.  
              Oregon / post-termination settlement.         ORS 653.295 generally requires a non-compete to be entered into at hire or upon a bona fide advancement, and imposes notice and salary requirements. A non-compete imposed on a             terminated         employee through a settlement may not satisfy the statute, and the tolling clause could extend the restriction beyond 18 months. We capped tolling so the total period does not exceed 18 months.  
          Recommendation:         the enforceability of a post-termination settlement non-compete in Oregon is uncertain. Consider whether the             non-solicitation (§10)         and             confidentiality (§6)         provisions—which are more readily enforceable—adequately protect Greenleaf, and whether the non-compete should be dropped entirely to avoid a provision that gives false security and invites challenge. This is flagged in an inline comment.  
        F. Pretext / Replacement-Hire Risk (CRITICAL — not curable by drafting)  
      The employment records show that Greenleaf hired             Raj Mehta         into a substantially similar supply-chain/logistics role on             March 20, 2025—six days after Delano’s termination—at $185,000    . This contemporaneous replacement directly contradicts the stated rationale that Delano’s position was         “    eliminated … due to restructuring,    ”         and constitutes powerful             pretext evidence         supporting Delano’s ORS 654.062 retaliation claim (protected activity → adverse action → replacement) and his ADEA claim (a 58-year-old replaced within days by a lower-paid hire).  
      This is the single largest risk in the matter and is             not curable by redlining the Agreement    . It materially weakens Greenleaf’s         “    legitimate business reorganization    ”         defense and strengthens Delano’s leverage.             Recommendation:         weigh this exposure in deciding whether the current settlement value (now $537,500) is appropriate; preserve all documents concerning the Mehta hire and the restructuring rationale; and ensure any future position statements are consistent with the documented facts. This is flagged in an inline comment on the         “    legitimate business reorganization    ”         recital.  
        G. EPLI Coordination (decision required — flagged in comment)  
      The records identify an EPLI carrier—        Timberline Insurance Group, Policy No. TIG-EPL-2024-00391    —but the Agreement does not address insurance. Before execution, coordinate with the carrier to confirm: (i) the settlement is within policy limits and covered; (ii) whether             carrier consent to settle         is required (many EPLI policies require it); (iii) allocation between covered employment claims and any uncovered matters (e.g., the underlying food-safety/OSHA issues); and (iv) the portion of the $537,500 the insurer will fund. If consent is required, consider adding a             condition precedent         making the Agreement subject to carrier approval. This is flagged in an inline comment.  
        H. PTO and 2024 Bonus Verification (verification required — flagged in comment)  
      Section 2 represents that Delano         “    has received all wages … including payment for all accrued but unused paid time off.    ”         The records do not confirm a PTO payout, and the             2024 performance bonus was not determined or paid         before termination. We added a Greenleaf representation confirming PTO payment under ORS 652.610 and acknowledging that the 2024 bonus was unpaid (with the release covering any bonus claim).             Recommendation:         verify that PTO was in fact paid at termination and document the 2024-bonus status before signing; if PTO was not paid, cure it or revise the representation, as a false wage representation could itself support a wage claim.  
        I. Employment Reference (revised in redline)  
      Section 11 requires Greenleaf to provide a             “        positive        ”         reference and to state that Delano         “    made significant contributions.    ”         Requiring affirmative positive statements is unusual and can create exposure if the statements are later challenged as inaccurate. We revised the obligation to a             neutral         reference (dates, title,         “    employed in good standing and eligible for rehire    ”    ).             Recommendation:         confirm this is acceptable to Delano’s counsel; the neutral formulation is more defensible for Greenleaf.  
        J. Other Items  
              Governing law / venue (added).         The original Agreement contained             no governing-law, venue, or jury-trial provisions    . We added §19 (Oregon law; exclusive venue in Multnomah County; jury-trial waiver). Note: if Delano later works in or relocates to California, California may still refuse to enforce the non-compete notwithstanding the Oregon choice of law; the narrowed scope mitigates but does not eliminate this.  
              Return of company property (added).         The original Agreement lacked a return-of-property representation. Given Delano’s access to supply-chain and confidential information, we added §20.             Verify         that property was in fact returned before relying on the representation.  
              §1542 waiver.         Section 4 contains a waiver of         “    unknown claims    ”         but does not cite California Civil Code § 1542. Under Oregon law this is acceptable. If any California connection remains (e.g., Delano worked with California operations), add the statutory citation and the post-2023 amended §1542 language.  
              Attorney-fee payment.         The $75,000 paid directly to Engel & Associates (§3(c)) is reported on Form 1099-NEC. The tax treatment of direct-pay attorney-fee awards has specific nuances post-2018; confirm the reporting and allocation with tax counsel.  
              Non-disparagement duration.         We changed         “    in perpetuity    ”         to a three-year term; perpetual non-disparagement is aggressive and a defined term is more defensible.  
              Cooperation clause.         The Agreement contains no cooperation clause. Given Delano’s knowledge and the pending OSHA matter, consider whether a limited cooperation provision is desirable (sensitive, given his whistleblower status)—we did not add one.  
              No-admission (§13).         Appropriate; no change.  
         V. Recommendations and Next Steps  
              Accept the drafted revisions         in the redline as the company’s counter, and circulate to Delano’s counsel.  
              Correct the RSU valuation         to $12.50/share and the aggregate to $537,500; confirm with the Compensation Committee and Delano’s counsel.

        VI. Conclusion  
      With the drafted revisions, the Agreement can be made legally sound as to the ADEA release, whistleblower/agency-reporting protections, and the non-compete scope.

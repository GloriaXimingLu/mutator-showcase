       A. The Five Contested Provisions

        III. Provision 1 — Management Committee Composition and Voting (Section 5.01)  
       A. Proposed Compromise Clause Language  
          Section 5.01 Management Committee; Composition; Voting; Deadlock.  
          (a) Composition.         The business and affairs of MKPoly shall be managed by a Management Committee (the         “    Board    ”    ) consisting of five (5) members, of whom three (3) shall be appointed by Meridian (the         “    Meridian Designees    ”    ) and two (2) shall be appointed by Kastor (the         “    Kastor Designees    ”    ). Each Member shall appoint and may remove its designees at its sole discretion, and shall fill any vacancy in its designees promptly. A designee may be removed with or without cause. Each Member shall notify the other Member and MKPoly in writing of any appointment, removal, or replacement of its designees.  
          (b) Quorum and Ordinary Voting.         A quorum for the transaction of business shall consist of four (4) members of the Board, including at least one (1) Meridian Designee and at least one (1) Kastor Designee. Except with respect to Reserved Matters (as defined in subsection (c)), all matters coming before the Board shall be approved by the affirmative vote of a simple majority of the members present at a duly convened meeting at which a quorum is present. The Board shall meet not less frequently than quarterly and may act by unanimous written consent in lieu of a meeting.  
          (c) Reserved Matters.         The following matters (the         “    Reserved Matters    ”    ) shall require the affirmative vote of at least four (4) of the five (5) members of the Board (which necessarily includes the affirmative vote of at least one (1) Kastor Designee and at least two (2) Meridian Designees), regardless of whether a quorum is otherwise present:  
          any amendment, modification, or restatement of this Agreement or the Certificate of Formation;  
          the voluntary dissolution, winding up, or liquidation of MKPoly, or the filing of a voluntary petition under any federal or state bankruptcy, insolvency, or receivership statute;  
          the sale, transfer, lease, or other disposition of all or substantially all of the assets of MKPoly, or the disposition, transfer, or encumbrance of any single material asset of MKPoly having a fair market value in excess of $5,000,000;  
          the admission of any new member to MKPoly, or the issuance of any new membership interests to any person other than the existing Members on a pro-rata basis;  
          the entry into, material amendment of, or termination of any transaction between MKPoly and either Member or any affiliate of either Member (a         “    Related-Party Transaction    ”    );  
          any material change to the nature or scope of the business of MKPoly as described in Section 2.04 and Schedule 2.04 (Business Purpose);  
          the authorization of any capital call in excess of $5,000,000 in the aggregate during any fiscal year;  
          the incurrence of indebtedness by MKPoly in excess of $5,000,000 in any single transaction or $12,500,000 in the aggregate outstanding at any time;  
          the initiation or settlement of any litigation or claim involving an amount in controversy exceeding $1,000,000; and  
          any transaction constituting a change of control of MKPoly.  
      For the avoidance of doubt, the following matters are             not         Reserved Matters and shall be approved by simple majority vote under subsection (b): approval of the annual operating budget; hiring, compensation, and termination of personnel below the level of Chief Executive Officer, Chief Financial Officer, Chief Operating Officer, and Chief Technology Officer; entry into customer, supplier, or vendor contracts in the ordinary course of business; pricing and product-development decisions; and any single-transaction expenditure that is within the approved annual operating budget and does not exceed $1,000,000.  
          (d) Officer Appointments.         The Chief Executive Officer and the Chief Financial Officer of MKPoly shall be appointed by Meridian and shall serve at the pleasure of the Board. The Chief Operating Officer and the Chief Technology Officer shall be appointed by the affirmative vote of a simple majority of the Board. All other officers and senior management below the C-suite level shall be appointed by the Chief Executive Officer.  
          (e) Delegated Management Authority.         Day-to-day management of MKPoly shall be vested in the Chief Executive Officer, who shall have authority to take any action, and to authorize any expenditure, that (i) is within the approved annual operating budget and (ii) does not involve a single-transaction expenditure in excess of $1,000,000. Actions or expenditures outside this delegation require Board approval under subsection (b) or (c), as applicable.  
          (f) Deadlock Resolution.         A         “    Deadlock    ”         exists when the Board is unable to reach the vote required to approve a matter after the matter has been considered at two (2) successive duly convened meetings. Upon the declaration of a Deadlock by either Member, the following tiered process shall apply:  
          Step 1 — Principals’ Negotiation.         The Chief Executive Officer of Meridian and the Chief Strategy Officer of Kastor (or their respective designees) shall engage in good-faith negotiation for a period of fifteen (15) business days to attempt to resolve the Deadlock. During this period, the status quo shall be maintained, and neither party shall take unilateral action on the deadlocked matter.             Step 2 — Mediation.         If the Deadlock is not resolved during the principals’ negotiation period, either party may initiate non-binding mediation administered by the American Arbitration Association (or a mediator mutually selected by the parties) within thirty (30) calendar days. The mediation period shall not exceed thirty (30) calendar days from the appointment of the mediator.             Step 3 — Final Resolution.         If the Deadlock persists after the mediation period:         (A) with respect to any matter that is             not         a Reserved Matter (an         “    Operational Matter    ”    ), the Chief Executive Officer shall cast the deciding vote to resolve the Deadlock; and         (B) with respect to any Reserved Matter, either party may initiate the buy-sell mechanism set forth in Section 10.03.

           C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Board composition (3–2).         Meridian holds 55% of MKPoly’s equity and contributed the venture’s core technology; Kastor holds 45% and contributed primarily cash. A board composition that tracks equity — three Meridian designees and two Kastor designees — is the standard mechanism for reflecting a majority equity position and is directly supported by the most comparable transaction in the dataset (a 60/40 specialty-coatings JV with a 3–2 board). Kastor identified board composition as an area of greater flexibility, signaling that the number of seats is secondary to the voting threshold on Reserved Matters. The compromise therefore adopts Meridian’s preferred 3–2 composition while preserving Kastor’s core protective mechanism through the Reserved Matters supermajority.             Meridian gains         its preferred board majority;             Kastor concedes         equal representation in exchange for the protective veto it values more.  
          Reserved Matters supermajority (4 of 5).         The supermajority threshold of four of five ensures that no Reserved Matter can be approved without the affirmative vote of at least one Kastor Designee — giving Kastor an effective veto on the matters most consequential to a minority member, while still requiring broad Meridian support (at least two Meridian Designees). This is the functional equivalent of the supermajority protection Kastor sought and is its single highest-priority term. The ten-item Reserved Matters list is a negotiated middle ground: it captures the genuinely existential decisions on which a minority partner’s protective interest is strongest (amendments, dissolution, asset sales, new members, related-party transactions, business-scope changes, material capital calls, material indebtedness, material litigation, and change of control), while expressly excluding the operational matters that Meridian identified as outside the scope of a minority veto (annual budget approval, sub-C-suite personnel, ordinary-course contracts, pricing, and product development). The express exclusion of these operational matters is critical: it prevents the supermajority requirement from becoming a de facto veto over day-to-day management, which would be inconsistent with Meridian’s 55% equity stake.             Kastor gains         a robust protective veto on the decisions that matter most to a minority member;             Meridian concedes         the supermajority itself but confines it to a defined, existential list and excludes operational decisions.  
          CEO casting vote on operational matters only.         The casting vote is the most sensitive element. Kastor’s position is that a casting vote held by a Meridian-appointed CEO, if applied to Reserved Matters, would render the supermajority protection illusory. Meridian’s position is that operational paralysis must be avoidable and that the majority equity holder should retain an ultimate tiebreak on operating decisions. The compromise resolves this by bifurcating the casting vote: it applies             only         to Operational Matters (matters that are not Reserved Matters), and only after a good-faith tiered escalation (principals’ negotiation and mediation). It does             not         apply to any Reserved Matter. This respects Kastor’s red line (no CEO casting vote on Reserved Matters) while preserving Meridian’s operational tiebreak.             Meridian gains         the operational tiebreak it requires to avoid paralysis;             Kastor gains         the assurance that the casting vote can never override its Reserved-Matter veto.  
          Tiered deadlock resolution.         The deadlock mechanism blends both parties’ approaches. Kastor sought a principals-escalation → mediation → forced-buyout sequence; Meridian sought a CEO casting vote. The compromise adopts Kastor’s escalation structure (principals’ negotiation for 15 business days, then non-binding mediation for 30 days) as a prerequisite to any unilateral resolution, which gives both parties a genuine opportunity to resolve disputes collaboratively. For Operational Matters, the CEO casting vote resolves the deadlock after escalation — preventing indefinite operational paralysis. For Reserved Matters, the buy-sell mechanism under Section 10.03 provides the terminal remedy — allowing either party to acquire the other’s interest at fair market value rather than permitting indefinite paralysis on an existential decision. This structure is consistent with the most directly comparable 55/45 transaction in the dataset, which used a chair casting vote on non-Reserved Matters and a mediation-then-buy-sell sequence on Reserved Matters.             Both parties gain         a credible, staged process;             Meridian concedes         the buy-sell as a terminal Reserved-Matter remedy (which it accepted as preferable to indefinite paralysis);             Kastor concedes         the CEO casting vote on operational matters (which it accepted in exchange for the Reserved-Matter carve-out).  
          Officer appointments and delegated authority.         Meridian’s sole right to appoint the CEO and CFO ensures that the officers with day-to-day management authority are aligned with the majority holder, which is consistent with both comparable majority-held transactions in the dataset. The delegation of authority to the CEO for in-budget actions below $1,000,000 ensures that routine operations are not subject to Board-level deadlock.             Meridian gains         officer-appointment control and operational delegation;             Kastor gains         Board approval of the COO and CTO (a check on senior technical leadership) and the protection that all out-of-budget or above-threshold actions return to the Board.

         IV. Provision 2 — Capital Calls: Cure Period, Dilution, and Loan Alternative (Section 4.03)  
       A. Proposed Compromise Clause Language  
          Section 4.03 Capital Calls; Failure to Fund; Dilution; Loan Alternative.  
          (a) Capital Call Authority and Notice.         The Board may authorize capital calls upon the Members at any time in accordance with Section 5.01; provided that any capital call in excess of $5,000,000 in the aggregate during any fiscal year shall constitute a Reserved Matter requiring the supermajority vote specified in Section 5.01(c). Unless otherwise agreed unanimously by the Members, capital calls shall be funded pro-rata in proportion to the Members’ respective equity interests. MKPoly’s Chief Financial Officer shall deliver written notice of each capital call to each Member specifying the amount, purpose, and due date of the call not less than fifteen (15) calendar days before the funding due date (the         “    Notice Period    ”    ).  
          (b) Funding Obligation; Cure Period.         Each Member shall fund its pro-rata share of any duly authorized capital call within sixty (60) calendar days after the expiration of the Notice Period (the         “    Cure Period    ”    ). A Member that funds its full pro-rata share within the Cure Period shall have no further obligation with respect to that capital call.  
          (c) Euro Funding Accommodation.         Notwithstanding any other provision of this Agreement, a Member may satisfy its funding obligation in euros at the European Central Bank reference rate of exchange in effect on the date of the capital call notice, with conversion to U.S. dollars to be effected by MKPoly’s treasury at MKPoly’s cost. This accommodation is available to either Member and is intended to accommodate multi-currency treasury operations without extending the Cure Period.  
          (d) Penalty Interest During Cure Period.         If a Member (the         “    Non-Funding Member    ”    ) has not funded its pro-rata share of a capital call as of the funding due date, penalty interest shall accrue automatically on the unfunded amount during the Cure Period at a rate per annum equal to the greater of (i) the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (    “    SOFR    ”    ) plus four hundred (400) basis points, or (ii) nine percent (9%). Penalty interest shall be payable to MKPoly for the benefit of the Funding Member upon funding or upon dilution, as applicable. If the Non-Funding Member funds its full pro-rata share plus accrued penalty interest within the Cure Period, no dilution shall occur.  
          (e) Loan Alternative.         At any time during the Cure Period, the Non-Funding Member may elect, by written notice to the Funding Member and MKPoly, to have the Funding Member advance the Non-Funding Member’s pro-rata share of the capital call as a member loan to MKPoly on behalf of the Non-Funding Member (a         “    Member Loan    ”    ). If the Member Loan is elected, no dilution shall occur with respect to that capital call, and the Non-Funding Member’s equity interest shall be preserved. The Member Loan shall be on the following terms:  
              Interest.         The Member Loan shall bear interest at a rate per annum equal to SOFR plus five hundred (500) basis points, compounding quarterly.  
              Repayment.         The Member Loan (principal plus accrued interest) shall be repayable in full within twelve (12) months of disbursement, in a single lump sum, from the Non-Funding Member’s share of distributions; no extensions or refinancing shall be permitted.  
              Security.         The Non-Funding Member shall grant to the Funding Member a first-priority security interest in its entire membership interest in MKPoly as collateral for the Member Loan, and the parties shall file UCC financing statements promptly upon disbursement.  
              Automatic Conversion on Default.         If the Member Loan is not repaid in full within the twelve-month repayment period, the outstanding balance (principal plus accrued interest) shall automatically convert to equity, resulting in dilution of the Non-Funding Member’s interest calculated on the same basis as dilution for failure to fund under subsection (f).  
              Single-Use Limitation.         The Member Loan alternative shall be available to a Member no more than once in any twenty-four (24) month period. Any subsequent failure to fund within that period shall result in automatic dilution under subsection (f) without the option of a Member Loan.  
              No Set-Off.         The Non-Funding Member shall not be entitled to set off any amounts owed to it by MKPoly or the Funding Member against the Member Loan repayment obligation.  
          (f) Dilution on Failure to Fund.         If the Non-Funding Member fails to fund its pro-rata share of a capital call (plus accrued penalty interest) within the Cure Period and has not elected the Member Loan alternative, the Non-Funding Member’s equity interest shall be diluted on a pro-rata basis in favor of the Funding Member. Dilution shall be calculated based on the unfunded capital call amount relative to total post-call capitalization at fair market value. The aggregate dilution of a Non-Funding Member’s equity interest shall not exceed fifteen percent (15%) of the Non-Funding Member’s then-current equity interest in any fiscal year (the         “    Dilution Cap    ”    ).  
          (g) Forced-Sale Trigger.         If a Member’s cumulative dilution equals or exceeds fifteen percent (15%) of its equity interest in any twenty-four (24) month period, the non-diluted Member may, at its option, deliver a written notice (a         “    Forced-Sale Notice    ”    ) requiring the diluted Member to either (i) sell its entire remaining interest in MKPoly to the non-diluted Member, or (ii) purchase the non-diluted Member’s entire interest in MKPoly, in each case at the valuation determined in accordance with Section 10.04. The diluted Member shall elect to buy or sell within thirty (30) days of the Forced-Sale Notice. This mechanism ensures that the Dilution Cap does not enable a Member to retain a substantial equity position indefinitely while persistently declining to fund the venture’s capital needs.

           C. Negotiation Rationale  
          Cure period (60 days; 75 days total).         Meridian’s opening 30-day cure period reflected a preference for funding certainty; Kastor’s 90-day request reflected the genuine operational complexity of converting euro-denominated reserves to U.S. dollars through a multi-level Swiss treasury process subject to regulatory reporting. The compromise cure period of 60 days (75 days total from the initial notice) sits at the midpoint of the parties’ positions and is supported by the comparable-transactions data, in which cure periods ranged from 45 to 60 days. The 60-day cure is materially shorter than Kastor’s request but, combined with the euro funding accommodation in subsection (c), directly addresses Kastor’s operational rationale: a Member may fund in euros at a locked exchange rate with MKPoly bearing the conversion cost, eliminating the foreign-exchange execution burden that justified the longer cure.             Meridian concedes         a doubling of its opening cure period (and accepts a dilution cap and loan alternative it initially opposed);             Kastor concedes         a reduction from its requested 90 days to 60 days, but receives the euro accommodation that makes the shorter period operationally workable.  
          Penalty interest (SOFR + 400 bps).         Penalty interest accruing during the Cure Period serves both parties’ interests: it compensates MKPoly (and, indirectly, the Funding Member) for the time value of delayed capital, and it creates a real economic incentive to fund promptly rather than free-ride on the cure period. The rate of SOFR + 400 basis points (or 9%, whichever is greater) is meaningfully above market, creating genuine cost for late funding, but is less punitive than the SOFR + 500 bps / 10% rate Meridian proposed at its second concession — a modest movement toward Kastor in recognition that the cure period exists to accommodate temporary, good-faith liquidity constraints rather than to punish them.             Meridian gains         a penalty mechanism that preserves the incentive to fund;             Kastor gains         a rate below Meridian’s punitive proposal, reflecting that short-term timing disruptions are not bad-faith conduct.  
          Dilution cap (15%) with forced-sale trigger.         This is the central compromise on dilution. Meridian’s opening position was uncapped immediate dilution; Kastor’s was a 10% annual cap. The 15% cap is the true midpoint and is directly supported by the most comparable 55/45 transaction in the dataset, which capped dilution at 15% per annum. A 15% cap is above Kastor’s 10% minimum (so Kastor accepts more dilution exposure than it sought) and below Meridian’s 20% walk-away floor (so Meridian accepts a cap it initially opposed). Critically, the cap is paired with a forced-sale trigger: if a Member’s cumulative dilution reaches 15% in any 24-month period, the non-diluted Member may compel a buy-sell at the Section 10.04 valuation. This backstop — also drawn from the comparable 55/45 transaction — addresses Meridian’s core concern that a dilution cap could enable a Member to underfund indefinitely while retaining governance rights disproportionate to its economic stake. The cap thus functions as a bridge to exit rather than a shield for persistent non-funding.             Meridian concedes         a cap it initially opposed, but gains the forced-sale backstop that prevents the cap from enabling free-riding;             Kastor gains         a dilution cap (its key ask), but concedes movement from 10% to 15% and accepts the forced-sale trigger that converts persistent non-funding into an exit event.  
          Loan alternative (SOFR + 500 bps).         Kastor’s minimum position required that a loan alternative be preserved; Meridian’s opening position was that no loan alternative should exist. The compromise includes a loan alternative — satisfying Kastor’s minimum — but at terms designed to preserve the incentive to fund. The rate of SOFR + 500 basis points is above the market rate Kastor sought (SOFR + 250 bps) but below the punitive rate Meridian proposed at its third concession (SOFR + 800 bps), landing at a commercially meaningful but not confiscatory level. The 12-month mandatory repayment, the first-priority security interest in the borrowing Member’s JV interest, the automatic conversion to equity on default, and the single-use-per-24-months limitation all ensure that the loan is a short-term bridge for temporary liquidity constraints — not an escape hatch from capital commitments. The automatic conversion on default guarantees that the end state for a non-repaying borrower is the same as dilution, with the lender compensated by interest in the interim.             Kastor gains         the loan alternative it required as a minimum;             Meridian concedes         the existence of a loan mechanism but structures its terms (rate, security, conversion, single-use) to preserve the economic pressure to fund.  
          Overall balance.         On this provision, both parties move substantially from their opening positions and land within the range supported by the comparable-transactions data (45–60 day cures; capped dilution with a forced-sale backstop). The provision is a middle-ground item on which both sides can claim concessions: Meridian accepts a longer cure, a cap, and a loan; Kastor accepts a shorter cure, a higher cap, a higher loan rate, and a forced-sale trigger.

          (f) Improvements to Contributed IP.         The parties acknowledge that, during the term of the JV, MKPoly’s research and development activity may produce improvements, modifications, derivative works, or adaptations of a party’s Contributed IP (each, an         “    Improvement    ”    ). The treatment of Improvements upon an IP Exit Event shall be as follows:  
              Classification.         An Improvement shall be classified as an Improvement to a party’s Contributed IP only if it is a derivative work that builds primarily upon that party’s underlying Contributed IP. An Improvement that builds equally upon both parties’ Contributed IP, or that is not primarily derivative of either party’s Contributed IP, shall be classified as Co-Developed IP and treated under subsection (c).  
              Improvements to Meridian Contributed IP.         Improvements to the Meridian Contributed IP shall revert to Meridian upon an IP Exit Event. Kastor shall receive a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to such Improvements for use outside North America, subject to the sublicensing restrictions in subsection (e).  
              Improvements to Kastor Contributed IP.         Improvements to the Kastor Contributed IP shall revert to Kastor upon an IP Exit Event. Meridian shall receive a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to such Improvements for use in the Americas.  
          (g) No Exclusive License to Kastor.         For the avoidance of doubt, no exclusive license to any Co-Developed IP shall be granted to Kastor in any territory. The license granted to Kastor under subsection (c)(ii) is non-exclusive, and Meridian retains the right to use the same Co-Developed IP in all territories pursuant to subsection (c)(i) and (c)(iii).

           C. Negotiation Rationale  
      This is the most complex of the five provisions and the one on which each party has identified its highest priority. The compromise is structured to respect both parties’ core objectives simultaneously: Meridian’s absolute requirement that its contributed formulations not reach its North American competitors, and Kastor’s requirement that it receive a meaningful, perpetual license to the intellectual property its capital helped create.  
          Contributed IP reversion and cross-license (asymmetric).         Both parties agree that contributed IP reverts to the contributing party — this is not contested. The cross-license terms reflect the acknowledged asymmetry between the parties’ contributions. Meridian’s 23 contributed IP items were independently appraised at $28,600,000 and include proprietary coating formulations (including the CR-7 and CR-9 systems) that constitute MKPoly’s core competitive differentiator and the reason the JV exists. Kastor’s 9 contributed items are primarily manufacturing process documentation and know-how — operationally important but not independently cash-valued and not competitively unique. The compromise grants no post-exit license to Kastor with respect to Meridian’s contributed IP (protecting Meridian’s red line that its proprietary formulations must not be licensable to Kastor or, through Kastor, to its competitors), while granting Meridian a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to Kastor’s contributed IP so that Meridian can continue to manufacture MKPoly-legacy products without interruption. This asymmetry is justified by the difference in value and competitive sensitivity of the two contributions and is consistent with every comparable transaction in the dataset, none of which permitted sublicensing of contributed IP.             Meridian gains         absolute protection of its contributed IP (its highest-priority red line);             Kastor concedes         that it receives no license to Meridian’s contributed IP, but retains reversion of its own contributed IP and the co-developed IP license-back described below.  
          Co-Developed IP license-back (both parties).         Kastor’s top priority is a broad co-developed IP license-back, on the rationale that its $43.8 million contribution — substantially in cash — directly funds the R&D that creates co-developed IP, and that it cannot fund the creation of valuable IP and then exit with no rights to use it. The compromise grants both parties a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual, irrevocable license to the co-developed IP — satisfying Kastor’s minimum requirement that the license be available to both parties on equivalent terms. Meridian receives an exclusive license in North America (and its active-operations territories), preserving its competitive position in its core market; Kastor receives a non-exclusive license outside North America, where its European and Asian operations are concentrated. Both parties may use the co-developed IP in their own internal operations globally.             Kastor gains         the broad, perpetual, bilateral license-back it identified as foundational to its participation;             Meridian concedes         a license to Kastor (from its opening position of a Meridian-only license) but retains exclusivity in its home market and the right to use the IP globally.  
          Sublicensing and the Restricted Competitors framework.         This is the mechanism that reconciles Kastor’s need to monetize co-developed IP through its global manufacturing and distribution partners with Meridian’s red line that its formulations must not reach its North American competitors. The compromise permits each party to sublicense co-developed IP within its respective territory (Kastor outside North America; Meridian in the Americas), subject to two overlapping restrictions: (i) a global prohibition on sublicensing to any entity on Schedule 12.05 (Restricted Competitors), their controlled affiliates, or their successors — a named-entity list of Meridian’s principal North American competitors, updated annually by mutual agreement; and (ii) a revenue-based negative covenant prohibiting sublicensing to any entity that derives more than 25% of its consolidated revenue from North American specialty polymer coatings, which operates as a dynamic anti-circumvention mechanism capturing new entrants and restructured entities not yet named on the schedule. The restrictions are reciprocal (applying equally to Meridian), addressing Kastor’s         “    equal treatment    ”         concern, and are accompanied by a mutual audit right. This structure gives Kastor meaningful commercial flexibility — it may sublicense to the vast majority of European and Asian chemicals companies, which are not North American coatings competitors — while ensuring that no pathway, direct or indirect, exists for co-developed IP to reach Meridian’s named competitors.             Kastor gains         sublicensing rights outside North America (its minimum requirement), structured with a reciprocal, named-entity, annually-updated framework (its minimum requirements for the Restricted Competitors carve-out);             Meridian gains         the global Restricted Competitors prohibition and the 25% revenue anti-circumvention covenant that together protect its red line, plus audit rights to verify compliance.             Meridian concedes         that Kastor may sublicense outside North America;             Kastor concedes         the global scope of the Restricted Competitors prohibition and the 25% revenue covenant.  
          Improvements to contributed IP (the                 “        improvements gap        ”        ).         Both parties flagged the treatment of JV-funded improvements to contributed IP as an unresolved issue. Kastor’s position was that improvements to contributed IP should be treated as co-developed IP (giving both parties broad license rights); Meridian’s position was that improvements to a party’s contributed IP should revert to that party, with a limited license to the other. The compromise adopts a         “    primarily derivative    ”         classification test: an improvement that builds primarily on one party’s contributed IP is treated as an improvement to that party’s IP (reverting to the contributor, with a non-exclusive territorial license to the other party subject to the sublicensing restrictions); an improvement that builds equally on both parties’ IP, or is not primarily derivative of either, is classified as co-developed IP and licensed to both under subsection (c). This approach is more nuanced than Kastor’s blanket         “    all improvements are co-developed IP    ”         proposal and protects the contributing party’s interest in improvements to its own underlying technology — an improvement to Meridian’s CR-7 formulation, for example, derives its value from the CR-7 base and reverts to Meridian — while still giving Kastor a non-exclusive, royalty-free, perpetual license to use such improvements outside North America, addressing Kastor’s legitimate interest in the return on the capital it contributed to fund the R&D that produced the improvement.             Meridian gains         reversion of improvements to its contributed IP and the         “    primarily derivative    ”         classification that prevents its base technology from being recharacterized as co-developed IP;             Kastor gains         a perpetual license to use improvements to Meridian’s IP outside North America (addressing its funding rationale), but concedes the         “    primarily derivative    ”         test and the territorial/sublicensing limits on that license.  
          Overall balance.         This provision is the most heavily negotiated and the one on which the parties’ priorities most directly intersect. The compromise gives Meridian its absolute red lines (no Kastor license or sublicensing of contributed IP; no sublicensing to Restricted Competitors globally; no exclusive co-developed IP license to Kastor) while giving Kastor its foundational requirements (a bilateral, perpetual, royalty-free co-developed IP license-back; sublicensing rights outside North America; a reciprocal, named-entity Restricted Competitors framework; and a meaningful license to improvements). The Restricted Competitors schedule and the 25% revenue covenant are the bridging mechanisms that make both sets of objectives achievable simultaneously. The structure is consistent with the comparable-transactions data, in which contributed IP sublicensing was universally prohibited and co-developed IP sublicensing was permitted subject to territorial and named-competitor restrictions.

         VIII. Closing Note  
      The five compromise provisions above are designed to be evaluated independently or as a package at the August 11, 2025 principals meeting. Each provision lands at or near the midpoint of the parties’ stated positions, is supported by the comparable-transactions data and (for the non-compete) by the multi-jurisdictional enforceability analysis, and respects the walk-away floors that each party has identified. No single provision requires either party to accept a term it has characterized as unacceptable, and the package as a whole preserves the balance that both parties seek: operational control and IP protection for the majority technology contributor, and protective governance rights, an independent and fair exit valuation, and a meaningful IP license-back for the minority capital contributor.  
      The parties’ counsel are available to refine the drafting of any provision, to populate Schedules 12.01, 12.02, and 12.05, and to integrate the agreed provisions into a clean consolidated draft of the JVA in advance of the target execution date of August 22, 2025.

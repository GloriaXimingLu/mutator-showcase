             I. PURPOSE  
           This memorandum flags the threshold issues the team should resolve or keep in view in connection with Defendant's Rule 12(b)(6) motion to dismiss (the "Motion"), which is due January 15, 2024. The Motion, the accompanying proposed order, and a separate certificate of conference are delivered herewith. The issues below are ordered by urgency. The most significant—a likely defect in the Court's diversity jurisdiction—is outside the merits but could affect the entire federal posture of the case and warrants a prompt strategic decision.  
             II. THRESHOLD ISSUES  
            1. Subject-Matter Jurisdiction — Apparent Diversity Defect (CRITICAL).  
           Our research identifies a likely defect in the Court's subject-matter jurisdiction. The case was removed on the basis of diversity under 28 U.S.C. § 1332(a). The Notice of Removal alleged that Arcadia is "a citizen of the State of Texas," relying solely on the domicile of Dr. Okonkwo. That allegation is incomplete and, on the present record, incorrect.  
           An LLC's citizenship is determined by the citizenship of all of its members.          Harvey v. Grey Wolf Drilling Co.        , 542 F.3d 1077, 1080 (5th Cir. 2008). A limited partnership's citizenship is determined by the citizenship of all of its partners, general and limited.          Carden v. Arkoma Assocs., Ltd.        , 494 U.S. 185, 195–96 (1990). The FAC and Arcadia's operating agreement disclose the following structure:  
            • Arcadia's members are (a) Dr. Okonkwo (Texas), (b) Martin Schreiber (Texas), and (c) Apex Medical Ventures, LP, a Delaware limited partnership. FAC ¶¶ 7–8.  
            • Apex's general partner is Apex Medical Ventures GP, Inc., a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Wilmington, Delaware—a citizen of Delaware. FAC ¶ 8; Operating Agreement Ex. A, Note 3(a).  
            • Apex's limited partner is the Okonkwo Family Trust, whose trustee and beneficiary are domiciled in Texas. FAC ¶ 8; Operating Agreement Ex. A, Note 3(b).  
           Accordingly, Arcadia is a citizen of both Texas (through Okonkwo, Schreiber, and the trust) and Delaware (through Apex's general partner). Meridian is a citizen of Delaware (incorporated there) and Texas (principal place of business in Austin).          Hertz Corp. v. Friend        , 559 U.S. 77, 92–93 (2010). Because both parties share citizenship in Delaware (and Texas), complete diversity does not exist. The Apex general partner's Delaware citizenship alone defeats diversity, regardless of how the trust's citizenship is treated.  
           Notably, the FAC is internally inconsistent on its face: ¶¶ 7–8 disclose the membership structure that destroys diversity, while ¶ 11 alleges that "complete diversity of citizenship exists." Subject-matter jurisdiction cannot be waived and may be raised at any time by any party or sua sponte by the Court.          Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp.        , 546 U.S. 500, 514 (2006).  
            Strategic considerations and recommendation:  
           (a) Estoppel. Meridian invoked diversity jurisdiction in the Notice of Removal. Under the voluntary-invocation / judicial-estoppel doctrine, a removing party may be estopped from challenging the very jurisdictional basis it alleged. Meridian therefore may be barred from itself moving to remand on diversity grounds. The Court, however, has an independent obligation to police jurisdiction and may raise the defect sua sponte at any time—including in response to the Motion.  
           (b) Forum. Federal court before Judge Alvarez is favorable to Meridian: rigorous Twombly/Iqbal pleading standards (Standing Order § 4.2) and a bench trial under the § 12.9 jury waiver (Scheduling Order). Texas state court applies liberal notice pleading and would be less hospitable to a pleading attack, and the jury waiver's enforceability there is less certain.  
           (c) Recommendation. File the Motion on schedule—the merits are strong and the federal forum is favorable. Do not move to remand (estoppel risk; unfavorable state forum). But prepare now for the Court to raise the jurisdictional issue sua sponte: (i) confirm the citizenship analysis (verify Apex GP's principal place of business and the trust's citizenship treatment under Fifth Circuit law); (ii) prepare a short jurisdictional position paper; and (iii) make a deliberate, documented decision on whether to proactively notify the Court. Because the FAC itself discloses the defect, the issue is effectively on the table and concealment is neither viable nor advisable. Please flag for the client that there is a meaningful risk of remand, which would reset the case in Bexar County state court. There is likely no cure on the present record because the Apex GP's Delaware citizenship defeats diversity independent of the trust.  
            2. Post-Answer Motion Characterization — Rule 12(b)(6) vs. 12(c).  
           The Standing Order (§ 3.4) provides that a preserved post-answer motion "shall [be] treat[ed] ... as a motion to dismiss, not as a motion for judgment on the pleadings under Rule 12(c)." The Scheduling Order, by contrast, states that the Court "will treat any such motion as a Rule 12(c) motion for judgment on the pleadings, evaluated under the same standard as a Rule 12(b)(6) motion." The two orders are in tension. The practical effect is nil: both apply the Twombly/Iqbal standard and the same scope of review. We titled the Motion a Rule 12(b)(6) motion, consistent with the Standing Order (the more specific and more recent authority on this point) and with the Answer's express preservation of "the right to file a motion to dismiss pursuant to Rule 12(b)(6)." No action is needed beyond awareness; if the Court questions the label, we can confirm the standard is identical.  
            3. Choice of Law — Delaware (Contract) vs. Texas (Torts).  
           Section 12.7 selects Delaware law to govern "this Agreement," without regard to conflict-of-law principles. That plainly governs Count I (breach of contract) and Count IV (unjust enrichment). For Counts II (fraud) and III (negligent misrepresentation), the scope of § 12.7 is debatable: a narrow reading covers only claims "under" the agreement; a broad reading covers all claims arising out of or relating to it. Under Texas's most-significant-relationship test (Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws §§ 145, 148), the tort claims likely are governed by Texas law (misrepresentations made and relied upon in Texas; injury in Texas).  
           We briefed the tort claims under both Delaware and Texas law, so the Motion succeeds under either outcome. Delaware law is more favorable (broader economic-loss doctrine; robust anti-reliance and integration-clause enforcement—         Eagle Industries        ,          Kuhn        ,          Brasby        ). Texas law is somewhat less favorable on the economic-loss rule (duty-focused,          Sharyland         /          Chapman        ) but still bars these claims (no independent duty; puffery; integration clause; no justifiable reliance). Recommendation: argue in the Motion that Delaware law governs all claims arising from the MSLSA, with Texas-law arguments in the alternative. Be prepared for Arcadia to argue Texas law governs the torts; our alternative arguments cover that.  
            4. DTPA § 17.505 Pre-Suit Notice — Possible Premature Filing.  
           Section 17.505(a) requires a consumer to give written notice at least 60 days before filing suit. Arcadia's DTPA pre-suit demand letter is dated June 15, 2023, although our July 10, 2023 response references a letter "dated June 26, 2023"—a date discrepancy in the record that should be reconciled. The Original Petition was filed August 7, 2023. If June 15 controls, the 60-day period expired August 14, making the August 7 suit seven days premature; if June 26 controls, the suit was roughly 18 days premature. Either way, Arcadia appears to have filed before the statutory notice period elapsed.  
           This is a potential additional ground to challenge the DTPA claim, but it is fact-dependent (we must confirm the correct notice date and whether any earlier notice was given), and courts are split on whether § 17.505 notice is mandatory or affects only timing and remedies. Because the § 17.49(f) exemption is cleaner and dispositive, we led with it. Recommendation: do not make § 17.505 a primary Motion argument; preserve it; reconcile the date discrepancy; and hold it as a strong alternative ground (likely for summary judgment or a developed record) should the § 17.49(f) exemption be rejected.  
            5. § 17.49(f) Exemption — Which Statutory Version Applies.  
           The statute has been amended over time. The current version exempts transactions with total consideration exceeding $500,000 unless the consumer is an individual. An earlier version also required the consumer to have assets exceeding $25 million. Our Answer's Thirteenth Affirmative Defense and Arcadia's own damages expert reference the asset-based version; the research memo relies on the current consideration-only version. Both are satisfied here: (a) consideration is $17,050,000 (well over $500,000) and Arcadia is an LLC, not an individual—dispositive under the current version; and (b) Arcadia's own damages expert (Grayson Whitmore) reports Arcadia's total assets at approximately $41 million, above the $25 million threshold—satisfying the asset-based version too. Recommendation: lead with the consideration-only argument (clean, dispositive, requires no asset proof) and plead the asset-based version in the alternative, supported by Arcadia's own ~$41 million asset figure. This insulates the argument against any dispute about which version applies.  
            6. Jury Trial Waiver (§ 12.9) — Enforceability.  
           The MSLSA contains a mutual jury-trial waiver (§ 12.9). The Scheduling Order confirms the parties waived jury trial and that the case will be tried to the bench. The FAC demands a jury (FAC ¶ 122), and our Answer demands a jury only "to the extent the Court determines the jury trial waiver ... is not enforceable." This does not affect the Motion, but it is a threshold issue for trial. The waiver was expressly negotiated (the negotiation correspondence confirms Arcadia's counsel agreed to it), and contractual jury waivers between sophisticated parties are generally enforceable under both Texas and Delaware law. Recommendation: no action on the Motion; if the case proceeds toward trial, move to enforce the waiver or strike the jury demand.  
            7. Scope of the Rule 12(b)(6) Record.  
           Under          Lormand         and Standing Order § 3.4, the Court may consider the MSLSA, SOW-1, and the four Change Orders (referenced in and central to the FAC) without converting the Motion to summary judgment. We deliberately did not rely on documents outside that record as the basis for dismissal—e.g., the August 25, 2022 project status report, the Schreiber due-diligence memo, the contract-negotiation emails, the support-ticket report, or the damages report—because doing so would risk conversion. Instead, we grounded the Motion in (a) the FAC's own admissions (¶¶ 27, 31, 32, 34, 46, 50, 61–66) and (b) the MSLSA/SOW-1 provisions. The extrinsic documents are preserved for summary judgment, where they will be powerful—particularly the negotiation emails in which Arcadia's counsel withdrew the proposed fraud carve-out and confirmed Arcadia was "proceeding on the basis of the express contractual terms rather than pre-contractual representations," and the Schreiber memo acknowledging that "[i]ntegration complexity is manageable but will require a skilled SI partner." Recommendation: keep the Motion within the Rule 12(b)(6) record; attach the MSLSA, SOW-1, and Change Orders as exhibits (or cite the docket) to make the record clear; and begin assembling the summary-judgment record now.

             I. INTRODUCTION  
           This is a breach-of-contract case recast as a tort and consumer-protection suit. Arcadia—a sophisticated healthcare-IT company with approximately $62 million in annual revenue, represented by experienced outside counsel throughout an arm's-length negotiation—entered into a comprehensive $14.7 million Master Software License and Services Agreement (the "MSLSA") that meticulously allocated the parties' risks. The MSLSA contains a Delaware choice-of-law clause (§ 12.7), an integration clause superseding all pre-contractual representations (§ 12.1), an "as is" warranty disclaimer (§ 9.4), a 90-day limited warranty with an exclusive repair-or-replace-then-refund remedy (§ 9.1), a 30-day deemed-acceptance provision (§ 5.3), a mutual consequential-damages waiver (§ 8.2), and an aggregate-liability cap (§ 8.1). The FAC itself alleges that Arcadia was represented by counsel and negotiated these very provisions (FAC ¶ 32) and that Arcadia's CTO conducted independent due diligence before signing (FAC ¶ 31).  
           When the implementation encountered difficulties, Arcadia did not invoke the contract's acceptance-testing, warranty, or cure mechanisms. It let the 30-day acceptance window close without delivering the written nonconformity notice that § 5.3 requires, thereby deemed-accepting the software. Only then did Arcadia recast its disappointment with a commercial transaction as fraud, negligent misrepresentation, unjust enrichment, and a violation of the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices–Consumer Protection Act (the "DTPA")—seeking $47.3 million, more than three times the contract price, in damages the contract forecloses.  
           Each count fails as a matter of law:  
            • Count V (DTPA) is barred by the § 17.49(f) transaction exemption: $17,050,000 in consideration, and Arcadia is a limited liability company, not an individual.  
            • Count IV (unjust enrichment) is barred because an express contract governs the same subject matter.  
            • Count II (fraud) fails Rule 9(b), is barred by the integration clause and anti-reliance doctrine, rests on non-actionable puffery, and is precluded by the economic-loss doctrine.  
            • Count III (negligent misrepresentation) fails for lack of an independent duty and is likewise barred by the economic-loss doctrine.  
            • Count I (breach of contract) is barred by deemed acceptance and the expired exclusive-remedy warranty; in all events, Arcadia's damages are limited by §§ 8.1–8.3.  
           The Court should dismiss the FAC in its entirety.  
             II. STATEMENT OF FACTS  
            A. The MSLSA's Comprehensive Risk Allocation.  
           On March 15, 2022, Meridian and Arcadia executed the MSLSA and Statement of Work #1 ("SOW-1") for the licensing and implementation of Meridian's NexusCore Healthcare Analytics Module. FAC ¶¶ 33–35. The total contract value was $14,700,000. FAC ¶ 34. The MSLSA is a fully integrated, negotiated commercial agreement that allocates risk through, among others, the following provisions, each of which is referenced in and central to the FAC and therefore properly before the Court on this motion:  
            • § 12.1 (integration clause): the MSLSA "constitutes the entire agreement between the Parties" and "supersedes all prior and contemporaneous agreements, proposals, representations, and understandings, whether oral or written"; each party "acknowledges that ... it has not relied upon any statement, representation, warranty, or agreement of the other Party except as expressly set forth in this Agreement."  
            • § 9.4 (warranty disclaimer): the software is provided "AS IS" and "AS AVAILABLE," and "Licensee acknowledges that ... Licensee has relied on its own due diligence and evaluation in selecting the Licensed Software."  
            • § 9.1 (limited warranty / exclusive remedy): a 90-day warranty that the software will "perform substantially in accordance with the Documentation," with repair or replacement as the sole remedy, and a pro-rata refund if Meridian cannot cure within 60 days.  
            • § 5.3 (deemed acceptance): a 30-day acceptance-testing window following deployment, with acceptance "deemed" if the licensee fails to deliver written notice of material nonconformity.  
            • § 8.2 (consequential-damages waiver): a mutual waiver of "indirect, incidental, special, consequential, punitive, or exemplary damages, including ... damages for loss of profits, goodwill, use, data, or other intangible losses."  
            • § 8.1 (liability cap) and § 8.3 (exclusive remedies): aggregate liability capped at fees paid or payable in the preceding 12 months, with contractual remedies designated as sole and exclusive; § 8.5 confirms these allocations are an "essential basis of the bargain" that survive even if a limited remedy fails of its essential purpose.  
            • § 12.7 (governing law): Delaware law, without regard to conflict-of-law principles.  
            B. Arcadia's Sophistication and Independent Due Diligence.  
           The FAC concedes Arcadia's sophistication. Arcadia is a $62-million healthcare-IT company. FAC ¶ 6. The FAC admits that Arcadia's CTO "conducted certain independent due diligence, including reference calls with two existing Meridian healthcare clients" before contracting. FAC ¶ 31. The FAC admits that "Arcadia engaged outside counsel to assist in the negotiation process" and that Arcadia's counsel sought "enhanced limitation of liability provisions and expanded warranty protections." FAC ¶ 32. And the FAC acknowledges that Meridian's December 9, 2021 written proposal "contained certain general disclaimers." FAC ¶ 27. Those disclaimers stated that "[e]stimated timelines and performance metrics are provided for planning purposes only and do not constitute guarantees," and that "[a]ctual results may vary based on client environment, data quality, and implementation decisions." Thus, on the face of the FAC, Arcadia was a counseled, sophisticated buyer that independently evaluated the product, received disclaimers, and negotiated the risk-allocation provisions it now seeks to evade.  
            C. Data Migration Was Arcadia's Sole Responsibility.  
           SOW-1 § 3.2 expressly provides that Arcadia "shall be solely responsible for all data migration activities, including extraction, transformation, and loading of legacy data into NexusCore," and that Meridian's role was limited to advisory consulting with "no responsibility for the accuracy, completeness, or integrity of migrated data." The FAC's own allegations of "data integrity failures" (FAC ¶¶ 43, 49, 52) thus concern a workstream that the contract assigned to Arcadia, not Meridian. The four Change Orders the FAC complains of (FAC ¶¶ 46–47)—including CO-004, $900,000 in "data remediation services" (FAC ¶ 47(d))—were mutually executed under § 5.4 and confirm that the parties used the contract's change-management process to address evolving requirements.  
            D. Arcadia Deemed-Accepted the Software.  
           NexusCore achieved Go-Live on January 15, 2023. FAC ¶ 45. Under § 5.3, Arcadia had 30 days to conduct acceptance testing and deliver written notice of any material nonconformity; the acceptance window therefore closed on February 14, 2023. The FAC alleges that Arcadia's first written complaint was Dr. Okonkwo's March 8, 2023 email, which referenced only "intermittent latency issues" and "occasional report generation errors." FAC ¶ 50. That email was sent 22 days after the acceptance window closed and did not constitute the written nonconformity notice § 5.3 requires. Acceptance was therefore deemed granted as of February 14, 2023. The 90-day limited warranty expired on April 15, 2023.  
            E. The Alleged Misrepresentations Are Puffery, Disclaimed, and Superseded.  
           The FAC's fraud and negligent-misrepresentation claims rest on three categories of pre-sale statements: (1) that NexusCore "delivers industry-leading performance for healthcare analytics" and that clients "typically see 30-40% improvement in reporting efficiency" (FAC ¶ 23); (2) that NexusCore "will integrate seamlessly with any EHR platform" (FAC ¶ 25); and (3) a list of general marketing statements (FAC ¶ 28). These are vague, subjective claims of product superiority and forward-looking projections—the very statements the December 2021 proposal disclaimed as non-guarantees (FAC ¶ 27) and that § 12.1 superseded.  
             III. LEGAL STANDARD  
           To survive a Rule 12(b)(6) motion, a complaint must contain sufficient factual matter to "state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face."          Ashcroft v. Iqbal        , 556 U.S. 662, 678 (2009);          Bell Atl. Corp. v. Twombly        , 550 U.S. 544, 570 (2007). The Court accepts all well-pleaded facts as true but is "not bound to accept as true a legal conclusion couched as a factual allegation."          Lormand v. US Unwired, Inc.        , 565 F.3d 228, 255–56 (5th Cir. 2009). Threadbare recitals of the elements, supported by mere conclusory statements, do not suffice.          Iqbal        , 556 U.S. at 678.  
           On a Rule 12(b)(6) motion, the Court may consider documents attached to the complaint, documents attached to the motion that are referenced in the complaint and central to the plaintiff's claims, and matters of which the Court may take judicial notice.          Lormand        , 565 F.3d at 255–57; see also Standing Order § 3.4. The MSLSA, SOW-1, and the four Change Orders are referenced throughout the FAC and central to every count; they are properly before the Court without conversion to summary judgment.  
           Claims sounding in fraud must satisfy Rule 9(b), which requires a plaintiff to "specify the statements contended to be fraudulent, identify the speaker, state when and where the statements were made, and explain why the statements were fraudulent."          Benchmark Elecs., Inc. v. J.M. Huber Corp.        , 343 F.3d 719, 724 (5th Cir. 2003). The Court rigorously enforces Rule 9(b). Standing Order § 4.2.  
             IV. ARGUMENT  
            A. Count V (DTPA) Is Barred by the § 17.49(f) Transaction Exemption.  
           Count V is barred as a matter of law by the DTPA's own transaction exemption. Section 17.49(f) provides that the DTPA "does not apply to a cause of action arising from a transaction ... involving total consideration by the consumer of more than $500,000, unless the consumer is an individual." Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 17.49(f). Both conditions are met here.  
           First, Arcadia is not an individual. The FAC alleges that "Plaintiff Arcadia Health Systems, LLC is a Texas limited liability company." FAC ¶ 6. A limited liability company is not an "individual" for purposes of § 17.49(f). Second, the total consideration vastly exceeds $500,000. The MSLSA alone had a total contract value of $14,700,000. FAC ¶ 34. The four Change Orders added $2,350,000. FAC ¶ 46. Total consideration was therefore $17,050,000—more than thirty-four times the statutory threshold. The exemption is determined by the total consideration involved in the transaction, not by the amount of damages claimed.          PPG Indus., Inc. v. JMB/Houston Ctrs. Partners Ltd.        , 146 S.W.3d 79, 89 (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 2004, no pet.).  
           The § 17.49(f) exemption is dispositive and purely legal: it turns on Arcadia's organizational form and the contract value, both of which are established by the FAC's own allegations. No factual dispute is presented. Count V must be dismissed.  
           In the alternative, even under the prior version of § 17.49(f)—which also exempted transactions where the consumer had assets exceeding $25 million—the exemption applies. Arcadia's own damages expert reports that Arcadia's total assets are approximately $41 million, well above that threshold. And, in the further alternative, Arcadia is not a "consumer" within the meaning of § 17.45(4): this is a sophisticated, $14.7 million enterprise-software transaction negotiated at arm's length between two commercial entities each represented by counsel—not the consumer transaction the DTPA was enacted to protect.          Riverside Nat'l Bank v. Lewis        , 603 S.W.2d 169, 173–75 (Tex. 1980). Finally, the alleged statements are non-actionable puffery (see Section IV.C.2), and the FAC does not adequately plead that any such statement was a "producing cause" of Arcadia's damages.          Melody Home Mfg. Co. v. Barnes        , 741 S.W.2d 349, 351–52 (Tex. 1987).  
            B. Count IV (Unjust Enrichment) Is Barred Because an Express Contract Governs.  
           Unjust enrichment is an equitable remedy unavailable where a valid, express contract governs the subject matter of the dispute.          Fortune Prod. Co. v. Conoco, Inc.        , 52 S.W.3d 671, 684 (Tex. 2000) ("recovery under quantum meruit [or unjust enrichment] is not available when there is a valid, express contract covering the subject matter of the dispute"). The "same subject matter" test is applied broadly: if the contract addresses the general subject area of the dispute, unjust enrichment is barred.          Excess Underwriters at Lloyd's v. Frank's Casing Crew & Rental Tools, Inc.        , 246 S.W.3d 42, 59–60 (Tex. 2008).  
           Count IV is irreconcilable with Count I. In Count I, Arcadia alleges that a valid, enforceable MSLSA exists and that Meridian breached it. FAC ¶¶ 69, 76. Arcadia does not contend that the MSLSA is void or unenforceable—to the contrary, it relies on the MSLSA as the foundation of its breach-of-contract claim. The MSLSA indisputably governs the entire scope of the parties' software-licensing and implementation relationship. Under          Fortune Production         and          Excess Underwriters        , unjust enrichment is unavailable as a matter of law.  
           Arcadia's only possible response—that the MSLSA's limitation of liability and consequential-damages waiver prevent full recovery—is foreclosed. The Texas Supreme Court has squarely rejected the argument that contractual limitations create a "gap" that equity may fill: "equity follows the law," and where parties have allocated risk through contract, equity will not rewrite the bargain.          Excess Underwriters        , 246 S.W.3d at 59–60. Count IV must be dismissed.  
            C. Count II (Fraud) Fails Rule 9(b), Is Barred by the Integration Clause and Anti-Reliance Doctrine, Rests on Puffery, and Is Precluded by the Economic-Loss Doctrine.  
            1. The FAC fails Rule 9(b).  
           Rule 9(b) requires Arcadia to plead the "who, what, when, where, and why" of the alleged fraud.          Benchmark Elecs.        , 343 F.3d at 724. The FAC identifies the speaker (Poletti) and the dates, but it does not plead          why         the statements were false          when made        . Arcadia's theory is entirely post-hoc: because the implementation was delayed and performance issues emerged after Go-Live, the pre-sale statements must have been false. That is insufficient. Post-hoc product failure does not, without more, support an inference that the defendant knew its pre-sale representations were false at the time they were made.          Flaherty & Crumrine Preferred Income Fund, Inc. v. TXU Corp.        , 565 F.3d 200, 207–08 (5th Cir. 2009). The FAC's scienter allegations are pleaded "on information and belief" (FAC ¶¶ 56–60) without a stated factual basis—precisely the type of pleading the Court's Standing Order § 4.2 disfavors. And the FAC improperly "lumps" together general marketing statements without distinguishing puffery and projections from actionable representations of fact.          Dorsey v. Portfolio Equities, Inc.        , 540 F.3d 333, 340 (5th Cir. 2008).  
            2. The alleged statements are non-actionable puffery.  
           Statements that NexusCore "delivers industry-leading performance" and that clients "typically see 30-40% improvement in reporting efficiency" (FAC ¶ 23) are vague, subjective claims of product superiority and generalized projections—textbook puffery.          Pizza Hut, Inc. v. Papa John's Int'l, Inc.        , 227 F.3d 489, 496–97 (5th Cir. 2000) (a general claim of superiority "so vague that it can be understood only as the seller's opinion" is non-actionable puffery). Forward-looking statements about a product's expected performance, made in a sales context to a sophisticated buyer, are likewise puffery upon which reasonable reliance is impossible as a matter of law.          Presidio Enters., Inc. v. Warner Bros. Distributing Corp.        , 784 F.2d 674, 679–80 (5th Cir. 1986). Puffery remains non-actionable even when embedded in a sales presentation.          Pizza Hut        , 227 F.3d at 498–99. The FAC's own acknowledgment that the December 2021 proposal "contained certain general disclaimers" (FAC ¶ 27) confirms that these were understood as non-guaranteed marketing statements.  
            3. The integration clause and anti-reliance doctrine bar reliance.  
           Arcadia's fraud claim rests entirely on extra-contractual, pre-sale representations. FAC ¶¶ 23, 25, 28, 79. Section 12.1 supersedes "all prior and contemporaneous agreements, proposals, representations, and understandings, whether oral or written," and provides that each party "acknowledges that ... it has not relied upon any statement, representation, warranty, or agreement of the other Party except as expressly set forth in this Agreement." Section 9.4 likewise states that "Licensee has relied on its own due diligence and evaluation in selecting the Licensed Software." Under Delaware law—which governs the MSLSA pursuant to § 12.7—"a party to a contract cannot promise, in a clear integration clause of a negotiated agreement, that it is not relying on promises or representations made by the other party outside of the agreement, and then assert a claim for fraud based on those very representations."          Eagle Indus., Inc. v. DeVilbiss Health Care, Inc.        , 702 A.2d 1228, 1232 (Del. 1997); see also          SIGA Techs., Inc. v. PharmAthene, Inc.        , 67 A.3d 330, 344 (Del. 2013);          Kuhn Constr., Inc. v. Diamond State Port Corp.        , 990 A.2d 393, 401–402 (Del. 2010).  
           The FAC itself alleges the facts that defeat reliance: Arcadia was represented by counsel and negotiated the limitation-of-liability and warranty provisions (FAC ¶ 32); Arcadia's CTO conducted independent due diligence (FAC ¶ 31); and Arcadia received the proposal's disclaimers (FAC ¶ 27). A sophisticated, counseled party that negotiates an integration clause and an anti-reliance acknowledgment cannot claim justifiable reliance on the very extra-contractual representations the contract disclaims.          Eagle Indus.        , 702 A.2d at 1232. Even under Texas law, justifiable reliance is negated where the plaintiff conducted an independent investigation and the written contract contains an integration clause. Count II fails for lack of justifiable reliance.  
            4. The economic-loss doctrine bars the fraud claim.  
           All of Arcadia's damages are purely economic—contract value, lost profits, increased operating costs, and reputational harm measured in dollars. FAC ¶¶ 61–66. Under Delaware law, "where a plaintiff's claims arise solely from the contractual relationship ... and the damages sought are exclusively economic losses ..., the economic loss doctrine bars recovery in tort."          Brasby v. Morris Dynamics, Inc.        , 947 A.2d 1042, 1049 (Del. 2008). The doctrine applies with particular force where sophisticated parties represented by counsel negotiated a comprehensive agreement with an integration clause and warranty disclaimers.          Kuhn Constr.        , 990 A.2d at 401–402. Under Texas law, the economic-loss rule bars tort recovery where (1) the losses are purely economic, (2) they relate to the subject matter of a contract, and (3) the defendant owes no duty independent of the contract.          Chapman Custom Homes, Inc. v. Dallas Plumbing Co.        , 445 S.W.3d 716, 718–19 (Tex. 2014);          Sharyland Water Supply Corp. v. City of Alton        , 354 S.W.3d 407, 415–16 (Tex. 2011). All three factors are met. Arcadia's fraud claim is, at core, a claim that Meridian did not deliver software that performed as promised—the essence of breach of contract. Count II must be dismissed.  
            D. Count III (Negligent Misrepresentation) Fails for Lack of an Independent Duty and Is Barred by the Economic-Loss Doctrine.  
           Negligent misrepresentation under Texas law requires the defendant to owe a duty of care arising from a source independent of the contract.          McCamish, Martin, Brown & Loeffler v. F.E. Appling Interests        , 991 S.W.2d 787, 792 (Tex. 1999). Meridian's relationship with Arcadia is purely contractual. There is no fiduciary relationship, no professional relationship giving rise to heightened duties, and no statutory duty to provide accurate pre-sale information beyond what the MSLSA provides. The MSLSA's express warranty (§ 9.1) and warranty disclaimer (§ 9.4) define the scope of Meridian's obligations regarding software performance. Arcadia cannot circumvent those provisions through a negligent-misrepresentation claim.  
           Moreover, the nature of Arcadia's damages confirms that Count III is a contract claim in tort's clothing. Arcadia seeks the $14.7 million contract value, $18.4 million in lost profits, and $6.7 million in increased operating costs—benefit-of-the-bargain and consequential damages recoverable only in contract.          Sloane (Fed. Land Bank Ass'n v. Sloane)        , 825 S.W.2d 439, 442–43 (Tex. 1992) (where the plaintiff's true complaint is that the other party did not deliver what was contractually promised, the claim sounds in contract). The economic-loss doctrine bars Count III for the same reasons it bars Count II. And because Count III sounds in fraud, it must also satisfy Rule 9(b), which it does not.          Benchmark Elecs.        , 343 F.3d at 724. Count III must be dismissed.  
            E. Count I (Breach of Contract) Is Barred by Deemed Acceptance and the Expired Exclusive-Remedy Warranty; In All Events, Arcadia's Damages Are Contractually Limited.  
            1. Arcadia deemed-accepted the software.  
           Section 5.3 gave Arcadia 30 days following deployment to conduct acceptance testing and deliver written notice of any material nonconformity, with acceptance "deemed" upon failure to do so. NexusCore achieved Go-Live on January 15, 2023 (FAC ¶ 45), so the acceptance window closed on February 14, 2023. The FAC alleges that Arcadia's first written complaint was Dr. Okonkwo's March 8, 2023 email—22 days after the window closed—and that it described only "intermittent latency issues" and "occasional report generation errors." FAC ¶ 50. That is not the written nonconformity notice § 5.3 requires. Acceptance was therefore deemed granted as of February 14, 2023. Deemed-acceptance provisions are enforceable bright-line rules; oral or post-deadline complaints do not satisfy a contractual written-notice requirement.          Simulados, Inc. v. Canton Health Mgmt. Co.        , 2019 WL 4573218 (W.D. Tex. Sept. 20, 2019) (unpublished) (deemed acceptance enforced where licensee failed to deliver written notice within 30-day period). The provision applies regardless of whether defects were latent.          Precision Healthcare Solutions v. Nextera Data Sys.        , 458 F. Supp. 3d 544, 551 (N.D. Tex. 2020). Once acceptance was deemed, Arcadia's remedy for any defect was the limited warranty, not rejection.  
            2. The limited warranty expired and provides the exclusive remedy.  
           Section 9.1 provides a 90-day warranty that the software will "perform substantially in accordance with the Documentation," with repair or replacement as the sole remedy and a pro-rata refund if Meridian cannot cure within 60 days. The warranty period expired on April 15, 2023. Section 8.3 designates the MSLSA's remedies as Arcadia's "sole and exclusive remedies," and § 8.5 confirms that the limitations survive even if a limited remedy fails of its essential purpose. The FAC does not allege that Arcadia delivered a warranty claim during the warranty period or pursued the repair-or-replace remedy. Instead, Arcadia demands $47.3 million—far beyond the exclusive remedy the contract provides. Limitation-of-liability clauses and consequential-damages waivers in negotiated commercial software agreements between sophisticated parties are enforceable, and the consequential-damages waiver survives independently even if the limited remedy fails of its essential purpose.          Dresser-Rand Co. v. Virtual Automation Inc.        , 361 F.3d 831, 838–40 (5th Cir. 2004); see also          Kana Software, Inc. v. Sealand Tech., Inc.        , 178 A.3d 1045, 1058–60 (Del. Ch. 2017) (integration clause, warranty disclaimer, and liability cap operate in concert; fraud cannot circumvent contractual limitations).  
            3. The damages sought are barred by §§ 8.1–8.2.  
           Section 8.2 expressly waives "indirect, incidental, special, consequential, punitive, or exemplary damages, including ... damages for loss of profits, goodwill, use, data, or other intangible losses." That waiver bars Arcadia's $18.4 million lost-profits claim, its $6.7 million increased-operating-costs claim, and its $5.0 million reputational-harm claim—a total of $30.1 million of the $47.3 million demanded. FAC ¶¶ 63–65. Section 8.1 further caps aggregate liability at the fees paid or payable during the 12 months preceding the event giving rise to liability. Section 8.3 makes the contractual remedies exclusive. The FAC's own damages allegations (FAC ¶¶ 61–66) confirm that the overwhelming majority of the recovery sought is contractually foreclosed.  
            4. Count I should be dismissed; in the alternative, damages must be limited.  
           Because Arcadia deemed-accepted the software, because the limited warranty expired without Arcadia invoking its exclusive remedy, and because the contractual limitations are enforceable, Count I fails to state a plausible claim for the $47.3 million in damages demanded. The Court should dismiss Count I. In the alternative, should the Court decline to dismiss Count I in full, the consequential-damages claims (lost profits, increased operating costs, and reputational harm) and any damages exceeding the § 8.1 cap must be dismissed as a matter of law, leaving Arcadia—at most—to direct damages not exceeding the contractual cap.  
             V. CONCLUSION  
           For the foregoing reasons, Meridian respectfully requests that the Court grant this motion and dismiss all five counts of the First Amended Complaint with prejudice. In the alternative, Meridian requests that the Court dismiss Counts II through V with prejudice and limit any recovery on Count I to direct damages not exceeding the contractual cap set forth in § 8.1 of the MSLSA, with all consequential, incidental, special, punitive, and exemplary damages barred by § 8.2. Meridian further requests its costs of court and all other relief to which it is justly entitled.

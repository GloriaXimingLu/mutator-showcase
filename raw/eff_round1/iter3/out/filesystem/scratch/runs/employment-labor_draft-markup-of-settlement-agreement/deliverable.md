           1. Executive Summary  
          We have reviewed the draft Settlement Agreement and General Release of All Claims (the "Draft") prepared by Mr. Delano's counsel against Greenleaf's employment records and the terms of the Greenleaf 2021 Equity Incentive Plan (the "Plan"). The Draft is favorable to Mr. Delano in several respects and contains provisions that are (a) legally defective and would jeopardize the release Greenleaf is paying for, (b) factually inconsistent with Greenleaf's own records, or (c) missing protections Greenleaf needs. The attached redline implements proposed revisions; this memo summarizes the issues, the risks, and the items that require your decision.  
          The most important points:  
           The ADEA release is invalid as drafted.         Mr. Delano is 58. The Older Workers Benefit Protection Act ("OWBPA") requires a 21-day consideration period and a 7-day revocation period. The Draft gives only 14 days and includes no revocation period. As written, the waiver of age-discrimination claims would likely be unenforceable, leaving Greenleaf exposed to the very claims it is paying to resolve.  
           The RSU valuation is wrong and contains a false representation.         The Draft values the 5,000 accelerated RSUs at $10.00/share, calling that "the most recent independent valuation." Greenleaf's records show the most recent 409A valuation (Ridgepoint Valuation Services, January 15, 2025) is $12.50/share. The correct RSU value is $62,500 (not $50,000), and the correct total settlement value is $537,500 (not $525,000). Greenleaf should not execute an agreement containing a knowingly false statement.  
           The release, confidentiality, and non-disparagement provisions are overbroad         in ways that are unenforceable and that could expose Greenleaf to retaliation/interference liability (and SEC/NLRB scrutiny), particularly given Mr. Delano's pending Oregon OSHA complaint.  
           The Draft lacks governing law, a dispute-resolution mechanism, a return-of-property obligation, and an ongoing confidentiality obligation         protecting Greenleaf's proprietary information.  
           A significant litigation risk supports settling.         Greenleaf hired a replacement (Raj Mehta) six days after terminating Mr. Delano for "elimination of position," which strongly undercuts the stated rationale and supports Mr. Delano's retaliation and age-discrimination claims.  
          We recommend that Greenleaf         not execute the Draft as received         and instead proceed on the redlined terms, after resolving the open items identified in Section 5.

           3. Key Issues and Risks  
           3.1 ADEA / OWBPA release is invalid as drafted — CRITICAL  
          Because Mr. Delano is 58, any waiver of ADEA claims must satisfy the OWBPA, 29 U.S.C. § 626(f). For an individual termination, the OWBPA requires (i)         at least 21 days         to consider the agreement and (ii) a         7-day revocation period         after signing (which cannot be waived). The Draft provides only 14 days (Sections 5(c) and 16) and         no revocation period at all        . As written, the ADEA waiver would likely fail the "knowing and voluntary" standard, and Mr. Delano could pursue age-discrimination claims after signing — defeating a principal purpose of the settlement.  
          Redline:         21-day consideration period (Sec. 5(c), 16(a)); 7-day revocation period (Sec. 5(g), 16(b)); "Effective Date" redefined as the day after the revocation period expires (Sec. 1(b)); and a carve-out confirming the release does not cover ADEA claims arising after execution (Sec. 5(h)). Note that payments run from the Effective Date, so they will occur approximately 8+ days after signing.  
           3.2 RSU valuation is incorrect and misrepresents the "most recent" valuation — CRITICAL  
          Section 3(d) values the 5,000 accelerated RSUs at $10.00/share, stated to be "based on the most recent independent valuation conducted by Ridgepoint Valuation Services." That is incorrect: the most recent 409A valuation is dated         January 15, 2025 at $12.50/share        . The correct value of 5,000 RSUs is         $62,500        , and the correct aggregate settlement value is         $537,500        , not $525,000. Two problems follow: (1) Greenleaf would be executing an agreement containing a false representation about its own valuation; and (2) RSU vesting is taxed at fair market value on the vesting date (at least $12.50), so the $10.00 figure is wrong for tax purposes in any event.  
          Redline:         Corrected to $12.50/share, $62,500, and $537,500 (Sec. 3(d), 3(e)); added a clarification that taxable income at vesting is based on FMV on the vesting date (Sec. 3(d), 8(d)).         Decision needed:         this increases the stated total by $12,500. If the parties intended to hold the total at $525,000, the economics must be restructured — but the false "most recent valuation" representation must be corrected regardless.  
           3.3 RSU acceleration requires Compensation Committee approval — HIGH  
          The Plan vests the discretion to approve accelerated vesting in separation agreements in the         Compensation Committee        , not in "Greenleaf" generally. The Draft says "Greenleaf shall cause" vesting without addressing Committee approval. If the Committee has not approved, Greenleaf may not be able to deliver the RSUs.  
          Redline:         Conditioned acceleration on Compensation Committee approval; added a forfeiture acknowledgment (which also supports adequate consideration for the release); and added a Greenleaf representation (Sec. 15(b)(iii)).         Action:         obtain Compensation Committee approval before signing and before RSU delivery.  
           3.4 Overbroad release and "no future charges" covenant — HIGH  
          The general release (Sec. 4) and the representation in Sec. 15(a)(ii) purport to bar Mr. Delano from filing any future "charge" with any governmental agency and from participating in any proceeding. Employees cannot be prohibited from filing charges with agencies such as the EEOC, Oregon OSHA, the NLRB, the DOL, or the SEC, or from cooperating with investigations. Such restrictions are unenforceable and can themselves expose Greenleaf to retaliation/interference liability and to SEC and NLRB scrutiny of confidentiality and non-disparagement terms.  
          Redline:         Added a carve-out preserving Mr. Delano's right to file charges, communicate with, and cooperate with government agencies and to receive whistleblower/bounty awards, while waiving only personal monetary recovery (Sec. 4); narrowed Sec. 15(a)(ii) to lawsuits and cross-referenced the Sec. 4 carve-out.  
           3.5 Confidentiality and non-disparagement lack agency/DTSA carve-outs — HIGH  
          Sections 6 and 7 contain no carve-out for truthful communications with government agencies and no notice of the immunity provided by the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. § 1833(b). Given Mr. Delano's pending OSHA complaint, these provisions could be read to chill protected activity and could create liability. The non-disparagement obligation also runs "in perpetuity," which is aggressive.  
          Redline:         Added agency and DTSA carve-outs to Sec. 6 (new (v), (vi)) and Sec. 7; changed the non-disparagement term from perpetuity to five (5) years.  
           3.6 Non-competition is largely unenforceable and strategically questionable — HIGH  
          Section 9 imposes an 18-month non-compete across Oregon, Washington, Idaho, and California.         California voids non-competes         (Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600), so the California portion is unenforceable, and the breadth invites challenge elsewhere. Even if Oregon's settlement exception (ORS 653.295(2)(b)) applies, common-law reasonableness limits remain. Separately, imposing a non-compete on a         terminated whistleblower         creates poor optics and a potential retaliation inference.  
          Redline:         Narrowed to 12 months / Oregon-Washington and tightened the "competitor" definition.         Recommendation:         the GC should consider dropping the non-compete entirely; the non-solicitation (Sec. 10) is more defensible and may provide adequate protection.  
           3.7 Unpaid 2024 bonus / inaccurate wage representation — MEDIUM  
          Greenleaf's records show the 2024 performance bonus (target $43,600) was never determined or paid. Section 2 represents that all wages and bonuses have been paid and that the Section's terms are "not in dispute" — both inaccurate. If the bonus is a "wage" under Oregon law, failure to pay could trigger final-wage and penalty-wage exposure (ORS 652.610 / 653.045).  
          Redline:         Revised Sec. 2 to acknowledge that any unpaid 2024 bonus or other wage amounts are included in and satisfied by the Settlement Payment, and removed the "not in dispute" representation.         Decision needed:         whether to pay the 2024 bonus separately rather than rely on the release.  
           3.8 Missing provisions — MEDIUM/HIGH  
          The Draft contains no governing-law clause, no venue or dispute-resolution mechanism, no return-of-company-property obligation, no ongoing confidentiality obligation protecting Greenleaf's proprietary information, and no survival clause. For a senior executive with access to supply-chain, sourcing, pricing, and food-safety information, these are significant gaps.  
          Redline:         Added Section 19 (return of property; ongoing protection of Confidential Information); Section 20 (Oregon governing law; binding AAA arbitration in Portland; carve-out for injunctive relief on restrictive covenants; Multnomah County venue); Section 21 (limited cooperation, expressly excluding the OSHA matter and any matter in which Mr. Delano is adverse); and Section 22 (survival).  
           3.9 Negotiated concessions to Mr. Delano — confirm acceptable  
           References (Sec. 11):         The Draft required a "positive" reference and mandated subjective praise ("valued member … significant contributions"). The redline softens this to a "neutral and non-negative" reference plus a statement that Mr. Delano is eligible for rehire. Confirm this is acceptable to Mr. Delano's counsel.  
           Re-employment (Sec. 14):         The Draft mandated that Greenleaf "consider" Mr. Delano for future openings, creating an ongoing duty and future breach exposure. The redline softens this to eligibility to apply, with no blanket "do-not-rehire" designation, while preserving Greenleaf's hiring discretion. Confirm acceptable.  
           3.10 Other observations  
           Releasees (Sec. 1(d))         include "future" officers and employees; releasing future persons for future conduct is overbroad but favors Greenleaf, so we left it in place.  
           401(k):         Confirm vesting of the employer match; the release covers ERISA claims.  
           Clawback / liquidated damages:         Consider adding a clawback or liquidated-damages remedy for breach of confidentiality or non-disparagement. We did not redline one in to avoid an unenforceable penalty, but can add a carefully calibrated provision if desired.  
           Entire agreement (Sec. 18):         Redlined to carve out the Plan and RSU Grant Agreement, which must continue to govern the RSUs.

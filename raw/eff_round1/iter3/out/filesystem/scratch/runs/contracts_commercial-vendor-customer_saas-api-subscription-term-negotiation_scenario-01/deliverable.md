             Rationale.         This is Meridian’s highest priority and the single most important term in the deal. The proposal is structured as a coordinated package consistent with the Playbook’s linkage rule: a lower general cap is paired with a robust super-cap that includes patient-safety.  
      The Vendor’s counter of 1.5× ACV on the general cap lands precisely at Meridian’s absolute floor and aligns with the comparable-deal intelligence (Cognitive Foundry agreed to 1.5× ACV in a comparable 12-hospital deal). We accept this as the landing zone for the general cap, but treat it as a bargaining chip — not a gift — extracting value on the super-cap structure in exchange.  
      The critical gap is the Vendor’s refusal to include patient-safety and HIPAA in the super-cap. Meridian’s absolute floor is that patient-safety liability must never be capped below 3× ACV (~$28.8M). The comparable-deal intelligence confirms that Cognitive Foundry agreed to a 3× ACV super-cap covering IP and data breach elsewhere — directly undermining the Vendor’s position that 3× is unavailable or that data-breach/HIPAA cannot be carved out. We anchor on this precedent.  
      The proposal places patient-safety, HIPAA/PHI breach, and IP indemnification in a 3× ACV super-cap, while keeping willful misconduct/gross negligence uncapped (a standard carve-out that the Vendor’s own opening draft included in the super-cap). This structure gives Meridian meaningful protection on the highest-severity categories while accommodating the Vendor’s stated 3× maximum and its board-level prohibition on uncapped liability.

        (iii) Acceptability Analysis for Vendor Team  
          Sandra Hu (VP, Commercial Legal) — anticipated objections and responses:  
              Objection:                 “        3× ACV super-cap for patient-safety exceeds our standard and our board’s risk tolerance.        ”         Response: Cognitive Foundry has already agreed to a 3× ACV super-cap covering IP and data breach in a comparable 12-hospital deal. The proposed structure uses the same 3× multiplier and the same categories the Vendor has accepted elsewhere. Patient-safety is added because this is a clinical decision-support deployment where AI outputs directly influence patient care — a risk profile distinct from non-clinical deployments. The 3× cap (not uncapped) respects the Vendor’s board prohibition on uncapped liability.  
              Objection:                 “        Including HIPAA in the super-cap is unprecedented.        ”         Response: The comparable deal included data breach in the 3× super-cap. HIPAA/PHI breach liability is functionally equivalent and is already governed by the BAA. Carving it into the super-cap simply ensures the cap structure aligns with the BAA’s breach obligations.  
          Rafael Bowen (Partner, Kessler Pratt & Lowe) — anticipated objections and responses:  
              Objection:                 “        Patient-safety claims are clinical malpractice matters outside a software vendor’s indemnification scope.        ”         Response: The super-cap is a limitation-of-liability provision, not an indemnification expansion. It does not create new obligations; it simply ensures that if patient-safety liability arises from AI outputs, the Vendor’s exposure is capped at 3× ACV rather than the lower general cap. This is a more favorable outcome for the Vendor than the uncapped position Meridian prefers.  
              Objection:                 “        The insurance step-in rights are punitive.        ”         Response: The step-in rights are a backstop, not a penalty. They activate only if the Vendor fails to meet a binding deadline to increase coverage it has already represented it is evaluating. The Vendor retains full control of the insurance procurement process.  
          Why this should be acceptable:         The proposal accepts the Vendor’s counter on the general cap (1.5× ACV), uses the Vendor’s stated 3× maximum for the super-cap, and keeps all categories capped (none uncapped except willful misconduct, which the Vendor’s own draft included in the super-cap). It is a coordinated package that gives the Vendor predictability while giving Meridian the patient-safety floor the Board Risk Committee requires.  
        (iv) Regulatory and Compliance Constraints  
              HIPAA / HITECH:         The BAA (Exhibit F, executed June 2, 2025) governs PHI obligations. Carving HIPAA/PHI breach into the super-cap ensures the liability framework is consistent with the BAA’s breach-notification and indemnification provisions. The 24-hour breach notification standard (Meridian’s BAA term) creates immediate exposure that the general cap would inadequately cover.  
              NJ AI Healthcare Transparency Act (effective March 1, 2025):         Civil penalties up to $50,000 per violation, with each day of a continuing violation constituting a separate violation. Penalties are assessed against Meridian as the covered entity. The super-cap structure backstops Meridian’s ability to recover penalties attributable to Vendor non-compliance through the indemnification framework (Term 7). Three NJ facilities (Atlantic Medical Center, South Jersey Regional, Princeton Medical Center) are subject to the Act.  
              FDA Clinical Decision-Support Guidance:         The ClinIQ™ modules (particularly radiology) may be subject to FDA oversight. The super-cap structure accommodates potential FDA enforcement exposure by ensuring patient-safety liability is not trapped in the lower general cap.  
              Board Risk Committee (Tier 1 Enterprise Risk):         The patient-safety super-cap at 3× ACV is the absolute floor approved by the Board. Any structure below this floor requires Board-level approval before execution.  
      
         III. TERM 2 — AI MODEL TRANSPARENCY & AUDIT RIGHTS  
          Agreement Sections:         §10.1–10.4 (AI Model Transparency and Audit); §5.7 (State Law Compliance)             Meridian Priority Rank:         2 |             Vendor Priority:         High             Related Materials:         NJ AI Healthcare Transparency Act; Langford AI Assurance Group Audit Scope Proposal  
       (i) Proposed Contract Clause Language  
          §10.1 Transparency Documentation.         Vendor shall provide Customer, on an initial and ongoing basis, with the following documentation for each clinical decision-support module deployed at the Covered Facilities: (a) documentation of training data sources, including provenance, demographic composition, and representativeness analysis; (b) documentation of validation methodology, including validation datasets, performance metrics, and statistical methods; (c) documentation of known limitations, including identified failure modes, edge cases, and population subgroups for which performance may be degraded; and (d) documentation of bias testing results, including analyses of differential performance across race, ethnicity, sex, age, socioeconomic status, language, and disability status. Vendor shall update such documentation within thirty (30) days following any material change to the Platform, including model updates, retraining, or changes to training data.  
          §10.2 Annual Independent Audit.         Customer shall have the right, at Customer’s expense, to engage the Independent Auditor to conduct one (1) annual independent audit of the Platform’s AI/ML models. The Independent Auditor as of the Effective Date is Langford AI Assurance Group, LLC (Dr. Anil Cherian, Managing Director), 101 Federal Street, Suite 1900, Boston, MA 02110, pre-approved by both Parties. Vendor shall cooperate with each such audit and shall provide the Independent Auditor with Tier 2 access — comprising model architecture documentation, training data provenance records, validation and test results, test query access, bias and fairness testing results, and change logs — within twenty (20) business days of Customer’s written audit request.  
          §10.3 Tier 3 Access for Cause-Based Investigations.         Notwithstanding the annual cadence, Customer may request an additional audit at any time in connection with a for-cause investigation, regulatory inquiry, or patient-safety concern. For-cause audits shall be conducted by the Independent Auditor under Tier 3 access protocols: model weights and embeddings shall be accessible solely within a Vendor-controlled secure enclave environment, with no data extraction, copying, photographing, or removal permitted. For-cause audit triggers include: (a) a documented patient-safety event potentially attributable to a Platform Output; (b) a formal regulatory inquiry or investigation by the New Jersey Department of Health, the FDA, or another governmental authority; (c) consecutive failure of clinical accuracy benchmarks over two or more quarterly measurement periods; or (d) mutual written agreement of the Parties.  
          §10.4 Auditor Confidentiality.         The Independent Auditor shall (a) execute a confidentiality agreement with Vendor, in a form reasonably acceptable to Vendor, prior to commencing any audit; (b) conduct all reviews of Tier 3 materials solely within Vendor’s designated secure enclave environment; (c) not extract, copy, photograph, or remove any model weights, embeddings, or source code from the secure environment; (d) destroy all working papers containing Vendor Confidential Information within thirty (30) days of delivering the audit report, and certify such destruction in writing to Vendor within five (5) business days. Audit reports shall be delivered to Customer’s SVP & General Counsel and VP, Digital Health & Technology Transactions, with copies to Vendor.  
          §10.5 Regulatory Cooperation.         Vendor shall cooperate fully with Customer in responding to any inquiry or investigation by the New Jersey Department of Health or other governmental authority under the NJ AI in Healthcare Transparency Act or any comparable law. Vendor shall (a) make qualified personnel available for interviews upon reasonable notice; (b) produce documentation through Customer within timeframes consistent with the Act’s fifteen (15) business-day production requirement; and (c) provide technical assistance in preparing regulatory responses. Vendor shall not seek protective orders or confidential treatment that would prevent Customer from satisfying its statutory obligations, except with Customer’s prior written consent. Audit reports shall be maintained in a form suitable for production to the NJ Department of Health upon request.  
          §10.6 Anti-Waiver.         No provision of this Agreement shall be construed to waive, limit, or condition Customer’s rights or obligations under the NJ AI in Healthcare Transparency Act or any comparable state or federal law in a manner that would prevent Customer from satisfying its statutory obligations.

        (iv) Regulatory and Compliance Constraints  
              NJ AI Healthcare Transparency Act (S.2314, effective March 1, 2025):         Section 5 mandates independent annual audits covering (1) model architecture documentation and training data provenance, (2) validation results and ongoing performance metrics, (3) bias and fairness across demographic groups, (4) incident reports and adverse-event correlation, and (5) data governance practices. This statutory scope corresponds to Langford Tier 2 access. Section 7(a) requires the contract to include the covered entity’s right to access model documentation and commission independent audits. Section 7(b) (anti-waiver) prohibits contractual terms that prevent compliance. Civil penalties up to $50,000 per violation per day; mandatory suspension of non-compliant AI systems is available. The Act’s anti-waiver provision means the Vendor cannot, through IP-protection provisions, contractually prevent Meridian from accessing the documentation and audit rights necessary for compliance — any such restriction is unenforceable under New Jersey law.  
              FDA Clinical Decision-Support Software Guidance:         The radiology module’s interaction with medical imaging data raises questions under the 21st Century Cures Act §3060 four-part exemption test. The audit framework must be flexible enough to accommodate evolving FDA requirements. The transparency documentation obligations (§10.1) support Meridian’s ability to respond to FDA inquiries.  
              HIPAA / HITECH:         Audit access must be conducted in a manner consistent with the BAA’s PHI protections. Langford’s secure-environment protocols and de-identification standards satisfy this requirement.  
              Compliance Timeline:         First mandatory audit must be completed by January 6, 2027 (12 months after go-live). Audit fieldwork should commence no later than Q4 2026. The contractual audit rights must be in place at execution (August 15, 2025) to enable timely audit engagement.

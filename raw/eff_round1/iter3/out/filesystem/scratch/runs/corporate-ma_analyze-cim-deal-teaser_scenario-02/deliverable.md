        1. Executive Summary and Recommendation  
          Recommendation: Proceed to submit a non-binding Indication of Interest by the July 14, 2025 deadline, but at a disciplined valuation anchored to a buy-side Adjusted EBITDA of approximately $15.5–16.3 million (versus the sell-side’s $17.1 million) and subject to a defined set of confirmatory diligence conditions.         CES is a genuinely attractive Pacific Northwest environmental services platform with real regulatory tailwinds and meaningful PFAS optionality. However, the sell-side materials contain (i) a Adjusted EBITDA figure that does not arithmetically foot, (ii) EBITDA adjustments of questionable defensibility, (iii) aggressive PFAS growth projections unsupported by disclosed contracted backlog, (iv) material inconsistencies between the CIM and the accompanying financial exhibits, and (v) a customer-concentration risk centered on a largest customer whose master service agreement expired before the IOI deadline. Taken together, these issues warrant a valuation meaningfully below the sell-side’s implied $136.8–$171.0 million enterprise value range.  
          Headline findings:  
             The Adjusted EBITDA bridge does not foot.         Reported EBITDA of $12.1 million plus the $4.7 million of listed adjustments equals $16.8 million, not the $17.1 million stated in both the CIM and the financial exhibits — a ~$0.3 million overstatement before any qualitative haircut to the adjustments.  
             Multiple adjustments are likely overstated.         The $0.8 million related-party lease add-back is internally contradicted by the CIM’s own industry section (which cites $1.0–$1.2 million market rent for comparable specialized facilities, implying only a $0.2–$0.4 million add-back). The $1.2 million regulatory add-back should be normalized to a recurring compliance run-rate (~$0.7–$0.9 million defensible). The $0.3 million phantom-equity add-back, while non-cash, corresponds to a cash settlement obligation at closing that should be treated as a debt-like item rather than a pure earnings normalization.  
             The growth story is overwhelmingly PFAS-dependent.         Ex-PFAS, revenue grows only ~4–8% annually (within sector norms of 5–10%); PFAS is projected to grow 126% in FY2025E and 57% in FY2026E — well above the 30–50% company-level rate supported by sector data and Thornfield’s GreenWorks Remediation experience. The CIM provides no PFAS-specific contracted backlog to support the $14.0 million FY2025E figure.  
             The CIM and the financial exhibits do not reconcile.         Material differences exist in gross profit, operating income, net income, cash, PP&E, total assets, and total debt. The top-10 customer list differs between the CIM and the backlog report (customers #6–#10 are entirely different companies). Stated DSO of 52 days is inconsistent with balance-sheet accounts receivable of $16.9 million (which implies ~70 days).  
             The largest customer’s contract expired mid-process.         Pacific Northwest Refining Co. (PNR), 21.4% of FY2024 revenue ($18.7 million), operates under an MSA that expired March 31, 2025 — before the IOI deadline and before the expected Q4 2025 close. Its renewal status is undisclosed.  
             Maintenance capex appears understated and free cash flow is weak.         Stated maintenance capex of 3.4% of revenue is ~150–200 bps below the sector floor of 5–7%; on a normalized capex basis, free cash flow is likely near zero or negative, even as the owner has taken $1.0–$2.7 million in annual distributions partly funded by debt.  
          Recommended IOI valuation:         approximately             $125–$145 million enterprise value    , equivalent to roughly             8.0x–8.5x buy-side QoE-adjusted trailing Adjusted EBITDA    , with willingness to move toward the upper end only upon confirmation of (i) PFAS contracted backlog, (ii) PNR contract renewal, and (iii) QoE-validated EBITDA adjustments. This range sits below the sell-side’s $136.8–$171.0 million implied range and reflects the risks identified herein. Final valuation is subject to investment committee approval.

        4. Investment Thesis — Genuinely Attractive Attributes  
      The following attributes are real and support continued pursuit of the asset:  
             Established platform with durable competitive moats.         16-year operating history, four-state hazardous waste transporter permits, Oregon DEQ contractor license, and licensed Professional Engineer oversight create meaningful barriers to entry that take years to replicate. The integrated remediation/industrial-cleaning/emergency-response model reduces customer procurement complexity and supports cross-selling.  
             Regulatory tailwinds are secular, not cyclical.         PFAS regulation (EPA’s April 2024 drinking water MCLs; state-level Oregon/Washington initiatives), CERCLA/RCRA enforcement, aging industrial infrastructure, and infrastructure-driven compliance demand underpin a multi-year demand backdrop. Government/municipal work (19.2% of revenue) adds a counter-cyclical revenue base.  
             Revenue growth track record is genuine, even after discounting PFAS.         Core (ex-PFAS) revenue growth of ~4–8% is within sector norms, and the 12.5% headline CAGR reflects real organic expansion and geographic extension, not solely the PFAS ramp.  
             Recurring revenue characteristics.         ~60% of revenue under MSAs, ~90% historical MSA/service-agreement renewal rate, no top-10 customer losses in five years, and a $42.6 million contracted backlog weighted toward multi-year remediation.  
             Platform for consolidation.         The fragmented Pacific Northwest market and CES’s permit infrastructure support a tuck-in acquisition strategy; management has identified 5–7 potential targets.  
             Strong safety record.         EMR of 0.87 and ISNetworld/Avetta qualifications are prerequisites for access to industrial/refining facilities and reinforce switching costs.  
             Sector fit with Thornfield’s experience base.         The operational profile (founder-led, regional, capital-intensive, regulatory-sensitive) maps closely to GreenWorks Remediation and Allied Waste Systems, providing a relevant benchmarking and value-creation playbook.

         9. PFAS Growth Thesis — Critical Assessment  
      The PFAS opportunity is real at the sector level, but the company-specific projections are aggressive and rest on undisclosed assumptions:  
             Projection vs. achievable growth.         PFAS is projected at $14.0M (FY2025E, +126%) and $22.0M (FY2026E, +57%). Sector data and Thornfield’s GreenWorks experience support 30–50% company-level growth once a practice exceeds ~$5 million — implying FY2025 PFAS of ~$8–9 million and FY2026 of ~$11–13 million under a moderated scenario, versus $14 million and $22 million projected.  
             No PFAS-specific contracted backlog.         The backlog report provides backlog only at the segment level (Remediation $26.8 million) and states qualitatively that         “    a significant portion of the Remediation pipeline relates to PFAS.    ”         The $14.0 million FY2025E PFAS figure is not tied to signed contracts. The contracted-vs.-pipeline split for PFAS must be requested.  
             Professional Engineer capacity constraint.         CES’s PE headcount declined from three to two in January 2025 following an unreplaced retirement. PFAS site assessments and remediation designs require PE sign-off. Sustaining the projected growth would likely require hiring two to three additional PEs; the environmental PE market is tight, with typical recruitment timelines of six to twelve months. The CIM does not disclose a PE hiring plan. This is a near-term execution bottleneck that could throttle the PFAS ramp independent of demand.  
             Competitive entry risk.         Larger national environmental services firms are entering PFAS with greater resources; CES’s early-mover advantage may erode as the market scales, consistent with the GreenWorks pattern of competitive pressure intensifying after initial demand was satisfied.  
             Margin assumption dependency.         The projected Adjusted EBITDA margin expansion from 19.6% to 21.5% is attributed to a mix shift toward higher-margin PFAS. If PFAS volume disappoints, both the revenue trajectory and the margin expansion weaken simultaneously — a compounding effect on forward EBITDA and valuation.

         11. Regulatory and Compliance Risk  
             DEQ enforcement action (August 14, 2024).         CES entered a consent agreement with Oregon DEQ to resolve an enforcement action related to improper storage of contaminated soils at the Portland facility — the same facility that is the Company’s headquarters and primary operations yard, owned personally by Mr. Oakes. The settlement was $875,000 plus $325,000 in legal fees ($1.2 million total), added back in full as         “    one-time.    ”         Oregon DEQ has been         “    particularly active in enforcement    ”         per the sector memo, and recurring regulatory/compliance costs of $200–$500K annually are inherent to this business model (corroborated by GreenWorks at ~$275K/yr and Allied at ~$310K/yr). The full $1.2 million add-back is likely overstated by $300–$500K; only the excess over a normalized run-rate is defensible.  
             Compliance history.         The deal team should request CES’s complete regulatory enforcement and notice-of-violation history for the past ten years to determine whether the DEQ action was truly isolated or symptomatic of systemic compliance issues — particularly given it occurred at the headquarters site.  
             Site environmental condition.         Because the enforcement action involved contaminated-soil storage at the Portland HQ (owned by the seller), the environmental condition of the site is a diligence priority, with implications for both real estate valuation and potential retained/assumed liability. Environmental insurance coverage (pollution, environmental liability) should be reviewed.  
             Permit continuity.         All listed permits (DEQ contractor license, four-state hazardous waste transporter permits, EPA Generator ID) are stated as current. Permit transferability to a new owner and renewal timing should be confirmed.

             PE staffing.         As noted in Section 9, the unreplaced PE retirement creates a technical-capacity risk for the remediation and PFAS practices. Management’s PE hiring plan (or lack thereof) should be assessed.

             Why these numbers drive the package.            The Total LTV of 97.66% means aggregate secured debt nearly equals enterprise value, leaving a second lien equity cushion of only 25.78% ($165M in absolute terms). A TEV decline of 25.78% would reduce second lien recovery to zero; a 15% decline (to ~$544M) would reduce second lien theoretical recovery to ~$69M, or roughly 46 cents on the dollar, before liquidation costs. This thinness is the 2L Agent’s central concern and the reason it requires structural protections (standstill triggers, optional revolver assumption, residual proceeds access, refinancing flexibility). At the same time, the near-unity Total LTV is the 1L Agent’s central concern: any leakage of collateral value or proceeds toward junior creditors directly threatens first lien recovery, which is the reason it requires a meaningful standstill, a par purchase price, a hard sweep on material dispositions, and refinancing guardrails. The package below gives each side the protections that its position in this capital structure actually requires, and no more.

                V.  Section 3.01(b) — Standstill Period  
             A.  Contested Positions  
             1L Agent:            a 270-day standstill following delivery of a Blockage Notice, with no early-termination events under any circumstance. The 1L Agent’s credit committee has established a hard floor of 150 days, below which Cartwright cannot agree without re-syndication. The committee has indicated it could accept a standstill in the 150-to-180-day range, and that it would consider “carefully defined, narrow, and objectively verifiable” early-termination triggers if paired with a standstill closer to 180 days.  
             2L Agent:            a 90-day standstill, with early-termination triggers. The 2L Agent has stated it can accept up to 180 days, but only if the ICA includes meaningful early-termination triggers — a firm condition that it has stated cannot be decoupled from the duration. The triggers the 2L Agent has identified include (i) cessation of active enforcement by the 1L Secured Parties, (ii) disposition of substantially all of the collateral, and (iii) a plan of reorganization impairing 2L claims without 2L consent.  
             B.  Proposed Compromise Language  
             Proposed replacement language for Section 3.01(b) of the ICA:  
                                         Section 3.01(b) — Standstill Period.  
           Following the occurrence and during the continuance of a Second Lien Default, the Second Lien Agent shall not exercise any rights or remedies with respect to the Collateral for a period of one hundred eighty (180) days following the date of receipt by the Second Lien Agent of a written notice (a “Blockage Notice”) from the First Lien Agent specifying the Second Lien Default giving rise thereto (the “Standstill Period”). Notwithstanding the foregoing, the Standstill Period shall terminate prior to the expiration of the one hundred eighty (180)-day period upon the occurrence of any of the following early termination events (each, an “Early Termination Event”):  
            (A) the First Lien Agent shall not have commenced an Enforcement Action with respect to any material portion of the Collateral within one hundred twenty (120) days following delivery of the Blockage Notice; provided that the First Lien Agent may extend such 120-day period by delivering to the Second Lien Agent a written notice certifying that it is actively pursuing a consensual restructuring, amendment, or workout with the Borrower, which notice shall extend such period by an additional sixty (60) days (and only one such extension shall be permitted per Standstill Period);  
            (B) the First Lien Agent shall credit bid the First Lien Obligations to acquire, or shall commence a liquidation of, all or substantially all of the Collateral;  
            (C) a sale, lease, or other disposition of all or substantially all of the Collateral shall be commenced or consummated (whether under Section 363 of the Bankruptcy Code or otherwise) without the consent of the Second Lien Agent (which consent shall not be unreasonably withheld, conditioned, or delayed); or  
            (D) a plan of reorganization shall be filed or confirmed that impairs the Second Lien Obligations without the consent of the Second Lien Agent (which consent shall not be unreasonably withheld, conditioned, or delayed).  
           Upon the occurrence of any Early Termination Event, the Standstill Period shall immediately terminate and the Second Lien Agent may exercise any and all rights and remedies available to it under the Second Lien Loan Documents and applicable law with respect to the Collateral, subject to Section 3.03 and Section 4.01. For the avoidance of doubt, the occurrence of an Early Termination Event shall not alter or affect the subordination of the Second Lien Obligations and the Liens securing the Second Lien Obligations to the First Lien Obligations and the Liens securing the First Lien Obligations.  
           The First Lien Agent may deliver no more than two (2) Blockage Notices in any rolling twelve (12)-month period, and the aggregate duration of all Standstill Periods in any rolling twelve (12)-month period shall not exceed three hundred sixty (360) days. If the Second Lien Default specified in a Blockage Notice is cured or waived in accordance with the Second Lien Credit Agreement prior to the expiration of the applicable Standstill Period, such Standstill Period shall immediately terminate, and any subsequent exercise of remedies by the Second Lien Agent shall require the delivery of a new Blockage Notice and the commencement of a new Standstill Period in accordance with this Section 3.01(b).  
       
             C.  Negotiation Rationale  
             1.  Duration — 180 days is the market median and sits within both agents’ stated flexibility.  
             The KTM survey of 42 comparable ICAs (January 2022–December 2024) shows a median standstill of 180 days (mean 172.9; 25th percentile 150; 75th percentile 202.5; range 90–270). 180 days is therefore the single most defensible market figure. It is also squarely within the zone both agents have signaled: it is 30 days above the 1L Agent’s hard credit-committee floor of 150 days, and it is exactly at the 2L Agent’s stated ceiling of 180 days. Neither agent is asked to move beyond the flexibility it has already communicated.  
             The 1L Agent’s own rationale supports 180 days. Its position letter states that a meaningful restructuring or orderly asset-sale process for a business of Meridian’s size “requires, at a minimum, six to nine months.” 180 days is six months — the low end of the 1L Agent’s own stated range — and thus provides the minimum period the 1L Agent itself has identified as necessary, while the early-termination triggers (discussed below) ensure the standstill cannot be used tactically to block 2L remedies indefinitely. The 1L Agent gives up 90 days relative to its 270-day opening; the 2L Agent gives up 90 days relative to its 90-day opening. The compromise is symmetrical.  
             2.  Early-termination triggers — the market-standard pairing and the 2L Agent’s firm condition.  
             76% of the surveyed deals (32 of 42) include early-termination triggers, and 25 of 42 deals pair a standstill of 180 days or longer with triggers — the combination the 2L Agent has identified as its firm condition and that the survey identifies as the “market standard pairing.” The four triggers proposed above are drawn from the most common triggers observed in the survey and from the specific triggers the 2L Agent has flagged:

             The triggers are narrow and objectively verifiable, as the 1L Agent’s credit committee has required. Trigger (A) turns on a date certain (120 days) and a written certification; triggers (B), (C), and (D) turn on discrete, externally observable events (a credit bid, a sale motion, a confirmed plan). The 60-day workout extension in trigger (A) is the key balance: it gives the 1L Agent the full 180 days if it is genuinely pursuing a consensual workout, while protecting the 2L Agent against pure inaction. This directly addresses the 2L Agent’s stated concern that a 270-day standstill “would permit the First Lien Lenders to run out the clock on Second Lien remedies while collateral value declines.”  
             3.  Blockage Notice cap — a new protection against perpetual standstill.  
             Neither agent’s opening proposal included a cap on Blockage Notices, but the working draft’s drafting note flags this as an open issue. The proposed cap — no more than two Blockage Notices and no more than 360 aggregate standstill days in any rolling 12-month period — gives the 1L Agent up to two full 180-day standstills per year, which is substantial protection, while assuring the 2L Agent (and the Borrower) that the standstill cannot be perpetually renewed to convert lien subordination into de facto payment subordination. This is a borrower- and 2L-friendly protection that the 1L Agent can accept because 360 days of aggregate annual standstill far exceeds what the market requires.  
             4.  Economic quantification — the floating-rate structure offsets the 2L’s time-value cost.  
             The 1L Agent correctly notes that the floating-rate structure mitigates the time-value cost of the standstill to the 2L. During a 180-day standstill, interest continues to accrue on the $150M Second Lien Term Loan B at SOFR + 625 bps (11.58% all-in), generating approximately $8.69M of accrued interest ($150M × 11.58% × 180/360). At the 1L Agent’s 270-day opening, the 2L would accrue approximately $13.03M; at the 2L Agent’s 90-day opening, approximately $4.34M. The 180-day compromise therefore leaves the 2L earning roughly $8.69M of interest during the standstill — a meaningful partial offset to the time-value and collateral-erosion concerns the 2L Agent has raised, while the triggers protect against the residual erosion risk.  
             5.  Comparable precedent.  
             Deal-007 in the survey — a non-sponsor industrial transaction with a $475M 1L facility, $150M 2L facility, and $625M total secured debt, structurally identical to Meridian — used a 150-day standstill with no triggers. Deal-038 ($470M 1L / $155M 2L / $625M total, sponsor-backed) used a 240-day standstill with a comprehensive trigger set. The proposed 180-day-with-triggers formulation sits squarely between these two comparable precedents and reflects the market-standard pairing identified across the full 42-deal population.  
                VI.  Section 5.01 — Second Lien Purchase Right  
             A.  Contested Positions  
             Four sub-issues are in dispute: (i) the purchase-price mechanic; (ii) the interest rate used to compute accrued interest; (iii) mandatory versus optional assumption of unfunded revolver commitments; and (iv) the scope of exercise triggers.  
             1L Agent:            par — 100% of outstanding principal plus accrued interest at the default rate (SOFR + 450 bps), plus all fees, costs, and expenses — with mandatory assumption of all unfunded First Lien Revolver commitments ($125M), exercisable only upon an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement. The 1L Agent has stated that par for funded debt is “non-negotiable,” but has signaled flexibility on (a) using the non-default contract rate instead of the default rate and (b) making revolver assumption optional rather than mandatory.  
             2L Agent:            the lesser of par (at the non-default contract rate) or fair market value determined by an independent dealer poll, with no obligation to assume unfunded revolver commitments and broader exercise triggers (1L Event of Default, 2L Event of Default, or acceleration). The 2L Agent has acknowledged that par for funded debt “may be acceptable,” conditioned on revolver assumption being optional — a condition it has explained is driven by structural constraints in its fund’s limited partnership agreement that prevent the fund from taking on open-ended revolving credit commitments.  
             B.  Proposed Compromise Language  
             Proposed replacement language for Section 5.01 of the ICA:  
                                         Section 5.01 — Second Lien Purchase Right.  
           At any time following the occurrence and during the continuance of an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement, or upon the delivery by the First Lien Agent of a notice of acceleration of the First Lien Obligations, in each case (a “Purchase Trigger”), the Second Lien Agent (on behalf of the Second Lien Lenders or any designee of the foregoing) shall have the right, but not the obligation (the “Second Lien Purchase Right”), to purchase all (but not less than all) of the First Lien Obligations by paying to the First Lien Agent in immediately available funds a purchase price (the “Purchase Price”) equal to the sum of: (i) one hundred percent (100%) of the aggregate outstanding principal amount of all funded First Lien Obligations (including all amounts outstanding under the First Lien Term Loan A and all drawn amounts under the First Lien Revolver); plus (ii) all accrued and unpaid interest thereon, calculated at the non-default contract rate applicable under the First Lien Credit Agreement (i.e., SOFR plus 250 basis points), through and including the date of purchase, calculated on the basis of a 360-day year for the actual number of days elapsed; plus (iii) all fees, costs, and expenses (including reasonable and documented fees and disbursements of counsel), indemnification obligations (to the extent then due and payable or asserted in writing), breakage costs, and other amounts then due and payable under the First Lien Loan Documents. The Purchase Notice must be delivered to the First Lien Agent within thirty (30) days following the occurrence of the Purchase Trigger.  
           The Second Lien Agent (or its designee) may, at its sole election, assume all unfunded commitments under the First Lien Revolver by executing and delivering to the First Lien Agent an assumption agreement in form and substance reasonably satisfactory to the First Lien Agent; provided that if the Second Lien Agent (or its designee) does not elect to assume such unfunded commitments, the unfunded commitments under the First Lien Revolver shall terminate upon consummation of the purchase, and the purchasing party shall have no obligation with respect thereto. The purchase shall be on a par, non-recourse, as-is, where-is basis, without representation or warranty of any kind by the First Lien Agent or any First Lien Lender (other than customary representations as to the authority of the assigning lender to assign and the enforceability of the transfer).  
       
             C.  Negotiation Rationale  
             1.  Par for funded debt — the 1L Agent’s non-negotiable, conceded by the 2L Agent.  
             The 1L Agent has stated that par for funded debt is non-negotiable, and the 2L Agent has acknowledged that par for funded debt “may be acceptable.” The compromise adopts par (100% of outstanding principal plus accrued interest plus fees) and drops the 2L Agent’s proposed FMV alternative. This is the market majority: 71% of surveyed deals use a par mechanic (19% hybrid; only 10% pure FMV). The 1L Agent therefore receives its core protection — the First Lien Lenders are made whole in any scenario in which junior creditors acquire the senior position, which is the basis on which the First Lien Lenders priced SOFR + 250 bps. The 2L Agent gives up the FMV alternative, which it had sought to preserve the economic viability of the purchase right in distressed scenarios; in exchange, it receives the concessions on interest rate and revolver assumption discussed below, which together preserve the practical exercisability of the right.  
             2.  Non-default contract rate — the market majority and a bounded, quantified concession.  
             69% of the surveyed deals exclude default-rate interest from the purchase price and use the non-default contract rate; 90% include the non-default contract rate. The compromise adopts the non-default contract rate (SOFR + 250 bps, 7.83% all-in). Both agents have signaled flexibility on this point. The economic impact is bounded and quantifiable:

             The default-rate increment — 200 basis points on $475M, or approximately $9.5M per annum — is the value the 1L Agent’s opening position would have captured. Using the non-default rate instead transfers at most $2.4M (90-day) to $7.1M (270-day) of that increment back to the purchasing 2L group, depending on when the right is exercised. This is a meaningful but bounded concession that does not impair the 1L Agent’s par recovery: the First Lien Lenders still receive 100% of principal, contract-rate interest, and all fees. The 1L Agent’s concern that the default rate is a “contractual entitlement” is respected to the extent that default-rate interest continues to accrue and be owed under the First Lien Credit Agreement itself; the compromise simply measures the purchase price at the contract rate, consistent with the market majority and with the comparable Deal-007 precedent (par at the non-default rate).  
             3.  Optional revolver assumption — a structural constraint, not a negotiating posture.  
             62% of the surveyed deals make unfunded revolver assumption optional (24% mandatory; 14% silent), and the trend is accelerating: 77% of 2024 deals use optional assumption versus 50% of 2022 deals. The comparable Deal-007 used optional assumption. The compromise makes assumption optional at the purchaser’s sole election; if the purchaser does not elect to assume, the unfunded revolver commitments terminate upon consummation of the purchase (the revolver lenders being made whole on their funded exposure through the par payment).  
             This is the term the 2L Agent has identified as a deal-breaker, and for a structural rather than positional reason: Ridgeline’s fund limited partnership agreement restricts the fund’s ability to take on open-ended revolving credit commitment obligations. Mandatory assumption of the $125M revolver would require the 2L to commit to fund up to $125M of future draws at the Borrower’s discretion — a fundamentally different economic proposition from acquiring existing funded debt, and one the 2L’s vehicle cannot undertake. The 1L Agent has acknowledged this is “a practical consideration, not a positional one” and has signaled willingness to discuss making assumption optional. The compromise honors that signal. The 1L Agent’s concern about bifurcation of the syndicate is addressed because, if the purchaser does not assume, the revolver commitments terminate cleanly upon the par payoff rather than remaining outstanding with a non-purchasing lender group.  
             4.  Exercise triggers and window — market-standard scope.  
             The compromise permits exercise upon an Event of Default under the First Lien Credit Agreement or upon acceleration of the First Lien Obligations, with a 30-day exercise window. Acceleration is a universal trigger in the survey (100% of deals); the 30-day window is the survey median (mean 34.8; range 10–60). This is narrower than the 2L Agent’s opening (which would have added a standalone 2L Event of Default trigger) but broader than the 1L Agent’s opening (which would have omitted acceleration). A standalone 2L Event of Default trigger is not market-standard for a purchase right that acquires the 1L position — the right presupposes 1L distress — and is therefore dropped. The 30-day window gives the 1L Agent certainty that the right, once triggered, must be exercised promptly or forfeited.  
             5.  Illustrative purchase price.  
             On the assumptions used throughout the negotiation (full draw, 90-day accrual, SOFR at 5.33%), the illustrative par purchase price for funded debt is approximately $485.8M, comprising $475.0M principal + $9.30M non-default-rate accrued interest + approximately $1.5M of fees and expenses. This matches the figure both agents have used in their communications and the comparable Deal-007 precedent ($484.3M illustrative par). The 2L’s total capital at risk following exercise (funded purchase price plus its existing $150M 2L position) would be approximately $635.8M — near, but not exceeding, the appraised TEV of $640M — which is the economic reality of a purchase right in a 97.66% LTV structure and is precisely why the optional-revolver and non-default-rate concessions are necessary to keep the right exercisable.  
                VII.  Section 2.03(c) — Permitted Second Lien Refinancing Indebtedness  
             A.  Contested Positions  
             Three sub-issues are in dispute: (A) the permitted principal increase; (B) the maturity floor; and (C) the restrictiveness test.  
             1L Agent:            0% principal increase (equal or lesser principal only); maturity no earlier than 91 days after the First Lien Maturity Date (September 14, 2030); a blanket “no more restrictive in any material respect” test. The 1L Agent has signaled openness to a narrowed restrictiveness test paired with an MFN right, and willingness to discuss the principal cap “in the context of the overall package.”  
             2L Agent:            up to 20% principal increase ($30M); maturity no earlier than the original Second Lien Maturity Date (June 15, 2031); no restrictiveness test. The 2L Agent has signaled it can accept a 10% cap ($15M) if limited to refinancing fees and costs, and openness to a narrowed test paired with an MFN right.  
             B.  Proposed Compromise Language  
             Proposed replacement language for Section 2.03(c) of the ICA:

             C.  Negotiation Rationale  
             1.  Principal cap — 10% limited to fees and costs is the market median and the 2L Agent’s stated landing point.  
             The survey shows a median principal-increase cap of 10% (mean 8.9%); 52% of deals fall in the 1–10% range, and 79% of deals that permit any increase limit it to fees, costs, and premiums. The comparable Deal-007 used a 10% cap ($15M) limited to fees and costs. The compromise adopts exactly that: a 10% cap ($15M on the current $150M 2L facility), limited to refinancing-related fees and costs (call premiums, arranger fees, OID, transaction expenses, and accrued interest). This is the 2L Agent’s explicitly stated landing point (“can accept a 10% principal increase cap … provided the increase is limited to refinancing-related fees and costs”) and is the market median.  
             The 1L Agent’s 0% position is not workable: virtually every refinancing in the leveraged loan market involves non-trivial transaction costs (the 2L Credit Agreement itself provides for call premiums of 102% in year one and 101% in year two; arranger fees typically run 200–350 bps; OID may run 97–99 cents on the dollar). A 0% cap would economically lock the 2L into the existing facility regardless of market conditions, defeating the purpose of a refinancing permission. The 10%-limited-to-fees formulation addresses the 1L Agent’s real concern — incremental permanent secured debt compressing the equity cushion — because the $15M of headroom is consumed by one-time costs paid to third parties (arrangers, exiting lenders, professionals), not retained as borrower cash or permanent leverage.

             3.  Maturity floor — both tests satisfied; the 2L position is more restrictive than the 1L’s.  
             93% of surveyed deals use the 91-days-after-1L-maturity test, and 81% also include an original-2L-maturity floor. The compromise requires the refinancing maturity to be no earlier than the later of (i) 91 days after the First Lien Maturity Date (September 14, 2030) and (ii) the original Second Lien Maturity Date (June 15, 2031). Because June 15, 2031 is approximately nine months after September 14, 2030, the effective floor is June 15, 2031 — which is more restrictive than the 1L Agent’s own proposal. This is a clean compromise in which both positions are incorporated and the more protective one governs. The 1L Agent cannot object: its 91-day floor is expressly included, and the result affords the First Lien Lenders a longer temporal cushion than they requested. The 2L Agent gets the original-maturity floor it sought. There is no scenario in which the 2L position disadvantages the 1L, which is the analysis you asked KTM to perform.  
             4.  Restrictiveness test — narrowed scope paired with an MFN right.  
             52% of surveyed deals use a narrowed restrictiveness test (33% blanket; 14% none). Critically, 100% of narrowed-test deals pair the test with an MFN provision, and 82% of those MFN provisions are limited to financial maintenance covenants. The trend is accelerating: the narrowed-test-plus-MFN combination rose from 38% of 2022 deals to 65% of 2024 deals. The comparable Deal-007 used a narrowed test with an MFN on financial covenants. The compromise adopts exactly this formulation.  
             The narrowed test is limited to three enumerated categories — (A) financial maintenance covenants, (B) negative covenants that would restrict actions expressly permitted under the First Lien Credit Agreement, and (C) cure periods for events of default — each of which maps to a specific risk the 1L Agent identified in its position letter (a tightening of the Total Leverage covenant triggering cross-default; an expansion of negative covenants conflicting with 1L-permitted actions; shorter cure periods accelerating 2L enforcement inconsistent with the standstill). The MFN right ensures that if any refinancing contains a financial maintenance covenant more favorable to lenders than the corresponding 1L covenant, the 1L lenders automatically receive the benefit — neutralizing the cross-default/destabilization risk the 1L Agent identified, while leaving the 2L free to negotiate its bilateral commercial terms outside those three categories. Contextualized against the existing 2L Total Leverage covenant of 6.5x, the MFN gives the 1L the benefit of any tighter covenant (e.g., a 6.0x step-down) introduced in a refinancing. This is the precise middle ground both agents have signaled.  
                VIII.  Section 4.02 — Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds  
             A.  Contested Positions  
             1L Agent:            a 100% sweep of all Net Cash Proceeds from any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event to the First Lien Obligations, expressly overriding the reinvestment rights in the First Lien Credit Agreement (the 365-day reinvestment period and the $5M individual / $15M annual aggregate thresholds in Section 2.05(b)), with no portion available to the Second Lien Obligations until the First Lien Obligations are paid in full. The 1L Agent has stated that a hard sweep on large or extraordinary sales is essential and not subject to significant compromise, but has acknowledged that reinvestment rights for smaller, ordinary-course sales may be acceptable, and that 2L residual access after full 1L satisfaction is “less of a concern” if the hard sweep on significant sales is preserved.  
             2L Agent:            follow the First Lien Credit Agreement’s reinvestment and mandatory-prepayment mechanics, with residual proceeds flowing to the Second Lien Obligations. The 2L Agent has stated it can accept the 1L reinvestment mechanics for ordinary-course sales provided the residual flows to the 2L rather than back to the Borrower, and has flagged the need for anti-evasion language. A $20M ordinary-course threshold has been discussed informally.  
             B.  Proposed Compromise Language  
             Proposed replacement language for Section 4.02 of the ICA:  
                                         Section 4.02 — Application of Asset Disposition and Casualty Proceeds.  
           All Net Cash Proceeds received by the Borrower or any other loan party in respect of any Asset Disposition or Casualty Event shall be applied as follows:  
            (a) Ordinary-Course Asset Dispositions. With respect to Net Cash Proceeds from any Asset Disposition (other than an Extraordinary Asset Disposition) where the Net Cash Proceeds of such Asset Disposition do not exceed $20,000,000 (the “Ordinary-Course Threshold”), such Net Cash Proceeds shall be applied in accordance with the reinvestment and mandatory prepayment provisions of the First Lien Credit Agreement, including (i) the Borrower’s right to reinvest such Net Cash Proceeds in replacement assets used or useful in the Borrower’s business during the three hundred sixty-five (365)-day reinvestment period set forth in the First Lien Credit Agreement, and (ii) the mandatory prepayment sweep of one hundred percent (100%) of any Net Cash Proceeds not so reinvested, in each case subject to the individual $5,000,000 and annual aggregate $15,000,000 thresholds set forth in Section 2.05(b) of the First Lien Credit Agreement. The reinvestment rights granted to the Borrower under the First Lien Credit Agreement shall be preserved with respect to such Net Cash Proceeds.  
            (b) Extraordinary Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events. With respect to Net Cash Proceeds from (i) any Asset Disposition where the Net Cash Proceeds exceed the Ordinary-Course Threshold (an “Extraordinary Asset Disposition”), or (ii) any Casualty Event, one hundred percent (100%) of such Net Cash Proceeds shall be applied to prepay the First Lien Obligations in accordance with the waterfall set forth in Section 4.01, until the Discharge of First Lien Obligations has occurred. The reinvestment rights granted to the Borrower under the First Lien Credit Agreement shall not apply to such Net Cash Proceeds, and this Section 4.02(b) shall govern the application of such Net Cash Proceeds notwithstanding any provision of the First Lien Credit Agreement to the contrary.  
            (c) Anti-Evasion. For purposes of this Section 4.02, all Asset Dispositions (and any series of related Asset Dispositions) shall be aggregated, and no Asset Disposition (or series of related Asset Dispositions) shall be structured, timed, or otherwise arranged in a manner designed to avoid the Ordinary-Course Threshold. Without limiting the generality of the foregoing, (i) all Asset Dispositions that are part of a single plan or series of related transactions shall be treated as a single Asset Disposition, and (ii) all Asset Dispositions during any rolling twelve (12)-month period shall be aggregated for purposes of determining whether the Ordinary-Course Threshold has been exceeded. This Section 4.02(c) is in addition to, and not in limitation of, the anti-evasion provisions set forth in the definition of “Asset Disposition” in the First Lien Credit Agreement.  
            (d) Residual Proceeds. After the Discharge of First Lien Obligations, any remaining Net Cash Proceeds shall be applied to the Second Lien Obligations in accordance with the mandatory prepayment provisions of the Second Lien Credit Agreement. No Net Cash Proceeds shall be distributed to the Borrower, its equity holders, or any other person until all First Lien Obligations and all Second Lien Obligations have been paid in full.  
            (e) Reporting. The Borrower shall deliver to the First Lien Agent and the Second Lien Agent (i) a quarterly certificate of compliance setting forth all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events during the applicable fiscal quarter and the application of Net Cash Proceeds therefrom, (ii) prompt written notice (and in any event within five (5) Business Days) of any Asset Disposition generating Net Cash Proceeds in excess of $5,000,000, and (iii) an annual aggregate disposition report setting forth an itemized list of all Asset Dispositions and Casualty Events during the applicable fiscal year and the application of Net Cash Proceeds therefrom.  
       
             C.  Negotiation Rationale  
             1.  Bifurcated waterfall — the market-standard compromise framework.  
             100% of the surveyed deals sweep asset-sale and casualty proceeds to the 1L as the first priority — the universal market standard, which the compromise preserves. The dispute is not whether the 1L is paid first, but whether reinvestment rights are preserved and whether the 2L receives residual proceeds. On those questions, the market is decisive: 81% of deals preserve reinvestment rights; 90% include a hard sweep for large or extraordinary sales; 79% provide 2L residual access after 1L paydown. The bifurcated approach in the compromise — reinvestment for ordinary-course sales, hard sweep for extraordinary sales and casualty, residual to the 2L — is the precise framework the survey identifies as the market-standard compromise, and is the framework used in the comparable Deal-007 ($20M ordinary-course threshold, 365-day reinvestment, hard sweep for extraordinary sales, 2L residual after full 1L paydown).  
             2.  The $20M ordinary-course threshold — market median, economically bounded.  
             The median ordinary-course threshold in the survey is $20M (mean $19.7; range $10M–$30M); 81% of deals exclude ordinary-course sales from the hard sweep. The $20M threshold represents approximately 22.4% of LTM EBITDA ($89.4M) — a meaningful but bounded amount that allows Meridian to conduct routine asset turnover (replacement of aging manufacturing equipment, upgrades to production lines, disposition of surplus inventory) without triggering the hard sweep, while ensuring that any material disposition is captured. The 1L Agent has expressly acknowledged that reinvestment rights for smaller ordinary-course sales “may be acceptable” and that “the Borrower’s business requires routine asset turnover.” The compromise gives the 1L Agent the hard sweep it deems essential on any sale exceeding $20M and on all casualty events, while preserving the operational flexibility the First Lien Credit Agreement itself grants for ordinary-course sales — flexibility Meridian’s management has emphasized is necessary to maintain and modernize its industrial asset base.  
             3.  Anti-evasion — essential and market-standard.  
             97% of deals that include an ordinary-course exclusion also include an anti-evasion clause at the same threshold. The compromise adopts anti-evasion language at the $20M threshold, aggregating (i) all Asset Dispositions that are part of a single plan or series of related transactions, and (ii) all Asset Dispositions during any rolling 12-month period. This directly addresses the concern both agents and you have raised: that a large disposition could be structured as a series of sub-threshold sales to evade the hard sweep. Notably, the First Lien Credit Agreement already contains anti-evasion language at the $5M / $15M level (in the definition of “Asset Disposition”), so the ICA provision builds on an existing, agreed mechanism rather than introducing a novel concept. The 2L Agent has specifically requested this protection, and the 1L Agent has flagged it as essential.  
             4.  Residual proceeds to the 2L — symbolically and economically important given the thin cushion.  
             79% of surveyed deals provide 2L residual access after 1L paydown. The compromise directs residual proceeds (after the Discharge of First Lien Obligations) to the Second Lien Obligations rather than back to the Borrower. The 2L Agent has characterized 2L residual access as “symbolically important even if economically limited” — the 2L lenders must be able to demonstrate to their investors and LPs that the intercreditor structure preserves at least a theoretical waterfall right after full 1L satisfaction. The 1L Agent has stated this is “less of a concern” provided the hard sweep on significant sales is preserved, which the compromise does. Directing residual to the 2L (rather than trapping it at the Borrower level) also deleverages the capital structure for the benefit of all stakeholders, which is consistent with the 2L Agent’s rationale and does not impair the 1L, which is paid in full before any residual flows.  
             5.  Reporting — administrability and audit trail.  
             The compromise adopts the reporting package observed in the market: a quarterly certificate of compliance (90% of deals), prompt event-driven notice of any sale exceeding the $5M individual trigger (79%), and an annual aggregate disposition report (38%). This gives both agents visibility into asset-sale activity, supports administration of the bifurcated waterfall, and provides the audit trail necessary to police the anti-evasion provision. Neither agent’s opening proposal included a reporting mechanism, but the working draft’s drafting note flags it as an open issue, and the market data supports the package proposed.

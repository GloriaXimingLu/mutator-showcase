## IV. Detailed Issue Analysis

### A. Specialty Occupation and Beneficiary Qualifications

**Issue 1 (Critical) — Field-of-study mismatch and inadequate credential evaluation.**

The proffered position requires, per the Employer Support Letter (Section 3), "a Bachelor's degree or higher in Computer Science, Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence, or a closely related field." The beneficiary's single documented degree is a Bachelor of Technology in **Electronics and Communication Engineering (ECE)** from JNTU Hyderabad (August 2010–May 2014). The cross-referenced documents reveal the following problems:

- **The credential evaluation does not bridge the gap.** Westbridge Credential Services Report No. WCS-2025-03187 equates the B.Tech to "a four-year Bachelor of Science (B.S.) in **Electronics and Communication Engineering**" — i.e., it confirms an ECE equivalency, not a CS/ML/AI equivalency. The evaluation therefore does not establish that the beneficiary's degree is in the specialty or a closely related field.
- **The evaluation expressly disclaims the necessary opinion.** Section 7 (Limitations) states: "This evaluation does not opine on whether the applicant's field of study satisfies the requirements of any particular position or petition." This is precisely the opinion the petition needs.
- **The evaluation undercuts the "closely related" argument.** Section 4 quantifies computer-related coursework at "approximately 12 credit hours out of the total approximately 160 credit hours … approximately 7.5% of the overall curriculum." The transcripts corroborate this: the only clearly CS/ML-relevant courses are CS101 (C Programming), CS201 (Advanced C), CS401 (Data Structures), and EC704 (Introduction to Artificial Intelligence, an elective). The balance of the curriculum is core ECE (signals, communications, VLSI, electromagnetics, antennas, microwave, radar, etc.).
- **The "alternative qualification" is not a recognized USCIS standard.** The Employer Support Letter states that "a Bachelor's degree in any engineering discipline combined with 5 years of progressive experience in machine learning may be accepted as an equivalent qualification." USCIS does not recognize "any engineering degree + 5 years" as a per se equivalency. Where a beneficiary holds a full four-year degree, the question is field relevance, not degree shortfall; the 3-for-1 experience rule (8 CFR 214.2(h)(4)(iii)(C)) is inapplicable because there is no credit-hour shortfall to cure. The petitioner must instead demonstrate that ECE is "closely related" to the CS/ML/AI specialty, or that the combination of the ECE degree and specialized experience equates to a degree in the specialty.
- **Internal tension weakens the specialty-occupation showing.** Asserting that the job requires a CS/ML/AI degree while simultaneously arguing that an ECE degree qualifies creates the classic USCIS dilemma: either the CS/ML/AI degree is not truly the minimum requirement (weakening the specialty-occupation prong), or the beneficiary does not meet it (weakening the qualification prong).

**Risk:** High likelihood of an RFE on both the specialty-occupation and beneficiary-qualification prongs, and elevated denial risk if not supplemented.

**Recommendation:** Obtain a **supplemental expert credential evaluation** that (a) opines specifically that the ECE coursework (probability/stochastic processes, signals and systems, DSP, linear-algebra-intensive content, computer architecture, the AI elective) combined with the beneficiary's progressive ML experience is equivalent to a U.S. bachelor's in a CS/ML-related field for this specialty, and (b) is authored by an evaluator with relevant technical credentials. Reconcile the "alternative qualification" language with a defensible standard. (See also Issue 2 — if an undocumented U.S. degree exists, it may resolve this issue.)

**Issue 10 (Moderate) — Insufficient specialty-occupation evidence.**

The Employer Support Letter (Section 3, "Industry Standard") asserts that peer companies "uniformly require equivalent educational backgrounds," but provides no corroborating evidence. USCIS routinely issues RFEs on the specialty-occupation prong for analytics/consulting roles, particularly where the petitioner relies on conclusory assertions.

**Recommendation:** Supplement with O*NET task/SVP data for SOC 15-2051, the DOL's JobZone and Specific Vocational Preparation (SVP) indicators, and 3–5 sample comparable job postings from peer employers requiring a bachelor's degree in the specialty. A position description keyed to O*NET tasks strengthens the showing.

### B. Prevailing Wage and Wage-Level Classification

**Issue 3 (Critical) — Wage level appears understated (Level 2 vs. Level 3/4).**

The PWD (P-300-25012-183456) and the certified LCA both classify the position at **Wage Level 2** ($113,734). The offered wage is $128,000, which exceeds Level 2 but is **below Level 3 ($137,946)**. Cross-referencing the position description against the DOL wage-level definitions printed in the PWD itself reveals a likely misclassification:

| DOL Level (per PWD) | DOL Definition | Position facts (per Employer Letter) |
|---|---|---|
| Level 2 — $113,734 | "good understanding … perform moderately complex tasks requiring **limited judgment**" | — |
| Level 3 — $137,946 | "sound understanding … may exercise **independent judgment**, and may have **supervisory responsibilities**" | "exercises **independent judgment** in the design and implementation of machine learning solutions"; "Leading and mentoring a team of … junior data scientists" |
| Level 4 — $162,157 | "advanced skills … **broad latitude** in determining objectives, and **supervise and train** other workers" | "Senior" title; leads/mentors junior team; reports to CTO; 3+ years required |

The Employer Support Letter expressly states the role "exercises independent judgment" and involves leading/mentoring junior data scientists — language that maps directly onto the **Level 3** definition, and arguably Level 4 ("supervise and train"). The "Senior" designation, the 3-year minimum experience requirement, and the supervisory duties are all inconsistent with Level 2 ("moderately complex tasks requiring limited judgment"). Notably, the PWD's own abbreviated duties summary includes "lead a team of data scientists," which itself signals supervisory responsibility inconsistent with Level 2.

**Risk:** If the correct level is Level 3, the offered wage ($128,000) is **below** the prevailing wage ($137,946), constituting a wage violation and a petition defect. Even if DOL certified at Level 2, USCIS and DOL's Wage and Hour Division may revisit the classification; the internal inconsistency between the duties and the level is a credibility and compliance risk.

**Recommendation:** Re-evaluate the wage level against the full duties. If the position is genuinely supervisory/senior with independent judgment, either (a) request a new PWD at the appropriate level and re-file the LCA, increasing the offered wage to meet Level 3 ($137,946) or Level 4 ($162,157); or (b) revise the position description to genuinely reflect Level 2 duties (remove "senior," supervisory, and independent-judgment language) — though this conflicts with the "Senior" title and the org chart. The current package is internally inconsistent on this point and should not be filed as-is.

### C. Experience Documentation and Date Discrepancies

**Issue 4 (Critical) — Résumé overstates employment end dates; unexplained gap.**

Cross-referencing the beneficiary's résumé against the two experience letters and the Employer Support Letter reveals material date discrepancies:

| Employer | Résumé dates | Experience letter / Employer letter dates | Discrepancy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kiran Software Solutions (Junior Software Developer) | June 2014 – **March 2017** | June 16, 2014 – **December 9, 2016** (Kiran letter; Employer Letter) | Résumé overstates end by ~3 months |
| NovaStar Data Systems (Machine Learning Analyst) | April 2017 – **August 2020** | April 2017 – **July 2020** (NovaStar letter; Employer Letter) | Résumé overstates end by ~1 month |

The résumé lengthens both tenures. Using the verified (letter) dates, there is an **unexplained gap of approximately four months between Kiran (ended December 9, 2016) and NovaStar (began April 2017)**. The résumé's March 2017 end date for Kiran appears to paper over this gap.

**Risk:** Date inconsistencies between the résumé and contemporaneous experience letters are a classic RFE trigger and can undermine the beneficiary's overall credibility. The unexplained gap may draw questions about status/location during that period.

**Recommendation:** Correct the résumé to match the verified letter dates. Account for the December 2016–April 2017 period (e.g., return to India, job search, etc.) and document status during that interval if relevant. Ensure all date references across the package are reconciled to the experience letters.

**Issue 5 (Critical) — No documentation for the independent consulting period (August 2020–August 2023).**

The Employer Support Letter and résumé both rely on a three-year period as an "Independent Machine Learning Consultant" (August 2020–August 2023), describing NLP pipelines, transformer fine-tuning, fraud-detection models, and LLM workflows. **No supporting documentation for this period is included** — there is no experience letter, no client letters, no 1099s or business tax records, no business registration, and no project descriptions. This is the single largest block of "progressive ML experience" cited and is entirely uncorroborated.

**Risk:** Self-employment is inherently difficult to document and is scrutinized. An uncorroborated three-year period that the petition relies upon for both the experience requirement and the "progressive experience" narrative is a significant evidentiary gap and a likely RFE target.

**Recommendation:** Obtain client letters (on client letterhead, signed by a supervisor/engagement lead) for each material engagement, describing duties, dates, and the beneficiary's role; provide 1099s or Schedule C/business tax returns; and provide any business registration. If the period cannot be adequately documented, re-evaluate whether the remaining verifiable experience (NovaStar + Brightfield) satisfies the 3-year minimum and the "progressive" characterization.

---

**Issue 19 (Housekeeping) — "8+ years progressive ML experience" overcounts non-ML period.**

The Employer Support Letter states the beneficiary has "over 8 years of progressive experience in software development and machine learning engineering." The Kiran period (June 2014–December 2016, ~2.5 years) was general **software development** (Java/Python applications, database management, basic reporting) per both the résumé and the Kiran experience letter — not machine learning. ML-specific experience is therefore approximately NovaStar (~3.3 yrs) + consulting (~3 yrs, undocumented) + Brightfield (~1.5 yrs) ≈ 7.8 years, and the "progressive" trajectory is interrupted by the software-development start and the undocumented consulting period.

**Recommendation:** Refine the characterization to distinguish software-development experience from ML experience, and avoid overstating the ML-specific duration.

### D. LCA Posting and Notice Compliance

**Issue 6 (Moderate) — Posting period is short of the 10-business-day requirement.**

Both the certified LCA (Section J) and the LCA Posting Notice state the notice was posted **March 10, 2025 through March 19, 2025**. Counting business days (Monday–Friday) over that span:

- Week 1: Mon Mar 10, Tue Mar 11, Wed Mar 12, Thu Mar 13, Fri Mar 14 (5)
- Weekend: Mar 15–16
- Week 2: Mon Mar 17, Tue Mar 18, Wed Mar 19 (3)

**Total = 8 business days.** 20 CFR 655.734(a)(1) requires that the notice be posted "for a total of 10 consecutive business days" in two conspicuous locations. The documented posting is short by two business days.

**Risk:** A deficient posting can invalidate the LCA notice attestation and expose the employer to DOL Wage and Hour enforcement; it is also a consistency defect USCIS may note.

**Recommendation:** Re-post for a full 10 consecutive business days before filing, retain evidence (photos/dated logs) of posting at two locations, and update the LCA notice attation dates accordingly. If the LCA must be re-filed to reflect corrected posting, coordinate with the PWD validity window (open through January 14, 2026).

The LCA Posting Notice states the notice is "posted in two conspicuous locations," but the certified LCA (Section J.3) documents only one location ("Employee common area / break room, first floor"). The regulation requires two conspicuous locations (or electronic notice where there is no physical worksite).

**Recommendation:** Document both physical posting locations and retain evidence of each.

**Issue 12 (Moderate) — Reporting structure described as permanent but is interim.**

The Employer Support Letter (Section 2) states the Senior Machine Learning Engineer "reports directly to the Chief Technology Officer (CTO)" as a settled arrangement. **Recommendation:** Clarify the reporting structure in the Employer Support Letter (note the interim CTO reporting and the planned VP level), or remove the forward-looking VP layer from the org chart if it will not be filled imminently. Inconsistency between the letter and the org chart on a basic organizational fact is avoidable.

**Issue 14 (Housekeeping) — Current title vs. petitioned title not reconciled.**

The résumé lists the beneficiary's current Brightfield title as **"Data Scientist"** (September 2023–present). ### F. Credential Evaluation Credibility

**Issue 9 (Moderate) — Evaluator's qualifications.**

The credential evaluation is signed by "Dr. Linda Farnsworth, Ph.D., Senior Credential Analyst," whose Ph.D. is listed as "Ph.D., Education — **Halford University**." Two concerns arise on cross-reference:

- **"Halford University" is not a recognized, regionally accredited U.S. institution.** If it is unaccredited or a non-traditional institution, the evaluator's own credential may be challenged, undermining the weight of the evaluation.
- **The evaluator's doctorate is in Education, not a technical field**, yet the evaluation opines on the equivalency of an engineering degree and (implicitly) its relevance to a machine-learning specialty.

Westbridge is represented as a NACES member, which lends some institutional weight, but USCIS evaluates the individual evaluator's qualifications, particularly where the opinion is central to the qualification prong.

**Recommendation:** Verify Halford University's accreditation status. Consider obtaining the supplemental evaluation recommended under Issue 1 from an evaluator who holds credentials in a relevant technical discipline (computer science, engineering, or data science) and who is willing to opine on field-of-study relevance.

### H. Beneficiary Status and Maintenance of Status

**Issue 2 (Critical) — F-1 OPT asserted without a documented U.S. degree; U.S. work history undocumented.**

The Employer Support Letter (Section 4) states the beneficiary "is currently maintaining valid F-1 Optional Practical Training (OPT) status and will transition to H-1B nonimmigrant status effective October 1, 2025." F-1 OPT eligibility, however, requires completion of a **U.S. degree program**. The only education documented anywhere in the package is the foreign B.Tech from JNTU Hyderabad (2010–2014). No U.S. degree appears in the résumé, the credential evaluation, or the transcripts.

This creates two possibilities, both problematic:

1. **A U.S. degree exists but is omitted.** If the beneficiary holds an unlisted U.S. degree (e.g., a U.S. master's in CS/ML), its omission is a major oversight — and, critically, a U.S. CS/ML degree would likely **resolve the field-of-study problem in Issue 1** and support STEM OPT. The package should be supplemented immediately.
2. **No U.S. degree exists.** Then the F-1 OPT assertion is inaccurate, and the basis for the beneficiary's current U.S. work authorization (at Brightfield since September 5, 2023, and previously at NovaStar in Dallas since April 2017) is unexplained. The beneficiary's entire U.S. employment history from 2017 forward lacks any immigration-status documentation in this package.

Either way, the package is internally inconsistent: it asserts F-1 OPT status while documenting only a foreign degree that cannot, by itself, support F-1 OPT.

**Risk:** A change-of-status request requires evidence of maintaining lawful status; an unsupported or inaccurate status assertion can result in denial of the change of status (requiring consular processing) or an RFE/NOID. The undocumented U.S. work-authorization history since 2017 is a significant gap.

**Recommendation:** Determine and document the beneficiary's actual current status and full U.S. immigration history. If a U.S. degree exists, include it (with transcripts and the corresponding I-20/EAD), which may also cure Issue 1. If the beneficiary is not on F-1 OPT, correct the Employer Support Letter and pursue the appropriate change-of-status or consular-processing path. Include all status-maintenance evidence (I-20s, EADs, I-94s, pay stubs) covering the period of stay.

## VI. Conclusion

The package presents a coherent, well-organized H-1B filing on its face, and the core elements (certified LCA, PWD, employer letter, credential evaluation, transcripts, and two experience letters) are present and largely internally consistent on identity, employer, wage, and position. However, the cross-reference review identifies **five Critical issues** that, if not addressed, create substantial RFE, denial, and compliance exposure: (1) the field-of-study mismatch and inadequate credential evaluation; (2) the unsupported F-1 OPT / undocumented U.S. status history; (3) a likely understated wage level with an offered wage below the level the duties support; (4) résumé date overstatements and an unexplained employment gap; and (5) the undocumented three-year consulting period. Several additional Moderate and Housekeeping items should be corrected to present a consistent and fully documented petition. Counsel should resolve the Critical items and address the Moderate items before filing.
